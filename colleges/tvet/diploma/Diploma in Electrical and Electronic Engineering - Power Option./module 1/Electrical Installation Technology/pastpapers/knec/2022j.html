(ii) State two causes of accidents in workshops.

(b) Describe the following fire extinguishers:

(i) carbondioxide;

(ii) water extinguisher;

(c) (i) List the tools and equipment required in steel conduit work.

(ii) Explain the care and maintenance of tools in a workshop.

(d) Explain the following as used in structured cabling:

(i) backbone cabling;

(ii) horizontal cabling.

Answer

This answer covers workshop safety wear and accident causes, describes fire extinguishers, lists tools for steel conduit work, explains tool care, and defines backbone and horizontal cabling.

(a) Workshop Safety Wear and Accident Causes

(i) Two Safety Protective Wear Items:**

  1. Safety Glasses/Goggles/Face Shield: Protect eyes from flying debris (drilling, cutting, grinding), chemical splashes, or electrical arcs.
  2. Safety Footwear (Boots): Protect feet from falling objects, sharp objects on the floor, and often provide electrical insulation or anti-static properties. Steel toe caps are common.
  3. Gloves: Various types protect hands - insulating gloves for electrical work, heat-resistant gloves for soldering/welding, cut-resistant gloves for handling sharp materials, chemical-resistant gloves.
  4. Overalls/Apron: Protect clothing and skin from dirt, oil, sparks, or chemicals.

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Causes of Accidents in Workshops:**

(See Answer to Q2(a)(i) in 2018n paper).

  1. Poor Housekeeping (slips, trips, falls).
  2. Incorrect Tool Use / Defective Tools.
  3. Lack of or Improper Use of PPE.
  4. Unsafe Electrical Practices / Lack of Isolation.
  5. Lack of Training / Inattention / Rushing.
  6. Improper Machine Guarding.

The question asked for two.

(b) Fire Extinguisher Descriptions

(i) Carbon Dioxide (CO₂):**

Contains liquefied CO₂ gas under pressure. When discharged, it emerges as a very cold gas and "snow," displacing oxygen around the fire and cooling the fuel. Identified by a black band/label and a distinctive discharge horn. Primarily used for Class B (flammable liquid) and Class E (electrical) fires. Leaves no residue but offers little post-fire cooling and can pose an asphyxiation risk in confined spaces.

(ii) Water Extinguisher:**

Contains water propelled by stored pressure (air/nitrogen) or a CO₂ cartridge. Works primarily by cooling the burning material below its ignition temperature. Identified by a solid red cylinder or red band. Suitable *only* for Class A fires (solid combustibles like wood, paper, fabric). **Dangerous** to use on electrical fires (risk of shock) or flammable liquid/fat fires (can spread the fire).

(iii) Dry Powder:**

Contains fine powder (e.g., sodium bicarbonate, potassium bicarbonate, monoammonium phosphate) propelled by stored pressure or a gas cartridge. Works mainly by chemically inhibiting the combustion chain reaction and forming a barrier between fuel and oxygen. ABC rated powder is versatile for Class A, B, and C fires and is safe for electrical fires (non-conductive). Identified by a blue band. Main disadvantage is the messy residue which can damage electronics and cause visibility issues during discharge.

(c) Steel Conduit Work: Tools and Maintenance

(i) Tools and Equipment for Steel Conduit Work:**

  • Measuring & Marking: Tape Measure, Pencil/Marker, Spirit Level, Square.
  • Cutting: Hacksaw (with fine-tooth metal cutting blades), Pipe Cutter (optional, for cleaner cuts).
  • Preparation: Pipe Vice (to hold conduit securely), Reamer or Round File (to remove internal burrs).
  • Threading (if using threaded conduit): Conduit Stock and Dies (matched to conduit size, e.g., 20mm, 25mm), Cutting Oil/Lubricant.
  • Bending (if forming bends on site): Conduit Bending Machine or Bending Springs (manual methods require skill).
  • Assembly & Fixing: Conduit Pliers (optional), Spanners (for locknuts/couplers), Screwdrivers (for fixing boxes/saddles), Drill and Bits (for fixing holes).
  • Cable Installation: Draw Wire/Tape or Fish Tape (for pulling cables).
  • Fittings: Conduit pipes, bends, elbows, tees, junction boxes (various types), couplers, saddles, male/female bushes, locknuts.

(ii) Care and Maintenance of Tools in a Workshop:**

  • Cleaning: Clean tools regularly after use to remove dirt, grease, metal filings, or chemical residues.
  • Storage: Store tools properly in designated racks, drawers, toolboxes, or shadow boards. Protect cutting edges and measuring surfaces. Keep tools dry to prevent rust.
  • Inspection: Regularly inspect tools for damage (cracked handles, mushroomed heads, worn tips/blades, damaged insulation). Report or replace defective tools immediately.
  • Lubrication: Apply oil or appropriate lubricant to moving parts (e.g., gears, hinges, screws) and metal surfaces prone to rust. Keep cutting fluid available for threading/drilling.
  • Sharpening: Keep cutting edges (chisels, drill bits, hacksaw blades - replace when dull) sharp for efficient and safe operation. Maintain file cleanliness with a file card.
  • Calibration: Ensure measuring instruments (torque wrenches, multimeters) are calibrated regularly according to manufacturer or workshop schedules.

(d) Structured Cabling Definitions

(i) Backbone Cabling:**

(See Answer to Q1(b) in 2021j paper). The part of the structured cabling system providing interconnection between key network locations, such as telecommunications rooms (TRs), equipment rooms (ERs), and entrance facilities (EFs). It includes vertical cabling between floors and horizontal cabling between buildings in a campus environment. Typically uses high-bandwidth media like fiber optic cable or high-pair-count copper cable.

(ii) Horizontal Cabling:**

(See Answer to Q5(a)(ii) in 2020j paper). The portion of the structured cabling system extending from the horizontal cross-connect (HC) in the telecommunications room (TR) on a floor out to the individual work area outlets (WAOs) or sockets. It includes the horizontal cables, terminations at the patch panel (HC) and the WAO. Usually installed in a star topology.

Workshop safety involves using PPE and understanding accident causes. Different fire extinguishers suit specific fire classes. Steel conduit work requires specialized tools for cutting, threading, bending, and assembly, while proper tool care ensures longevity and safety. Structured cabling uses backbone and horizontal subsystems for organized communication wiring.

(a) Outline the procedure of determining the cable size for an electrical installation. (5 marks)

(b) (i) List two types of cable joints.

(ii) State three I.E.E.E regulations requirements regarding electrical cables. (5 marks)

(c) (i) State two merits of metallic conduits.

(ii) Outline three I.E.E.E regulations requirements regarding conduits installation. (6 marks)

(d) (i) Outline two factors considered when selecting a wiring system for a particular electrical installation.

(ii) Draw a labelled cross-section diagram of a PVC sheathed cable twin with earth. (4 marks)

Answer

This answer outlines cable sizing procedure, lists joint types and cable regulations, states merits of metallic conduit and related regulations, outlines wiring system selection factors, and diagrams PVC Twin & Earth cable.

(a) Procedure for Determining Cable Size (Simplified - BS 7671)

  1. Determine Design Current (Ib): Calculate the current the circuit is expected to carry under normal operating conditions (Power / Voltage, considering diversity if applicable).
  2. Select Protective Device Type and Rating (In): Choose a suitable protective device (MCB, fuse) and its nominal current rating (In). Ensure In ≥ Ib.
  3. Determine Installation Method: Identify how the cable will be installed (e.g., clipped direct, in conduit, in trunking, buried) by referring to reference methods in BS 7671 Appendix 4.
  4. Apply Correction Factors: Identify and apply all relevant correction factors based on installation conditions:
    • Ca (Ambient Temperature)
    • Cg (Grouping - mutual heating from adjacent cables)
    • Ci (Thermal Insulation)
    • Cf (Protective device type - only 0.725 for rewireable fuses, usually 1 otherwise)
    • (Other factors like Cs for semi-enclosed fuses may apply).
    Calculate the total correction factor C = Ca * Cg * Ci * Cf * ...
  5. Calculate Minimum Required Tabulated Current (It): The cable must be chosen based on a tabulated rating (It) found in BS 7671 Appendix 4 tables, such that It ≥ In / C. (Note: Some sources use It ≥ Ib / C, ensure coordination rule Iz ≥ In is also met).
  6. Select Potential Cable Size: Look up the relevant table in BS 7671 Appendix 4 for the chosen installation method and cable type. Find the smallest conductor cross-sectional area (CSA) whose tabulated current rating (Iz, which corresponds to It under standard conditions) is greater than or equal to the calculated minimum required rating (It).
  7. Check Voltage Drop: Calculate the voltage drop for the selected cable size over the circuit length using the (mV/A/m) value from BS 7671 Appendix 4 tables ( Vd = (mV/A/m) * Ib * Length / 1000 ). Ensure the calculated voltage drop does not exceed the permissible limits (e.g., 3% for lighting, 5% for power). If it does, select the next larger cable size and recheck.
  8. Check Thermal Constraints / Fault Protection (Adiabatic Equation): Verify that the cable can withstand the energy let-through (I²t) of the protective device under fault conditions without excessive temperature rise (using the adiabatic equation S = sqrt(I²t)/k). Ensure disconnection times are met (related to Earth Fault Loop Impedance check).
  9. Final Selection: The chosen cable size must satisfy all conditions: current carrying capacity (after derating), voltage drop, and thermal constraints.

(i) Two Types of Cable Joints:**

  2. Tee Joint
  3. Crimped Splice/Butt Connector

(ii) Three IEEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Electrical Cables (BS 7671 Principles):**

  1. Selection and Erection: Cables must be selected and installed appropriately for the external influences they are likely to encounter (temperature, water, impact, UV radiation, corrosive substances etc.) and supported correctly to avoid damage. (Ref: Chapter 52).
  2. Identification: Cores of cables must be identifiable by colour (or numbering) according to the standards (e.g., Brown/Black/Grey for phases, Blue for Neutral, Green/Yellow for Protective Earth). (Ref: Regulation Group 514.3).
  3. Bending Radii: Cables must not be bent tighter than their minimum recommended internal bending radius during installation, to avoid damaging conductors or insulation. (Ref: Regulation 522.8.3).
  4. Protection Against Damage: Cables must be protected against mechanical damage, impact, abrasion, tension, and damage from fauna/flora throughout their run. (Ref: Regulation Group 522).

The question asked for three.

(c) Metallic Conduits Merits and Regulations

(i) Two Merits of Metallic Conduits (e.g., Galvanized Steel):**

  1. High Mechanical Protection: Offers excellent protection for cables against impact, crushing, and abrasion compared to PVC conduit or surface clipping.
  2. Earthing (Can act as CPC): The steel conduit itself can serve as the circuit protective conductor (CPC) provided it is correctly installed with appropriate fittings (e.g., threaded joints, bushes/locknuts) ensuring low resistance continuity throughout the run and is adequately sized.
  3. Fire Resistance: Provides a degree of fire resistance and containment compared to plastic systems.
  4. Screening: Can offer some electromagnetic screening for cables inside.

(ii) Three IEEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Conduit Installation (BS 7671 Principles):**

  1. Secure Fixing & Support: Conduit runs must be securely fixed and adequately supported using appropriate saddles/clips at specified intervals to prevent sagging and strain. (Ref: Regulation 522.8.5).
  2. Protection Against Corrosion: Metallic conduits installed in potentially corrosive environments must be suitably protected against corrosion (e.g., galvanized, painted, or appropriate material selection). (Ref: Regulation 522.5).
  3. Earth Continuity (Metallic Conduit): If used as a CPC, metallic conduit systems must ensure reliable electrical continuity throughout their length, including at joints and terminations (e.g., using appropriate fittings, locknuts, bushes). Resistance checks may be required. (Ref: Regulation 543.2.1).
  4. Prevention of Water Accumulation: Conduit systems installed where condensation or water ingress is likely should be arranged to allow drainage (e.g., drainage holes at lowest points). (Ref: Regulation 522.3).
  5. Cable Protection at Entries/Exits: Conduit ends must be fitted with bushes or suitably shaped openings to prevent abrasion of cable insulation during drawing in or in service. (Ref: Regulation 522.8.1).

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Explain the trickle charge method of charging a battery.

(ii) State two merits of an alkaline battery.

(b) A car battery of 6 cells is to be charged at 8 A from a 20 V d.c power supply. The terminal voltage of each cell is 1.9 V . Determine the resistance required for this charging current. Neglect the internal resistance of the cells. (6 marks)

(c) (i) Draw a circuit diagram of an open-circuit alarm system.

(ii) State three merits of the system in c(i).

(d) With aid of a circuit diagram, explain the operation of a wattmeter connected to a single phase supply.

Answer

This answer explains trickle charging, lists alkaline battery merits, calculates a charging resistor, diagrams/explains an open-circuit alarm and its merits, and diagrams/explains wattmeter operation.

(a) Trickle Charging and Alkaline Battery Merits

(i) Trickle Charge Method:**

(See Answer to Q2(c)(i) in 2018n paper). A method involving continuous charging at a very low current rate (e.g., C/50 to C/100, where C is Ah capacity). Its purpose is not to recharge a flat battery quickly, but rather to compensate for the battery's natural self-discharge rate, keeping it fully charged during long periods of storage or inactivity without causing significant overcharge or damage.

(ii) Two Merits of an Alkaline Battery (e.g., NiMH, NiCd vs Lead-Acid, or primary Alkaline vs Zinc-Carbon):**

Assuming comparison relates to rechargeable types (like NiMH vs Lead-Acid) or primary types (Alkaline vs Zinc-Carbon):

  • Higher Energy Density (Rechargeable NiMH/Li-ion vs Lead-Acid): Store more energy for a given weight or volume compared to traditional lead-acid batteries.
  • Longer Shelf Life (Primary Alkaline vs Zinc-Carbon): Primary alkaline cells retain their charge longer in storage compared to older zinc-carbon types.
  • Better Low-Temperature Performance (Some types): Certain alkaline chemistries perform better in cold conditions than lead-acid.
  • No Free Electrolyte / Less Maintenance (Sealed types like NiMH/NiCd/Li-ion vs Flooded Lead-Acid): Sealed alkaline types don't require checking electrolyte levels or topping up water.
  • Higher Voltage Output (Primary Alkaline vs Zinc-Carbon): Maintain a higher operating voltage under load for longer compared to zinc-carbon.

The question asked for two. Context is slightly ambiguous.

(b) Charging Resistor Calculation

Given:

  • Number of cells = 6
  • Terminal voltage per cell (Vcell) = 1.9 V (Treat as back EMF during charge)
  • Total battery voltage (Vbatt_total) = 6 cells * 1.9 V/cell = 11.4 V
  • Supply Voltage (Vsupply) = 20 V
  • Desired Charging Current (Icharge) = 8 A
  • Internal resistance (Rint) = Negligible (0 Ω)
  • Let Rseries be the required series resistance.

The voltage drop across the series resistor must be the difference between the supply voltage and the total battery voltage:

Vresistor = Vsupply - Vbatt_total = 20 V - 11.4 V = 8.6 V

Using Ohm's Law (V = I * R) for the resistor:

Vresistor = Icharge * Rseries

8.6 V = 8 A * Rseries

Rseries = 8.6 V / 8 A = 1.075 Ω

A resistance of 1.075 Ohms is required in series.

(c) Open-Circuit Alarm System

(i) Circuit Diagram:**

An open-circuit alarm triggers when a normally open circuit is closed.

(ii) Three Merits of the Open-Circuit System:**

  1. Simplicity/Low Cost: Generally simpler circuitry and potentially lower cost compared to closed-circuit systems, especially using basic sensors like pressure mats or trip contacts.
  2. Low Standby Power Consumption: No current flows through the sensor loop when the system is armed and undisturbed, consuming minimal power until triggered.
  3. Suitable for Specific Sensors: Ideal for sensors that naturally create a closed circuit upon activation, such as pressure mats or some types of panic buttons.

Main disadvantage: Not fail-safe; cutting the wire or a sensor failing open will *not* trigger the alarm.

The question asked for three merits.

(d) Wattmeter Operation and Connection

(See Answer to Q4(d) in 2019j paper for diagram).

Operation Explanation:**

A wattmeter measures electrical power (Watts). It typically contains two internal coils:

  1. Current Coil (CC): Has low resistance and is connected **in series** with the load, like an ammeter. The magnetic field produced by this coil is proportional to the load current (I).
  2. Voltage Coil (VC) (or Potential Coil): Has high resistance and is connected **in parallel** across the load, like a voltmeter. The current flowing through this coil (and thus its magnetic field) is proportional to the load voltage (V).

The interaction between the magnetic fields produced by both coils causes a deflecting torque on the meter's pointer (in analog meters) or is processed electronically (in digital meters). This torque/reading is proportional to the product of Voltage, Current, and the Cosine of the phase angle between them (Power Factor, Cos φ) for AC circuits: **Power = V × I × Cos φ**. In DC circuits, Cos φ = 1, so Power = V × I.

Trickle charging maintains battery charge. Alkaline batteries offer benefits like higher energy density or longer shelf life depending on type. Charging circuits require series resistors to limit current. Open-circuit alarms trigger when a sensor closes the circuit. Wattmeters use current and voltage coils to measure power.

(a) Accessories in PV Solar Installations

(i) Switches (DC Isolators/Breakers):**

(See Answer to Q8(c)(i) in 2016n paper). Used primarily for safely isolating DC components like the PV array, battery bank, DC loads, or inverter input for maintenance or emergency shutdown. Must be specifically rated for DC voltage and current due to the difficulty of interrupting DC arcs.

(b) PV Installation Tests and Wiring Systems

(i) Outline of Tests Carried Out in Completed Solar Installations:**

(See Answer to Q6(d) in 2019j paper for detailed list). Key tests include:

  • Continuity of Protective Conductors (Earthing/Bonding).
  • PV Array Insulation Resistance.

(ii) Two Types of Wiring Systems Used in PV Installations:**

  2. Specialized Solar Cable (Direct Run): UV-resistant, double-insulated DC cables designed specifically for PV array wiring, often run directly between modules and combiner boxes/controller, potentially clipped or in trays where protected.
  3. Trunking System: Used indoors (or sometimes outdoors if weatherproof) to manage multiple cable runs neatly, providing access and protection.
  4. (Standard internal wiring systems like PVC T&E clipped direct might be used for AC circuits powered by the inverter).

(d) Charge Controller Functions and PV Tracking

(i) Three Functions of a Charge Controller:**

  1. Overcharge Protection: Prevents the battery from being charged beyond its safe maximum voltage, which would damage the battery and shorten its life. It does this by regulating or stopping the current flow from the PV array when the battery is full.
  2. Deep Discharge Protection (Low Voltage Disconnect - LVD): Disconnects the connected DC loads when the battery voltage drops below a preset minimum level, preventing excessive discharge which damages the battery. (Not all controllers have load terminals/LVD).
  3. Prevent Reverse Current Flow: Stops current from flowing back from the battery to the PV array at night, which would discharge the battery. (Often done using blocking diodes or MOSFETs).
  4. (MPPT controllers add): Maximum Power Point Tracking: Optimizes the match between the PV array and the battery, ensuring the array operates at its maximum power output voltage under varying sunlight conditions, increasing energy harvest.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Tracking for a PV Module:**

(See Answer to Q7(a)(ii) in 2016n paper). A mechanical system that automatically adjusts the orientation (tilt and/or azimuth angle) of a PV module or array throughout the day to keep it pointed directly at the sun. This maximizes the incident solar radiation on the module surface, significantly increasing the total daily energy production compared to a fixed (non-tracking) module.

Switches and sockets provide isolation and connection points in PV systems. Completion tests verify safety and function. Conduit and specialized solar cables are common wiring methods. Charge controllers protect batteries and manage energy flow. Tracking systems maximize energy capture by following the sun.

Answer

This answer explains irradiance and insolation, distinguishes direct/diffuse radiation, lists solar conversions, outlines harvesting methods, diagrams a direct solar dryer, and states its merits.

(a) Solar Energy Terminology

(i) Irradiance (Solar Irradiance):**

(See Answer to Q6(a)(i) in 2016n paper). The instantaneous rate of solar power received per unit area on a surface, measured in Watts per square meter (W/m²). It represents sunlight intensity.

(ii) Insolation (Incident Solar Radiation):**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(i) in 2017n paper). The total amount of solar energy received on a surface per unit area over a specific period of time (e.g., a day or year). Measured in kWh/m²/day or similar energy units, representing the available solar energy resource over time.

(b) Radiation Types and Energy Conversions

(i) Distinction Between Direct and Diffuse Radiation:**

  • Direct Radiation (Beam Radiation): Solar radiation received directly from the sun's disc without being scattered by the atmosphere. It creates distinct shadows. Concentrating solar collectors primarily utilize direct radiation.
  • Diffuse Radiation (Sky Radiation): Solar radiation that has been scattered by atmospheric components (molecules, aerosols, clouds) before reaching the surface. It appears to come from all parts of the sky, not just the sun's disc. Flat plate collectors and PV panels can utilize both direct and diffuse radiation.

Total global radiation on a horizontal surface = Direct Horizontal Radiation + Diffuse Radiation.

(ii) Three Solar Energy Conversions:**

(See Answer to Q7(a)(i) in 2019j paper).

  1. Solar Thermal Conversion (to Heat)
  2. Solar Photovoltaic Conversion (to DC Electricity)
  3. Solar Chemical Conversion (e.g., Photosynthesis)

(i) Three Methods of Harvesting Solar Energy:**

(See Answer to Q6(d) in 2017n paper).

  1. Photovoltaics (PV Panels)
  2. Solar Thermal Collectors (Flat Plate, Evacuated Tube)
  3. Concentrating Solar Power (CSP - Troughs, Dishes, Towers)
  4. Passive Solar Design (Buildings)

  1. Improved Hygiene and Quality: Protects drying crops from rain, dust, insects, and animals compared to open-air sun drying, resulting in a cleaner, higher-quality product.
  2. Faster Drying Speed: The enclosed structure traps heat (greenhouse effect), raising the temperature and often creating airflow (convection), which accelerates the drying process compared to open sun drying.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Outline three:

(i) factors considered when mounting a P.V solar module;

(ii) maintenance procedures carried out on a P.V solar module.

(b) State three factors when sizing a:

(i) solar battery;

(ii) D.C - A.C solar inverter.

(c) A house has the following electrical appliances:

  • Two, 8 watts energy saving lamps used 6 hours per day
  • One, 60 watts coloured T.V used for 3 hours per day.
  • One, 3 watts mobile charger used for 2 hours per day.

If the system losses are 20%, determine the:

(i) total daily load energy demand;

(ii) total daily system energy requirements;

(iii) charge requirement if the system voltage is 12 V . (8 marks)

Answer

This answer outlines factors for mounting PV modules and their maintenance, lists factors for sizing batteries and inverters, and calculates energy demand, system requirements, and charge requirement for a given load profile.

(a) PV Module Mounting Factors and Maintenance

(i) Three Factors Considered When Mounting a PV Module:**

  1. Orientation (Azimuth): Modules should ideally face the equator (due South in Northern Hemisphere, due North in Southern Hemisphere) to maximize daily energy capture. Deviations may be necessary due to roof layout but reduce output.
  2. Tilt Angle: The angle relative to the horizontal. Optimal tilt depends on latitude and desired seasonal performance (e.g., steeper angle favors winter sun, shallower angle favors summer sun, latitude angle often used for year-round).
  3. Shading Avoidance: Mount modules in a location free from shading from trees, buildings, chimneys, or other obstructions, especially during peak sunlight hours (e.g., 9 am to 3 pm), as even partial shading drastically reduces output.
  4. Structural Integrity & Fixing Method: The mounting surface (roof, ground mount) must be strong enough to support the weight of the modules and mounting system, plus wind and snow loads. Appropriate, secure, and weatherproof fixings must be used.
  5. Accessibility & Airflow: Allow space for maintenance access if possible. Ensure some airflow behind modules to help dissipate heat, as high temperatures reduce efficiency.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Three Maintenance Procedures for PV Module:**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(ii) in 2016n paper).

  1. Cleaning of module surface.
  2. Visual inspection for damage (glass, frame, backsheet).
  3. Checking mounting security.
  4. Inspecting wiring and connections.

The question asked for three.

(b) Sizing Factors for Battery and Inverter

(i) Three Factors When Sizing a Solar Battery:**

  1. Total Daily System Energy Requirement (Wh): The amount of energy the battery needs to supply daily, including load demand and system losses.
  2. Days of Autonomy Required: The number of consecutive cloudy days the battery must power the loads without solar input.
  3. Maximum Allowable Depth of Discharge (DoD): The percentage of the battery's total capacity that can be safely used regularly without significantly shortening its life (e.g., 50% for lead-acid, 80-90% for lithium). Required Ah Capacity = (Wh/day * Autonomy Days) / (Voltage * Max DoD%).
  4. Temperature Correction: Battery capacity decreases at low temperatures; sizing may need adjustment for cold climates.
  5. Charge/Discharge Rate Limits (C-Rate): Ensure the battery can safely handle the expected maximum charge current from the controller and discharge current to the loads/inverter.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Three Factors When Sizing a DC-AC Solar Inverter:**

(See Answer to Q6(b)(i) in 2016j paper).

  1. Continuous Power Rating (W/VA): Must exceed the sum of the power ratings of all AC loads expected to run simultaneously.
  2. Surge Power Rating (W/VA): Must be high enough to handle the starting surge of motor loads (fridges, pumps).
  3. DC Input Voltage: Must match the nominal voltage of the battery bank (12V, 24V, 48V).
  4. AC Output Voltage/Frequency: Must match the requirements of the AC loads (e.g., 230V/50Hz).
  5. Waveform Type (Pure Sine Wave vs Modified Sine Wave): PSW needed for sensitive electronics/motors.

The question asked for three.

(c) Energy and Charge Requirement Calculations

Loads:

  • Lamps: 2 * 8 W * 6 h = 96 Wh/day
  • TV: 1 * 60 W * 3 h = 180 Wh/day
  • Charger: 1 * 3 W * 2 h = 6 Wh/day

System Voltage = 12 V

System Losses = 20% (Loss Fraction = 0.20)

(i) Total Daily Load Energy Demand:**

Load Energy = 96 Wh + 180 Wh + 6 Wh = 282 Wh/day

(ii) Total Daily System Energy Requirements:**

System Energy = Load Energy / (1 - Loss Fraction)

System Energy = 282 Wh / (1 - 0.20) = 282 Wh / 0.80

System Energy = 352.5 Wh/day

(iii) Charge Requirement if the System Voltage is 12 V:**

(Assuming "Charge Requirement" means the Ampere-hour capacity needed to be supplied by the battery daily to meet the System Energy Requirement).

Daily Charge Requirement (Ah) = System Energy (Wh) / System Voltage (V)

Daily Charge Requirement (Ah) = 352.5 Wh / 12 V

Daily Charge Requirement (Ah) ≈ 29.38 Ah/day

PV module mounting requires considering orientation, tilt, and shading. Maintenance involves cleaning and inspection. Battery sizing depends on energy needs, autonomy, and DoD, while inverter sizing depends on AC load power and surge needs. Energy calculations determine daily load and system requirements, which can be converted to Ampere-hour charge requirements based on system voltage.
