Answer

This answer outlines electrical safety measures, fire classes, diagrams DC and AC two-wire systems, and identifies the components and type of the power plant shown in Figure 1.

(a) Four Safety Measures When Working with Electrical Equipment

  1. Isolation and Lockout/Tagout: Always isolate the equipment from the power supply before starting work. Use appropriate isolators/breakers and apply locks and tags (LOTO) to prevent accidental re-energization.
  2. Test Before Touch: Use an approved voltage indicator or multimeter (checked on a known live source first) to confirm that the circuit or equipment is truly dead before touching any conductors or terminals.
  3. Use Insulated Tools and PPE: Employ tools with appropriate insulation ratings (e.g., VDE certified) and wear suitable Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), such as insulating gloves, eye protection, and safety footwear.
  4. Maintain Safe Working Area: Keep the work area clear, dry, and well-lit. Avoid distractions and ensure secure footing. Use insulating mats where necessary.
  5. Inspect Equipment Regularly: Check electrical equipment, cords, and plugs for damage (frayed wires, cracked insulation, bent pins) before use. Do not use damaged equipment.

The question asked for four.

(b) Four Classes of Fire and Examples

(Based on common UK/European classification)

  1. Class A: Fires involving solid organic materials like wood, paper, textiles, and plastics. (Example: Burning waste paper bin).
  2. Class B: Fires involving flammable liquids or liquefiable solids like petrol, oil, paint, fats, and waxes. (Example: Spilled petrol igniting).
  3. Class C: Fires involving flammable gases like natural gas (methane), LPG (propane, butane), hydrogen, and acetylene. (Example: Leaking gas cylinder catching fire).
  4. Class D: Fires involving combustible metals like magnesium, aluminium, sodium, and potassium. Require special dry powder extinguishers. (Example: Magnesium shavings burning).
  5. Class F (or K in US): Fires involving cooking oils and fats in deep fat fryers. Require special wet chemical extinguishers. (Example: Chip pan fire).
  6. Electrical Fires (Formerly Class E): Not a distinct class anymore, but refers to fires involving live electrical equipment. The electricity must be isolated first, then an appropriate extinguisher (COâ‚‚, Dry Powder) used.

This answer identifies lead-acid cell parts, indicators of full charge, explains constant voltage charging, calculates a charging resistor value, states fire alarm functions, explains closed alarm operation, and provides a wiring diagram for parallel push buttons controlling a bell.

(a) Lead Acid Cell Parts and Charge Indicators

(i) Three Parts of a Lead Acid Cell:**

  1. Positive Plate(s): Made of lead dioxide (PbOâ‚‚) when charged, supported on a lead alloy grid.
  2. Negative Plate(s): Made of spongy lead (Pb) when charged, supported on a lead alloy grid.
  3. Electrolyte: A solution of sulfuric acid (Hâ‚‚SOâ‚„) and distilled water.
  4. (Others: Separators - insulating porous material between plates, Cell Container/Case, Terminals/Posts, Vent Caps - for flooded cells).

The question asked for three.

(ii) Three Indications of a Fully Charged Lead Acid Cell:**

(See Answer to Q4(a)(i) in 2019j paper for details).

  1. Voltage: Reaches a stable peak value (e.g., ~2.1V+/cell resting, or ~2.3-2.45V/cell under charge) while charging current drops low.
  2. Specific Gravity (Flooded): Electrolyte density reaches a maximum stable value (e.g., 1.265-1.280).
  3. Gassing (Flooded): Vigorous bubbling of hydrogen and oxygen occurs as charging completes.

(b) Constant Voltage Charging and Calculation

(i) Constant Voltage Charging Method:**

(See Answer to Q3(b) in 2016n paper for diagram and explanation).

In this method, a fixed voltage is applied across the battery terminals. The charging current is initially high (if the battery is discharged) and automatically decreases (tapers) as the battery's voltage rises towards the charger's set voltage. It's often used for the absorption and float stages of charging.

(ii) Charging Resistor Calculation:**

Given:

  • Number of cells = 6
  • Terminal voltage per cell (Vcell) = 2 V (Assume this is the effective back EMF during charge)
  • Total battery voltage (Vbatt_total) = 6 cells * 2 V/cell = 12 V
  • Supply Voltage (Vsupply) = 20 V
  • Desired Charging Current (Icharge) = 5 A
  • Let Rseries be the required series resistance.

The voltage drop across the series resistor must be the difference between the supply voltage and the total battery voltage:

Vresistor = Vsupply - Vbatt_total = 20 V - 12 V = 8 V

Using Ohm's Law (V = I * R) for the resistor:

Vresistor = Icharge * Rseries

8 V = 5 A * Rseries

Rseries = 8 V / 5 A = 1.6 Î©

A resistance of 1.6 Ohms is required in series.

  3. (Others: Notification of emergency services, activation of other safety systems).

(a) Three IEE Regulation Requirements Regarding Consumer's Intake Point (BS 7671 Principles)

  1. Provision for Isolation: A main linked switch or circuit-breaker must be provided near the origin of the installation for isolating the entire supply (disconnecting all live conductors). This must be readily accessible. (Ref: Regulation Group 537.2).
  2. Overcurrent Protection: The supplier provides main overcurrent protection (cut-out fuse). The consumer's installation must also have appropriate overcurrent protection (e.g., main switch rating, MCBs/fuses in consumer unit) coordinated with the supply characteristics and downstream circuits. (Ref: Chapter 43, Regulation 533).
  3. Earthing Arrangement Connection: A Main Earthing Terminal (MET) must be provided, to which the earthing conductor, main protective bonding conductors, and circuit protective conductors are connected. The connection to the means of earthing (supplier's terminal or consumer's electrode) must be correctly made here. (Ref: Regulation Group 542.4, 543.2).
  4. Accessibility and Marking: The intake equipment (cut-out, meter, main switch) should be installed in an accessible location and clearly marked where necessary. (Ref: Regulation 513.1, 514.1).

(c) Three Heat Switch and Water Heater Parts

(i) Illustration of Three Heat Switch Operation Positions:**

(See Answer to Q4(b) in 2018n paper for diagram and detailed explanation).

In summary:

  • Low Heat: Connects Line to Element A only.
  • Medium Heat: Connects Line to Element B only (assuming B is higher power than A).
  • High Heat: Connects Line to Element A AND Element B (usually in parallel).

(ii) Four Parts of an Instantaneous Water Heater:**

  1. Heating Element: A high-power electrical resistance element that heats the water directly as it flows past.
  2. Heat Exchanger/Water Channel: The chamber or pipework through which water flows and comes into contact with, or close proximity to, the heating element to absorb heat. Often made of copper or stainless steel.

  5. Outer Casing: The protective enclosure housing the components.
  6. Water Inlet/Outlet Connections: Points for connecting cold water supply and delivering hot water.
  7. Power Connection Terminals: Point for connecting the mains electrical supply.

Answer

This answer covers causes of electrical fires, merits of cartridge fuses, how earthing is achieved, exceptions to earthing, diagrams an HBC fuse, and identifies the type and parts of the ELCB shown.

(a) Electrical Fires and Cartridge Fuse Merits

(i) Two Causes of Fire in Electrical Installations:**

(See Answer to Q1(b) in 2016n paper for more examples).

  • Overloading: Circuits or cables carrying excessive current overheat, potentially igniting insulation or nearby materials.
  • Faulty/Loose Connections: High resistance at poor connections generates localized heat, leading to fire.
  • Short Circuits/Earth Faults: Cause very high currents which can create intense heat and arcing, igniting materials.
  • Damaged Insulation/Cables: Allows conductors to touch, causing short circuits or leakage currents that can overheat.
  • Faulty Appliances: Internal failures in equipment can cause overheating or sparks.

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Merits of Cartridge Fuses (over rewireable):**

  • Accurate Rating & Performance: Manufactured under controlled conditions, providing more precise and reliable current ratings and operating characteristics (time/current curves).
  • High Breaking Capacity (HBC/HRC): Designed to safely interrupt very high fault currents without shattering or causing external arcing, due to the ceramic body and arc-quenching filler.
  • Non-Deterioration: The fuse element does not significantly degrade over time under normal operating currents, unlike rewireable fuse wire which can weaken with age and temperature cycling.
  • Safety & Convenience: Fully enclosed element prevents exposure to molten metal upon blowing. Easy and safe to replace (though requires replacement, unlike resetting a breaker).

The question asked for two.

(b) Achieving Earthing and Exemptions

(i) How Earthing is Achieved:**

Earthing involves creating a deliberate, low-impedance connection between specific parts of an electrical installation and the general mass of earth. This is achieved by:

  1. Connecting all exposed-conductive-parts (metal casings of Class I equipment) via Circuit Protective Conductors (CPCs) back to a central point.
  2. Connecting all extraneous-conductive-parts (metal pipes, structural steelwork) via Main Protective Bonding conductors back to the same central point.
  3. This central point is the Main Earthing Terminal (MET).
  4. Connecting the MET via an Earthing Conductor (Earth Lead) to the Means of Earthing (either the earth electrode system installed by the consumer, e.g., rods driven into the ground for a TT system, or the earth terminal provided by the electricity supplier for TN-S or TN-C-S systems).

This complete system ensures that under fault conditions, dangerous voltages do not persist on accessible metalwork.

(ii) Two Parts Exempted from Earthing:**

(See Answer to Q5(c)(ii) in 2016n paper for more examples).

  • Small isolated metal parts (screws, nameplates) unlikely to become live or be touched simultaneously with live/earthed parts.
  • Class II (Double Insulated) equipment (marked with concentric squares).
  • Equipment supplied by a SELV system.
  • Short lengths of metal conduit used purely for mechanical protection.

The question asked for two.

(c) Labelled Diagram of an H.B.C Fuse

(See Answer to Q5(b)(ii) in 2015j paper or Q1(d) in 2018n paper for diagram).

(d) Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker (Figure 4)

(i) Type of Circuit Breaker:**

Based on the typical appearance with a core balance transformer visible internally, this is a **Current Operated Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker (ELCB)**, more commonly known today as a **Residual Current Device (RCD)**.

(ii) Identification of Labelled Parts:**

  • X: Test Button - Used to manually trip the device to verify its mechanical operation.
  • Y: Sensing Coil / Core Balance Transformer (CBT) - The toroidal transformer through which the line and neutral conductors pass; detects current imbalance.

Electrical fires often result from overloading or faults. Cartridge fuses offer reliable high-current interruption. Earthing provides safety by creating a path for fault current, though some parts are exempt. RCDs detect earth leakage currents via a core balance transformer to rapidly disconnect the supply.

(i) Entrance Facilities (EF):**

(b) Procedure for Terminating Flexible Cord to a Three-Pin Plug (BS 1363 UK Type)

  1. Prepare the Cord: Carefully cut the outer sheath of the flexible cord back (e.g., 30-40mm) without damaging the insulation of the inner cores (Live, Neutral, Earth).
  2. Strip Inner Cores: Strip a small amount of insulation (e.g., 5-7mm) from the end of each inner core (Brown - Live, Blue - Neutral, Green/Yellow - Earth), exposing the conductor strands. Twist the strands tightly for each core.
  3. Open the Plug: Unscrew and remove the plug cover. Loosen the screws on the three terminal pins (Live, Neutral, Earth) and the cord grip screw(s).
  4. Fit the Cord: Pass the prepared cord through the plug's cord grip.
  5. Connect Cores to Pins:
    • Connect the **Green/Yellow** (Earth) core to the longest, top pin (Earth terminal - E). Ensure all strands are under the screw/clamp. Tighten securely.
    • Connect the **Blue** (Neutral) core to the left-hand pin (Neutral terminal - N). Tighten securely.
    • Connect the **Brown** (Live) core to the right-hand pin (Live terminal - L), which also houses the fuse. Tighten securely.
    Ensure no stray strands are bridging terminals and no bare conductor is visible outside the terminal. The insulation should reach close to the terminal.
  6. Secure Cord Grip: Arrange the cores neatly within the plug body. Position the cord so the outer sheath is firmly held by the cord grip. Tighten the cord grip screw(s) securely onto the outer sheath (NOT the inner cores) to prevent strain on the terminal connections if the cord is pulled.
  7. Check Fuse: Ensure the correct rated fuse (e.g., 3A, 5A, 13A, appropriate for the appliance) is fitted in the Live pin holder.
  8. Replace Cover: Refit the plug cover and tighten the fixing screw(s). Check the plug is secure and neat.

(c) Armoured Cable Identification (Figure 5)

(i) Identify the Cable:** This is a **Steel Wire Armoured (SWA)** cable, likely with PVC insulation and sheath (PVC/SWA/PVC).

(ii) Name Parts Labelled A, B, C, D:**

  • A: Outer Sheath (typically PVC)
  • B: Armour (Galvanized Steel Wires - SWA)
  • C: Bedding (material between inner sheath/cores and armour, e.g., PVC, LSF)
  • D: Inner Insulation (around individual conductors, typically PVC or XLPE) - Assuming D points to the insulation of one core. If it points to the conductor itself, it's the Conductor. If it points to the sheath covering the cores but under the bedding, it's the Inner Sheath. Based on typical diagrams, D is most likely **Inner Core Insulation**.

(iii) One Application:** Used for mains electricity supply (sub-mains, distribution circuits) where high mechanical protection is needed, suitable for direct burial underground, running externally on walls/trays, or in industrial environments. E.g., supplying power to outbuildings, machinery, distribution boards.

Answer

This answer explains the photoelectric effect (and clarifies its relation to PV), lists factors affecting PV output, illustrates module earthing, describes mono- and polycrystalline construction, and diagrams a hybrid AC/DC solar system.

(a) Photoelectric Effect and PV Output Factors

(i) Photoelectric Effect:**

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a material (typically a metal surface) when electromagnetic radiation, such as light, shines on it. For emission to occur, the photons of the incident light must have an energy greater than the material's 'work function' (the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface). The number of electrons emitted is proportional to the light intensity, and the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons depends on the light frequency.

While related (involving photon-electron interaction), the primary principle of solar cell operation is the **Photovoltaic Effect**, which generates a voltage within the semiconductor material itself, rather than electron emission *from* the surface.

(ii) Four Factors Affecting PV Solar Module Output:**

(See Answer to Q8(b) in 2014j paper for details).

  1. Solar Irradiance (Sunlight Intensity): Higher intensity = higher current output = higher power.
  2. Cell Temperature: Higher temperature = lower voltage = lower power.
  3. Shading: Partial shading significantly reduces output of module/string.
  4. Load / Operating Point: Output power depends on the connected load; maximum power is delivered only at the MPP.
  5. (Others: Angle of incidence, dirt/soiling, spectrum, age/degradation).

(b) Illustration of PV Solar Module Earthing

(See Answer to Q6(c)(i) in 2019j paper for diagram).

Essential for safety, connecting module frames and mounting structure via a Circuit Protective Conductor (CPC) to the main installation earth.

(c) PV Solar Module Constructions

(i) Monocrystalline:**

(See Answer to Q7(b)(ii) in 2016n paper for details).

Made from single-crystal silicon ingots. Characterized by uniform dark color and often cut corners. Offers highest efficiency and good low-light performance, but typically more expensive.

(ii) Polycrystalline:**

(See Answer to Q7(b)(ii) in 2016n paper for details).

Made from multiple silicon fragments melted and cast into blocks. Has a characteristic "marbled" blue appearance with visible crystal boundaries. Less efficient but generally cheaper to produce than monocrystalline.

(d) Block Diagram of AC/DC Solar Electric System

(See Answer to Q7(a) in 2016j paper for diagram).

The photovoltaic effect drives solar cells. Module output depends on irradiance, temperature, and shading. Earthing module frames is a key safety measure. Monocrystalline and polycrystalline cells differ in manufacturing, appearance, efficiency, and cost. Hybrid AC/DC systems use controllers, batteries, and inverters to power both types of loads.

(a) Solar System References

(i) Angle of Incidence:**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(ii) in 2016j paper). The angle between the sun's rays and the line perpendicular (normal) to the receiving surface. A smaller angle (closer to 0Â°) means more direct sunlight and higher energy intensity on the surface.

(ii) Diffuse Radiation:**

Solar radiation that reaches the Earth's surface after being scattered from the direct solar beam by molecules, aerosols, or clouds in the atmosphere. It comes from all parts of the sky, not just directly from the sun's disc. Flat plate collectors and PV panels can utilize both direct and diffuse radiation, while concentrating collectors primarily use only direct radiation.

(b) Solar Energy Conversions and Applications

(i) Two Types of Solar Energy Conversions:**

  1. Solar Thermal Conversion: Converting solar radiation into heat energy.
  2. Solar Photovoltaic (PV) Conversion: Converting solar radiation directly into DC electricity.

(ii) Two Applications for Each Conversion:**

  • Solar Thermal Applications:
    1. Domestic Water Heating (Showers, Taps)
    2. Space Heating (Buildings)
    3. Swimming Pool Heating
    4. Industrial Process Heat
    5. Solar Cooking / Crop Drying
    6. Electricity Generation via CSP (Concentrating Solar Power)
  • Solar Photovoltaic Applications:
    1. Grid-Tied Residential/Commercial Electricity
    2. Off-Grid Power for Homes/Clinics/Schools
    3. Water Pumping for Irrigation/Livestock
    4. Street Lighting / Signage
    5. Powering Remote Telecoms/Sensors
    6. Consumer Electronics (Calculators, Chargers)

(c) Solar Concentrators

(i) How Solar Concentrators Harvest Energy:**

Solar concentrators use reflective surfaces (mirrors) or refractive elements (lenses) to gather solar radiation over a large aperture area and focus it onto a much smaller receiver area. This increases the solar flux density (W/mÂ²) significantly, allowing much higher temperatures to be achieved at the receiver compared to non-concentrating collectors. The heat absorbed by the receiver is then transferred to a working fluid or used directly.

(ii) Three Types of Solar Energy Concentrators:**

  1. Parabolic Trough Collectors: Long, U-shaped mirrors focus sunlight onto a receiver tube running along the focal line.
  2. Parabolic Dish Collectors: Dish-shaped mirrors focus sunlight onto a point receiver at the focal point.
  3. Power Tower (Central Receiver) Systems: A large field of computer-controlled mirrors (heliostats) track the sun and reflect sunlight onto a central receiver mounted on top of a tower.
  4. Linear Fresnel Reflectors: Use long, flat or slightly curved mirror strips to approximate a parabolic trough, reflecting light onto an elevated receiver.

The question asked for three.

(d) Thermo-siphon Water Heating System (Figure 6)

(i) Identification of Parts:**

  • A: Cold Water Inlet (to the collector or storage tank, depending on exact layout shown - typically to bottom of collector or tank).
  • B: Solar Collector Panel (Flat Plate or Evacuated Tube type) - Absorbs solar energy.
  • C: Hot Water Outlet (from the collector to the storage tank, or from the tank to the point of use - typically from top of collector to top of tank).
  • (Other key parts not labelled might include: Storage Tank, Connecting Pipes, Mounting Frame).

(ii) How Part B (Solar Collector Panel) Operates:**

The solar collector panel (B) is designed to absorb incoming solar radiation and transfer that heat energy to the water (or heat transfer fluid) flowing through it. Sunlight passes through the transparent glazing (if present) and strikes the dark absorber plate inside. The absorber plate heats up significantly. This heat is conducted to the fluid flowing within tubes (risers) attached to the absorber. As the fluid heats up, its density decreases, causing it to naturally rise (thermosiphon effect) through the collector and up into the insulated storage tank located *above* the collector. Cooler, denser water from the bottom of the tank flows down into the bottom of the collector to replace the rising hot water, creating a natural circulation loop driven purely by density differences (temperature differences) without needing a pump.

Angle of incidence affects energy intensity, while diffuse radiation is scattered sunlight. Solar energy is converted mainly to heat or electricity for various applications. Concentrators use optics to achieve high temperatures. Thermosiphon systems utilize natural convection driven by solar heating in the collector panel to circulate water.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Outline the procedure of planning and designing a solar-electric system of a house. (6 marks)

(b) Explain each of the following as used in P.V solar installation design:

(i) total daily energy demand;

(ii) days of autonomy. (4 marks)

(c) Describe the procedure of topping the electrolyte for a solar battery during maintenance. (4 marks)

(d) Identify three possible causes for each of the following faults in a solar electric installation:

(i) battery does not charge when connected to a PV solar module;

(ii) lamps do not come ON even on a sunny day. (6 marks)

Answer

This answer outlines the planning/design procedure for a domestic PV system, explains key sizing terms, describes electrolyte topping-up, and lists possible causes for common PV system faults.

(a) Procedure of Planning and Designing a Domestic Solar-Electric (PV) System

  1. Load Assessment: Identify all electrical appliances (loads) to be powered. Determine their power rating (Watts) and estimate their daily usage duration (hours/day). Calculate the total daily energy demand (Watt-hours/day).
  2. System Type Selection: Decide if the system will be off-grid (standalone), grid-tied (feeding excess to grid), or hybrid (grid-tied with battery backup). Determine the required system voltage (e.g., 12V, 24V, 48V for off-grid DC).
  3. Site Assessment: Evaluate the installation site (usually rooftop) for available unshaded area, orientation (towards equator), tilt angle possibilities, structural integrity, and cable routing options.
  4. Insolation Data: Obtain average daily solar insolation data (Peak Sun Hours or kWh/mÂ²/day) for the location, ideally month-by-month, focusing on the least sunny period ('design month').
  5. PV Array Sizing: Calculate the required total Watt-peak (Wp) rating of the PV array needed to generate the total daily system energy requirement (Load Energy + System Losses) based on the available insolation during the design month. Select appropriate modules based on power, efficiency, size, cost, warranty. Determine series/parallel configuration for desired voltage/current.
  6. Battery Sizing (Off-grid/Hybrid): Calculate the required battery bank capacity (Ampere-hours, Ah) based on the daily system energy requirement, desired days of autonomy (storage for sunless days), maximum allowable depth of discharge (DoD), system voltage, and battery efficiency. Select appropriate battery type (flooded, AGM, Gel, Lithium).
  7. Charge Controller Sizing: Select a charge controller (PWM or MPPT) with voltage and current ratings (PV input Voc/Isc, battery voltage, charging current) exceeding the array/battery specifications, including safety margins.
  8. Inverter Sizing (if AC loads): Select an inverter with appropriate continuous and surge power ratings for the AC loads, matching DC input voltage to the battery bank, and providing the correct AC output voltage/frequency/waveform.
  9. Component Selection & Balance of System (BOS): Choose appropriate mounting structures, cables (sized for current and voltage drop), DC/AC isolators, fuses/breakers, combiner boxes, monitoring systems, and other necessary hardware.
  10. System Diagram & Documentation: Create a detailed wiring diagram (schematic) and installation plan. Document all component choices and calculations.

(b) PV Solar Installation Design Terms

(i) Total Daily Energy Demand:**

The sum total of electrical energy (measured in Watt-hours, Wh) consumed by all the individual electrical appliances (loads) connected to the solar installation over a typical 24-hour period. It's calculated by multiplying the power rating (W) of each appliance by its estimated daily operating time (h) and summing these values for all appliances.

(ii) Days of Autonomy:**

(See Answer to Q8(c)(ii) in 2019j paper). The number of consecutive days the system's battery bank is expected to supply the normal daily load energy requirements without receiving any charge from the PV array (due to prolonged bad weather). It's a key factor in sizing batteries for off-grid system reliability.

(c) Procedure for Topping Up Electrolyte (Flooded Lead-Acid Battery)

  1. Safety First: Wear appropriate PPE (eye protection, acid-resistant gloves). Ensure good ventilation and no ignition sources nearby.
  2. Check Charge State: Ideally, top up *after* the battery has been fully charged, as the electrolyte level rises during charging. If topping up a discharged battery, leave some space for expansion.
  3. Clean Battery Top: Clean any dirt or corrosion from the battery top and around the vent caps to prevent contaminants from falling into the cells.
  4. Remove Vent Caps: Carefully unscrew or pry off the vent caps or manifold cover.
  5. Inspect Level: Look inside each cell. The electrolyte level should cover the tops of the internal plates, typically reaching the bottom of the vent well or an indicator mark.
  6. Add Distilled Water: If the level is low, slowly add **only distilled or deionized water** using a clean funnel or battery filler bottle until the correct level is reached. **Never add acid.**
  7. Avoid Overfilling: Do not fill right to the top of the vent well; leave appropriate space for expansion during charging and temperature changes.
  8. Replace Vent Caps: Securely replace the vent caps or manifold cover.
  9. Clean Up: Wipe away any spilled water.

(d) Possible Causes for Solar System Faults

(i) Battery Does Not Charge When Connected to PV Module:**

  1. PV Array Issue: No/low output from panels (shading, dirty, damaged, disconnected), reverse polarity connection from array.
  2. Wiring/Connection Fault: Open circuit in wiring between array and controller OR controller and battery; blown fuse or tripped breaker in these paths; high resistance connection.
  3. Charge Controller Fault: Controller itself is faulty; incorrect settings for battery type/voltage; internal fuse blown.
  4. Battery Issue: Battery already fully charged (controller stops current); battery severely damaged or sulfated (unable to accept charge); internal open circuit.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Lamps Do Not Come ON Even on a Sunny Day:**

  1. Battery State: Battery completely discharged or damaged (despite sunny conditions, maybe load is excessive or battery failed); Battery disconnected or faulty connection.
  2. Load Circuit Fault: Blown fuse or tripped breaker protecting the lamp circuit; open circuit in wiring to the lamps; faulty light switch.
  3. Lamp Fault: Bulb(s) blown or faulty lamp fitting(s).
  4. Charge Controller Issue: Low Voltage Disconnect (LVD) feature activated (if lamps run via controller) and battery voltage hasn't recovered sufficiently; faulty controller load output.

The question asked for three.

Planning a PV system involves careful assessment of loads, site conditions, and available sunlight, followed by sizing of array, battery, and control gear. Autonomy dictates storage capacity. Battery maintenance, including correct watering, is vital. Troubleshooting faults requires systematically checking components from source (PV) through controls to storage (battery) and loads.
