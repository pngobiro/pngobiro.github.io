Question 1

(a) State one application of the following workshop tools and in each case describe one safety precaution to be observed while using the tool:

(i) combination pliers;

(ii) screw driver;

(iii) adjustable conduit pipe stock and die.

(b) Explain application areas of each of the following cables:

(i) PVC-insulated single core;

(ii) MIMS (Mineral Insulated Metal Sheathed - typo corrected);

(iii) Armoured cable.

(c) Explain how Lug terminations of cables is achieved.

(d) A friend of yours accidentally touches a live conductor in a workshop and becomes unconscious while still holding the conductor. Explain what you will do.

Answer

This answer details the applications and safety precautions for common workshop tools, cable types, lug termination procedures, and electric shock first aid.

(a) Tools: Applications and Safety Precautions

(i) Combination Pliers:

  • Application: Gripping, holding flat or round objects, bending wires, cutting soft wires.
  • Safety Precaution: Ensure handles are insulated if working near potentially live circuits. Do not use for cutting hardened steel wire as this can damage the cutting edges. Avoid excessive force that could cause slipping.

(ii) Screw Driver:

  • Application: Driving (inserting or tightening) and removing screws.
  • Safety Precaution: Use the correct type (e.g., flathead, Phillips, Pozidriv) and size of screwdriver for the screw head to prevent slipping and damage. Ensure the tip is in good condition (not worn or rounded). Never hold the workpiece in your hand while driving a screw; place it on a stable surface. Ensure the handle is insulated if working near electricity.

(iii) Adjustable Conduit Pipe Stock and Die:

  • Application: Cutting external threads onto metal conduit pipes to allow connection using threaded fittings.
  • Safety Precaution: Secure the conduit pipe firmly in a pipe vice before threading. Use appropriate cutting fluid/lubricant to ease cutting and preserve the die. Wear eye protection against metal filings. Keep hands clear of the rotating die holder. Ensure the die is sharp and correctly assembled in the stock.

(b) Cable Application Areas

(i) PVC-insulated Single Core: Commonly used for fixed wiring installations within conduits, trunking, or embedded in plaster for lighting, power circuits, and control wiring in domestic, commercial, and industrial buildings where mechanical protection is provided by the containment system.

(ii) MIMS (Mineral Insulated Metal Sheathed) Cable: Used in harsh environments requiring high fire resistance, mechanical protection, and durability. Applications include fire alarm circuits, emergency lighting, hazardous areas (petrol stations, chemical plants), high-temperature locations, and situations where resistance to oils and chemicals is needed.

(iii) Armoured Cable (e.g., SWA - Steel Wire Armoured): Used for mains power supply distribution, control circuits, and auxiliary power, especially where direct burial in the ground is required or where high mechanical protection against impact or crushing is necessary. Common in industrial sites, outdoor installations, and underground supplies to buildings.

(c) Lug Termination Procedure

Lug termination provides a secure and reliable connection point for larger cables, typically to busbars, circuit breakers, or equipment terminals. The general procedure involves:

  1. Cable Preparation: Accurately strip the cable insulation to the required length, exposing the conductor strands without damaging them. The length depends on the barrel depth of the selected lug.
  2. Lug Selection: Choose the correct size and type of lug (e.g., copper, aluminium) that matches the cable conductor size and material, and the terminal connection type (e.g., hole size).
  3. Conductor Insertion: Ensure all conductor strands are neatly gathered and fully inserted into the barrel of the lug. Check the inspection hole (if present) to confirm full insertion.
  4. Crimping: Use the appropriate calibrated crimping tool with the correct die set for the lug and cable size. Crimp the lug barrel onto the conductor according to the tool manufacturer's instructions (often involving one or more crimps along the barrel). A proper crimp creates a solid, low-resistance connection.
  5. Insulation (Optional but Recommended): If required, apply heat-shrink tubing or insulation tape over the lug barrel and the end of the cable insulation to provide electrical insulation and strain relief.
  6. Inspection: Visually inspect the crimp for quality (correct die marks, no excessive flashing, conductor visible in inspection hole). Check for mechanical security by gently pulling the cable.

(d) Electric Shock First Aid (Unconscious Victim Holding Conductor)

Safety First! Do NOT touch the victim directly while they are still in contact with the live conductor.

  1. Break Electrical Contact Safely:
    • Immediately switch off the power supply at the main switch, circuit breaker, or socket outlet, if possible and safe to do so.
    • If the power cannot be switched off quickly, use a dry, non-conductive object (e.g., a wooden broom handle, dry plank of wood, rubber mat) to push or pull the victim away from the live source, or push the live source away from the victim. Stand on a dry insulating surface if possible.
  2. Assess the Victim: Once clear of danger, check for consciousness, breathing, and pulse. Shout for help or call emergency services immediately (e.g., 999/112/911 depending on location).
  3. Check Breathing: Look, listen, and feel for signs of breathing for no more than 10 seconds. Tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway.
  4. Perform CPR (If Trained): If the victim is not breathing normally, begin Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) immediately if you are trained. Continue CPR until help arrives, the victim starts breathing, or you are unable to continue.
  5. Treat for Shock: Keep the victim lying down, warm (cover with a blanket), and elevate their legs slightly if there are no suspected spinal injuries. Do not give them anything to eat or drink.
  6. Monitor: Continue monitoring their breathing and pulse until emergency medical services arrive and take over.

Following correct procedures for tool use, cable selection, termination, and emergency response is crucial for safety in electrical work.

Question 2

(a) State any four factors that affect the choice of a wiring system.

(b) Explain how the following factors affect cable rating:

(i) ambient temperature;

(ii) type of excess current protection;

(iii) thermal insulation.

(c) A PVC twin and earth cable domestic installation consists of the following:

  • A 4-way consumer unit (ccu)
  • 4 lamps, each 100 W controlled by 1 switch, and 2 lamps each 15 W controlled by 2 switches. (Wattage corrected)
  • An immersion heater rated at 3 kW (Power corrected)
  • An electric cooker rated 12 kW plus a socket outlet on the cooker control unit
  • 6, 13 A socket outlets and a spur

Draw the wiring diagram for the above installation.

Answer

This answer outlines factors influencing wiring system selection, explains cable rating factors, and describes the circuit arrangement for the specified domestic installation, including component ratings and cable sizes.

(a) Factors Affecting Choice of Wiring System

Four factors include:

  • Durability & Mechanical Protection: The expected level of physical stress or potential damage influences the choice (e.g., conduit or armoured cable offer more protection than surface-clipped PVC cables).
  • Cost: The budget available impacts the choice of materials and complexity (e.g., PVC surface wiring is generally cheaper than steel conduit or MIMS).
  • Appearance: In visible areas, aesthetic considerations may favor concealed systems (e.g., embedded conduit) over surface-mounted ones (e.g., trunking or surface conduit).
  • Flexibility & Future Alterations: Systems like trunking or accessible conduit allow easier modification or addition of circuits compared to concealed or directly embedded cables.
  • Safety & Regulations: The type of environment (e.g., damp, hazardous) and compliance with IEE Wiring Regulations dictate permissible systems and materials.
  • Type of Building/Construction: The building structure (solid walls, partitions, suspended ceilings) affects the feasibility and ease of installing different systems.

(b) Factors Affecting Cable Rating

Cable rating (current carrying capacity) is reduced (derated) by factors that prevent heat generated by current flow from dissipating effectively.

(i) Ambient Temperature (Ca): Cables are rated based on a standard ambient temperature (e.g., 30°C in air). If the actual surrounding temperature is higher, the cable cannot dissipate as much heat, and its current rating must be reduced using a correction factor (Ca < 1) to prevent overheating and insulation damage.

(ii) Type of Excess Current Protection (Cf for rewireable fuses): Different protective devices have different operating characteristics. Rewireable fuses (BS 3036) require a higher current for a longer time to operate compared to cartridge fuses or circuit breakers. To ensure the fuse operates before the cable is damaged under sustained overload, the cable rating must be effectively increased, or rather, the required *tabulated* current rating is divided by a correction factor (typically 0.725 for rewireable fuses) before selecting the cable size. This ensures the chosen cable can withstand the higher potential overload current for the duration it takes the slower fuse to blow. For MCBs and cartridge fuses, this factor is typically 1.0.

(iii) Thermal Insulation (Ci): When cables are installed within or surrounded by thermal insulation material (e.g., loft insulation), heat dissipation is significantly restricted. This requires a substantial reduction in the cable's current carrying capacity using a correction factor (Ci < 1) to prevent excessive temperature rise within the insulation.

Other factors like Grouping (Cg - mutual heating of closely spaced cables) and Installation Method also significantly affect cable ratings.

(c) Domestic Installation Wiring Diagram (Description and Component Sizing)

A full graphical wiring diagram is complex to draw here. The description outlines the circuit arrangement and connections within the 4-way consumer unit (CCU).

Consumer Unit (4-Way): Contains the main switch and four Miniature Circuit Breakers (MCBs) protecting the final circuits.

  1. Lighting Circuit:
    • Total Load: (4 * 100W) + (2 * 15W) = 400W + 30W = 430W. Current (I) = P/V = 430W / 230V ≈ 1.87A.
    • Protection: Typically a 6A MCB (Type B).
    • Cabling: Typically 1.0mm² or 1.5mm² PVC Twin & Earth cable. Wired using loop-in method at ceiling roses or junction boxes. One switch controls the four 100W lamps, two separate switches control the 15W lamps individually.
  2. Socket Outlet Circuit (Ring Final Circuit):
    • Load: Supplies 6x 13A sockets + 1 spur. Designed to supply a floor area, assuming diverse load up to 32A.
    • Protection: Typically a 32A MCB (Type B).
    • Cabling: Typically 2.5mm² PVC Twin & Earth cable, wired in a ring starting and ending at the MCB terminals. The spur is taken from a socket on the ring or a junction box using the same cable size.
  3. Immersion Heater Circuit (Radial):
    • Load: 3kW (3000W). Current (I) = P/V = 3000W / 230V ≈ 13.04A.
    • Protection: Typically a 16A or 20A MCB (Type B). (16A usually sufficient).
    • Cabling: Typically 2.5mm² PVC Twin & Earth cable (or 4mm² if long run/derated). Wired as a radial circuit directly from the CCU to a dedicated isolator switch (e.g., double pole switch) near the heater.
  4. Cooker Circuit (Radial):
    • Load: 12kW (12000W) + Socket. Applying diversity (e.g., first 10A + 30% of remainder + 5A for socket) ≈ 10A + 0.3*(12000W/230V - 10A) + 5A ≈ 10A + 0.3*(52.2A - 10A) + 5A ≈ 10A + 12.7A + 5A ≈ 27.7A. Design current often taken higher for safety, e.g., 30-40A.
    • Protection: Typically a 32A or 40A MCB (Type B). (Often 32A is sufficient with diversity).
    • Cabling: Typically 6.0mm² PVC Twin & Earth cable (or 10mm² if 40A protection or long run). Wired as a radial circuit from the CCU to a Cooker Control Unit (CCU) near the cooker, which incorporates a switch and the socket outlet. A final connection (cooker tail) connects the CCU outlet to the cooker appliance.

The selection of wiring systems, cable sizes, and protective devices must comply with the latest IEE Wiring Regulations (BS 7671), considering all relevant derating factors.

Question 3

(a) Define the following terms as used in electric circuit protection:

(i) fuse rating;

(ii) fusing current;

(iii) fusing factor;

(iv) discrimination.

(b) Explain the significance of the following tests in an installation:

(i) earth electrode resistance;

(ii) polarity;

(iii) continuity test.

(c) (i) With the aid of a labelled diagram, describe the operation of a single phase current-operated Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker (ELCB) - now more commonly RCD.

(ii) Explain two drawbacks of the ELCB (RCD) in c(i).

Answer

This answer defines key terms related to circuit protection using fuses and explains the importance of fundamental electrical installation tests. It also describes the operation and drawbacks of a current-operated ELCB (RCD).

(a) Circuit Protection Definitions

(i) Fuse Rating: The current value, specified by the manufacturer, that the fuse is designed to carry continuously under standard conditions without deteriorating or melting. It represents the normal operating current capability of the fuse.

(ii) Fusing Current: The minimum current value at which the fuse element will melt and break the circuit. This value is always greater than the fuse rating.

(iii) Fusing Factor: The ratio of the minimum fusing current to the current rating of the fuse. (Fusing Factor = Fusing Current / Current Rating). It is always greater than 1. A lower fusing factor (closer to 1) indicates a faster-acting fuse for a given overload.

(iv) Discrimination (Selectivity): The ability of a protection system, involving multiple protective devices in series (e.g., upstream and downstream fuses or MCBs), to ensure that only the device nearest to a fault operates, leaving the rest of the circuit unaffected. This prevents unnecessary power outages to healthy parts of the installation.

(b) Significance of Installation Tests

(i) Earth Electrode Resistance Test: This test measures the resistance of the connection between the main earthing terminal of the installation and the general mass of earth, primarily determined by the earth electrode itself and the soil resistivity. Significance: A low earth electrode resistance is crucial for ensuring that, in the event of an earth fault, sufficient fault current can flow to operate the protective device (fuse, MCB, RCD) quickly, disconnecting the supply and minimizing the risk of electric shock.

(ii) Polarity Test: This test verifies that all single-pole switches, fuses, and circuit breakers are connected only in the line (phase) conductor, not the neutral, and that the centre contact of Edison screw lampholders is connected to the line conductor. Significance: Correct polarity ensures that switching off a device isolates the phase conductor, preventing accidental contact with live parts during maintenance (like changing a bulb). It also ensures protective devices operate correctly by interrupting the phase supply.

(iii) Continuity Test: This test confirms that conductors are electrically continuous (not broken) and have low resistance. It is particularly important for protective conductors (Circuit Protective Conductors - CPCs or 'earth wires') and main/supplementary bonding conductors. Significance: Ensures that the protective earth path is intact from all exposed conductive parts back to the main earthing terminal, providing a safe route for fault current and ensuring protective devices can operate under fault conditions. It also verifies the integrity of line and neutral conductors.

(c) Current-Operated ELCB / RCD (Residual Current Device)

(i) Operation and Diagram:

A current-operated RCD continuously monitors the balance of current flowing in the line (L) and neutral (N) conductors supplying a circuit or installation. It works on the principle of Kirchhoff's Current Law (current in = current out).

  • The line and neutral conductors pass through a toroidal (ring-shaped) core balance transformer (CBT).
  • Coil 1 (on L) and Coil 2 (on N) are wound on the CBT such that under normal conditions, the magnetic fluxes they produce are equal and opposite, cancelling each other out. No net flux is induced in the core.
  • A secondary sensing coil is also wound on the CBT, connected to a sensitive relay or electronic circuit that operates the trip mechanism.
  • Normal Operation: Current flowing to the load via Line (IL) returns via Neutral (IN). Since IL = IN, the fluxes cancel, and no voltage is induced in the sensing coil. The RCD remains closed.
  • Earth Fault: If a fault occurs causing current to leak from the line conductor to earth (e.g., through a person or faulty appliance casing), the current returning via the neutral (IN) will be less than the current flowing out via the line (IL). This imbalance (IL - IN = Ileakage) creates a net magnetic flux in the CBT core.
  • This changing flux induces a voltage in the sensing coil, which activates the relay/electronic circuit.
  • The relay operates the trip mechanism, rapidly opening the main contacts in both the line and neutral conductors, disconnecting the supply.
  • A test button creates a deliberate small imbalance to allow periodic checking of the trip mechanism.

(ii) Drawbacks:

  • Nuisance Tripping: RCDs can be sensitive to small, harmless earth leakage currents present in some appliances (especially those with heating elements or electronic filters) or accumulated leakage in large installations, causing unwanted tripping.
  • No Overload/Short Circuit Protection: An RCD provides earth fault protection only. It does *not* detect or protect against overloads (drawing too much current between L and N) or line-to-neutral short circuits. These conditions must be handled by fuses or MCBs installed upstream or within the RCD itself (in the case of an RCBO - Residual Current Breaker with Overcurrent protection).

Understanding protective device terminology and the purpose of installation testing is essential for ensuring electrical safety. RCDs provide vital additional protection against electric shock from earth faults but must be used in conjunction with overcurrent protection.

(a) (i) Explain how an electric shock occurs.

(ii) Draw a labelled diagram of main sections of PILC/SWA cable.

(c) Draw an alarm system for the protection of a three-storey building with each floor having two push buttons and a bell. Incorporate a main alarm bell and an indicator board.

Answer

This answer explains the mechanism of electric shock, details the construction of PILC/SWA cable, illustrates the Kenyan electrical grid structure, and describes a multi-floor alarm system.

(a) Electric Shock and Cable Construction

(i) How Electric Shock Occurs:

An electric shock occurs when a person's body becomes part of an electrical circuit, allowing current to pass through it. This typically happens under the following conditions:

  • Contact with both the line (phase) conductor and the neutral conductor simultaneously.
  • Contact with the line (phase) conductor and earth (ground) simultaneously, provided there is a path to earth (e.g., standing on a damp floor).
  • Contact with a faulty appliance's metal casing that has become live due to an internal fault, while also being in contact with earth.

The severity of the shock depends on several factors, including the magnitude of the current flowing through the body, the path the current takes through the body (e.g., across the heart is most dangerous), the duration of the contact, the voltage of the source, and the resistance of the body (which varies with skin condition - wet vs. dry).

Question 5

(a) Define the following terms with respect to solar energy:

(i) solar module,

(ii) array.

(b) Explain:

(i) using a diagram how a solar cell works;

(ii) four factors to be considered while choosing a solar panel for a domestic house.

(c) Explain three routine maintenance procedures that need to be carried on a solar charged lead acid battery.

Answer

This answer defines basic solar PV terminology, explains the working principle of a solar cell, lists factors for panel selection, and details routine maintenance for lead-acid batteries in solar systems.

(a) Solar Energy Definitions

(i) Solar Module (or Solar Panel): The basic building block of a photovoltaic (PV) system. It consists of multiple solar cells connected electrically (usually in series and parallel combinations) and encapsulated within a protective weatherproof frame (typically with glass on the front and a backsheet) to form a durable, installable unit that generates a specific voltage and current under sunlight.

(ii) Array: A collection of two or more solar modules electrically interconnected (in series strings and/or parallel combinations) and mechanically mounted together to achieve the desired voltage and power output for a specific application. An array forms the main power-generating component of a PV system.

(b) Solar Cell Operation and Panel Selection

(i) How a Solar Cell Works (Photovoltaic Effect):

A solar cell, typically made from silicon, utilizes the photovoltaic effect to convert sunlight directly into electricity. It consists of a P-N junction formed by doping silicon with different impurities.

(ii) Factors for Choosing a Domestic Solar Panel (Module):

  • Energy Needs & System Size: Calculate the household's daily energy consumption (kWh) to determine the total power output required from the array, which dictates the number and wattage of panels needed.
  • Available Roof Space & Orientation: The physical area available for mounting panels, its orientation (ideally facing the equator - North or South depending on hemisphere), and tilt angle affect the type and number of panels that can be installed efficiently.
  • Budget: The cost per watt varies between panel types (monocrystalline, polycrystalline, thin-film) and manufacturers. Balance performance and efficiency against the available budget.
  • Panel Efficiency: Higher efficiency panels generate more power per unit area, useful for limited roof space, but often come at a higher cost. Consider the efficiency rating (% sunlight converted to electricity).
  • Warranty & Manufacturer Reputation: Look for panels with long performance warranties (typically 25 years for power output) and product warranties (10-15 years for materials). Choose reputable manufacturers with a good track record.
  • Local Climate & Panel Type: Consider temperature coefficients (how efficiency drops with heat - relevant in hot climates) and performance in low light conditions. Polycrystalline panels sometimes perform slightly better in high temperatures than monocrystalline, though efficiency is generally lower.

(c) Routine Maintenance for Solar Lead-Acid Batteries

Three essential procedures include:

  1. Checking Electrolyte Level (for Flooded Batteries): Regularly (e.g., monthly or quarterly, depending on usage and type) inspect the electrolyte level in each cell. If the level is below the top of the internal plates or the indicator mark, top up *only* with distilled or deionized water. Never add acid. Ensure plates remain covered. (Sealed batteries like AGM or Gel do not require this).
  2. Cleaning Terminals and Connections: Periodically inspect battery terminals for corrosion (white or bluish powder). Disconnect terminals (Negative first), clean them and the cable clamps with a wire brush or terminal cleaner. Apply a thin layer of petroleum jelly or anti-corrosion spray before reconnecting (Positive first) to prevent future corrosion. Ensure all connections are tight.
  3. Visual Inspection & Voltage Check: Regularly inspect the battery casing for cracks, leaks, or swelling. Check that vents (on flooded batteries) are clear. Periodically measure the battery's resting voltage (after being idle for a few hours) with a voltmeter to get an indication of the state of charge (compare with manufacturer's specifications). Optionally, check specific gravity with a hydrometer for flooded batteries for a more accurate state of charge measurement.

Proper selection of PV components and regular maintenance, especially for batteries, are crucial for the longevity and reliable performance of a solar electric system.

Question 6

(a) Distinguish between:

(i) constant voltage charging and constant current charging;

(ii) self discharge and overcharge.

(b) Explain three indicators of a fully charged lead acid battery.

(c) Explain:

(i) two advantages of solar electric power over conventional generators;

(ii) three essential features for equipment at consumers intake point.

Answer

This answer differentiates between battery charging methods and conditions, describes indicators of a full charge, lists advantages of solar power, and identifies key features of a consumer's electrical intake point.

(a) Distinctions

(i) Constant Voltage vs. Constant Current Charging:

  • Constant Voltage (CV) Charging: The charging voltage is held constant at a specific level (e.g., float voltage or absorption voltage). The charging current starts high when the battery is discharged and gradually decreases (tapers) as the battery's state of charge increases and its internal resistance changes. Commonly used in float charging (maintenance) and the absorption stage of multi-stage charging.
  • Constant Current (CC) Charging: The charging current is held constant at a specific rate (e.g., C/10, where C is the battery capacity in Ah). The battery voltage gradually increases as it accepts charge. This method is often used in the initial bulk charging phase but requires careful voltage monitoring to prevent overcharging as the battery nears full capacity.

(ii) Self Discharge vs. Overcharge:

  • Self Discharge: The gradual loss of stored charge in a battery over time due to internal chemical reactions, even when the battery is not connected to a load. The rate of self-discharge varies with battery type, temperature (higher temperature increases rate), and age.
  • Overcharge: Continuing to force current into a battery after it has reached a full state of charge. This is harmful, especially to lead-acid batteries, causing excessive gassing (electrolysis of water into hydrogen and oxygen), overheating, loss of water (in flooded types), accelerated corrosion of positive plates, and potential damage or reduced lifespan.

(b) Indicators of a Fully Charged Lead-Acid Battery

Three common indicators are:

  1. Stable Voltage Reading: During the final stage of charging (absorption or float), the battery voltage stabilizes at the level set by the charger (e.g., around 13.8V-14.4V for a 12V system in absorption, or ~13.5V in float, depending on type and temperature) while the charging current drops to a very low, stable level (typically 1-2% of the Ah capacity). A resting voltage (measured after several hours off charge) of around 12.7V or higher (for a 12V battery) also indicates a high state of charge.
  2. Specific Gravity Reading (Flooded Batteries Only): Using a hydrometer, the specific gravity of the electrolyte in each cell reaches a maximum, stable value, typically between 1.265 and 1.280 (corrected for temperature), and is consistent across all cells. This is the most accurate indicator for flooded batteries.
  3. Gassing (Flooded Batteries Only): Towards the end of the charge cycle, particularly during the absorption phase or equalization charge, the battery will begin to gas freely (bubble) as the charging energy causes electrolysis of water. While some gassing is normal at this stage, excessive or violent gassing indicates overcharging.

(c) Solar Advantages and Intake Features

(i) Two Advantages of Solar Electric Power over Conventional Generators:

  • Renewable and Clean Energy Source: Solar power utilizes sunlight, an inexhaustible resource, and produces no greenhouse gas emissions, air pollutants, or noise during operation, unlike generators that burn fossil fuels (diesel, petrol).
  • Lower Operating Costs and Maintenance: Once installed, solar panels have no fuel costs. They are generally very reliable with minimal moving parts, requiring less maintenance compared to generators which need regular refueling, oil changes, and servicing of engine components.

(ii) Three Essential Features for Equipment at Consumer's Intake Point:

  • Main Isolator/Switch: A readily accessible switch (often double pole) capable of disconnecting the entire installation from the incoming supply for maintenance or emergencies.
  • Overcurrent Protection: A main fuse or circuit breaker provided by the electricity supplier (cut-out fuse) and/or the consumer's main switchgear to protect the installation and supplier's equipment from excessive current due to overloads or short circuits.
  • Metering Equipment: An electricity meter installed by the supplier to measure the energy consumed by the installation for billing purposes.
  • Earthing Arrangement Connection: The point where the supplier's earthing facility (if provided, e.g., TN-C-S, TN-S) connects to the consumer's main earthing terminal, or the connection point for the consumer's own earth electrode (TT system).

Understanding battery characteristics and charging methods is vital for system longevity, while appreciating the benefits of solar power and the layout of supply intake points provides context for installation work.

Question 7

(a) Define the following terms:

(i) photovoltaic effect;

(ii) photoelectric effect.

(b) Using diagrams explain how solar energy is harvested using the following:

(i) parabolic dish collectors;

(ii) flat plate collectors.

(c) Explain three factors that may change the output power of a solar system.

Answer

This answer defines the photovoltaic and photoelectric effects, explains the operation of parabolic dish and flat plate solar collectors with diagrams, and lists factors affecting solar system output.

(a) Definitions

(i) Photovoltaic Effect: The process of generating a voltage and electric current in a material (typically a semiconductor P-N junction) upon exposure to light (photons). It is the fundamental principle behind how solar cells convert sunlight directly into DC electricity.

(ii) Photoelectric Effect: The emission of electrons from a material (usually a metal surface) when light shines on it. The energy of the incident photons must be above a certain threshold (the work function of the material) to overcome the binding energy of the electrons. This effect is the basis for devices like photomultiplier tubes but is distinct from the photovoltaic effect used in solar cells.

(b) Solar Energy Harvesting Methods

(i) Parabolic Dish Collectors:

These collectors use a large, dish-shaped mirror with a parabolic cross-section to concentrate sunlight onto a receiver located at the dish's focal point. This high concentration generates very high temperatures at the receiver.

(ii) Flat Plate Collectors:

These are the most common type for solar water and space heating. They absorb both direct and diffuse solar radiation without concentration, achieving lower temperatures than concentrating collectors.

(c) Factors Changing Solar System Output Power

Three key factors include:

  1. Solar Irradiance (Insolation): The amount of solar power received per unit area (measured in W/m² or kWh/m²/day). Output power is directly proportional to the intensity of sunlight. Cloudy weather, haze, time of day, and season significantly affect irradiance and thus system output.
  2. Ambient Temperature & Cell Temperature: While sunlight is needed, excessive heat reduces the efficiency of most PV modules. As the module temperature increases above the standard test condition (STC) temperature (25°C), its voltage output drops, leading to a reduction in overall power output. This is quantified by the module's temperature coefficient.
  3. Shading: Even partial shading of a single cell within a module, or shading of one module in a string, can disproportionately reduce the power output of the entire module or string due to the series connection of cells and bypass diode operation. Trees, buildings, chimneys, and even dirt can cause shading losses.
  4. Angle of Incidence / Tilt & Orientation: Output is maximized when sunlight strikes the panel surface perpendicularly. Incorrect tilt angle for the latitude or non-optimal orientation (not facing the equator) reduces the effective irradiance received by the panel surface.
  5. Dirt, Dust, and Soiling: Accumulation of dust, dirt, pollen, or bird droppings on the panel surface blocks sunlight and reduces the amount of light reaching the cells, thereby lowering power output.

Different solar technologies harvest energy in distinct ways suitable for various applications. Optimizing system output requires considering environmental factors like irradiance, temperature, and shading.
