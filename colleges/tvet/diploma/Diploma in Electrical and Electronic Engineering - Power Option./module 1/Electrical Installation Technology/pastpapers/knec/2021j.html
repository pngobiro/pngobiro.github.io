(a) Structured Cabling System Explanation

A structured cabling system is a standardized and organized approach to installing building telecommunications cabling infrastructure (typically for data networks, voice, and multimedia). It uses a predefined architecture, specified media (e.g., twisted-pair copper, fiber optic), standardized connection interfaces, and defined subsystems (like entrance facilities, equipment rooms, backbone cabling, horizontal cabling, work area). This approach creates a flexible, reliable, and manageable cabling plant that can support current and future communication technologies, simplifying moves, adds, and changes compared to ad-hoc wiring.

(b) Two Components of Backbone Cabling

Backbone cabling provides the interconnection between key locations like entrance facilities, equipment rooms, and telecommunications rooms (floor distributors) within a building or across a campus.

  1. Backbone Cables: These are the actual cables used for the backbone runs. They can be high-pair-count copper cables (e.g., multi-pair Cat 5e/6 for voice), but more commonly today are fiber optic cables (multimode or singlemode) due to their higher bandwidth, longer distance capability, and immunity to electromagnetic interference. These cables run vertically between floors or horizontally between buildings.
  2. Cross-Connects (Main, Intermediate, Horizontal): These are points where backbone cables are terminated and interconnected, usually using patch panels or termination blocks located in Equipment Rooms (ER), Main Cross-connect (MC), Intermediate Cross-connects (IC), or Telecommunications Rooms (TR). They allow for flexible patching and connection between different parts of the backbone and horizontal cabling subsystems.
  3. (Others include: Termination Hardware, Miscellaneous Support Facilities like cable trays, conduits).

Answer

This answer outlines fire safety procedures, burn first aid, explains cable current rating and temperature effects, distinguishes joints/terminations, and provides cutting/material lists for the conduit layout (with assumptions on dimensions).

(a) Fire Safety and Burn Treatment Procedures

(i) Procedure for Saving Personnel and Property from Fire (RACE Principle):**

(See Answer to Q2(a)(ii) in 2018n paper for RACE details).

  1. Rescue: Remove anyone in immediate danger, if safe.
  2. Alarm: Activate alarm, shout "FIRE!", call emergency services.
  3. Contain: Close doors/windows (don't lock) to slow spread.
  4. Extinguish/Evacuate: Fight fire only if small, trained, and safe. Otherwise, evacuate immediately via safe routes to assembly point.

(ii) Procedure for Treating a Person with Burns (First Aid):**

Note: Applying 'smooth jelly' (like petroleum jelly) or creams is generally NOT recommended first aid for significant burns as it can trap heat and interfere with medical assessment.

  1. Safety: Ensure the scene and casualty are safe from further harm (e.g., stop the burning process, move away from hazard).
  2. Cooling: Immediately cool the burn under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 20 minutes. This helps reduce pain, swelling, and tissue damage.
  3. Remove Constrictions: Carefully remove any tight clothing, jewelry, or watches from the burned area *before* it starts to swell, unless stuck to the burn.
  4. Covering: Cover the burn loosely with a sterile, non-fluffy dressing or clean plastic film (like cling film) to prevent infection and keep the area clean. Do not wrap tightly.
  5. Warmth: Keep the casualty warm using blankets, avoiding the burned area.
  6. Pain Relief: Offer appropriate pain relief if available and casualty can take it (e.g., paracetamol).
  7. Medical Attention: Seek medical advice for all but minor superficial burns. Call emergency services for severe, large, deep burns, or burns involving face, hands, feet, genitals, or airways. Treat for shock if necessary.

(b) Cable Rating and Temperature Effects

(i) Current Rating of a Cable:**

The maximum electric current (in Amperes) that a cable can carry continuously under specified installation conditions (e.g., ambient temperature, installation method, grouping with other cables) without its conductor temperature exceeding the maximum permissible operating temperature limit for its insulation type. Exceeding this rating leads to overheating, potential insulation damage, reduced lifespan, and fire risk.

(ii) Effect of Temperature Rise in Cables:**

When current flows through a cable, heat is generated due to the conductor's resistance (I²R losses). This heat causes the cable's temperature to rise above the ambient temperature.

  • Increased Resistance: The electrical resistance of metallic conductors (like copper, aluminum) increases with temperature. This means for a constant current, more heat is generated as the cable gets hotter, potentially leading to thermal runaway if heat cannot dissipate.
  • Insulation Degradation: Cable insulation materials (like PVC, XLPE) have maximum safe operating temperatures. Exceeding these temperatures causes the insulation to soften, degrade, become brittle over time, or even melt, reducing its insulating properties and potentially leading to short circuits, earth faults, or fire.
  • Reduced Current Capacity: Because excessive temperature rise must be avoided, the amount of current a cable can safely carry (its rating) is limited by how effectively it can dissipate heat. Higher ambient temperatures or poor heat dissipation (e.g., grouped cables, covered in insulation) means the cable must carry less current to stay within its temperature limit.

(c) Distinction Between Joint and Termination

  • Joint: A connection made between two or more separate lengths of cable conductors to create a continuous electrical path. Joints typically occur within junction boxes or specialised enclosures and are intended to be permanent parts of the wiring run.
  • Termination: The prepared end of a cable conductor connected securely to an electrical terminal point. This occurs where a cable connects to equipment (e.g., switches, sockets, MCBs, motors, light fittings, busbars) or to another wiring system component (e.g., terminals in a junction box before a joint is made). It's the point where the cable 'ends' functionally within the circuit connection.

(ii) State four requirements of a P.M.E system.

Answer

This answer explains the functions of circuit breakers and contactors, lists fuse classes with fusing factors, states PME requirements, and diagrams/explains an electromagnetic overload trip mechanism.

(a) Functions of Protective Devices

(i) Circuit Breaker (e.g., MCB, MCCB):**

A reusable electrical switch designed to automatically interrupt an electrical circuit when it detects an overcurrent condition (either an overload - current slightly above rating for a period, or a short circuit - very high current instantly). Its function is to protect the circuit wiring and connected equipment from damage due to excessive current and prevent potential fire hazards. It can also be manually switched ON or OFF.

(ii) Contactor:**

An electrically controlled switch (essentially a heavy-duty relay) used for repeatedly switching a power circuit ON and OFF. It is typically operated by a control circuit applying voltage to its electromagnetic coil, which closes (or opens) the main power contacts. Contactors are not primarily protective devices against overcurrent (though they often work in conjunction with overload relays), but rather control devices for frequently starting/stopping high-power loads like motors, heaters, or large lighting banks.

(b) Fuse Classes and PME Requirements

(i) Two Classes of Fuses and Fusing Factors (BS 88 Examples):**

  1. Class gG (General Purpose - Full Range): Used for general protection of cables and distribution circuits against both overload and short circuit.
    • Typical Fusing Factor: Generally between 1.6 and 1.9 (meaning it will blow at 1.6 to 1.9 times its rated current after a specified time).
  2. Class aM (Motor Protection - Partial Range): Designed specifically for motor circuits. They have a time delay characteristic to withstand high motor starting currents (overload) but operate quickly under high short-circuit conditions. They provide short-circuit protection only and must be used with a separate overload protection device (like an overload relay).
    • Fusing Factor: Not typically defined in the same way as gG fuses, as they are not intended for overload protection. Their characteristic focuses on letting through starting current while clearing short circuits rapidly.
  3. (Other classes exist, e.g., gR/aR for semiconductors).

Note: Semi-enclosed rewireable fuses (BS 3036) have a fusing factor typically around 2.0 or higher, requiring a correction factor (0.725) in cable calculations.

(ii) Four Requirements of a P.M.E (Protective Multiple Earthing / TN-C-S) System:**

(Based on UK Electricity Safety, Quality and Continuity Regulations (ESQCR) and BS 7671 implications)

  1. Supplier's Neutral/PEN Conductor Earthing: The supplier's combined neutral and earth (PEN) conductor must be effectively earthed at the source (transformer) and at multiple points along the distribution network to maintain a low earth potential.
  2. Main Equipotential Bonding: The consumer installation must have effective main equipotential bonding conductors connecting extraneous-conductive-parts (metal water/gas pipes, structural steel etc.) to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET). This is critical because under certain fault conditions (e.g., loss of the supply neutral), bonding prevents dangerous potential differences appearing within the premises.
  3. Supplier Provided Earth Terminal: The electricity supplier must provide a suitable earthing terminal connected to their PEN conductor at the consumer's intake position.
  4. Suitability Check: PME terminals should generally not be used in specific high-risk situations unless special precautions are taken (e.g., some agricultural locations, construction sites with generators, marinas, caravan parks).
  5. PEN Conductor Integrity: The integrity of the supplier's PEN conductor is crucial for safety. Regulations require specific minimum sizes and protection against mechanical damage for these conductors.

The question asked for four.

(c) Electromagnetic Overload Trip Mechanism

(i) Labelled Diagram:**

(Note: Most common MCBs use a thermal element for overload and an electromagnetic element for short circuit. This question specifically asks for an electromagnetic *overload* trip, which is less common in standard MCBs but principle exists in some breakers/relays).

(ii) Operation Explanation:**

This type of trip mechanism uses the magnetic field produced by the load current flowing through a coil (electromagnet) to initiate tripping under sustained overload conditions.

  1. Load current flows through the electromagnet coil.
  2. Under normal conditions, the magnetic field produced is not strong enough to overcome a restraining spring and/or time-delay mechanism acting on an armature or plunger.
  3. When a sustained overload current flows, the magnetic field strength increases significantly.
  4. This stronger magnetic field starts to pull the armature/plunger.
  5. A time-delay mechanism (often an oil dashpot or similar viscous damper) slows down the movement of the armature. This prevents tripping on brief, harmless overloads (like motor starting currents).
  6. If the overload persists long enough, the armature moves sufficiently against the delay mechanism to engage a trip bar or latch.
  7. The trip bar releases the main contacts of the circuit breaker, interrupting the circuit.

The time taken to trip is typically inversely proportional to the magnitude of the overload current – a larger overload causes faster tripping. This mechanism provides overload protection, distinct from the instantaneous electromagnetic trip used for short-circuit protection in most MCBs (which usually lacks a significant time delay).

Circuit breakers protect circuits automatically, while contactors are for switching loads. Fuses are classified by characteristics like range and speed. PME earthing relies on supplier earthing and good consumer bonding. Electromagnetic trips use current-induced magnetism to operate breakers, with overload types incorporating a time delay.

Answer

This answer identifies causes for low PV module output, outlines battery and module maintenance checks, explains factors for battery installation, and gives reasons for correct cable sizing in solar systems.

This question seems misplaced in the OCR as Section B Q6, but addresses core Solar Installation topics often included in Section A or B.

(a) Four Causes of Low/No PV Module Power Output

  1. Insufficient Sunlight (Irradiance): Low light levels due to heavy cloud cover, time of day (early/late), or season.
  2. Shading: Obstructions like trees, buildings, chimneys casting shadows over part or all of the module surface.
  3. Dirt/Soiling: Accumulation of dust, dirt, pollen, leaves, or bird droppings on the module surface blocking sunlight.
  4. High Temperature: Excessive cell temperature reduces module voltage and overall power output (though current might slightly increase).
  5. Wiring/Connection Issues: Loose, corroded, or open-circuit connections in module junction box, connectors (e.g., MC4), or wiring to combiner/controller.
  6. Module Damage/Fault: Cracked cells, damaged backsheet, faulty bypass diodes, internal connection failures within the module.

The question asked for four.

(b) Maintenance Checks

(i) Solar Battery (Lead-Acid Example):**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(i) in 2016n paper for details).

  • Check/Clean Terminals & Connections (Tightness, Corrosion).
  • Check Electrolyte Level & Top Up (Flooded types, use distilled water).
  • Check State of Charge (Voltage/Specific Gravity).
  • Clean Battery Case/Top.
  • Ensure Adequate Ventilation.
  • Perform Equalization Charge periodically (Flooded types).

(ii) P.V. Solar Module:**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(ii) in 2016n paper for details).

  • Clean Module Surface (Remove dirt, dust, debris).
  • Visual Inspection (Glass, Frame, Backsheet, Junction Box for damage).
  • Check Mounting Security (Frame bolts, structure integrity).
  • Inspect Wiring & Connectors (Damage, tightness, security).
  • Check for Shading Issues.

(c) Factors for Installing a Solar Battery

(i) Location:**

  • Temperature Stability: Choose a location protected from extreme temperatures (both hot and cold) as this affects battery performance and lifespan. A cool, stable environment is ideal. Avoid direct sunlight or placing near heat sources.
  • Accessibility: Ensure the location allows easy access for regular inspection, maintenance (cleaning, watering), and eventual replacement.
  • Proximity: Keep cable runs between the battery, charge controller, and inverter as short as possible to minimize voltage drop and wiring costs.

(ii) Ventilation:**

  • Gas Dispersion: Lead-acid batteries (especially flooded) release flammable hydrogen gas during charging. The location MUST be well-ventilated (natural or forced) to prevent accumulation of this gas, reducing explosion risk. Avoid sealed enclosures without proper venting.

(iii) Safety and Security:**

  • Protection from Damage/Tampering: Locate batteries where they are protected from physical damage (impact, vibration) and unauthorized access or tampering, especially if accessible to children or the public. Use sturdy enclosures or dedicated rooms.
  • Spill Containment (Flooded): Provide secondary containment (e.g., acid-resistant trays or bunding) for flooded batteries to contain potential electrolyte spills.
  • Electrical Safety: Ensure terminals are protected against accidental shorting (e.g., using terminal covers). Keep flammable materials away. Post appropriate warning labels (Acid, Electrical Hazard, No Smoking/Flames).

(d) Four Reasons for Correct Cable Sizing in Solar Installations

  1. Safety (Prevent Overheating/Fire): Cables must be large enough to carry the maximum expected current (including safety factors) without overheating. Undersized cables can overheat, damaging insulation and creating a fire hazard. Protection devices (fuses/MCBs) must be rated to protect the chosen cable size.
  2. Minimize Voltage Drop: All cables have resistance, causing voltage to drop along their length (V=IR). Excessive voltage drop, especially in low-voltage DC systems (like 12V/24V solar), leads to significant power loss (P=VI=I²R) and inefficient operation (e.g., charge controller not receiving enough voltage, lights dim, inverter low voltage shutdown). Correct sizing limits voltage drop to acceptable levels (typically 2-5%).
  3. System Efficiency: Minimizing voltage drop and power loss (I²R) in cables improves the overall energy efficiency of the solar installation, ensuring more of the generated power reaches the batteries and loads.
  4. Component Performance: Devices like charge controllers and inverters have specific operating voltage ranges. Excessive voltage drop can cause them to operate inefficiently or even shut down (e.g., LVD activation due to voltage drop under load, not actual low battery).

Low PV output can stem from environmental factors or system faults. Regular maintenance of batteries and modules is crucial. Battery installation requires considering location, ventilation, and safety. Correct cable sizing prevents hazards, minimizes losses, ensures efficiency, and allows components to perform correctly.

Answer

This answer explains solar tracking and insolation, lists harvesting methods and thermal applications, outlines factors affecting flat plate collector heat absorption, and diagrams a flat plate collector.

(a) Solar Terminologies

(i) Tracking (Solar Tracking):**

(See Answer to Q7(a)(ii) in 2016n paper). A system that orients solar collectors (PV or thermal) towards the sun as it moves across the sky, maximizing the incident solar radiation on the collector surface and thus increasing energy capture compared to fixed systems.

(ii) Insolation (Incident Solar Radiation):**

(See Answer to Q8(a)(i) in 2017n paper). The amount of solar radiation energy received on a surface per unit area over a given period of time. Often measured in kWh/m²/day or Peak Sun Hours (PSH), it represents the available solar energy resource for a location.

(b) Harvesting Methods and Thermal Applications

(i) Three Methods of Harvesting Solar Energy:**

(See Answer to Q6(d) in 2017n paper).

  1. Photovoltaics (PV): Direct conversion to DC electricity using solar cells.
  2. Solar Thermal (Low/Medium Temp): Absorbing heat into a fluid (water/air) using flat plate or evacuated tube collectors.
  3. Concentrating Solar Power (CSP - High Temp Thermal): Using mirrors/lenses to concentrate sunlight for high-temperature heat, often for electricity generation via turbines.
  4. Passive Solar Design: Utilizing building design features (orientation, windows, thermal mass) to capture and use solar energy for heating, cooling, or lighting.

(c) Four Factors Determining Heat Absorbed in Flat Plate Collector

  1. Incident Solar Radiation (Insolation): The amount of solar energy (direct and diffuse) actually striking the collector's glazing per unit area. Higher insolation means more available energy to absorb.
  2. Collector Orientation (Tilt & Azimuth): The angle of the collector relative to the sun's position. Maximum absorption occurs when sunlight is perpendicular (0° angle of incidence) to the glazing. Incorrect tilt or azimuth reduces the effective incident radiation.
  3. Absorber Plate Properties (Absorptance & Emittance): The effectiveness of the dark absorber coating in absorbing incoming solar radiation (high absorptance, α) and its tendency to re-radiate heat back outwards (low emittance, ε). Selective coatings aim to maximize α and minimize ε.
  4. Heat Losses (Ambient Temp, Wind, Insulation): The rate at which absorbed heat is lost back to the environment through the glazing (convection, radiation), the back/sides (conduction), and fluid connections. Losses increase with higher collector temperature, lower ambient temperature, and higher wind speed. Better insulation and glazing (e.g., double glazing, low-e coatings) reduce losses, increasing net heat absorbed by the fluid.
  5. Fluid Flow Rate & Temperature: The rate at which heat is removed from the absorber by the circulating fluid. If flow is too slow, the collector gets very hot, increasing losses. If too fast, the fluid may not heat up sufficiently. The temperature difference between the absorber and the fluid also drives heat transfer.
