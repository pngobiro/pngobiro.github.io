Answer

This answer discusses advantages of copper and rubber in cables, diagrams MIMS cable and its merits, explains cost/safety factors in wiring system choice, reasons for using flexible conduit, diagrams busbar trunking, and lists its merits over conduit.

(a) Advantages of Cable Materials

(i) Copper:**

  1. High Electrical Conductivity: Copper has excellent conductivity (second only to silver), allowing it to carry more current for a given size compared to other common conductors like aluminum, minimizing energy loss as heat.
  2. Ductility and Malleability: Copper is easily drawn into wires (ductile) and can be bent or shaped (malleable) without breaking easily, facilitating installation. It is also readily soldered or crimped for reliable connections.

(ii) Rubber (as used historically or in specific modern compounds):**

  1. Flexibility: Natural and some synthetic rubbers offer excellent flexibility, making cables suitable for portable appliances or applications requiring frequent movement.
  2. Water Resistance: Certain rubber compounds offer good resistance to moisture ingress.
  3. Note: Traditional rubber insulation/sheathing had significant drawbacks (poor ageing, low temperature limits, poor oil resistance) and has largely been replaced by PVC, XLPE, EPR, CSP etc. for general wiring, though specialized rubber compounds are still used for demanding flexible cables.

(b) MIMS Cable and Advantages

(i) Labelled Construction Diagram of MIMS Cable:**

(See Answer to Q2(b) in 2019j paper).

(ii) Two Advantages of MIMS Cable:**

  1. Exceptional Fire Resistance: The inorganic mineral insulation (MgO) and metal sheath do not burn and can withstand very high temperatures for extended periods, maintaining circuit integrity during a fire. Ideal for fire alarm and emergency lighting circuits.
  2. High Mechanical Strength & Durability: The solid metal sheath provides excellent protection against impact, crushing, and abrasion. It is also impervious to water, oil, and many chemicals (especially with copper sheath).
  3. Small Diameter: Offers a relatively small overall diameter for its current rating compared to other armoured cables.
  4. Can Act as CPC: The metal sheath provides an effective circuit protective conductor.

The question asked for two.

(c) Wiring System Selection Factors and Flexible Conduit

(i) Factors Determining Type of Wiring System:**

  • I. Cost: The overall budget for the project significantly influences the choice. Material costs (PVC vs Steel conduit vs Trunking vs SWA cable) and labour costs (ease/speed of installation for different systems) are major considerations. Cheaper systems may be suitable for some applications but might lack durability or flexibility needed elsewhere.
  • II. Safety: The primary consideration. The chosen system must provide adequate protection against electric shock (proper insulation, earthing, containment), fire (appropriate materials, correct cable sizing, secure connections), and mechanical damage appropriate for the environment and usage. Compliance with safety regulations (e.g., BS 7671) is mandatory.

(ii) Two Reasons for Using Flexible Conduits:**

  1. Connection to Vibrating Equipment: To provide a final connection to machinery or equipment that vibrates during operation (e.g., motors, pumps). The flexible conduit absorbs the vibration, preventing stress and potential damage to rigid conduit connections or the enclosed cables.
  2. Navigating Difficult Routes/Offsets: Used for short runs where complex bends or offsets around obstacles would be difficult or impractical to achieve with rigid conduit bending techniques. Offers easier routing in tight or awkward spaces.
  3. Temporary Installations: Sometimes used in temporary setups where ease of installation and removal is beneficial.

The question asked for two.

(d) Busbar Trunking System

(i) Labelled Diagram:**

(See Answer to Q5(d) in 2020j paper).

(ii) Two Merits of Busbar Trunking over Conduit System:**

(See Answer to Q1(d)(ii) in 2016j paper).

  1. Flexibility for Tap-offs: Allows electrical loads (via distribution boards) to be easily connected at various points along the run using dedicated tap-off units without needing extensive modifications to the main run, ideal for changing layouts.
  2. Higher Current Capacity (Often): Busbar systems are designed to carry high currents efficiently with low voltage drop compared to running multiple large cables in parallel conduits.
  3. Faster Installation (Potentially): For distributing high power over long distances with multiple connection points, installing prefabricated busbar sections can be faster than installing equivalent capacity using multiple large conduits and cables.
  4. Reduced Space (Potentially): Can sometimes occupy less space than multiple large conduits needed for the same power capacity.

The question asked for two.

Copper offers high conductivity for cables, while specialized rubbers provide flexibility. MIMS cable excels in fire resistance and durability. Wiring system choice balances cost, safety, and other factors. Flexible conduit aids connection to vibrating equipment. Busbar trunking offers flexible, high-capacity power distribution, advantageous over conduit in certain applications.

(a) (i) Distinguish between ring and radial power circuits.

(ii) State two I.E.E regulations requirements regarding the circuits in (a)(i).

(b) Figure 1 shows the electrical layout of a plan of a room. A three gang, 1-way switch controls lamps A, B and C. Draw the wiring diagram. (Assume standard loop-in)

(i) earthing;

Answer

This answer distinguishes ring/radial circuits, states related regulations, provides a wiring diagram for the room layout, defines earthing terms, and diagrams an RCD.

(a) Ring vs Radial Power Circuits and Regulations

(i) Distinction:**

(See Answer to Q2(c)(ii) in 2021n paper).

  • Radial Circuit: Cable runs from the consumer unit (CU) protection device outwards to supply one or more points (sockets/appliances) in a line or branch, ending at the last point.
  • Ring Circuit (Ring Final Circuit): Cable runs from the CU protection device, loops through multiple socket outlets, and returns back to the *same* protection device terminals in the CU, forming a closed ring.

(ii) Two IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Ring/Radial Socket Circuits (BS 7671 Principles):**

  1. Protection Rating vs Cable Size:** For standard socket circuits:
    • Ring (common): Typically uses 2.5mm² cable protected by a 32A device (serving up to 100m² floor area).
    • Radial (common): Using 2.5mm² cable typically protected by a 20A device (unlimited points/area), or 4mm² cable protected by a 32A device (unlimited points, area often limited by voltage drop). (Ref: Appendix 15 guidance).
  2. RCD Protection:** Socket outlets up to 32A for general use by ordinary persons, or supplying mobile equipment outdoors, typically require additional protection by a 30mA RCD. (Ref: Regulation 411.3.3).
  3. Number of Spurs (Ring):** The number of unfused spurs from a ring should generally not exceed the number of sockets/fixed appliances connected directly to the ring. Fused spurs can be added more freely (protected by spur fuse). (Ref: Appendix 15 guidance).

The question asked for two.

(b) Wiring Diagram for Room Layout (Figure 1)

Assumes loop-in wiring at ceiling roses/light points. Switch D is 3-gang, 1-way controlling lamps A, B, C independently.

(c) Earthing Definitions

(i) Earthing:** The practice of connecting specific metallic parts of an electrical installation (exposed-conductive-parts like equipment casings, and extraneous-conductive-parts like pipes) and the neutral point of the supply system (at the source) to the main body of the Earth (ground), usually via electrodes. Its primary purpose is safety, particularly protection against electric shock under fault conditions.

(ii) Earth Lead (Earthing Conductor):**

(See Answer to Q5(a)(ii) in 2019j paper). The conductor connecting the installation's Main Earthing Terminal (MET) to the means of earthing (earth electrode or supplier's earth terminal).

(iii) Earth Continuity Conductor (Circuit Protective Conductor - CPC):**

(See Answer to Q2(a)(ii)(III) in 2016j paper). A conductor connecting exposed-conductive-parts of equipment back to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET), providing the path for fault current to flow under earth fault conditions.

(d) Labelled Schematic Diagram of Current Operated ELCB (RCD)

(See Answer to Q4(c) in 2017n paper or Q5(b) 2019j for diagram).

Ring circuits provide power via a loop, while radials run outwards. Both have specific regulatory requirements. Loop-in wiring is common for domestic lighting. Earthing involves connecting metalwork to earth via electrodes, leads, and CPCs for safety. RCDs detect earth leakage current imbalances.

(c) Structured Cabling Description and Components

(i) Description of 'Structured Cabling':**

(See Answer to Q5(d)(i) in 2019j paper).

A standardized, organized methodology for installing building communications cabling (data, voice, video) using specific types of media (copper twisted pair, fiber optic), defined connection interfaces, and a hierarchical star topology. It creates a flexible, manageable, and future-proof infrastructure supporting multiple applications.

(ii) Five Components of Structured Cabling:**

Structured cabling systems are typically divided into subsystems, which contain various components:

  1. Horizontal Cabling: Includes the cables (e.g., Cat 6 copper) running from the floor distributor (TR) to the work area outlets.
  2. Backbone Cabling: High-capacity cabling (fiber optic or multi-pair copper) connecting Entrance Facilities, Equipment Rooms, and Telecommunications Rooms vertically between floors or horizontally between buildings.
  3. Work Area Outlet (WAO): The socket/faceplate assembly where users connect their devices (computers, phones) to the horizontal cabling.
  4. Patch Panels / Cross-Connects: Termination points (in TRs, ERs) where horizontal and backbone cables are connected, allowing flexible interconnection using patch cords.
  5. Telecommunications Room (TR) / Equipment Room (ER): Dedicated spaces housing termination hardware (patch panels), network equipment (switches, routers), and potentially servers. The ER often houses main backbone connections and servers, while TRs serve individual floors.
  6. Entrance Facility (EF): The point where external service provider cables enter the building and connect to the internal backbone.
  7. Patch Cords / Equipment Cords: Short cables used for connecting devices to WAOs or linking ports on patch panels and network equipment.

The question asked for five components, which can be drawn from these subsystems.

Wattmeters measure power, ohmmeters measure resistance. Ammeters connect in series, voltmeters in parallel. Installation testing verifies safety and compliance, including continuity checks. Structured cabling provides organized communication infrastructure using standard components like horizontal/backbone cables, outlets, and patch panels within designated rooms.

Answer

This answer lists bell indicator types, explains trembler bell operation, outlines battery charging room precautions, diagrams trickle charging, and calculates the required resistance for a charging circuit.

(a) Bell Indicators and Trembler Bell Operation

(i) Three Types of Bell Indicator Elements:**

These are mechanisms used in annunciator panels to visually indicate which bell push or circuit has been activated.

  1. Pendulum Type (Drop Flag): An electromagnet releases a latch, allowing a flag or shutter (often attached to a pendulum) to drop into view. Requires manual reset. (See diagram Q3(b)(i) 2018n).
  2. Mechanical Replacement Type: An electromagnet moves an armature that pushes a flag into view. The flag may automatically reset when the coil de-energizes or require a separate reset mechanism. (See diagram Q3(b)(ii) 2018n).
  3. Latching Indicator Lamp: An indicator lamp (LED or incandescent) associated with each circuit that illuminates and remains lit (latched) when the circuit is activated, until manually reset.
  4. Dolly Type Indicator: A mechanical indicator resembling a small switch lever that flips position when activated.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Operation of a Trembler Bell (with Diagram):**

(See Answer to Q4(b) in 2016j paper for diagram and explanation).

Summary: Current energizes electromagnet -> Armature attracted, hammer hits gong -> Armature movement breaks contact -> Electromagnet de-energizes -> Spring returns armature, contact remakes -> Cycle repeats rapidly.

(b) Four Safety Precautions in Battery Charging Room

(See Answer to Q1(d)(i) in 2021n paper).

  1. Adequate Ventilation (prevent hydrogen buildup).
  2. No Smoking, Naked Flames, or Sparks (explosion risk).
  3. Use Correct PPE (Eye protection, acid-resistant gloves).
  4. Correct Polarity Connections (+ to +, - to -).
  5. Keep Area Clean & Dry; Neutralizing agent (baking soda) available.
  6. Use Insulated Tools.

The question asked for four.

(c) Trickle Charging and Resistor Calculation

(i) Labelled Circuit Diagram of Trickle Charging:**

Trickle charging uses a very low, constant current. This can be achieved with a simple voltage source and a high-value series resistor, or a dedicated low-current charger.

(ii) Variable Resistor Setting Calculation:**

Given:

  • Supply Voltage (Vsupply) = 30 V
  • Number of cells = 6
  • EMF per cell (Vcell) = 1.9 V (Treat as back EMF)
  • Total battery back EMF (Vbatt_total) = 6 * 1.9 V = 11.4 V
  • Desired Initial Charging Current (Icharge) = 4 A
  • Let Rvariable be the required variable resistor setting. Assume battery internal resistance is negligible.

The voltage drop across the variable resistor must be the difference between the supply voltage and the total battery back EMF:

Vresistor = Vsupply - Vbatt_total = 30 V - 11.4 V = 18.6 V

Using Ohm's Law (V = I * R) for the resistor:

Vresistor = Icharge * Rvariable

18.6 V = 4 A * Rvariable

Rvariable = 18.6 V / 4 A = 4.65 Ω

The variable resistor should be set to 4.65 Ohms.

Bell indicators show activation points visually. Trembler bells use an electromagnetic interrupter. Battery charging rooms require strict safety measures against explosion and acid hazards. Trickle charging maintains battery state. Series resistors are used to limit charging current based on voltage differences and Ohm's law.

Answer

This answer explains electric shock, outlines rescue procedures, states functions of common tools, discusses nuclear plant siting factors, and diagrams the electrical grid system.

(a) Electric Shock Explanation and Rescue Procedure

(i) 'Electric Shock' Explanation (Safety Reference):**

(See Answer to Q3(a)(i) in 2016j paper). An electric shock is the dangerous physiological effect caused by electric current passing through the body. From a safety perspective, it's a primary hazard associated with electricity. Contact with live parts can cause current to flow through the body to earth or between live conductors, leading to effects ranging from muscle spasms and burns to respiratory arrest and fatal cardiac fibrillation. Safety measures like insulation, earthing, isolation, and using RCDs are designed to prevent or mitigate the risk of electric shock.

(ii) Procedure for Rescuing Colleague in Contact with Live Wires:**

(See Answer to Q1(d) in 2017n paper - RACE principles apply).

  1. Safety First! Do NOT Touch Victim Directly:** Assess danger to yourself.
  2. Break Contact Safely:** Switch off power source if possible *and* safe. If not, use a dry non-conductive object (wood, rubber, dry cloth) to push/pull victim or wire apart. Stand on insulating material.
  3. Call for Help:** Shout for assistance, have someone call emergency services immediately.
  4. Check Response & Breathing:** Assess victim once clear. Open airway, check for breathing.
  5. CPR if Necessary:** If unresponsive and not breathing normally, start CPR if trained.
  6. First Aid for Injuries:** Treat burns (cool water), manage other injuries, keep warm, monitor until help arrives.

(b) Function of Workshop Tools

(i) Combination Pliers:** Multi-purpose tool for gripping flat/round objects, bending wire, cutting soft wire. (See Q1(a)(i) 2012).

(ii) Hacksaw:** Cutting metal (conduit, bolts, stock) using a replaceable fine-toothed blade held in a frame. (See Q2(b)(i) 2018n).

(iii) Screw Driver:** Driving (inserting/tightening) and removing screws. Different types (flathead, Phillips etc.) exist for different screw heads. (See Q1(a)(ii) 2012).

(i) Two Factors Considered When Selecting Site for Nuclear Power Station:**

  1. Proximity to large Cooling Water Source (Sea, River, Lake).
  2. Geological Stability / Low Seismicity.
  3. Low Population Density / Sufficient Exclusion Zone.
  4. Accessibility / Infrastructure for construction & transport.

(a) Regulations Governing Solar PV Technicians Licensing in Kenya

Licensing is primarily governed by the **Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA)** under the **Energy Act, 2019** and associated regulations:

(c) Charge Controller Functions and Types

(i) Two Functions of a Charge Controller:**

(See Answer to Q6(d)(i) in 2022j paper).

  1. Prevent Battery Overcharging.
  2. Prevent Deep Battery Discharge (Low Voltage Disconnect - LVD, if equipped).
  3. Prevent Reverse Current Flow (from battery to panel at night).
  4. Optimize Power Transfer (MPPT type).

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Types of Charge Controllers:**

  1. Pulse Width Modulation (PWM):** Simpler, less expensive type that connects the PV array directly to the battery during bulk charge and then uses rapid ON/OFF switching (PWM) to regulate the voltage during absorption phase. Most efficient when PV array nominal voltage closely matches battery voltage.
  2. Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT):** More advanced and efficient type. It decouples the array voltage from the battery voltage, allowing the array to operate at its Maximum Power Point (MPP) voltage (which varies with conditions). It then converts the power electronically to the required battery charging voltage. Significantly more efficient than PWM, especially with higher voltage arrays, in colder temperatures, or when battery SoC is low.

(d) Procedure for Installing a Domestic Solar PV System (Outline)

  1. Site Survey & Planning: Assess roof suitability (space, orientation, tilt, shading, structure), confirm load requirements, finalize system design (component sizing, layout). Obtain necessary permits.
  2. Mounting System Installation: Securely install the roof anchors/fixings and mounting rails according to manufacturer instructions and structural requirements, ensuring waterproofing.
  3. Module Mounting & Earthing: Mount the PV modules onto the rails, ensuring correct orientation and secure clamping. Install bonding jumpers between module frames and connect the array framework to the installation earth (CPC).
  4. DC Wiring (Array): Interconnect modules within strings (using MC4 connectors) and run string cables (appropriately sized DC solar cable) down from the roof to the DC isolator/combiner box location, usually via conduit for protection. Ensure correct polarity.
  5. Install Indoors Components: Mount the DC isolator(s), charge controller, battery bank (in suitable ventilated, secure location), inverter (if AC system), DC/AC distribution boxes/consumer units.
  6. Component Interconnection: Wire components together according to the system diagram: Array -> DC Isolator -> Controller PV Input -> Controller Battery Output -> Battery Isolator -> Battery Bank. Connect Controller Load Output -> DC Distribution/Loads. Connect Battery -> Inverter DC Input (via Isolator/Fuse). Connect Inverter AC Output -> AC Distribution. Ensure all connections are correct polarity and torqued appropriately.
  7. Earthing & Bonding Completion: Ensure all metallic components (controller case, inverter case, etc.) are correctly earthed via CPCs run back to the MET.
  8. System Checks (Pre-Commissioning): Perform dead tests: verify continuity of all conductors (especially CPCs), check polarity throughout DC side, measure insulation resistance of array and DC wiring.
  9. Commissioning: Systematically energize the system: connect array to controller, connect controller to battery, check charging operation. Connect loads/inverter and verify correct function. Measure operating voltages/currents (Voc, Isc, charge current, load current) and record results.
  10. Handover & Documentation: Provide the client with all manuals, warranties, test certificates, and explain system operation and basic maintenance.

PV technician licensing in Kenya is regulated by EPRA under the Energy Act. Cell output depends on irradiance and temperature. Solar cells use a P-N junction to convert light to electricity. Charge controllers (PWM/MPPT) protect batteries. PV system installation involves careful planning, mounting, wiring, component interconnection, testing, and commissioning.

(c) PV Solar Sizing Terms Explanation

(i) Total Daily System Energy Requirement:**

(See Answer to Q8(c)(i) in 2019j paper). The total daily energy (Wh) the source (PV/battery) must supply to cover load demand PLUS system losses.

(ii) Voltage Drop:**

(See Answer to Q8(c)(ii) in 2021n paper). The reduction in voltage along a cable due to its resistance (V=IR). Critical to minimize in low-voltage PV systems to avoid power loss and ensure component function.

(d) Load Demand and PV Module Calculation

Loads:

  • Lamp: 1 x 8W x 4h = 32 Wh/day
  • Refrigerator: 1 x 60W x 24h = 1440 Wh/day
  • TV: 1 x 40W x 3h = 120 Wh/day

(i) Total Daily Load Demand (Energy):**

Total Load Energy = 32 Wh + 1440 Wh + 120 Wh = 1592 Wh/day

(ii) Number of PV Modules Required:**

Given: PV Module Generation Factor = 3.4 (Wh/day per Wp rating)

N.B. Neglect system losses.

Required PV Array Watt-peak (Wp) = Total Daily Load Demand (Wh/day) / Generation Factor (Wh/day/Wp)

Required Wp = 1592 Wh/day / 3.4 Wh/day/Wp

Required Wp ≈ 468.2 Wp

Number of modules depends on the chosen module Wp rating. E.g., if using 150Wp modules:

Number = 468.2 / 150 ≈ 3.12 => **4 modules** are needed.

Regular battery maintenance and adherence to installation regulations are crucial. System failures often stem from wiring, battery state, or faulty components. PV system sizing involves calculating energy needs considering factors like voltage drop, while the generation factor helps estimate required panel wattage.
