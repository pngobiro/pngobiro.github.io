Answer

This answer provides reasons for IEE regulations, live working precautions, combustion conditions, completes the fire extinguisher table, details tool safety, and explains/diagrams the insulation resistance test for a lighting circuit.

(a) IEE Regulations and Live Working Precautions

(i) Three Reasons for IEE Regulations (BS 7671):

  1. Safety:** To provide requirements for electrical installations to ensure the safety of persons, property, and livestock from dangers like electric shock, fire, burns, and equipment damage arising from the use of electricity.
  2. Functionality:** To ensure electrical installations function correctly and efficiently for their intended purpose.
  3. **Standardization:** To provide a common technical standard for the design, erection, inspection, testing, and maintenance of electrical installations, ensuring consistency and quality across the industry.

(ii) Three Safety Precautions When Working on Live Electrical Circuits:**

Note: Working live should be avoided whenever possible and only undertaken when absolutely necessary, by competent persons, using specific procedures and precautions. Dead working is always preferred.

  1. Competence and Authorization:** Only trained, competent, and authorized personnel should undertake live working.
  2. Use of Insulated Tools and PPE:** Use tools specifically designed and rated for live working (e.g., VDE certified). Wear appropriate PPE, including insulating gloves rated for the voltage, eye protection (face shield often required), and potentially arc flash clothing.
  3. Insulating Barriers and Mats:** Use insulating barriers or shrouding to cover adjacent live or earthed parts to prevent accidental contact. Stand on an insulating rubber mat where appropriate.
  4. Correct Procedures:** Follow established safe working procedures, use approved voltage indicators to confirm presence/absence of voltage, avoid distractions, work methodically, and ideally have a second person present as a safety observer.

The question asked for three.

(b) Combustion Conditions and Fire Extinguishers

(i) Three Conditions for Combustion (Fire Triangle/Tetrahedron):**

  1. Fuel:** A combustible material (solid, liquid, or gas) that can burn.
  2. Oxygen (Oxidizer):** Usually oxygen from the air, needed to support the combustion process.
  3. Heat (Ignition Source):** Sufficient heat energy to raise the fuel to its ignition temperature and initiate the burning process.
  4. (Often added as the fourth element - Fire Tetrahedron) Chemical Chain Reaction:** The self-sustaining chemical process of burning. Extinguishers often work by interrupting one or more of these elements.

(c) Tool Safety Precautions

(i) Screwdrivers:

  1. Use the correct type (flat, Phillips, Pozidriv) and size tip to match the screw head to prevent slipping, which can damage the screw/workpiece or cause hand injury.
  2. Ensure the handle and shaft are intact and clean. Use insulated screwdrivers (e.g., VDE rated) when working near potentially live circuits. Never use a screwdriver as a chisel or pry bar.

(ii) Files:

  1. Always use a file with a securely fitted handle to prevent the tang (sharp pointed end) from causing hand/wrist injury.
  2. Apply pressure only on the forward stroke (cutting stroke) and lift slightly on the return stroke. Do not use excessive force. Keep files clean using a file card (wire brush) to prevent clogging (pinning).

(i) Circuit Diagram and Test Setup:**

This test checks the quality of the insulation between live conductors and between live conductors and earth.

(ii) Instrument Used and Reading Obtained:**

  • Instrument:** Insulation Resistance Tester (often called a "Megger" after a popular brand).
  • Reading Obtained:** The test measures insulation resistance in Megaohms (MΩ). For a healthy 230V circuit like lighting, the reading should be very high, typically >>1 MΩ (significantly higher values like >100 MΩ or "infinity"/Out Of Limits are common and desirable). BS 7671 specifies minimum acceptable values (e.g., ≥1.0 MΩ for circuits up to 500V). A low reading indicates an insulation fault.

IEE regulations ensure safety and functionality. Live working requires extreme caution and specific PPE/tools. Understanding fire conditions aids prevention. Proper tool use prevents injuries. Insulation resistance testing is a fundamental safety check for electrical installations.

Answer

This answer distinguishes between PVC and TRS cables, describes MIMS cable construction, outlines advantages of trunking, explains catenary wiring, and lists common conduit fittings.

(a) Distinction Between Cable Types

(i) P.V.C. (Polyvinyl Chloride) Cable: This refers broadly to cables using PVC as the primary insulating material around the conductors and often also as the outer sheath. PVC is a thermoplastic material.

  • Characteristics: Widely used due to its good electrical insulation properties, reasonable flexibility, resistance to moisture and many chemicals, flame retardancy, and relatively low cost. Common examples include PVC Twin & Earth (for fixed wiring) and PVC insulated single cores (for conduit/trunking). Its main limitation is its performance at very high or very low temperatures and its production of dense smoke and corrosive gases (HCl) when burning.

(ii) T.R.S. (Tough Rubber Sheathed) Cable: An older type of cable construction where the conductors were insulated with vulcanized rubber, and the outer protective sheath was made of a tough rubber compound.

  • Characteristics: Offered good flexibility and reasonable toughness. However, rubber degrades over time, becoming brittle and cracked, especially when exposed to heat, UV light, or oils. It has largely been superseded by more modern materials like PVC and elastomeric compounds (e.g., PCP/Neoprene, EPR/CSP like H07RN-F) which offer better durability, temperature range, and specific resistances. Rarely used or specified in new installations today.

Distinction Summary: The main difference lies in the insulating and sheathing materials. PVC is a thermoplastic widely used currently, while TRS used thermosetting rubber compounds and is now largely obsolete for general wiring due to degradation issues and better alternatives.

(b) Mineral Insulated Metal Sheathed (MIMS / MICC) Cable Construction

MIMS cable offers exceptional fire resistance and mechanical durability.

(c) Trunking Advantages and Catenary Wiring

(i) Four Advantages of Trunking over Conduit Wiring System:

(See Answer to Q4(b)(ii) 2017n and Q1(d)(ii) 2016j)

  1. Ease of Access/Modification: Removable lid allows easy laying, inspection, and addition of cables.
  2. Higher Cable Capacity: Accommodates more cables in a single run compared to typical conduit sizes.
  3. Reduced Installation Time (Potentially): Can be quicker for complex routes with many cables versus multiple conduit runs.
  4. Less Cable Stress During Installation: Cables are laid in, not pulled through bends.

The question asked for four.

(ii) Catenary Wiring System Explanation:

(See Answer to Q4(c) 2017n for diagram and detailed explanation).

In summary: A system using a tensioned steel support wire (catenary) stretched between anchor points, from which electrical cables are suspended using hangers or clips. Used for overhead cable runs across large open spaces like workshops or factories, providing support without continuous structures underneath.

(d) Six Conduit Fittings Used in Installation

  1. Coupler/Coupling: Sleeve used to join two straight lengths of conduit together end-to-end.
  2. Bend/Elbow: Pre-formed curved fitting used to change the direction of the conduit run (e.g., 90 degrees, 45 degrees). Inspection bends have removable covers.
  3. Junction Box (Terminal Box, Through Box, Angle Box, Tee Box, Intersection Box): Enclosure fitted within the conduit run to allow access for pulling cables, making connections (joints), or changing direction (Tee, Angle, Intersection types).
  4. Adaptor (Male/Female Bush): Fitting used to connect conduit to an enclosure (e.g., consumer unit, switch box) or to another type of fitting, often providing a threaded connection. Bushes protect cable insulation from sharp conduit edges.
  5. Saddle/Spacer Bar Saddle: Clip used to securely fasten surface-mounted conduit to a wall or structure. Spacer bar saddles provide a small gap behind the conduit.
  6. Lock Ring/Nut: Threaded ring used in conjunction with a male bush to secure conduit firmly to the wall of an enclosure or adaptable box.
  7. Reducers: Fittings used to connect conduits of different diameters.

PVC and TRS cables differ mainly in their materials and longevity. MIMS cable offers high durability and fire resistance. Trunking provides easier access and higher capacity than conduit in many cases. Catenary wiring is used for overhead spans. Various fittings are essential for constructing a complete conduit system.

(a) (i) Name three constituents of a gas electric power plant.

(ii) Outline three advantages of a steam electric power station.

(b) Explain the function of a surge tank in a hydro-power station.

(c) (i) Draw a line diagram showing the sequence of control at the consumers intake point.

(ii) List two final circuits and indicate their fuse ratings and cable sizes.

(d) Explain the two insulation resistance tests carried out in an electrical installation.

Answer

This answer identifies gas power plant constituents, outlines steam power advantages, explains surge tank function, diagrams consumer intake control, lists final circuit examples, and explains insulation resistance tests.

(a) Gas and Steam Power Plants

(i) Three Constituents of a Gas Turbine Electric Power Plant:

  1. Compressor: Draws in ambient air and compresses it to high pressure.
  2. Combustion Chamber: Mixes the compressed air with fuel (typically natural gas or liquid fuel) and ignites the mixture, producing high-temperature, high-pressure combustion gases.
  3. Gas Turbine: The hot combustion gases expand through the turbine blades, causing the turbine to rotate at high speed.
  4. Generator: Coupled to the turbine shaft, converts the rotational mechanical energy into electrical energy.
  5. (Often used in Combined Cycle): Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG): Uses the hot exhaust gases from the gas turbine to boil water and create steam.
  6. (If Combined Cycle): Steam Turbine & Generator: The steam produced by the HRSG drives a separate steam turbine coupled to another generator, increasing overall efficiency.

The core components are Compressor, Combustion Chamber, and Gas Turbine (+ Generator).

(ii) Three Advantages of a Steam Electric Power Station (Thermal - Coal/Oil/Gas Fired):

  1. Fuel Flexibility (Historically): Can be designed to utilize various fuels like coal, oil, natural gas, or biomass, depending on availability and cost (though modern designs are often fuel-specific).
  2. High Power Output / Scalability: Steam power plants can be built to very large capacities (hundreds or thousands of Megawatts), suitable for providing base-load power to large populations.
  3. Mature Technology: The Rankine cycle (steam cycle) technology is well-established and understood, with extensive operational experience worldwide.
  4. Relatively Lower Cost per MW (Historically for large coal plants): For very large base-load plants, the capital cost per megawatt could be competitive, although this is changing rapidly with renewables and gas prices, and doesn't account for environmental externalities.

The question asked for three.

(b) Function of a Surge Tank in a Hydro-Power Station

A surge tank (or surge chamber) is typically incorporated in hydro stations with long penstocks (the pipes carrying water from the reservoir to the turbines). Its primary functions are:

  • To Absorb Water Hammer Effects: When the turbine gates are closed quickly (e.g., due to sudden load rejection), the rapidly decelerating water column in the long penstock creates a high-pressure surge (water hammer) that could damage the penstock or turbine. The surge tank provides a reservoir into which the water can surge upwards, absorbing the pressure wave.
  • To Supply Water During Load Increase: When the turbine gates are opened quickly to meet increased load demand, the water in the penstock takes time to accelerate. The surge tank provides an immediate supply of water close to the turbine, preventing a negative pressure surge (water column separation) and allowing the turbine output to increase more rapidly until flow from the main reservoir catches up.

Essentially, it acts as a hydraulic shock absorber and a temporary storage/supply point close to the turbine to manage pressure fluctuations caused by rapid changes in water flow rate in long penstocks.

(d) Two Insulation Resistance Tests

Insulation resistance tests are performed with the circuit DE-ENERGIZED and measure the resistance of the insulation material surrounding conductors. A high resistance indicates good insulation.

  1. Test Between Live Conductors: Measures the insulation resistance between the phase (Line) conductor(s) and the Neutral conductor.
    • Procedure: Isolate the circuit. Connect the test instrument leads between the Line and Neutral conductors at the origin (e.g., consumer unit). Apply the test voltage (typically 500V DC for 230V circuits).
    • Purpose: To verify there is no breakdown or low resistance path (potential short circuit) between the Line and Neutral conductors due to damaged insulation.
  2. Test Between Live Conductors and Earth: Measures the insulation resistance between the phase (Line) conductor(s) AND the Neutral conductor (connected together temporarily for this test) and the installation's protective earthing system (CPC/MET).
    • Procedure: Isolate the circuit. Temporarily link Line and Neutral conductors together at the origin. Connect one test lead to this linked L-N connection and the other lead to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET) or the circuit's CPC. Apply the test voltage (e.g., 500V DC).
    • Purpose: To verify there is no breakdown or low resistance path between any of the live conductors and the earthing system due to damaged insulation, which could lead to earth leakage or earth faults.

BS 7671 requires minimum acceptable readings (e.g., ≥1.0 MΩ for LV circuits).

Gas turbines are core to gas power plants, while steam stations offer scalability. Surge tanks protect hydro penstocks. Consumer intake equipment provides control and protection. Final circuits have specific protection/cable requirements. Insulation resistance tests verify the integrity of conductor insulation, crucial for preventing short circuits and earth faults.

Answer

This answer lists indicators of a fully charged battery, charging methods, describes fire alarm systems, diagrams a closed alarm circuit, distinguishes ammeter/voltmeter connections, and diagrams a wattmeter connection.

(a) Battery Charge Indicators and Charging Methods

(i) Three Indications of a Fully Charged Lead-Acid Cell:

  1. Voltage Stabilization: Cell voltage rises during charging and stabilizes at a peak value (e.g., 2.3-2.45V per cell during absorption phase, or ~2.25V per cell on float, depending on type/temp) while charging current drops to a low, steady level. Resting voltage (off charge) is high (e.g., ~2.1V+ per cell).
  2. Specific Gravity (Flooded Cells): Electrolyte specific gravity reaches a maximum, stable value (e.g., 1.265-1.280 corrected for temperature) and is consistent across all cells.
  3. Gassing (Flooded Cells): Cells begin to gas freely (bubble hydrogen and oxygen) as the charging process nears completion and enters the absorption/equalization phase.

(ii) Three Methods of Charging Batteries:

  1. Constant Current (CC) Charging: Charging with a fixed current; voltage increases as the battery charges. Often used for the initial bulk phase.
  2. Constant Voltage (CV) Charging: Charging at a fixed voltage; current starts high and tapers down as the battery charges. Often used for absorption and float phases.
  3. Multi-Stage Charging (e.g., CC/CV): A common method combining stages: Bulk (Constant Current until voltage reaches a set point), Absorption (Constant Voltage while current tapers), and Float (lower Constant Voltage to maintain charge).
  4. Trickle Charging: Charging at a very low, continuous current to compensate for self-discharge and maintain full charge.
  5. Pulse Charging: Using controlled pulses of current, sometimes claimed to help reduce sulfation.

The question asked for three.

(b) Fire Alarm System and Closed Alarm Circuit

(i) Description of a Fire Alarm System:**

A fire alarm system is an integrated network designed to detect the presence of fire through various sensors (smoke detectors, heat detectors, flame detectors) or manual activation (manual call points/break-glass units). Upon detection, it activates audible alarms (bells, sirens, sounders) and often visual alarms (strobes) to alert occupants of the danger, facilitating evacuation. The system typically includes a central Fire Alarm Control Panel (FACP) which monitors detectors, receives signals, activates alarms, indicates the location of the alarm, and may automatically notify the fire brigade or control other building systems (like ventilation or lifts).

(Typically refers to Intruder Alarm concept)

(c) Distinction Between Ammeter and Voltmeter Connection

  • Ammeter: Measures electric current (flow rate of charge). It has a very **low internal resistance** and is always connected **in series** with the component or circuit path through which the current is to be measured. Breaking the circuit is necessary to insert the ammeter.
  • Voltmeter: Measures electric potential difference (voltage) between two points. It has a very **high internal resistance** and is always connected **in parallel** across the component or points between which the voltage is to be measured. It does not require breaking the main circuit path.

(d) Labelled Circuit Diagram of a Wattmeter

A wattmeter measures electrical power and has both a current coil (low resistance) and a voltage coil (high resistance).

Recognizing battery charge state and using appropriate charging methods are key for battery health. Fire alarm systems provide life safety alerts. Ammeters (series, low R) measure current, while voltmeters (parallel, high R) measure voltage. Wattmeters combine current and voltage sensing coils to measure power.

Question 5

(a) Describe the following parts of an earthing system:

(i) earth electrode;

(ii) earth lead.

(b) Draw a labelled diagram of current operated earth leakage circuit breaker.

(c) Distinguish between a class P fuse and a class R fuse.

(d) (i) Define structured cabling;

(ii) Describe the entrance facilities of a structured cabling.

Answer

This answer describes earth electrode and lead, diagrams an RCD (current-operated ELCB), distinguishes fuse classes, defines structured cabling, and describes entrance facilities.

(a) Parts of an Earthing System

(i) Earth Electrode: The conductive part (or group of parts like rods, plates, tapes) which is installed in intimate contact with the general mass of the Earth, providing the physical connection between the installation's earthing system and the ground itself. Its purpose is to dissipate fault currents safely into the earth and establish a reference earth potential. Its effectiveness depends on its size, shape, depth, and the resistivity of the surrounding soil.

(ii) Earth Lead (Earthing Conductor): The protective conductor that connects the Main Earthing Terminal (MET) of an electrical installation to the means of earthing, which could be the earth electrode itself (in a TT system) or the earth terminal provided by the electricity supplier (in TN-S or TN-C-S systems).

(b) Labelled Diagram of Current Operated ELCB (RCD)

(See Answer to Q4(c) in 2017n paper for diagram and detailed explanation).

(c) Distinction Between Fuse Classes (BS 88)

The question asks for Class P vs Class R. Class P exists (as described in Q5a 2016n, relating to full-range protection). Class 'R' is less standard in BS 88 / IEC terminology compared to classes like gG, aM, gR, aR etc. Perhaps it refers to North American fuse classes (like Class R - Rejection type fuses) or is a typo for gR/aR (semiconductor fuses). Assuming it might be a typo for 'aR':

  • Class P (Protection): Offers specified performance for both overload and short-circuit conditions (full-range protection) often in specific industrial applications.
  • Class aR (Partial Range Semiconductor Fuse): Extremely fast acting fuse designed specifically for short-circuit protection of power semiconductors (diodes, thyristors, IGBTs). It does *not* provide overload protection and must be used in conjunction with another device (like an MCB or gG fuse) for overload protection.

If 'Class R' refers to North American standard fuses (UL/CSA), Class R fuses are cartridge fuses with specific dimensions and a rejection feature (a slot or groove) preventing lower-rated or different class fuses from being inserted into a Class R fuse holder, primarily used for high fault current circuits (e.g., 200kA interrupting rating).

(d) Structured Cabling Definition and Entrance Facilities

(i) Structured Cabling Definition:**

A standardized approach to installing building cabling infrastructure (typically for data, voice, and multimedia) using specified types of cables and connectors, laid out in a hierarchical topology (usually star). It creates a flexible, organized, and future-proof system that can support various communication technologies and makes moves, adds, and changes easier to manage compared to point-to-point or ad-hoc wiring.

(ii) Entrance Facilities (Structured Cabling - TIA/EIA Standards):**

The Entrance Facility (EF) is the point where the external telecommunications cabling (from service providers) enters the building and connects to the building's internal backbone cabling. It encompasses the physical space and associated hardware. Key components include:

  • Service Entrance Pathway: The conduit or opening through the building wall where external cables physically enter.
  • Termination Hardware: Connection blocks, patch panels, or other devices where the external service provider cables are terminated.
  • Protection Devices: Surge protectors for electrical power and telecommunication lines to protect against voltage spikes (e.g., from lightning).
  • Grounding Infrastructure: Connection point to the building's telecommunications grounding system (TGB - Telecommunications Grounding Busbar).
  • Demarcation Point (Demarc): The point where the operational control or ownership transfers from the service provider to the building owner/occupant.
  • Physical Space: The room or space housing this equipment, which needs adequate environmental controls (temperature, humidity), security, lighting, and access.

The earth electrode and lead are key components connecting an installation to earth. RCDs detect earth leakage currents for safety. Fuse classes denote specific protection characteristics. Structured cabling provides an organized approach to data/voice wiring, starting at the entrance facility where external services connect to the building infrastructure.

This answer lists cable joint and wiring system types, explains functions of switches/lamps in solar context, diagrams/explains PV module earthing, and lists tests for completed solar installations.

(a) Lists of Joints and Wiring Systems

(i) Four Types of Cable Joints:

  1. Married Joint: Interlacing and twisting strands of two conductors, usually soldered.
  2. Tee Joint: Connecting a branch conductor to a main conductor without cutting the main.
  3. Britannia Joint: Overlapping solid conductors bound tightly with wire, for overhead lines.
  4. Scarf Joint: Tapering ends of solid conductors, overlapping and binding/soldering.
  5. Crimped Splice/Butt Connector: Using a metal sleeve and crimping tool to join conductors mechanically.
  6. Terminal Block/Strip Connector: Using screw or spring cage terminals within a block to join multiple wires.

(ii) Four Types of Wiring Systems:

  1. Conduit Wiring System (PVC or Steel): Cables drawn into protective tubes.
  2. Trunking Wiring System (PVC or Steel): Cables laid into accessible ducts with removable lids.

  6. Mineral Insulated Cable (MIMS/MICC) System: Using MIMS cable, often surface run due to its inherent protection.

The question asked for four.

(b) Function of Accessories in Solar Electric Installations

(i) Switches (DC Isolators/Breakers): In a solar context, these are primarily used for safely isolating sections of the DC circuit (PV array, battery, loads, inverter) for maintenance, inspection, or emergency shutdown. They must be rated for DC voltage and current.

(ii) Lamps (Lights): These are electrical loads that consume power from the solar system to produce light. In DC solar systems, energy-efficient DC lamps (usually LED) operating at the system voltage (e.g., 12V or 24V) are used. In AC systems, standard AC lamps are used, powered via the inverter.

(c) PV Module Earthing

(i) Labelled Diagram:**

(ii) Two Reasons for Earthing the Module:**

  1. Electric Shock Protection: To provide a safe path to ground for fault current if the metallic frame accidentally becomes live due to internal wiring faults or insulation breakdown. This allows the protective device (fuse/breaker/RCD) to operate and disconnect the circuit, preventing the frame from posing a shock hazard.
  2. Lightning Protection (Indirect): While not providing direct lightning strike protection, earthing the framework helps to dissipate static build-up and provides a path for induced currents from nearby lightning strikes, reducing the risk of damage to modules and associated equipment. (Dedicated lightning protection systems offer more comprehensive protection).

(d) Four Tests Carried Out on Completed Solar Electric Installation

(Similar to Q2(b) 2016n - key tests specific to PV)

  1. Continuity of Protective Conductors: Ensuring all module frames, mounting structure, and other metallic parts are effectively bonded to earth (low resistance measurement).
  2. PV Array Insulation Resistance: Measuring the resistance between the DC conductors (+ and - tied together) and Earth (frame/structure) to ensure high insulation integrity (Megaohm range).
  3. Polarity Check: Verifying correct positive and negative connections throughout the DC side of the system (modules, controller, battery, etc.).
  4. PV String Open Circuit Voltage (Voc) Test: Measuring the Voc of each string and comparing it to expected values based on module datasheets and temperature, checking for consistency between strings.
  5. PV String Short Circuit Current (Isc) Test (or Operating Current): Measuring the Isc (carefully, brief test) or operating current of each string under load and comparing to expected values based on datasheets and irradiance, checking for consistency.

Various jointing and wiring methods exist, chosen based on application. Switches and lamps serve control and load functions in solar systems. Earthing PV module frames is crucial for safety. Comprehensive testing verifies the safety and performance of a completed solar installation.

Question 7

(a) (i) State two conversions of solar energy.

(ii) Explain how a parabolic dish mirror maximizes harvesting of solar energy.

(b) With aid of a labelled diagram describe the constructional features of a flat plate collector.

(c) Draw the connections and show the total output of two 12 V identical batteries rated 50 Ah connected in:

(i) parallel;

(ii) series.

Answer

This answer lists solar energy conversions, explains parabolic dish concentration, describes flat plate collector construction, and diagrams series/parallel battery connections with calculated outputs.

(a) Solar Energy Conversions and Parabolic Dish

(i) Two Conversions of Solar Energy:

  1. Solar Thermal Conversion: Conversion of solar radiation directly into thermal energy (heat). This heat is then used for applications like water heating, space heating, cooking, crop drying, or generating electricity via heat engines (CSP).
  2. Solar Photovoltaic (PV) Conversion: Direct conversion of solar radiation (sunlight) into Direct Current (DC) electricity using semiconductor materials (solar cells) based on the photovoltaic effect.
  3. (Others include Solar Chemical Conversion - e.g., photosynthesis, artificial photosynthesis).

The question asked for two.

(ii) How Parabolic Dish Maximizes Harvesting:

A parabolic dish mirror maximizes energy harvesting through **concentration**. Its precisely curved parabolic shape reflects all incoming parallel sunlight rays that strike its surface onto a single, small area – the focal point. By concentrating the sunlight that falls over the large area of the dish onto the small receiver placed at the focus, it achieves very high solar flux density and consequently very high temperatures at the receiver, allowing for efficient high-temperature thermal energy collection or direct conversion (e.g., Stirling engine).

Answer

This answer outlines wiring/control gear checks, lists useful service documents, describes battery stratification and sulphation, explains system sizing terms, and calculates daily module energy output considering losses.

(a) Wiring/Control Gear Checks and Service Information

(i) Inspection and Checks on Wiring and Control Gear:

  • Visual Inspection: Look for signs of damage, overheating (discoloration), corrosion, water ingress, or loose components on cables, connectors, isolators, fuses/breakers, charge controller, inverter, and within enclosures/combiner boxes.
  • Security of Fixings: Check that all enclosures, conduit, trunking, and components are securely mounted.
  • Connection Tightness: Periodically check (or re-torque if specified) screw terminals in isolators, breakers, controllers, and combiner boxes to ensure secure, low-resistance connections (requires isolation).
  • Cable Condition: Inspect visible cable insulation for damage (cuts, abrasion, UV degradation). Ensure cables are properly supported and protected from mechanical stress.
  • Protective Device Check: Verify correct ratings of fuses/MCBs. Test RCDs (if fitted) using the test button. Check functionality of isolators.
  • Controller/Inverter Status: Check indicator lights or display panels on the charge controller and inverter for normal operation status and any fault codes.

(ii) Three Documents/Information for Service and Maintenance:

  1. System Wiring Diagram (Schematic): Shows how components (panels, controller, battery, inverter, loads, protection devices) are interconnected. Essential for tracing circuits and troubleshooting.
  2. Component Manuals/Datasheets: Provide specifications, operating parameters, installation instructions, troubleshooting guides, and maintenance recommendations for specific components (modules, controller, inverter, battery).
  3. Installation & Commissioning Records: Include initial test results (Voc, Isc, insulation resistance, polarity checks), system settings (controller parameters), component serial numbers, and date of installation. Useful for comparison during maintenance checks.
  4. Maintenance Logbook: A record of previous maintenance activities, observations, measurements (e.g., battery voltage/SG), and any repairs carried out.

The question asked for three.

(b) Conditions Affecting Lead-Acid Batteries

(i) Stratification: (See Answer to Q7(c)(i) in 2016n paper for diagram and detailed explanation). Occurs mainly in flooded lead-acid batteries where the electrolyte separates due to density differences, with heavier, concentrated sulfuric acid settling at the bottom and lighter, watery electrolyte accumulating at the top. This leads to uneven plate usage, reduced capacity, and increased corrosion/sulfation.

(ii) Sulphation: The formation of lead sulphate crystals on the surface and within the pores of the battery plates. This occurs naturally during normal discharge. However, if a battery is left in a discharged state for extended periods, or chronically undercharged, these crystals grow larger and harder (hard sulphation). Hard sulphation reduces the active surface area of the plates, permanently decreasing battery capacity, increasing internal resistance, and making the battery difficult or impossible to recharge fully.

(c) Solar Sizing Terminology

(i) Total Daily System Energy Requirement: The total amount of electrical energy (usually expressed in Watt-hours, Wh) that the solar PV system's energy source (PV array and/or battery) needs to supply *per day* to meet both the energy demand of the connected electrical loads *and* compensate for all the energy losses within the system (e.g., wiring losses, battery inefficiency, inverter inefficiency, controller consumption). It is calculated as: (Total Daily Load Energy) / (1 - Total System Loss Fraction).

(ii) Days of Autonomy: The number of consecutive days that a fully charged battery bank in an off-grid PV system is expected to be able to supply the required daily load energy without any significant energy input from the PV array (e.g., during extended periods of very cloudy weather). Sizing for autonomy (e.g., 2-5 days) ensures system reliability when sunlight is unavailable and requires a significantly larger battery bank compared to sizing just for overnight use.

(d) Module Output Calculation

Given:

  • Module Rating (Prated) = 150 W (This is peak power, Wp)
  • Daily Insolation = 7 hours/day (This likely refers to Peak Sun Hours, PSH, which simplifies calculation)
  • System Losses = 20% (Loss Fraction = 0.20)

Method 1: Using Peak Sun Hours (PSH) directly

If the "7 hours/day" refers to PSH, this represents the equivalent number of hours the panel would need to operate at its peak rating (150W) under standard irradiance (1000 W/m²) to produce the total energy collected over the actual day.

Ideal Daily Energy Generated (Eideal) = Prated * PSH

Eideal = 150 W * 7 h = 1050 Wh/day

Now, account for system losses. The *usable* output after losses is:

Actual Daily Output = Eideal * (1 - Loss Fraction)

Actual Daily Output = 1050 Wh * (1 - 0.20) = 1050 Wh * 0.80

Actual Daily Output = 840 Wh/day

Method 2: Interpreting 'insulation' as 'irradiation time' (Less likely but possible)

If "insulation of 7 hours/day" meant the panel receives *some* sunlight for 7 hours, but not necessarily at peak intensity, this calculation is less accurate without knowing the average irradiance over those 7 hours. However, if we assume it *implies* an average generation equivalent to running at its rated power for 7 hours (which aligns with the PSH concept), the result is the same.

The output of the module per day, considering losses, is 840 Wh/day.

Regular inspection of wiring and control gear, along with access to system documentation, aids solar maintenance. Understanding and mitigating battery issues like stratification and sulphation is crucial for lead-acid types. System sizing relies on estimating energy needs, available insolation, desired autonomy, and accounting for inevitable system losses. Daily energy output can be estimated using module rating, peak sun hours, and loss factors.
