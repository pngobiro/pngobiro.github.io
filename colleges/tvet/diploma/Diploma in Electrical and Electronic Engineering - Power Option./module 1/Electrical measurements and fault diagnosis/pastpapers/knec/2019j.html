Answer

This question covers precautions for analog multimeters, a frequency measurement method, derivation for a capacitor bridge, and calculations for 3-phase power using the two-wattmeter method.

(a) Analog Multimeter and Zero-Beat Method (10 marks):

**(i) Two precautions when using analogue multi range multi-meters:**

  • **Select the correct range:** Always start with the highest possible range for the quantity being measured (voltage or current) and then switch down to a lower range if necessary for a more accurate reading. This prevents damaging the meter by overranging.
  • **Select the correct function:** Ensure the meter is set to measure the desired quantity (Voltage, Current, Resistance) and type (AC or DC). Incorrect function selection can damage the meter or the circuit.
  • **Observe polarity (for DC measurements):** Connect the leads with the correct polarity when measuring DC voltage or current to ensure the needle deflects in the positive direction and to avoid damaging the meter.
  • **Connect correctly for current measurement:** Always connect the ammeter in series with the circuit element through which the current is to be measured. Never connect an ammeter in parallel with a voltage source or load, as its low resistance will cause a short circuit.

**(ii) Zero-beat method of frequency measurement with diagram:**

The zero-beat method (or heterodyne method) measures an unknown frequency by comparing it with a known, adjustable frequency from a variable frequency oscillator (VFO). When the two frequencies are equal, a "zero beat" condition is detected.

+-----------------+    +-------+    +---------------+    +-----------+
|   Input Signal  |--->| Mixer |--->| Low Pass Filter |--->| Amplifier |
| (Unknown freq)  |    +-------+    +---------------+    +-----------+
+-----------------+        ^                                  |
                           |                                  |
+---------------------+    |    +-----------------+    +-----------------+
| Variable Frequency  |----+----| Frequency Meter/|--->| Zero Beat       |
| Oscillator (VFO)    |         | Calibrated Dial |    | Indicator (e.g.,|
| (Known freq)        |         +-----------------+    | Headphones/Meter)|
+---------------------+                                  +-----------------+

**Description:** The unknown frequency signal and the signal from the VFO are fed into a mixer circuit. The mixer's output contains sum and difference frequencies. A low-pass filter selects the difference (beat) frequency. This beat frequency is amplified and sent to a zero-beat indicator (e.g., headphones, a sensitive meter). The VFO frequency is adjusted until the beat frequency is zero, which is indicated by minimum or zero output from the indicator. At this point, the unknown frequency is equal to the known VFO frequency, which is read from the calibrated dial of the VFO.

Question 2

(a) Define each of the following with respect to reliability: (6 marks)

(i) availability;

(ii) redundancy.

(b) With aid of a labelled diagram, describe reliability bathtab curve. (8 marks)

(c) Ten electronic devices are tested for 500 hours. During the test, 2 failures occurred. Determine the: (6 marks)

(i) Mean Time to Failure (MTTF);

(ii) Mean Time Between Failure (MTBF);

(iii) Failure rate;

(iv) Probability that a particular device will be operated at time equal to MTBF.

Answer

This question covers key concepts in equipment reliability, the bathtub curve model, and calculations of reliability metrics from test data.

(a) Reliability Definitions (6 marks):

  • **(i) Availability:** The probability that an equipment is in an operable state at any given time when required for use. It is a measure of operational readiness and depends on both reliability (how often it fails) and maintainability (how quickly it can be repaired). Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR).
  • **(ii) Redundancy:** The inclusion of extra components, subsystems, or functions in a system that are not strictly necessary for normal operation but are available to perform the intended function if a primary component fails. Redundancy is a technique used to improve system reliability, as the system can continue to operate even after one or more component failures. Example: Having two power supplies in a system, where one can take over if the other fails.

(b) Reliability Bathtub Curve Description (8 marks):

The reliability bathtub curve is a graphical representation of the failure rate of a population of components or equipment over its lifespan. It illustrates how the likelihood of failure changes with time.

Failure Rate ^
             |
             | \       /
             |  \     /
             |   \   /
             +----+---+----> Time
             |    |   |
       Infant   Useful  Wear-out
      Mortality  Life

**Description:** The curve is typically divided into three main periods:

  • **Infant Mortality Period (Early Failures):** This occurs at the beginning of the equipment's life. The failure rate starts high and decreases rapidly over time. Failures are usually due to manufacturing defects, weak components ("early failures" or "bugs"). Reliability improves as these initial problems are corrected.
  • **Useful Life Period (Random Failures):** Follows the infant mortality period. The failure rate is relatively constant and low. Failures in this phase are typically random events, often caused by unexpected stresses or environmental factors, rather than the age of the equipment itself. This period is the target for the design life of most equipment, where reliability is modeled using a constant failure rate (\(\lambda\)).
  • **Wear-out Period:** Occurs towards the end of the equipment's life. The failure rate increases rapidly with time. Failures are caused by aging, wear and tear, fatigue, and material degradation. These failures are often predictable based on the age of the components.

(c) Reliability Calculations from Test Data (6 marks):

Ten electronic devices were tested for 500 hours, and 2 failures occurred. This data allows calculation of reliability metrics.

Given:

  • Number of units tested \(N = 10\)
  • Test duration \(T = 500\) hours
  • Number of failures \(f = 2\)

Total operating time (device-hours) = \(N \times T = 10 \times 500 = 5000\) device-hours.

**(i) Mean Time to Failure (MTTF):**

MTTF is the average time a non-repairable item operates before failure. It is calculated as total operating time divided by the number of failures.

\[ MTTF = \frac{\text{Total Operating Time}}{\text{Number of Failures}} = \frac{5000 \text{ device-hours}}{2 \text{ failures}} = 2500 \text{ hours/failure} \]

**(ii) Mean Time Between Failure (MTBF):**

MTBF is the average time a repairable item operates between failures. If the devices are assumed to be repairable and put back into test after repair, the calculation is the same as MTTF in this context (total test time / number of failures). If they are non-repairable, only MTTF is applicable.

Assuming the question intends for these to be metrics of the population, and the test is characteristic of the useful life, \(MTBF \approx MTTF\).

\[ MTBF \approx MTTF = 2500 \text{ hours/failure} \]

**(iii) Failure rate (\(\lambda\)):**

Failure rate is the reciprocal of MTTF or MTBF (assuming constant failure rate).

\[ \lambda = \frac{1}{MTTF} = \frac{\text{Number of Failures}}{\text{Total Operating Time}} = \frac{2 \text{ failures}}{5000 \text{ device-hours}} \] \[ \lambda = 0.0004 \text{ failures/hour} \]

Often expressed per 1000 hours: \( \lambda = 0.0004 \times 1000 = 0.4 \) failures per 1000 hours.

**(iv) Probability that a particular device will be operated at time equal to MTBF:**

The probability of a device operating for a time \(t\) (its reliability \(R(t)\)) is given by \( R(t) = e^{-\lambda t} \), assuming a constant failure rate. We want to find \( R(t) \) when \( t = MTBF \). We know \( \lambda = 1/MTBF \).

\[ R(MTBF) = e^{-\lambda \times MTBF} = e^{-(1/MTBF) \times MTBF} = e^{-1} \] \[ R(MTBF) = e^{-1} \approx 0.36788 \]

The probability that a device will be operating at time equal to MTBF is approximately 0.3679 or 36.79%. This means about 36.8% of devices are expected to survive up to the MTBF.

Reliability concepts and calculations are important for predicting the performance and lifespan of electronic devices and systems.

Answer

This question covers maintenance policies, system diagrams, and calculations for maintainability, availability, and unavailability.

(a) Maintenance Policy and System Diagrams (14 marks):

**(i) Four factors determining maintenance policy:**

The choice of maintenance policy (e.g., reactive/corrective, preventive, predictive) depends on various factors:

  • **Criticality of the equipment:** How essential is the equipment to the overall operation? Failure of critical equipment warrants a more proactive (preventive or predictive) policy.
  • **Cost of failure:** The financial and non-financial consequences of equipment failure (e.g., lost production, repair costs, safety hazards, environmental damage). High failure costs favor policies that minimize unexpected breakdowns.
  • **Cost of maintenance:** The cost of implementing different maintenance policies (e.g., cost of planned inspections, predictive monitoring equipment, reactive repairs, labor).
  • **Failure patterns/Reliability characteristics:** Is the equipment prone to random failures (useful life) or wear-out failures (end of life)? Preventive/predictive maintenance is more effective for wear-out failures.

This question covers atomic ionization, properties of silicon vs. germanium diodes, PN junction formation, and calculations involving current flow and energy in a semiconductor.

(a) Ionization and Silicon vs. Germanium Diodes (6 marks):

**(i) Two ways of ionizing an atom:**

Ionization is the process by which an atom or molecule gains or loses electrons, becoming a charged ion.

  • **By Collision (Impact Ionization):** An atom can be ionized when it is struck by a high-energy particle (like an electron or another ion). If the incident particle has enough kinetic energy, it can knock out one or more electrons from the atom, creating a positive ion and free electron(s). This process occurs in gas discharges and semiconductor breakdown (avalanche effect).
  • **By Photoionization:** An atom can absorb a photon (a particle of light) with sufficient energy. If the photon's energy is greater than the ionization energy of the atom, it can knock out an electron, leaving a positive ion. This occurs when materials are exposed to ultraviolet light or X-rays.
  • **By Thermal Ionization:** At very high temperatures, atoms collide with each other with enough kinetic energy to cause ionization.
  • **By Chemical Reaction:** In some chemical reactions (e.g., redox reactions), electrons can be transferred between atoms or molecules, forming ions.
  • **By Strong Electric Field (Field Ionization):** A very strong external electric field can distort the potential barrier holding an electron in an atom, allowing the electron to tunnel out of the atom.

**(ii) Two advantages of silicon over germanium diodes:**

  • **Higher Operating Temperature:** Silicon diodes can operate reliably at much higher temperatures (typically up to 150-175°C) compared to germanium diodes (typically up to 75-100°C). This is due to silicon's wider band gap.
  • **Lower Reverse Saturation Current:** Silicon diodes have a significantly lower reverse saturation current (\(I_S\)) than germanium diodes. This makes silicon diodes behave more like ideal open circuits in reverse bias and less sensitive to temperature variations in the reverse direction.
  • **Higher Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) Rating:** Silicon diodes can withstand much higher reverse voltages before breaking down compared to germanium diodes.
  • **Higher Power Handling Capability:** Generally, silicon diodes can handle higher currents and dissipate more power than germanium diodes of similar size.

(b) Formation of Depletion Layer at the P-N Junction (8 marks):

A p-n junction is formed by joining a p-type semiconductor and an n-type semiconductor. At the interface, unique processes lead to the formation of a depletion layer (or depletion region).

     P-type Side         N-type Side
    (Majority Holes)   (Majority Electrons)

    Initial State:
    +----------+----------+
    | o  o  o  |  .  .  . |
    |  o  o  o | .  .  .  |
    +----------+----------+

    Diffusion across junction:
    +----------+----------+
    | o  o  o  | .  .  .  |
    |  o  o    |.o  o.  . |  <-- Carriers diffuse
    +----------+----------+

    Formation of Depletion Layer:
    +---------+ +---------+
    | o  o  @ |-|-|+|+| . |  <-- Holes move right, expose fixed acceptors (-)
    |  o    @ |-|-|+|+|.  |  <-- Electrons move left, expose fixed donors (+)
    +---------+ +---------+
      Bulk P   Depletion   Bulk N
               Region
    (Fixed -ve ions) (Fixed +ve ions)

**Description:**

  • When p-type and n-type semiconductors are joined, there is a high concentration of holes in the p-type material and a high concentration of free electrons in the n-type material.
  • Due to this concentration gradient, holes from the p-side diffuse across the junction into the n-side, and free electrons from the n-side diffuse across the junction into the p-side.
  • When a free electron diffuses into the p-side, it recombines with a hole. When a hole diffuses into the n-side, it recombines with a free electron.
  • This diffusion and recombination process causes the region around the junction to be depleted of free charge carriers (both electrons and holes). This region is called the **depletion layer**.
  • As electrons move from the n-side to the p-side, they leave behind positively charged donor impurity ions (\(N_D^+\)) in the n-type material near the junction.
  • Similarly, as holes move from the p-side to the n-side, they leave behind negatively charged acceptor impurity ions (\(N_A^-\)) in the p-type material near the junction.
  • These fixed, exposed ions create a built-in electric field across the depletion layer, directed from the positive ions in the n-region to the negative ions in the p-region. This electric field opposes further diffusion of majority carriers, and eventually, a state of equilibrium is reached where the diffusion current is balanced by the drift current caused by the built-in field. The potential difference across the depletion layer at equilibrium is called the built-in potential barrier.

(b) Derivation of Common-Base Gain (\(\alpha\)) in terms of Common-Emitter Gain (\(\beta\)) (6 marks):

This derivation uses the definitions of the DC current gains \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) and the fundamental current relationship in a BJT.

Definitions:

  • DC Common-Base Current Gain: \( \alpha = \frac{I_C}{I_E} \)
  • DC Common-Emitter Current Gain: \( \beta = \frac{I_C}{I_B} \)
  • Current relationship: \( I_E = I_B + I_C \)

From \( I_E = I_B + I_C \), we can write \( I_B = I_E - I_C \).

Substitute this into the definition of \(\beta\):

\[ \beta = \frac{I_C}{I_E - I_C} \]

Divide the numerator and the denominator by \(I_E\):

\[ \beta = \frac{I_C / I_E}{(I_E - I_C) / I_E} = \frac{I_C / I_E}{I_E/I_E - I_C / I_E} \]

Substitute \( \alpha = I_C / I_E \) into the equation:

\[ \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \]

The question asks for the expression for \(\alpha\) in terms of \(\beta\). Rearrange the equation to solve for \(\alpha\):

\[ \beta (1 - \alpha) = \alpha \] \[ \beta - \beta \alpha = \alpha \] \[ \beta = \alpha + \beta \alpha \] \[ \beta = \alpha (1 + \beta) \] \[ \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta} \]

(c) Transistor Operating Regions and Common-Collector Circuit (6 marks):

**(i) Three operating regions of a transistor (BJT):**

  • **Cutoff Region:** Both EBJ and CBJ are reverse-biased. The transistor is off; \(I_C \approx 0\).
  • **Active Region:** EBJ is forward-biased, CBJ is reverse-biased. The transistor amplifies; \(I_C = \beta I_B\).
  • **Saturation Region:** Both EBJ and CBJ are forward-biased. The transistor is fully on (like a closed switch); \(I_C\) is limited by the external circuit, \(V_{CE}\) is very low.

The diagram shows the collector connected directly to \(V_{CC}\) (or AC ground). The input signal is applied to the base, and the output is taken from the emitter. This configuration has high input impedance, low output impedance, a voltage gain slightly less than 1, and a significant current gain. It is often used as a buffer or for impedance matching.

Question 8

(a) Explain the function of the following parts of a cathode-ray oscilloscope: (6 marks)

(i) time base generator,

(ii) trigger pulse generator,

(iii) Y -amplifier.

(b) An electrostatic CRT has a final anode voltage of 2000 V and parallel deflecting plates 1.5 cm long. Taking electronic charge, \( \mathrm{e}=1.6 \times 10^{-19} \mathrm{~C} \) and mass of electron, \( \mathrm{m}=9.1 \times 10^{-31} \mathrm{~kg} \), determine the following for an electron emitted from the cathode: (10 marks)

(i) maximum velocity;

(ii) transit time through the deflecting plates;

(iii) kinetic energy.

(c) (i) State the properties of capacitors that make them suitable for use as power supply filters. (3 marks)

(ii) With the aid of a circuit diagram, describe the operation of a zener diode voltage regulator. (3 marks)

Answer

This question focuses on the Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO), electron ballistics within a CRT, capacitor filtering in power supplies, and Zener diode voltage regulation.

(a) Function of CRO Parts (6 marks):

The CRO displays voltage waveforms against time by deflecting an electron beam.

  • **(i) Time base generator (Sweep Generator):** Produces a sawtooth voltage waveform that is applied to the horizontal deflection plates (X-plates). This voltage causes the electron beam to sweep horizontally across the screen at a constant speed from left to right, and then rapidly return to the starting point. This provides the time axis for displaying the input signal waveform. (Same as Q8a(i) Nov 2018)
  • **(ii) Trigger pulse generator (Trigger Circuit):** Synchronizes the start of the horizontal sweep with a specific point (e.g., a rising or falling edge at a particular voltage level) of the input signal (or an external signal). This ensures that the waveform display starts at the same point of the input signal cycle each time, creating a stable, non-moving display on the screen. (Same as Q8a(ii) Nov 2018)
  • **(iii) Y-amplifier (Vertical Amplifier):** Amplifies the input signal that is to be displayed on the CRO. This signal is applied to the vertical deflection plates (Y-plates). The amplification allows the CRO to display signals with small amplitudes and provides the user with control over the vertical size (Volts/Div) of the displayed waveform. (Same as Q8a(iii) Nov 2018)

(b) Electrostatic CRT Electron Calculations (10 marks):

Calculations for an electron beam accelerated by an anode voltage and passing through deflection plates.

Given:

  • Final anode voltage \(V_a = 2000 \, V\).
  • Deflecting plates length \(l_d = 1.5 \, cm = 0.015 \, m\).
  • Electronic charge \(e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C\).
  • Electron mass \(m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg\).

Assume the electron starts with negligible velocity from the cathode and is accelerated only by \(V_a\).

**(i) Maximum velocity:**

The maximum velocity (\(v\)) is achieved after acceleration by the final anode voltage. Kinetic energy gained equals potential energy lost: \( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = e V_a \).

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2 e V_a}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V)}{9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg}} \] \[ v = \sqrt{\frac{6.4 \times 10^{-16}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{0.70329 \times 10^{15}} = \sqrt{7.0329 \times 10^{14}} \] \[ v \approx 2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s} \]

The maximum velocity of the electron is approximately \(2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s}\). (Same as Q8b(i) Nov 2018)

**(ii) Transit time through the deflecting plates:**

The transit time (\(t_{transit}\)) is the time it takes for the electron to travel the length of the deflection plates at the constant horizontal velocity \(v\) (assuming no deflection voltage or small deflection voltage). We assume the velocity calculated above is the horizontal velocity (\(v_x\)).

\[ t_{transit} = \frac{l_d}{v_x} = \frac{0.015 \, m}{2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s}} \] \[ t_{transit} \approx 0.005656 \times 10^{-7} \, s = 5.656 \times 10^{-11} \, s \]

The transit time through the deflecting plates is approximately \(56.56 \, ps\) (picoseconds).

**(iii) Kinetic energy:**

The kinetic energy (KE) of the electron after acceleration by \(V_a\) is equal to the potential energy \(e V_a\).

\[ KE = e V_a = (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V) = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

The kinetic energy can also be calculated using \( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \).

\[ KE = \frac{1}{2} (9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg) \times (2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s})^2 \] \[ KE = 0.5 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 7.0329 \times 10^{14} \] \[ KE = 4.55 \times 10^{-31} \times 7.0329 \times 10^{14} \approx 32.00 \times 10^{-17} = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

The kinetic energy of the electron is \(3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J\).

(c) Capacitors for Power Supply Filters and Zener Regulator (6 marks):

**(i) Properties of capacitors suitable for power supply filters (3 marks):**

Capacitors are used as filters in DC power supplies (after rectification) to smooth out the pulsating DC voltage and reduce ripple. Properties that make them suitable include:

  • **Ability to store electrical charge:** They can store energy during the peaks of the rectifier output voltage and discharge during the valleys, smoothing the output.
  • **Low impedance to AC signals:** Capacitors offer low impedance to the AC ripple component, effectively shunting it away from the load.
  • **High impedance to DC signals:** Ideally, capacitors block DC current, preventing the filter from affecting the DC voltage level significantly (in a shunt filter configuration).
  • **Appropriate Capacitance Value:** The capacitance value must be large enough to effectively filter the lowest ripple frequency. Electrolytic capacitors are often used due to their high capacitance in a relatively small size.
  • **Sufficient Voltage Rating:** The capacitor's voltage rating must be higher than the peak voltage it will be subjected to.

**(ii) Operation of a zener diode voltage regulator with a circuit diagram (3 marks):**

      Vin (Unregulated)
   o------/\/\/\----+----o Vout (Regulated)
          Rs        |    |
                    |    ---
                    |    --- Load RL
                  |>|    |
                  | | Zener|
                  | | Diode|
                  ---    |
                   -     |
                    +----o Ground

**Description:** The circuit consists of a series resistor \(R_S\) and a Zener diode connected in parallel (shunt) with the load \(R_L\). The Zener diode is connected in reverse bias. \(R_S\) limits the current flowing into the Zener and the load. When the input voltage \(V_{in}\) is greater than the Zener breakdown voltage \(V_Z\), the Zener diode enters the breakdown region and maintains a nearly constant voltage \(V_Z\) across its terminals and, therefore, across the parallel load \(R_L\). If \(V_{in}\) increases, the current through \(R_S\) increases; the Zener current \(I_Z\) increases while \(V_L\) remains nearly constant. If the load current \(I_L\) decreases (due to increasing \(R_L\)), \(I_Z\) increases to keep \(I_S\) constant (\(I_S = I_Z + I_L\)), maintaining \(V_L\) constant. Regulation is maintained as long as the Zener stays in breakdown (\(I_Z \ge I_{Z,min}\)) and its maximum current rating is not exceeded (\(I_Z \le I_{Z,max}\)). (Same as Q8c(ii) Nov 2018)

This question tests knowledge of CRO components, basic electron dynamics in vacuum tubes, passive filtering techniques, and the application of Zener diodes for voltage regulation.
