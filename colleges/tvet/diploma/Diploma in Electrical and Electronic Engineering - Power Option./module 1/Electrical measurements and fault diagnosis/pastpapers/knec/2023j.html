Question 1

(a) Define each of the following with respect to measurements: (4 marks)

(i) resolution;

(ii) dimensions.

(b) Derive, from first principles, the dimensional equation for capacitance in c.g.s electrostatic units. (8 marks)

(c) (i) State the units for each of the following electrical quantities: (4 marks)

I. electric flux density;

II. electric field strength;

III. magnetomotive force.

(ii) Write down the physical equations for each of the following mechanical quantities: (9 marks)

I. momentum;

II. torque;

III. stiffness.

Answer

This question covers fundamental concepts in electrical measurements, including resolution, dimensions, unit systems (specifically c.g.s electrostatic), SI units, and dimensional analysis for mechanical quantities.

(a) Measurement Definitions (4 marks):

  • **(i) Resolution:** The smallest change in the measured quantity that an instrument can detect and indicate reliably. It is the level of detail an instrument can provide.
  • **(ii) Dimensions:** In physics, dimensions refer to the physical nature of a quantity and the type of units used to measure it (e.g., length, mass, time, electric current). Dimensional analysis expresses physical quantities in terms of these fundamental dimensions.

(b) Dimensional equation for capacitance in c.g.s electrostatic units (8 marks):

In the c.g.s. electrostatic system (esu), units are based on the centimeter (L), gram (M), and second (T). Electrical quantities are defined via Coulomb's law, where the permittivity of vacuum (\(\epsilon_0\)) is dimensionless and set to 1. Charge (Q) is derived from force.

Capacitance (C) is defined as the ratio of charge (Q) stored on a conductor to the potential difference (V) across it: \(C = Q/V\).

Potential difference (V) is energy (W) per unit charge (Q): \(V = W/Q\). So, \( C = Q / (W/Q) = Q^2 / W \).

Energy (Work, W) has dimensions \([ML^2T^{-2}]\).

We need the dimensions of charge (Q) in esu. From Coulomb's law, \(F = \frac{Q^2}{r^2}\) (in esu, the constant is 1). Force \(F\) has dimensions \([MLT^{-2}]\), distance \(r\) has dimensions \([L]\).

\[ F = \frac{Q^2}{L^2} \implies Q^2 = F \times L^2 \] \[ [Q^2]_{esu} = [MLT^{-2}] [L^2] = [ML^3T^{-2}] \] \[ [Q]_{esu} = [M^{1/2}L^{3/2}T^{-1}] \]

Now substitute \([Q^2]_{esu}\) and \([W]\) into the expression for \(C\):

\[ [C]_{esu} = \frac{[Q^2]_{esu}}{[W]} = \frac{[ML^3T^{-2}]}{[ML^2T^{-2}]} \] \[ [C]_{esu} = [M^{1-1} L^{3-2} T^{-2-(-2)}] \] \[ [C]_{esu} = [L] \]

The dimensional equation for capacitance in c.g.s. electrostatic units is \( [L] \). This means capacitance has the dimensions of length in the ESU system.

(c) Units and Physical Equations (13 marks):

**(i) SI Units for electrical quantities (4 marks):**

  • **I. Electric flux density (D):** Coulomb per square meter (C/mÂ²).
  • **II. Electric field strength (E):** Newton per Coulomb (N/C) or Volt per meter (V/m).
  • **III. Magnetomotive force (MMF):** Ampere (A) or Ampere-turn (At).

**(ii) Physical equations for mechanical quantities (9 marks):**

Provide a common equation defining or relating each quantity:

  • **I. Momentum (p):** The product of mass (m) and velocity (v). \[ p = mv \]
  • **II. Torque (\(\tau\)):** A measure of how much a force acting on an object causes that object to rotate about a pivot. It is the product of the force (F) and the perpendicular distance (r) from the pivot to the line of action of the force. \[ \tau = F \times r \] (or \( \tau = I \alpha \) where I is moment of inertia and \(\alpha\) is angular acceleration).
  • **III. Stiffness (k):** A measure of the resistance of an elastic body to deformation by an applied force. For a spring, it is the ratio of the force (F) applied to the resulting displacement (\(\Delta x\)). \[ k = \frac{F}{\Delta x} \] (or \( k = -\frac{F}{\Delta x} \) in Hooke's Law).

This question tests fundamental knowledge of metrology, unit systems, dimensional analysis, and basic physical definitions.

This question covers methods for minimizing observational errors, the operation of a thermocouple ammeter, and detailed error analysis for resistance measurement using the ammeter-voltmeter method.

(a) Observational Errors and Thermocouple Ammeter (8 marks):

**(i) Two ways of minimizing observational errors:**

Observational errors are human errors during the measurement process.

  • **Using instruments with clear digital displays:** Digital readouts eliminate parallax error and reduce the chance of misreading scales.
  • **Taking multiple readings and averaging:** Repeating the measurement several times by the same or different observers and calculating the average value can reduce the impact of random observational errors.
  • **Using appropriate techniques:** Ensuring the observer's eye is directly in front of the pointer for analog scales (to avoid parallax error), and following standard operating procedures correctly.
  • **Using trained personnel:** Experienced and well-trained observers are less likely to make mistakes.

**(ii) Operation of a thermocouple ammeter with diagram:**

A thermocouple ammeter measures current (AC or DC) based on the heating effect of the current and the Seebeck effect (thermo-electric effect). It is useful for measuring the RMS value of currents regardless of waveform.

Diagram shows current flowing through a heating element. A thermocouple is placed in close proximity (indirect contact) to the heating element. A DC moving coil meter is connected across the thermocouple.

**Description:** The current to be measured (\(I\)) passes through a heating element, causing it to heat up (\(P = I^2 R_{heater}\)). A thermocouple, consisting of two dissimilar metals (e.g., copper and constantan), has one junction (the hot junction) placed in close thermal contact with the heating element. The other junction(s) (cold junctions) are typically located at the terminals where the thermocouple connects to a sensitive DC moving-coil meter. The temperature difference between the hot and cold junctions generates a small DC voltage (thermo-electric e.m.f.) proportional to the temperature difference. This DC voltage is applied to the DC moving-coil meter, causing a deflection. Since the heating effect is proportional to \(I^2\), the deflection is proportional to the RMS value of the input current. The scale is calibrated to read current directly.

Question 3

(a) (i) State three factors that may affect the reliability of an engineering equipment: (6 marks)

(ii) Explain the effects of humidity on the performance of engineering equipment and state two methods of minimizing them. (6 marks)

(b) Sketch the failure rate versus time curve of a batch of electrical components and explain its shape. (8 marks)

(c) An equipment has a failure rate of \( 16 \% \) per 1000 hours. If it is operated for a period of 1000 hours, determine the: (10 marks)

(i) mean time between failures;

(ii) reliability;

(iii) probability of failure.

Answer

This question covers factors affecting reliability, the effects of humidity, the bathtub reliability curve, and calculations of reliability metrics from a given failure rate.

(a) Factors affecting Reliability and Effects of Humidity (12 marks):

**(i) Three factors that may affect the reliability of an engineering equipment:**

  • **Quality of components:** Using low-quality or out-of-spec components increases the likelihood of early failures or reduced lifespan.
  • **Environmental stress:** Operating the equipment outside its specified temperature, humidity, vibration, or atmospheric conditions can accelerate degradation and cause failures.
  • **Design robustness:** How well the design anticipates and handles operating stresses, tolerances, and potential failure modes.
  • **Manufacturing and assembly quality:** Errors during manufacturing (e.g., poor soldering, incorrect component placement) introduce defects that affect reliability.
  • **Operating time and stress level:** Longer operating hours and higher stress levels (voltage, current, temperature) generally lead to increased wear and probability of failure.
  • **Maintenance quality:** Poor or inadequate maintenance can introduce new faults or fail to prevent age-related failures.

**(ii) Effects of humidity on performance and minimization:**

Humidity, the amount of water vapor in the air, can significantly impact the performance and reliability of electrical and electronic equipment.

**Effects:**

  • **Corrosion:** Moisture combined with contaminants can accelerate the corrosion of metal parts (leads, contacts, traces), leading to increased resistance, intermittent connections, or open circuits.
  • **Insulation Degradation:** High humidity reduces the insulation resistance of materials, leading to leakage currents, especially on PCB surfaces, which can cause malfunction or short circuits.
  • **Component Changes:** Moisture can affect the dielectric properties of capacitors, change the resistance of some materials, or even be absorbed by some components (like IC packages), leading to internal damage.
  • **Electrolysis:** In the presence of moisture and voltage, ionic contaminants on PCBs can migrate, leading to dendritic growth that causes short circuits.

**Two methods of minimizing them:**

  • **Environmental Control:** Operating the equipment in a climate-controlled environment where humidity is kept within specified limits.
  • **Conformal Coating:** Applying a protective coating (e.g., acrylic, silicone, epoxy) to the surface of circuit boards and components to seal them against moisture and contaminants.
  • **Hermetic Sealing:** Sealing components or entire enclosures to prevent the ingress of moisture.
  • **Using Moisture-Resistant Components and Materials:** Selecting components and PCB materials that are less susceptible to the effects of moisture.

(b) Failure rate versus time curve (Bathtub Curve) and explanation (8 marks):

The failure rate versus time curve, known as the Bathtub Curve, depicts the typical failure rate of a population of products over their lifespan. (Same as Q2a(ii) Nov 2018 or Q3b July 2015)

Failure Rate ^
             |
             | \       /
             |  \     /
             |   \   /
             +----+---+----> Time
             |    |   |
       Infant   Useful  Wear-out
      Mortality  Life

**Explanation:** The curve has three distinct periods:

  • **Infant Mortality:** High, decreasing failure rate due to manufacturing defects and initial weaknesses.
  • **Useful Life:** Low, constant failure rate due to random events. This is the target operational period.
  • **Wear-out Period:** Increasing failure rate due to aging and degradation of components.

(c) Reliability Calculations from Given Failure Rate (10 marks):

Given an equipment's failure rate and an operating period. We can calculate MTTF/MTBF, reliability, and probability of failure for that period.

Given: Failure rate \( \lambda = 16 \% \) per 1000 hours. Operating period \(t = 1000\) hours.

Convert percentage failure rate to a decimal rate per 1000 hours: \( \lambda_{per \, 1000hrs} = 16\% = 0.16 \).

Convert to failures per hour: \( \lambda_{per \, hour} = \frac{16 / 100}{1000} = \frac{0.16}{1000} = 1.6 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{failures/hour} \).

**(i) mean time between failures:**

MTBF is the reciprocal of the failure rate (assuming constant failure rate).

\[ MTBF = \frac{1}{\lambda_{per \, hour}} = \frac{1}{1.6 \times 10^{-4}} \, \text{hours/failure} = \frac{10000}{1.6} \, \text{hours/failure} = 6250 \, \text{hours/failure} \]

**(ii) reliability:**

Reliability \(R(t)\) for a time \(t\). Use \(R(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\). Given \(t = 1000\) hours. Use \(\lambda_{per \, hour}\).

\[ R(1000) = e^{-(1.6 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{failures/hour}) \times (1000 \, \text{hours})} = e^{-0.16} \] \[ R(1000) \approx 0.8521 \]

Alternatively, using the failure rate per 1000 hours: \( R(1000) = e^{-\lambda_{per \, 1000hrs}} = e^{-0.16} \approx 0.8521 \).

The reliability for an operating period of 1,000 hours is approximately 0.8521 or 85.21%.

**(iii) probability of failure:**

Probability of failure (unreliability \(U(t)\)) is \( 1 - R(t) \).

\[ U(1000) = 1 - R(1000) \approx 1 - 0.8521 = 0.1479 \]

The probability of failure for an operating period of 1,000 hours is approximately 0.1479 or 14.79%.

Understanding factors affecting reliability, modeling failure rates, and calculating reliability metrics are essential for predicting equipment performance and lifespan.

Answer

This question covers reliability and maintainability definitions, preventive maintenance, analyzing a signal generator's block diagram and faults, and calculating reliability/maintainability metrics from operational data.

(a) Reliability/Maintainability Definitions (6 marks):

  • **(i) availability:** The probability that an equipment is in an operable state at any given time when required for use. It considers both how often the equipment fails (reliability) and how quickly it can be repaired (maintainability).
  • **(ii) failure rate:** The frequency with which a system or component fails, typically expressed as the number of failures per unit of time (e.g., failures per hour) or per unit of operation (e.g., failures per cycle). It is often assumed to be constant during the useful life period of equipment (\(\lambda\)).
  • **(iii) mean time to repair:** The average time required to fix a failed system or component and restore it to a fully functional state. It includes the time spent on diagnosis, troubleshooting, repair/replacement, and testing.

(b) Preventive Maintenance Description (8 marks):

Preventive maintenance is a proactive approach to maintaining equipment. (Same as Q5a(ii) in Nov 2015 or Q2a(ii) in July 2021)

**Description:** Preventive maintenance involves performing routine inspections, servicing, adjustments, and component replacements at scheduled intervals (time-based, usage-based, or condition-based) *before* a failure occurs or before performance degrades significantly. Its main goal is to prevent unexpected breakdowns, minimize unplanned downtime, extend the lifespan of the equipment, and maintain optimal performance and safety. This contrasts with corrective maintenance, which is reactive (performed after failure).

(a) Semiconductor Definitions and Avalanche Breakdown (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to semiconductors:**

  • **I. Hole:** A vacancy or absence of an electron in a covalent bond within the crystal structure of a semiconductor. It behaves as if it were a positive charge carrier with a mobility. Holes are the majority carriers in p-type semiconductors and minority carriers in n-type semiconductors.
  • **II. Forbidden gap:** The range of energy levels that electrons cannot occupy in a solid material according to quantum mechanics. **(ii) Explain avalanche breakdown with respect to p-n junctions (6 marks):**

Avalanche breakdown is a mechanism of conduction that can occur in a reverse-biased p-n junction when the reverse voltage becomes sufficiently high (at or above the breakdown voltage \(V_{BR}\)). (Same as Q6a(ii) Oct/Nov 2018)

**Explanation:** When a reverse bias voltage is applied, a strong electric field exists across the depletion region. Minority carriers (electrons on the p-side, holes on the n-side) that are thermally generated are accelerated by this field. As they gain energy, they collide with atoms in the crystal lattice. If the energy gained is sufficient, these collisions can break covalent bonds, generating new electron-hole pairs (impact ionization). The newly created carriers are also accelerated by the field and cause further impact ionization collisions, leading to a cumulative multiplication effect (avalanche effect). When the reverse voltage reaches \(V_{BR}\), the avalanche process becomes self-sustaining, causing a rapid and significant increase in the reverse current.

(a) Disadvantages of Field Effect Transistors (FETs) compared to Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) (4 marks):

  • **Sensitivity to Electrostatic Discharge (ESD):** Especially MOSFETs, the thin gate oxide is easily damaged by static electricity, requiring careful handling. While BJTs can also be damaged by ESD, MOSFETs are generally more sensitive.
  • **Lower Transconductance:** FETs typically have lower transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_D / \Delta V_{GS}\)) compared to the transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_C / \Delta V_{BE}\) or \(g_m = I_C/V_T\)) of BJTs for the same operating current. This means that for the same change in input voltage, a BJT can provide a larger change in output current.
  • **Limited Current Driving Capability:** Power BJTs can generally handle larger currents and powers compared to FETs of similar size, making BJTs often preferred for high-power switching or amplification applications.
  • **Switching Speed (sometimes):** Although MOSFETs can switch very fast, BJTs sometimes offer faster switching speeds in specific high-current applications due to lower ON-resistance limitations in some power FETs.

(b) Operation of a P-channel Junction Field Effect Transistor (JFET) with Diagram (7 marks):

A P-channel JFET consists of a P-type semiconductor channel with N-type regions diffused or implanted on opposite sides to form the gate. The operation relies on varying the effective width of the channel using a gate-source voltage to control current flow between the drain and source.

     Source (P) o---/\/\/\--o VSS (+)
                  |
                  | RS
                  |
        Gate (N) o---|>|--- Channel (P) --|>|---o Drain (P)
                 /
                /  Gate Regions (N)
               |
               +
             Ground or VGG (-)

Simplified diagram: P-channel between N-type gates. Arrow on gate symbol points outwards for P-channel JFET.

**Principle of Operation:**

For proper operation, the gate-source PN junction is typically reverse-biased. For a P-channel JFET, this means applying a positive voltage to the gate relative to the source (\(V_{GS} > 0\)). The drain is connected to a negative voltage relative to the source (\(V_{DS} < 0\)) to cause holes to flow from source to drain.

  • A negative voltage \(V_{DS}\) is applied between the drain and source. This causes holes (majority carriers in the P-channel) to flow from the source (positive terminal) through the channel to the drain (negative terminal), constituting the drain current \(I_D\). The current direction is usually shown as flowing from drain to source (conventional current, positive direction).
  • A positive voltage \(V_{GS}\) is applied between the gate and source. This reverse-biases the PN junction between the N-type gate and the P-channel.
  • The reverse bias voltage creates a depletion region within the P-channel, depleted of free charge carriers. This narrows the conductive path of the channel.
  • As the positive \(V_{GS}\) voltage is increased (made more positive), the reverse bias across the gate-channel junction increases, widening the depletion region and further constricting the channel.
  • If \(V_{GS}\) is made sufficiently positive, the depletion regions can meet, closing off the channel and reducing \(I_D\) to zero. This \(V_{GS}\) voltage is called the pinch-off voltage (\(V_p\)), which is positive for a P-channel JFET.

The P-channel JFET acts as a voltage-controlled resistor or current source, where the positive \(V_{GS}\) controls the negative \(I_D\) (or positive conventional \(I_D\) flowing S to D).

(c) Common Emitter Amplifier DC Bias Calculation (14 marks):

Figure 3 shows an NPN BJT common emitter amplifier with voltage divider bias. We need to determine the DC bias voltages and currents (Q-point). We are given \(V_{BE} = 0.7 \, \mathrm{V}\) and the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E\).

From Figure 3: \(V_{CC} = 12 \, \mathrm{V}\), \(R_1 = 20 \, k\Omega\), \(R_2 = 4.7 \, k\Omega\), \(R_C = 2.2 \, k\Omega\), \(R_E = 1 \, k\Omega\).

**(i) \(V_{B}\) (Base voltage):**

The base voltage is set by the voltage divider \(R_1\) and \(R_2\). Assume the base current \(I_B\) is small enough to be neglected compared to the current through the divider (valid if \(R_2 \ll \beta R_E\)).

\[ V_B = V_{CC} \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \] \[ V_B = 12 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{4.7 \, k\Omega}{20 \, k\Omega + 4.7 \, k\Omega} = 12 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{4.7}{24.7} \] \[ V_B \approx 12 \times 0.19028 \approx 2.2834 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The base voltage \(V_B\) is approximately 2.2834 V.

**(ii) \(V_{E}\) (Emitter voltage):**

The emitter voltage is \(V_B\) minus the base-emitter voltage drop \(V_{BE}\).

\[ V_E = V_B - V_{BE} = 2.2834 \, \mathrm{V} - 0.7 \, \mathrm{V} = 1.5834 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The emitter voltage \(V_E\) is approximately 1.5834 V.

**(iii) \(I_{E}\) (Emitter current):**

The emitter current \(I_E\) flows through \(R_E\).

\[ I_E = \frac{V_E}{R_E} = \frac{1.5834 \, V}{1 \, k\Omega} = \frac{1.5834}{1000} \, A = 0.0015834 \, A = 1.5834 \, mA \]

The emitter current \(I_E\) is approximately 1.5834 mA.

**(iii) \(I_{B}\) (Base current):** Using the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E\), the base current \(I_B = I_E - I_C\) is very small compared to \(I_E\) and \(I_C\). The exact value depends on the transistor's \(\beta\), where \(I_B = I_E / (1+\beta)\). Without knowing \(\beta\), we cannot calculate the exact value of \(I_B\).

Let's assume the intended calculation requires a typical \(\beta\). Given the marks, let's assume \(\beta \approx 100\). Then \(I_B = I_E / (1+\beta) = 1.5834mA / 101 \approx 0.01568mA = 15.68 \mu A\).

**(v) \(V_{C}\) (Collector voltage):** Using the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E \approx 1.5834 \, \mathrm{mA}\) for calculating the voltage drop across \(R_C\).

\[ V_C = V_{CC} - I_C R_C \approx 12 \, \mathrm{V} - (1.5834 \, \mathrm{mA}) \times (2.2 \, k\Omega) \] \[ V_C \approx 12 \, \mathrm{V} - (1.5834 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{A}) \times (2.2 \times 10^3 \, \Omega) = 12 \, \mathrm{V} - 3.4835 \, \mathrm{V} \] \[ V_C \approx 8.5165 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The collector voltage \(V_C\) is approximately 8.5165 V.

**(vi) \(V_{CE}\) (Collector-to-emitter voltage):**

\[ V_{CE} = V_C - V_E = 8.5165 \, \mathrm{V} - 1.5834 \, \mathrm{V} \] \[ V_{CE} \approx 6.9331 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The collector-to-emitter voltage \(V_{CE}\) is approximately 6.9331 V.

Check operating region: \(V_{CE} \approx 6.93V\). \(V_{BE}=0.7V\). Since \(V_{CE} > V_{BE}\), the transistor is in the active region as expected for an amplifier. Also check \(V_C > V_E\). \(8.5V > 1.58V\).

Summary of results (assuming \(\beta \approx 100\) for \(I_B\)):

  • **(i) Base voltage \(V_B\):** \( \approx 2.2834 \, V \)
  • **(ii) Emitter voltage \(V_E\):** \( \approx 1.5834 \, V \)
  • **(iii) Emitter current \(I_E\):** \( \approx 1.5834 \, mA \)
  • **(iv) Base current \(I_B\):** \( \approx 15.68 \, \mu A \) (based on \(\beta \approx 100\))
  • **(v) Collector voltage \(V_C\):** \( \approx 8.5165 \, V \)
  • **(vi) Collector-to-emitter voltage \(V_{CE}\):** \( \approx 6.9331 \, V \)
