Question 1

(a) (i) Distinguish between modifying and sustaining feedback signals. (6 marks)

(ii) Figure 1 shows a diagram for the speed control of a d.c motor.

(I) Explain the effect of an open circuit fault in the feedback line on the speed of the motor.

(II) Outline the procedure of locating the fault in a(ii) I and state the expected results.

(b) State four elements contained in a circuit diagram that can assist in the repair and maintenance of an electrical equipment. (4 marks)

(c) An electrical equipment has a repair rate of 0.6 and mean time between failure of 15,000 hours. Determine the: (10 marks)

(i) failure rate;

(ii) mean time to repair;

(iii) unavailability;

(iv) availability.

Answer

This question covers feedback control systems, documentation for maintenance, and reliability/maintainability calculations.

(a) Feedback Signals and DC Motor Speed Control (6 marks):

**(i) Distinction between modifying and sustaining feedback signals:**

  • **Modifying feedback signals:** These are signals from a measurement or sensor that represent the actual output or condition of the system and are fed back to be compared with the desired input or reference. The comparison generates an error signal that is used to *modify* the system's input or parameters to reduce the error. These are characteristic of negative feedback systems used for control and regulation (e.g., speed feedback in a motor control system, voltage feedback in a power supply).
  • **Sustaining feedback signals:** These are signals that are fed back in such a way as to *sustain* or reinforce the existing condition or oscillation in the system. These are characteristic of positive feedback systems, often used in oscillators or regenerative circuits, where a portion of the output is fed back in phase with the input to sustain oscillation.

**(ii) Effect and location procedure for open circuit fault in feedback line (Figure 1):**

Figure 1 shows a closed-loop DC motor speed control system where the tachogenerator provides negative feedback proportional to motor speed.

**(I) Effect of an open circuit fault in the feedback line:** If the feedback line is open, the comparator receives no actual speed feedback signal (or it receives a constant signal like 0V if pulled to ground or a reference). The comparator then perceives a large error equal to the difference between the input speed reference (desired speed) and the perceived feedback (e.g., 0V). This large error signal is amplified by the controller and power amplifier, causing the motor to accelerate to its maximum speed (limited by the supply voltage and motor characteristics) regardless of the desired speed setting. The control loop is broken, and the system is no longer regulated.

**(II) Procedure of locating the fault and expected results:**

  1. **Observation:** Observe the motor behavior. If it runs at maximum speed uncontrollably when power is on, it strongly suggests a feedback issue.
  2. **Check Input Reference:** Verify the input speed reference signal is present and can be varied (e.g., measure voltage at the input reference terminal).
  3. **Check Tachogenerator Output:** Measure the output voltage of the tachogenerator (usually at the tachogenerator terminals) while the motor is running. This voltage should be proportional to the motor's speed.
  4. **Check Feedback Line Continuity:** With power OFF, disconnect the feedback wire at both ends (at the tachogenerator and the comparator input). Measure the resistance of the feedback wire using a multimeter. An open circuit will show a very high or infinite resistance (OL).
  5. **Check Comparator Input:** If the wire shows continuity, check the voltage at the comparator input terminal where the feedback line connects, while the system is powered and running. If the tachogenerator output is present but this point shows 0V (or unexpected level), the open circuit is in the wiring or connection between the tachogenerator output and the comparator input.

**Expected Results:** If the feedback line is open, the multimeter continuity check will show infinite resistance. When the system is powered, the tachogenerator might show a voltage proportional to speed at its terminals, but the voltage at the comparator input terminal connected to the feedback line will be disconnected and likely floating or pulled to a different potential, not showing the speed-proportional feedback.

(b) Circuit Diagram Elements for Repair (4 marks):

A circuit diagram (schematic) is a crucial tool for repair and maintenance. Four elements it contains that assist this are:

  • **Component Identifiers and Values:** Labels for each component (e.g., R1, C2, Q5, IC1) and their nominal values (e.g., \(10k\Omega\), \(47\mu F\), LM741, 2N2222) make it easy to identify and find components on the physical board and determine replacement parts.
  • **Component Interconnections:** Lines representing wires or PCB traces show how components are electrically connected, allowing signal paths and power distribution to be traced.
  • **Test Points and Expected Voltages/Waveforms:** Specific points in the circuit may be labeled as test points (TP) with indicated DC voltages or expected AC waveforms/amplitudes in a working circuit. These provide diagnostic checkpoints.
  • **Functional Blocks/Stages:** Complex circuits are often broken down into logical functional blocks (e.g., Power Supply, Oscillator, Amplifier Stage, Filter). The diagram shows how these blocks connect, aiding in isolating the faulty section.
  • **Power Supply Connections:** Indications of voltage rails (e.g., +VCC, -VEE, GND) and how they connect to components.
  • **Pin Numbers for ICs and Transistors:** Pinout information for multi-pin components.

(c) Reliability/Maintainability Calculations (10 marks):

Given the repair rate and mean time between failure for an equipment. We can calculate other maintainability and availability metrics.

Given:

  • Repair rate (\(\mu\)) = 0.6
  • Mean Time Between Failure (MTBF) = 15,000 hours.

Assuming the repair rate unit is repairs per hour, then MTTR = \(1/\mu\).

**(i) failure rate:**

The failure rate (\(\lambda\)) is the reciprocal of the MTBF, assuming a constant failure rate (useful life period).

\[ \lambda = \frac{1}{MTBF} = \frac{1}{15000 \, \text{hours}} \approx 6.667 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{failures/hour} \]

**(ii) mean time to repair:**

The Mean Time To Repair (MTTR) is the reciprocal of the repair rate (\(\mu\)).

\[ MTTR = \frac{1}{\mu} = \frac{1}{0.6 \, \text{repairs/unit time}} \approx 1.667 \, \text{unit time/repair} \]

The unit of time for the repair rate (0.6) is not specified. Assuming it's repairs per hour, then MTTR is in hours.

Assuming repair rate is 0.6 repairs/hour, MTTR \(\approx 1.667\) hours.

**(iii) unavailability:**

Unavailability (\(U\)) is the long-term probability that the equipment is NOT operational. \( U = \frac{MTTR}{MTBF + MTTR} \).

\[ U = \frac{1.667 \, \text{hours}}{15000 \, \text{hours} + 1.667 \, \text{hours}} = \frac{1.667}{15001.667} \approx 0.0001111 \]

**(iv) availability:**

Availability (\(A\)) is the long-term probability that the equipment is operational. \( A = 1 - U \) or \( A = \frac{MTBF}{MTBF + MTTR} \).

\[ A = 1 - 0.0001111 = 0.9998889 \]

or

\[ A = \frac{15000}{15000 + 1.667} \approx 0.9998889 \]

Maintainability, reliability, and availability metrics are crucial for assessing the performance and readiness of electrical equipment.

Question 2

(a) State three factors that can introduce errors in power measurements when using wattmeters. (6 marks)

(b) An amplitude modulated signal has a maximum amplitude of \( \mathrm{V}_{\max} \) and a minimum amplitude of \( \mathrm{V}_{\text {min}} \). (8 marks)

(i) Obtain the expression of the modulation index in terms of the carrier voltage, \( \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{c}} \) and the modulating signal voltage, \( \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{m}} \).

(ii) Sketch the waveform of the signal in b(i).

(c) A search coil has 500 turns and a mean area of \( 450 \mathrm{~mm}^{2} \). The coil is placed at the centre of a solenoid 1.5 m long, wound with 700 turns. When a current of 5.5 A is reversed, there is a deflection of 26 scale divisions on a flux meter connected to the search coil. Determine the flux linkages per scale division. (11 marks)

Answer

This question covers errors in wattmeters, properties of amplitude modulated signals, and magnetic flux measurement calculations.

(a) Errors in Power Measurements using Wattmeters (6 marks):

Wattmeters are electromechanical or electronic instruments used to measure electrical power. Sources of error include:

  • **Pressure coil inductance:** The voltage coil (pressure coil) is assumed to be purely resistive, but it has some inductance. This causes a small phase shift between the voltage applied to the pressure coil and the current through it, leading to errors, especially at low power factors or high frequencies.
  • **Power consumption of the coils:** Both the current coil and the pressure coil consume a small amount of power. This power is added to the power being measured, introducing an error. The error depends on how the pressure coil is connected (on the supply side or load side).
  • **Eddy currents:** Alternating magnetic fields induce eddy currents in metal parts of the wattmeter, creating opposing magnetic fields and affecting the deflecting torque. This error is frequency-dependent.
  • **Stray magnetic fields:** External magnetic fields can interact with the fields produced by the wattmeter coils, creating an erroneous torque.
  • **Friction:** Friction in the bearings of the moving system introduces a retarding torque, affecting accuracy, especially at low power levels.

(b) Amplitude Modulated (AM) Signal (8 marks):

An AM signal is formed by varying the amplitude of a high-frequency carrier signal according to the instantaneous amplitude of a lower-frequency modulating signal.

Let the carrier signal be \( v_c(t) = V_c \sin(\omega_c t) \) and the modulating signal be \( v_m(t) = V_m \sin(\omega_m t) \), where \(\omega_c \gg \omega_m\).

The instantaneous amplitude of the AM wave is \( A(t) = V_c + v_m(t) = V_c + V_m \sin(\omega_m t) \).

The AM waveform is \( v_{AM}(t) = A(t) \sin(\omega_c t) = (V_c + V_m \sin(\omega_m t)) \sin(\omega_c t) \).

The maximum amplitude of the envelope occurs when \(\sin(\omega_m t) = +1\), so \( V_{max} = V_c + V_m \).

The minimum amplitude of the envelope occurs when \(\sin(\omega_m t) = -1\), so \( V_{min} = V_c - V_m \).

**(i) Expression for the modulation index (\(m\)) in terms of \(V_c\) and \(V_m\):**

The modulation index is a measure of the depth of modulation. It is defined as the ratio of the amplitude of the modulating signal to the amplitude of the carrier signal.

\[ m = \frac{V_m}{V_c} \]

Using \(V_{max}\) and \(V_{min}\):

\[ V_{max} + V_{min} = (V_c + V_m) + (V_c - V_m) = 2V_c \implies V_c = \frac{V_{max} + V_{min}}{2} \] \[ V_{max} - V_{min} = (V_c + V_m) - (V_c - V_m) = 2V_m \implies V_m = \frac{V_{max} - V_{min}}{2} \]

Substituting these into the definition of \(m\):

\[ m = \frac{V_m}{V_c} = \frac{(V_{max} - V_{min})/2}{(V_{max} + V_{min})/2} = \frac{V_{max} - V_{min}}{V_{max} + V_{min}} \]

The expression in terms of \(V_c\) and \(V_m\) is \( m = \frac{V_m}{V_c} \).

The envelope shows the shape of the modulating signal (V_m).

The sketch should show a high-frequency carrier wave with its amplitude varying according to a lower-frequency signal. The upper and lower envelopes should be clearly indicated, and they should trace the shape of the modulating signal. The peak amplitude of the envelope corresponds to \(V_c + V_m\), and the minimum amplitude corresponds to \(V_c - V_m\).

(c) Magnetic Flux Linkages per Scale Division (11 marks):

This problem involves calculating the change in magnetic flux produced by reversing the current in a solenoid and relating it to the deflection of a fluxmeter connected to a search coil.

Given:

  • Search coil: \(N_s = 500\) turns, mean area \(A_s = 450 \, \mathrm{mm}^2 = 450 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{m}^2\).
  • Solenoid: Length \(l = 1.5 \, m\), \(N_{sol} = 700\) turns.
  • Current in solenoid \(I = 5.5 \, A\), reversed (change is \(2I\)).
  • Fluxmeter deflection \(\theta = 26\) scale divisions.
  • Permeability of free space \( \mu_{0}=4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{H} / \mathrm{m} \).

The magnetic field \(H\) at the center of a long solenoid is \( H = \frac{N_{sol} I}{l} \). The flux density \(B\) inside the solenoid (assuming air core) is \( B = \mu_0 H = \mu_0 \frac{N_{sol} I}{l} \).

\[ B = (4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \mathrm{H/m}) \times \frac{700 \text{ turns} \times 5.5 \, \mathrm{A}}{1.5 \, \mathrm{m}} \] \[ B = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times \frac{3850}{1.5} \quad \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \] \[ B \approx 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 2566.67 \approx 10266.68\pi \times 10^{-7} \approx 0.001027\pi \approx 0.003228 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \]

When the current in the solenoid is reversed, the flux density changes from \(B\) to \(-B\), a total change of \( \Delta B = 2B \).

\[ \Delta B = 2 \times (0.003228 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2}) \approx 0.006456 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \]

The change in flux through the search coil (placed at the center of the solenoid) is \( \Delta\Phi = \Delta B \times A_s \).

\[ \Delta\Phi = (0.006456 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2}) \times (450 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{m}^2) \] \[ \Delta\Phi \approx 2.9052 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb} \]

The total change in flux linkage (\(\Delta\Psi\)) with the search coil is the change in flux multiplied by the number of turns in the search coil:

\[ \Delta\Psi = N_s \times \Delta\Phi = 500 \text{ turns} \times (2.9052 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb}) \] \[ \Delta\Psi = 1452.6 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns} = 1.4526 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns} \]

This change in flux linkage causes a deflection of 26 scale divisions on the fluxmeter. The flux linkages per scale division is the total change in flux linkage divided by the deflection:

\[ \text{Flux linkages per scale division} = \frac{\Delta\Psi}{\theta} \] \[ \text{Flux linkages per scale division} = \frac{1.4526 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns}}{26 \text{ divisions}} \] \[ \text{Flux linkages per scale division} \approx 0.05587 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns/division} \] \[ \text{Flux linkages per scale division} \approx 55.87 \, \mu\mathrm{Wb-turns/division} \]

This question covers instrument errors, AM modulation concepts, and magnetic measurement calculations involving solenoids, search coils, and fluxmeters.

This question covers equipment failure types, the economic relationship with reliability, and calculating reliability metrics from component data.

(a) Equipment Failure Definitions (6 marks):

  • **(i) Misuse failure:** A failure caused by operating the equipment outside its specified environmental or operational limits, or using it for a purpose or in a manner for which it was not designed. Examples: operating at excessive temperature/voltage, incorrect handling, using incompatible accessories.
  • **(ii) Sudden failure:** A failure that occurs abruptly and unexpectedly, with no prior indication or warning. Often leads to the immediate cessation of function (catastrophic failure). Example: A fuse blowing, a component failing internally and open/shorting instantaneously.
  • **(iii) Partial failure:** A failure where the equipment still performs some of its intended functions, but its performance is degraded or below the specified requirements. Example: A power supply providing voltage with excessive ripple, an amplifier producing distorted output, intermittent operation.

  • **(i) Production Cost:** The cost of manufacturing the equipment. The sum of these costs (Production + Design + Maintenance/Repair) results in a total cost curve that is typically U-shaped. The minimum point of the total cost curve indicates the economically optimal level of reliability.

Question 4

(a) (i) Define each of the following with respect to electrical measurements: (6 marks)

I. standard;

II. derived unit.

(ii) Describe each of the following standards of measurement: (6 marks)

I. international;

II. primary.

(b) Using LMTI system of dimensions, prove that the equation \( \mathrm{e}=\mathrm{Blv} \) is dimensionally correct, where \( e= \) e.m.f induced in a conductor, \( B= \) flux density, \( I= \) effective length of conductor and \( \mathrm{V}= \) velocity at which the conductor cuts the magnetic field. (8 marks)

(c) Determine the mechanical quantity represented by the dimensional equation. \( (\mathrm{MLT}^{-1}) \) (5 marks)

Answer

This question focuses on the fundamental concepts of units, standards, and dimensional analysis.

(a) Definitions and Standards of Measurement (12 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to electrical measurements:**

  • **I. Standard:** A standard, in the context of measurements, is a reference quantity used to define, realize, or reproduce a unit of a physical quantity. It provides a consistent magnitude of a quantity used for comparison to measure other quantities of the same kind. Examples include the standard kilogram or the atomic clock.
  • **II. Derived unit:** A unit of measurement for a physical quantity that is defined as a combination of fundamental units. Example: The unit of force (Newton) is derived from mass, length, and time units (kg⋅m/s²).

**(ii) Description of standards of measurement:**

  • **I. International Standard:** These are the highest accuracy standards, maintained at international metrology laboratories (like BIPM). They are based on the fundamental definitions of SI units and serve as the ultimate reference for all measurements globally. They are used to calibrate national standards.
  • **II. Primary Standard:** These are standards of the highest metrological quality available at national standards laboratories. Their values are established directly from the fundamental definitions of the units or by comparison with international standards. They are used to calibrate secondary standards but are not used for routine measurements.

(b) Dimensional Analysis of \(e = Blv\) in LMTI System (8 marks):

We need to show that the dimensions on both sides of the equation \(e = Blv\) are the same in the LMTI system (Length L, Mass M, Time T, Current I).

Left-hand side: \(e\) is e.m.f. induced in a conductor. e.m.f. is a voltage, which is energy per unit charge. Energy has dimensions \([ML^2T^{-2}]\). Charge has dimensions \([IT]\).

\[ [e] = \frac{[\text{Energy}]}{[\text{Charge}]} = \frac{[ML^2T^{-2}]}{[IT]} = [ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}] \]

Right-hand side: \(B\) is flux density, \(l\) is length, \(v\) is velocity.

Magnetic flux density \(B\). From the force on a conductor in a magnetic field \(F = BI l\), \(B = F / (Il)\). Force \(F\) has dimensions \([MLT^{-2}]\). Current \(I\) is \([I]\). Length \(l\) is \([L]\).

\[ [B] = \frac{[MLT^{-2}]}{[I][L]} = [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \]

Length \(l\) has dimensions \([L]\).

Velocity \(v\) has dimensions \([LT^{-1}]\).

Now, multiply the dimensions of \(B, l, v\):

\[ [Blv] = [B] \times [l] \times [v] \] \[ [Blv] = [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \times [L] \times [LT^{-1}] \] \[ [Blv] = [M^1 L^{1+1} T^{-2-1} I^{-1}] = [ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}] \]

LHS dimensions: \([ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}]\).

RHS dimensions: \([ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}]\).

Since the dimensions on both sides are the same, the equation \(e = Blv\) is dimensionally correct.

(c) Mechanical quantity represented by \((\mathrm{MLT}^{-1})\) (5 marks):

Let's consider common mechanical quantities and their dimensions in the MLT system:

  • Force: \(F = ma\). \([F] = [M][LT^{-2}] = [MLT^{-2}]\).
  • Energy/Work: \(W = Fd\). \([W] = [MLT^{-2}][L] = [ML^2T^{-2}]\).
  • Power: \(P = W/t\). \([P] = [ML^2T^{-2}]/[T] = [ML^2T^{-3}]\).
  • Velocity: \(v = d/t\). \([v] = [L]/[T] = [LT^{-1}]\).
  • Acceleration: \(a = v/t\). \([a] = [LT^{-1}]/[T] = [LT^{-2}]\).
  • Momentum: \(p = mv\). \([p] = [M][LT^{-1}] = [MLT^{-1}]\).

The dimensional equation \( [MLT^{-1}] \) represents **momentum**.

This question tests fundamental understanding of units, standards, and the application of dimensional analysis to verify physical equations and identify quantities based on their dimensions.

This question focuses on maintenance practices, fault diagnosis in DC motors and power supplies, and calculating maintainability metrics.

(a) Routine Maintenance Actions and Preventive Maintenance (8 marks):

**(i) Three service actions during routine maintenance:**

  • **Inspection:** Visually checking the equipment for signs of wear, damage, leaks, corrosion, overheating, or loose connections.
  • **Cleaning:** Removing dust, dirt, debris, and contaminants from the equipment surfaces and internal components to prevent overheating, insulation breakdown, or interference with moving parts.
  • **Lubrication:** Applying appropriate lubricants to moving parts (bearings, gears) as per manufacturer recommendations to reduce friction, wear, and heat generation.
  • **Testing:** Performing basic operational checks or measurements (e.g., voltage, current, speed, temperature) to verify the equipment is functioning within specified parameters.
  • **Adjustments:** Making minor adjustments (e.g., belt tension, alignment, calibration) to maintain optimal performance.

**(ii) Explain preventive maintenance:**

Preventive maintenance is a proactive maintenance strategy where maintenance tasks are performed at predetermined intervals or according to specified criteria (e.g., time-based, usage-based) while the equipment is still operating, in order to reduce the likelihood of failure or degradation of performance. Its goal is to prevent breakdowns by addressing potential issues before they cause significant problems. Examples include scheduled inspections, lubrication, cleaning, and component replacements based on expected lifespan (e.g., replacing filters, belts, or capacitors after a certain operating time).

(b) Causes of Faults in a DC Motor (6 marks):

Common operational issues in DC motors and their causes:

  • **(i) High bearing temperature:**
    • Lack of lubrication or incorrect lubricant type.
    • Worn or damaged bearings.
    • Excessive load or mechanical misalignment.
    • Contamination (dirt, moisture) in the bearing grease.
  • **(ii) Excessive sparking at motor commutator:**
    • Incorrect brush position (shifting neutral axis).
    • Worn, damaged, or incorrect grade of brushes.
    • Rough, dirty, or damaged commutator surface (e.g., pitting, bar wear, high mica).
    • Overload on the motor.
    • Short-circuited or open-circuited armature coils.
    • Incorrect brush pressure.
  • **(iii) Rapid wear of brushes:**
    • Rough or abrasive commutator surface.
    • Incorrect brush material for the application.
    • Excessive brush pressure.
    • Operation in a contaminated or aggressive atmosphere.
    • Excessive sparking.

(c) Effects of Faults in Regulated DC Power Supply (Figure 2) (8 marks):

Figure 2 shows a typical linear regulated DC power supply consisting of a transformer, rectifier, filter capacitor (C1), IC regulator, and output capacitor (C2).

**(i) Diode D1 open circuit:**

  • D1 is one of the diodes in the bridge rectifier. If D1 opens, the bridge rectifier will effectively become a half-wave rectifier circuit (using D2 and D3, or D4 depending on connection).
  • **Effect:** The output of the bridge will be pulsating DC from only half of the AC cycle. The ripple frequency will be the same as the input frequency (e.g., 50 Hz or 60 Hz) instead of double (e.g., 100 Hz or 120 Hz). The average voltage after the rectifier will be lower.
  • **Symptom:** The voltage across C1 will have a much larger ripple amplitude and a lower average value. The IC regulator might fail to maintain a stable output voltage if its input voltage drops too low during parts of the cycle, resulting in a regulated output with significant ripple or a lower average value.

**(ii) Capacitor C1 short circuit:**

  • C1 is the main filter capacitor after the rectifier. If it shorts out, it creates a very low resistance path across the output of the rectifier.
  • **Effect:** This will essentially short-circuit the rectifier's output terminals. A large current will flow from the transformer secondary through the rectifier diodes and C1. This large current will likely blow a fuse (if present) or damage the transformer secondary winding and/or the rectifier diodes. The IC regulator will receive no voltage or a very low/shorted voltage at its input.
  • **Symptom:** No output voltage from the entire power supply. Blown fuse, possible smell of burning, rectifier diodes or transformer may be hot or damaged.

**(iii) Terminal 3 of the IC regulator open to ground:**

Terminal 3 is typically the "Ground" or "Adjust" terminal of a 3-terminal regulator IC (like a 78xx or LM317). It provides the reference for the output voltage. For fixed regulators (like 78xx), it's directly connected to ground. For adjustable regulators (like LM317), it's connected to ground via a resistor.

  • **Effect (Assuming 78xx type where terminal 3 is Ground):** If the ground connection is open, the regulator loses its reference. The internal feedback loop requires this terminal to be at ground potential relative to the output voltage regulation. Without a stable ground reference, the regulator cannot function correctly.
  • **Symptom:** The output voltage will likely be unregulated and much higher than the rated voltage, potentially rising close to the input voltage (\(V_{in}\) to the IC). This can damage the connected load. Or, the regulator might simply shut down or produce unstable output depending on the IC design.

**(iv) Capacitor C2 open circuit:**

  • C2 is the output filter capacitor after the IC regulator. Its function is to smooth out any remaining high-frequency ripple or transient voltages from the regulator output and improve load transient response.
  • **Effect:** If C2 is open, the filtering of high-frequency components at the output is reduced or eliminated. The regulator's transient response to sudden changes in load current might also be degraded.
  • **Symptom:** The output DC voltage may appear stable for steady loads, but closer inspection with an oscilloscope will reveal more high-frequency noise or ripple, and potentially larger voltage dips or overshoots when the load current changes rapidly. The DC voltage level should remain correct.

(d) Mean Time to Repair (MTTR) Calculation (3 marks):

The repair rate (\(\mu\)) is the number of repairs completed per unit of time. It is the reciprocal of the Mean Time To Repair (MTTR).

Given: Repair rate \( \mu = 0.5 \).

\[ MTTR = \frac{1}{\mu} \] \[ MTTR = \frac{1}{0.5} = 2 \, \text{time units} \]

Since the unit for the repair rate (0.5) is not specified (e.g., repairs/hour, repairs/day), the unit for MTTR will be the reciprocal of that unit. Assuming the repair rate is in repairs/hour, then MTTR is in hours.

Answer

This question covers fundamental semiconductor physics, atomic models, and diode characteristics and specifications.

(a) Semiconductor Definitions and p-type Formation (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to semiconductors:**

  • **(I) Covalent bond:** A type of chemical bond formed between atoms by the sharing of valence electrons. In intrinsic semiconductors like silicon or germanium, each atom forms four covalent bonds with its neighboring atoms in a crystal lattice structure.
  • **(II) Drift current:** The movement of charge carriers (electrons and holes) in a semiconductor due to the presence of an electric field. The electric field exerts a force on the carriers, causing them to move in a specific direction (electrons opposite to the field, holes in the direction of the field), resulting in a net current flow.

**(ii) Formation of a p-type semiconductor with a labelled diagram:**

A p-type semiconductor is formed by doping an intrinsic semiconductor (e.g., silicon) with a small amount of trivalent impurity atoms (accepter impurities) like Boron, Gallium, or Indium.

        Silicon Atom (Si)   Boron Atom (B)
                                      (Trivalent Impurity)
        Si      Si        Si       Si
        |       |         |        |
      Si-Si-----Si-------Si-Si   <-- Si lattice
        |       |         |        |
        Si------B---------Si   <-- B forms 3 bonds, needs 1 electron
        |       |         |        |    (creates a HOLE)
      Si------Si---------Si
        |       |
        Si      Si

Diagram showing Silicon atoms forming 4 covalent bonds, and one Boron atom in the lattice forming only 3 covalent bonds with Si atoms, leaving a "hole". Boron becomes a fixed negative ion (Acceptor).

**Description:** Silicon is a group IV element with 4 valence electrons. A trivalent impurity atom (group III element) has only 3 valence electrons. When a trivalent atom substitutes a silicon atom in the crystal lattice, it forms covalent bonds with three neighboring silicon atoms. The bond with the fourth silicon atom is incomplete, as the trivalent atom can only share 3 electrons, while the silicon atom has 4 electrons to share. This deficiency of an electron in the covalent bond creates a "hole". These holes are free to move throughout the crystal lattice, acting as positive charge carriers. The trivalent impurity atoms, having accepted an electron to complete three bonds, become fixed negative ions in the lattice. In a p-type semiconductor, holes are the majority carriers, and electrons are the minority carriers.

(b) Rutherford's atomic structure (4 marks):

Ernest Rutherford proposed a model of the atom based on the results of his gold foil scattering experiment (Geiger-Marsden experiment).

**Explanation:** Rutherford's model, sometimes called the nuclear model, described the atom as having:

  • A very small, dense, positively charged **nucleus** located at the center of the atom. This nucleus contains most of the atom's mass (attributed to protons and neutrons, although neutrons were discovered later).
  • Negatively charged **electrons** orbiting around the nucleus in definite paths, much like planets orbiting the sun.
  • The atom is mostly **empty space**, as evidenced by the fact that most alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil without deflection.
  • The total positive charge of the nucleus is equal to the total negative charge of the orbiting electrons, making the atom electrically neutral.

This model replaced the earlier plum-pudding model but had limitations, particularly in explaining why orbiting electrons (which are accelerating charges) did not lose energy and spiral into the nucleus according to classical physics.

(c) Semiconductor Diode Specifications and V-I Curve (6 marks):

**(i) Three parameters for semiconductor diode specifications:**

  • **Maximum Forward Current (\(I_F\)):** The maximum average current the diode can safely handle in the forward-biased direction without overheating or damage.
  • **Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) or Maximum Reverse Voltage (\(V_R\)):** The maximum instantaneous reverse voltage the diode can withstand without entering the breakdown region.
  • **Forward Voltage Drop (\(V_F\))**: The typical voltage across the diode when it is conducting a specified forward current (e.g., 0.7V for silicon at a certain current). This might be listed as \(V_{F(typ)}\) at a given \(I_F\).
  • **Maximum Power Dissipation (\(P_D\)):** The maximum power the diode can dissipate safely, usually a product of forward voltage and current, or reverse voltage and leakage current (in breakdown, \(P_D = V_Z \times I_Z\)).
  • **Reverse Saturation Current (\(I_S\) or \(I_R\)):** The small leakage current in the reverse-biased direction (specified at a certain reverse voltage).
  • **Reverse Breakdown Voltage (\(V_{BR}\) or \(V_Z\)):** The reverse voltage at which the diode enters the breakdown region. (Crucial for Zener diodes).
  • **Operating Temperature Range:** The range of ambient or junction temperatures over which the diode is specified to operate reliably.

**(ii) Sketch a labelled voltage-current characteristic curve of a semiconductor diode:**

         ^ Forward Current (Id)
         |
         |      /
         |     / Forward Bias Region
         |    /
         |   /
         |  /
  -------+--*---*------------> Voltage (Vd)
         |  Vk  0
  Reverse|
  Current|
   (Ir)  | Reverse Bias Region (very small current)
         |    I_s (Reverse Saturation Current)
  -------+-------------------
         | Vbr (Reverse Breakdown Voltage)
         V

The sketch should show the forward bias region (voltage positive, current positive, with a knee voltage \(V_K\)), the reverse bias region (voltage negative, current very small and negative, \(I_S\)), and the reverse breakdown region (voltage becomes very negative beyond \(V_{BR}\), current increases rapidly negative).

(c) An electron released from the cathode CRT is accelerated to the anode by an accelerating potential of 2 kV in a time of \( 0.25 \mu \mathrm{S} \). Taking the mass of electron, \( =9.1 \times 10^{-31} \mathrm{~kg} \) and electronic charge, \( \mathrm{e}=1.6 \times 10^{-19} \mathrm{~C} \), determine the: (10 marks)

(i) Velocity of the electron;

(ii) acceleration of the electron;

(iii) Loss in potential energy;

Answer

This question covers DC power supply components, Zener diode regulators, power supply performance metrics (regulation, ripple), and electron acceleration in a CRT.

(a) DC Power Supply Components and Zener Regulator (10 marks):

**(i) Function of power supply parts:**

  • **I. Filter:** A circuit (typically using capacitors and/or inductors) placed after the rectifier to reduce the amount of AC ripple in the pulsating DC output. It stores energy during peaks and releases it during valleys of the waveform, providing a smoother DC output.
  • **II. Transformer:** An electromagnetic device used to step up or step down the AC input voltage to a suitable level for the rectifier and rest of the power supply circuit. It also provides electrical isolation between the AC mains and the power supply circuit.

**(ii) Operation of a Zener diode voltage regulator with a circuit diagram:**

      Vin (Unregulated)
   o------/\/\/\----+----o Vout (Regulated)
          Rs        |    |
                    |    ---
                    |    --- Load RL
                  |>|    |
                  | | Zener|
                  | | Diode|
                  ---    |
                   -     |
                    +----o Ground

**Description:** The circuit consists of a series resistor \(R_S\) connected between the unregulated input voltage \(V_{in}\) and the Zener diode, which is connected in parallel (shunt) with the load \(R_L\). The Zener diode is connected in reverse bias. \(R_S\) limits the current flowing into the Zener and the load. When the input voltage \(V_{in}\) is greater than the Zener breakdown voltage \(V_Z\), the Zener diode enters the breakdown region and maintains a nearly constant voltage \(V_Z\) across its terminals and, therefore, across the parallel load \(R_L\). If \(V_{in}\) increases, the current through \(R_S\) increases; the Zener current \(I_Z\) increases while \(V_L\) remains nearly constant. If the load current \(I_L\) decreases (due to increasing \(R_L\)), \(I_Z\) increases to keep \(I_S\) constant (\(I_S = I_Z + I_L\)), maintaining \(V_L\) constant. Regulation is maintained as long as the Zener stays in breakdown (\(I_Z \ge I_{Z,min}\)) and its maximum current rating is not exceeded (\(I_Z \le I_{Z,max}\)). (Same as Q8c(ii) Nov 2018)

(b) DC Power Supply Performance Metrics (10 marks):

Given information about a DC power supply's output voltage under different load conditions and its ripple.

Given:

  • No load output voltage (\(V_{NL}\)) = 60 V
  • Full load output voltage (\(V_{FL}\)) = 56 V
  • Ripple component RMS voltage (\(V_{ripple,rms}\)) = 1.25 V rms

**(i) Percentage voltage regulation:**

Voltage regulation is a measure of how well a power supply maintains a constant output voltage as the load changes. It is usually expressed as the percentage change in output voltage from no load to full load, relative to the full load voltage.

\[ \% \text{Voltage Regulation} = \frac{V_{NL} - V_{FL}}{V_{FL}} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Voltage Regulation} = \frac{60 \, V - 56 \, V}{56 \, V} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Voltage Regulation} = \frac{4 \, V}{56 \, V} \times 100\% = \frac{1}{14} \times 100\% \approx 7.143 \% \]

**(ii) Percentage ripple:**

Percentage ripple is a measure of the amount of AC voltage superimposed on the DC output voltage. It's typically the ratio of the RMS ripple voltage to the DC output voltage, expressed as a percentage.

The DC output voltage can be taken as the full load voltage \(V_{FL}\) if the ripple is measured at full load, or the average DC voltage. Assuming the ripple is measured at full load, the DC output voltage reference is \(V_{FL}\).

\[ \% \text{Ripple} = \frac{V_{ripple,rms}}{V_{dc}} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Ripple} = \frac{1.25 \, V_{rms}}{56 \, V} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Ripple} \approx 0.02232 \times 100\% \approx 2.232 \% \]

(c) CRT Electron Acceleration Calculations (10 marks):

Calculations for an electron accelerated in a Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) by an accelerating potential.

Given:

  • Accelerating potential \(V_a = 2 \, kV = 2000 \, V\).
  • Time taken for acceleration = \(0.25 \, \mu S = 0.25 \times 10^{-6} \, s\). (Note: Time for acceleration is usually very short, this value might be for transit through some region, but the question states "accelerated ... in a time of").
  • Mass of electron \(m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg\).
  • Electronic charge \(e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C\).

Assume the electron starts with negligible initial velocity from the cathode.

**(i) Velocity of the electron:**

The velocity (\(v\)) after acceleration by \(V_a\) is found from the kinetic energy gained (\( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \)) equaling the potential energy lost (\(e V_a\)).

\[ \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = e V_a \] \[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2 e V_a}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V)}{9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg}} \] \[ v = \sqrt{\frac{6.4 \times 10^{-16}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{0.70329 \times 10^{15}} = \sqrt{7.0329 \times 10^{14}} \] \[ v \approx 2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s} \]

**(ii) Acceleration of the electron:**

Acceleration (\(a\)) is the change in velocity divided by the time taken. Assuming linear acceleration over the given time.

\[ a = \frac{\text{Final Velocity} - \text{Initial Velocity}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{v - 0}{t_{accel}} \] \[ a = \frac{2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s}}{0.25 \times 10^{-6} \, s} \] \[ a = \frac{2.652}{0.25} \times 10^{7 - (-6)} \, \mathrm{m/s}^2 = 10.608 \times 10^{13} \, \mathrm{m/s}^2 \] \[ a \approx 1.061 \times 10^{14} \, \mathrm{m/s}^2 \]

Note: This assumes the 0.25 µS is the actual time taken to accelerate from rest to the final velocity, which occurs within the electron gun region. This might be an oversimplification depending on the specific electron gun design.

**(iii) Loss in potential energy:**

The loss in potential energy is equal to the charge multiplied by the potential difference it falls through.

\[ \text{Loss in PE} = e V_a = (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V) = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

**(iv) Gain in kinetic energy:**

According to the work-energy theorem, the gain in kinetic energy is equal to the work done by the electric field, which is equal to the loss in potential energy (assuming no other forces like friction are doing work). Starting from rest (initial KE = 0), the gain in KE is equal to the final KE.

\[ \text{Gain in KE} = \text{Final KE} - \text{Initial KE} = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 - 0 \] \[ \text{Gain in KE} = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

The gain in kinetic energy is \(3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J\).

(ii) \( V_{B} \);

(iii) \( I_{B} \);

(iv) \( V_{C} \);

(v) \( V_{CE} \)

Answer

This question compares FETs and BJTs, describes JFET operation, and involves DC bias calculations for a BJT common-emitter amplifier.

(a) Disadvantages of Field Effect Transistors (FETs) compared to Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) (4 marks):

  • **Sensitivity to Electrostatic Discharge (ESD):** Especially MOSFETs, the thin gate oxide is easily damaged by static electricity, requiring careful handling. While BJTs can also be damaged by ESD, MOSFETs are generally more sensitive.
  • **Lower Transconductance:** FETs typically have lower transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_D / \Delta V_{GS}\)) compared to the transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_C / \Delta V_{BE}\) or \(g_m = I_C/V_T\)) of BJTs for the same operating current. This means that for the same change in input voltage, a BJT can provide a larger change in output current.
  • **Limited Current Driving Capability:** Power BJTs can generally handle larger currents and powers compared to FETs of similar size, making BJTs often preferred for high-power switching or amplification applications.
  • **Switching Speed (sometimes):** Although MOSFETs can switch very fast, BJTs sometimes offer faster switching speeds in specific high-current applications due to lower ON-resistance limitations in some power FETs.

(b) Operation of a P-channel Junction Field Effect Transistor (JFET) with Diagram (7 marks):

A P-channel JFET consists of a P-type semiconductor channel with N-type regions diffused or implanted on opposite sides to form the gate. The operation relies on varying the effective width of the channel using a gate-source voltage to control current flow between the drain and source.

     Source (P) o---/\/\/\--o VSS (+)
                  |
                  | RS
                  |
        Gate (N) o---|>|--- Channel (P) --|>|---o Drain (P)
                 /
                /  Gate Regions (N)
               |
               +
             Ground or VGG (-)

Simplified diagram: P-channel between N-type gates. Arrow on gate symbol points outwards for P-channel JFET.

**Principle of Operation:**

For proper operation, the gate-source PN junction is typically reverse-biased. For a P-channel JFET, this means applying a positive voltage to the gate relative to the source (\(V_{GS} > 0\)). The drain is connected to a negative voltage relative to the source (\(V_{DS} < 0\)) to cause holes to flow from source to drain.

  • A negative voltage \(V_{DS}\) is applied between the drain and source. This causes holes (majority carriers in the P-channel) to flow from the source (positive terminal) through the channel to the drain (negative terminal), constituting the drain current \(I_D\). The current direction is usually shown as flowing from drain to source (conventional current, positive direction).
  • A positive voltage \(V_{GS}\) is applied between the gate and source. This reverse-biases the PN junction between the N-type gate and the P-channel.
  • The reverse bias voltage creates a depletion region within the P-channel, depleted of free charge carriers. This narrows the conductive path of the channel.
  • As the positive \(V_{GS}\) voltage is increased (made more positive), the reverse bias across the gate-channel junction increases, widening the depletion region and further constricting the channel.
  • If \(V_{GS}\) is made sufficiently positive, the depletion regions can meet, closing off the channel and reducing \(I_D\) to zero. This \(V_{GS}\) voltage is called the pinch-off voltage (\(V_p\)), which is positive for a P-channel JFET.

The P-channel JFET acts as a voltage-controlled resistor or current source, where the positive \(V_{GS}\) controls the negative \(I_D\) (or positive conventional \(I_D\) flowing S to D).

(c) Common Emitter Amplifier DC Bias Calculation (14 marks):

Figure 3 shows an NPN BJT common emitter amplifier with voltage divider bias. We need to determine the DC bias voltages and currents (Q-point). We are given \(V_{BE} = 0.7 \, \mathrm{V}\) and the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E\).

From Figure 3: \(V_{CC} = 12 \, \mathrm{V}\), \(R_1 = 20 \, k\Omega\), \(R_2 = 4.7 \, k\Omega\), \(R_C = 2.2 \, k\Omega\), \(R_E = 1 \, k\Omega\).

**(i) \(V_{B}\) (Base voltage):**

The base voltage is set by the voltage divider \(R_1\) and \(R_2\). Assume the base current \(I_B\) is small enough to be neglected compared to the current through the divider (valid if \(R_2 \ll \beta R_E\)).

\[ V_B = V_{CC} \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \] \[ V_B = 12 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{4.7 \, k\Omega}{20 \, k\Omega + 4.7 \, k\Omega} = 12 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{4.7}{24.7} \] \[ V_B \approx 12 \times 0.19028 \approx 2.2834 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The base voltage \(V_B\) is approximately 2.2834 V.

**(ii) \(V_{E}\) (Emitter voltage):**

The emitter voltage is \(V_B\) minus the base-emitter voltage drop \(V_{BE}\).

\[ V_E = V_B - V_{BE} = 2.2834 \, \mathrm{V} - 0.7 \, \mathrm{V} = 1.5834 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The emitter voltage \(V_E\) is approximately 1.5834 V.

**(iii) \(I_{E}\) (Emitter current):**

The emitter current \(I_E\) flows through \(R_E\).

\[ I_E = \frac{V_E}{R_E} = \frac{1.5834 \, V}{1 \, k\Omega} = \frac{1.5834}{1000} \, A = 0.0015834 \, A = 1.5834 \, mA \]

The emitter current \(I_E\) is approximately 1.5834 mA.

**(iii) \(I_{B}\) (Base current):** Using the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E\), the base current \(I_B = I_E - I_C\) is very small compared to \(I_E\) and \(I_C\). The exact value depends on the transistor's \(\beta\), where \(I_B = I_E / (1+\beta)\). Without knowing \(\beta\), we cannot calculate the exact value of \(I_B\).

Let's assume the intended calculation requires a typical \(\beta\). Given the marks, let's assume \(\beta \approx 100\). Then \(I_B = I_E / (1+\beta) = 1.5834mA / 101 \approx 0.01568mA = 15.68 \mu A\).

**(v) \(V_{C}\) (Collector voltage):** Using the approximation \(I_C \simeq I_E \approx 1.5834 \, \mathrm{mA}\) for calculating the voltage drop across \(R_C\).

\[ V_C = V_{CC} - I_C R_C \approx 12 \, \mathrm{V} - (1.5834 \, \mathrm{mA}) \times (2.2 \, k\Omega) \] \[ V_C \approx 12 \, \mathrm{V} - (1.5834 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{A}) \times (2.2 \times 10^3 \, \Omega) = 12 \, \mathrm{V} - 3.4835 \, \mathrm{V} \] \[ V_C \approx 8.5165 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The collector voltage \(V_C\) is approximately 8.5165 V.

**(vi) \(V_{CE}\) (Collector-to-emitter voltage):**

\[ V_{CE} = V_C - V_E = 8.5165 \, \mathrm{V} - 1.5834 \, \mathrm{V} \] \[ V_{CE} \approx 6.9331 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The collector-to-emitter voltage \(V_{CE}\) is approximately 6.9331 V.

Check operating region: \(V_{CE} \approx 6.93V\). \(V_{BE}=0.7V\). Since \(V_{CE} > V_{BE}\), the transistor is in the active region as expected for an amplifier. Also check \(V_C > V_E\). \(8.5V > 1.58V\).

Summary of results (assuming \(\beta \approx 100\) for \(I_B\)):

  • **(i) Base voltage \(V_B\):** \( \approx 2.2834 \, V \)
  • **(ii) Emitter voltage \(V_E\):** \( \approx 1.5834 \, V \)
  • **(iii) Emitter current \(I_E\):** \( \approx 1.5834 \, mA \)
  • **(iv) Base current \(I_B\):** \( \approx 15.68 \, \mu A \) (based on \(\beta \approx 100\))
  • **(v) Collector voltage \(V_C\):** \( \approx 8.5165 \, V \)
  • **(vi) Collector-to-emitter voltage \(V_{CE}\):** \( \approx 6.9331 \, V \)
