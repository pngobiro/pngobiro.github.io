(ii) With the aid of a labelled diagram, describe the operation of a multicellular-type electrostatic voltmeter. (6 marks)

(b) A multi range d.c milliammeter has an internal resistance of \( 50 \Omega \). A current of 2 mA gives full scale deflection on the meter. The meter has current ranges of \( 0-10 \mathrm{~mA}, 0-50 \mathrm{~mA}, 0-100 \mathrm{~mA} \) and \( 0-500 \mathrm{~mA} \). Determine the value of the shunt resistor for each range. (10 marks)

(c) (i) Sketch a labelled waveform of an amplitude modulated wave displayed on an oscilloscope. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers common measurement errors, the operation of an electrostatic voltmeter, calculations for a multi-range ammeter shunt, and oscilloscope display of AM waves.

(a) Measurement Errors and Electrostatic Voltmeter (10 marks):

**(i) Three errors that can occur in electrical measurement systems:**

  • **Gross Errors:** Human errors due to carelessness or inexperience (e.g., misreading a scale, wrong connections).
  • **Systematic Errors:** Consistent errors due to instrument defects, environmental factors, or faulty measurement procedures (e.g., faulty calibration, temperature effects).
  • **Random Errors:** Unpredictable errors that remain after systematic errors are accounted for, often due to small, unavoidable fluctuations.
  • **Loading Errors:** Errors caused by the measuring instrument altering the circuit conditions it is measuring (e.g., voltmeter drawing current, ammeter adding resistance).

**(ii) Operation of a multicellular-type electrostatic voltmeter with diagram:**

Electrostatic voltmeters measure voltage based on the electrostatic force between charged conductors. The multicellular type uses multiple sets of fixed and movable vanes to increase the deflecting torque and sensitivity, making it suitable for lower voltages than the single-cell type.

       +-----------------+
       | Fixed Vanes (F) |
       |                 |
       | <-------------> |  Movable Vanes (M)
       | (Air Gaps)    |
       |                 |
       +-----------------+
       ^        |        ^
       |        |        |
     Voltage   Pivot  Counterweight/
     Input     & Shaft   Spring Control

**Description:** The voltmeter consists of several sets of fixed metal vanes (F) and movable metal vanes (M). The movable vanes are mounted on a central spindle, which is controlled by a spring and bearings and carries the pointer. The fixed vanes are connected to one terminal of the voltage source, and the movable vanes are connected to the other terminal. When a voltage is applied, the fixed and movable vanes become oppositely charged (or acquire charges proportional to the potential difference). An electrostatic force of attraction is exerted between the charged vanes. Since there are multiple sets of vanes and large surface areas facing each other across narrow air gaps, the force is significant even for moderate voltages. This force produces a deflecting torque on the movable system. The torque is proportional to the square of the applied voltage (\(V^2\)). This deflecting torque is opposed by a controlling torque from a spring. The movable system deflects until the deflecting torque is balanced by the controlling torque. The deflection is proportional to \(V^2\), resulting in a non-linear scale (cramped at lower voltages, spread out at higher voltages). Because they draw almost no current (only leakage and initial charging current), they are useful for measuring high voltages and in circuits where loading must be minimized.

(b) Multi-range DC Milliammeter Shunt Calculation (10 marks):

To extend the range of a DC milliammeter, shunt resistors are connected in parallel with the meter. The shunt diverts excess current, allowing the meter to measure a fraction of the total current corresponding to its full-scale deflection (FSD).

Given:

  • Meter internal resistance \(R_m = 50 \, \Omega\).
  • Meter FSD current \(I_m = 2 \, mA = 2 \times 10^{-3} \, A\).
  • Current ranges \(I\): 10 mA, 50 mA, 100 mA, 500 mA.

For each range, the shunt resistor \(R_{sh}\) is calculated such that when the total current \(I\) equals the range maximum, the current through the meter is \(I_m\). The current through the shunt is \(I_{sh} = I - I_m\).

Since the meter and shunt are in parallel, the voltage across them is equal: \( V_m = V_{sh} \). \[ I_m R_m = I_{sh} R_{sh} \] \[ I_m R_m = (I - I_m) R_{sh} \] \[ R_{sh} = \frac{I_m R_m}{I - I_m} \]

Calculate \(R_{sh}\) for each range:

  • **Range 0 - 10 mA:** \( I = 10 \, mA = 10 \times 10^{-3} \, A \). \(I_m = 2 \times 10^{-3} \, A\). \[ R_{sh,10mA} = \frac{(2 \times 10^{-3}) \times 50}{(10 \times 10^{-3}) - (2 \times 10^{-3})} = \frac{100 \times 10^{-3}}{8 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{100}{8} = 12.5 \, \Omega \]
  • **Range 0 - 50 mA:** \( I = 50 \, mA = 50 \times 10^{-3} \, A \). \(I_m = 2 \times 10^{-3} \, A\). \[ R_{sh,50mA} = \frac{(2 \times 10^{-3}) \times 50}{(50 \times 10^{-3}) - (2 \times 10^{-3})} = \frac{100 \times 10^{-3}}{48 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{100}{48} \approx 2.083 \, \Omega \]
  • **Range 0 - 100 mA:** \( I = 100 \, mA = 100 \times 10^{-3} \, A \). \(I_m = 2 \times 10^{-3} \, A\). \[ R_{sh,100mA} = \frac{(2 \times 10^{-3}) \times 50}{(100 \times 10^{-3}) - (2 \times 10^{-3})} = \frac{100 \times 10^{-3}}{98 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{100}{98} \approx 1.020 \, \Omega \]
  • **Range 0 - 500 mA:** \( I = 500 \, mA = 500 \times 10^{-3} \, A \). \(I_m = 2 \times 10^{-3} \, A\). \[ R_{sh,500mA} = \frac{(2 \times 10^{-3}) \times 50}{(500 \times 10^{-3}) - (2 \times 10^{-3})} = \frac{100 \times 10^{-3}}{498 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{100}{498} \approx 0.201 \, \Omega \]

The values of the shunt resistors for the ranges 10 mA, 50 mA, 100 mA, and 500 mA are \(12.5 \, \Omega\), approximately \(2.083 \, \Omega\), approximately \(1.020 \, \Omega\), and approximately \(0.201 \, \Omega\) respectively.

(c) Oscilloscope Display of AM Wave (5 marks):

An amplitude modulated (AM) wave is a high-frequency carrier wave whose amplitude is varied in proportion to a lower-frequency modulating signal. When displayed on an oscilloscope with the timebase active, the waveform shows the carrier signal contained within an envelope that follows the shape of the modulating signal.

**(i) Sketch of a labelled waveform:**

 Waveform+-------------------+----------------> Time
         |\              /

The sketch should show a high-frequency sine wave (the carrier) whose amplitude varies over time. The outer boundary of the peaks (upper envelope) and the outer boundary of the troughs (lower envelope) should trace the shape of the modulating signal (often a sine wave itself). The vertical axis represents the instantaneous amplitude, and the horizontal axis represents time.

**(ii) Expression for the modulation index (\(m\)):**

From the displayed waveform, the modulation index \(m\) can be determined by measuring the maximum amplitude of the modulated wave (\(V_{max}\), from the center axis to the highest point of the envelope) and the minimum amplitude of the modulated wave (\(V_{min}\), from the center axis to the lowest point of the envelope).

\[ m = \frac{V_{max} - V_{min}}{V_{max} + V_{min}} \]

Alternatively, if measuring peak-to-peak values from the display, \(A_{max}\) is the peak-to-peak amplitude of the widest part of the envelope (\(2V_{max}\)) and \(A_{min}\) is the peak-to-peak amplitude of the narrowest part (\(2V_{min}\)). Then \(m = \frac{A_{max} - A_{min}}{A_{max} + A_{min}}\).

This question covers various topics in electrical measurements, from fundamental errors and instrument operation to practical calculations for meter range extension and interpreting waveforms on an oscilloscope.

Answer

This question covers equipment failure types, the bathtub reliability curve, calculating system reliability from component data, and environmental effects on equipment.

(a) Failure Types and Failure Rate Curve (10 marks):

**(i) Distinction between sudden failure and complete failure:**

  • **Sudden Failure:** A failure that is unexpected and abrupt, with little or no warning signs. It typically leads to the equipment ceasing to function immediately (often also catastrophic). Example: A fuse blowing, a transistor shorting out suddenly.
  • **Complete Failure:** The equipment totally stops performing its intended function. This is a state of failure where the equipment provides no output or cannot fulfill its primary purpose at all. It can be the result of a sudden failure (e.g., power supply failure) or the final state of a degradation failure. Example: A radio producing no sound or not tuning to any station.

Note: Complete failure describes the *state* of the equipment, while sudden failure describes the *manner* in which the state was reached. A sudden failure often results in a complete failure.

**(ii) Failure rate versus time curve (Bathtub Curve) and performance description:**

The failure rate versus time curve is the Bathtub Curve (see Q3c in July 2015 or Q1a in November 2016 for sketch and description). It shows the probability of failure per unit time over the equipment's lifespan.

Performance description across the curve:

  • **Infant Mortality (Early Life):** Failure rate is high and decreasing. Performance is initially unpredictable due to "bugs" and manufacturing defects. Equipment reliability improves as these early failures are weeded out.
  • **Useful Life (Mid-Life):** Failure rate is low and relatively constant. Performance is stable and predictable. Reliability is at its best during this period. Failures are random and not age-related.
  • **Wear-out Period (End of Life):** Failure rate is increasing rapidly. Performance degrades as components age, wear out, or fatigue. The equipment becomes less reliable and requires more frequent maintenance or replacement.

(b) Equipment Reliability Calculations from Component Data (8 marks):

To determine the reliability of an equipment based on its components connected in series, we first need to calculate the total failure rate of the system. The failure rate contribution of each component type is its failure rate multiplied by the quantity and weighting factor.

From Table 1 (Figure 4):

  • Transistors: \( \lambda = 0.01 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=50\), \(w=1.5\).
  • Diodes: \( \lambda = 0.01 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=100\), \(w=1.0\).
  • Composition resistors: \( \lambda = 0.005 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=80\), \(w=1.0\).
  • Ceramic capacitors: \( \lambda = 0.025 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=30\), \(w=1.0\).
  • Electrolytic capacitors: \( \lambda = 0.2 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=20\), \(w=3.0\).
  • Film resistors: \( \lambda = 0.1 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=40\), \(w=1.5\).
  • Inductors: \( \lambda = 0.05 \% / 1000 \text{ hrs} \), \(n=15\), \(w=15\). (Note: Weighting factor of 15 for inductors seems unusually high compared to others).

Individual component failure rate per 1000 hours (adjusted for quantity and weighting): \( \lambda_{comp} = \lambda_{%/1000hrs} \times n \times w \).

  • Transistors: \( (0.01) \times 50 \times 1.5 = 0.75 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Diodes: \( (0.01) \times 100 \times 1.0 = 1.0 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Composition resistors: \( (0.005) \times 80 \times 1.0 = 0.4 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Ceramic capacitors: \( (0.025) \times 30 \times 1.0 = 0.75 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Electrolytic capacitors: \( (0.2) \times 20 \times 3.0 = 12.0 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Film resistors: \( (0.1) \times 40 \times 1.5 = 6.0 \) %/1000 hrs
  • Inductors: \( (0.05) \times 15 \times 15.0 = 11.25 \) %/1000 hrs

Total equipment failure rate per 1000 hours (\( \lambda_{equipment, \%/1000hrs} \)) is the sum of individual contributions:

\[ \lambda_{equipment, \%/1000hrs} = 0.75 + 1.0 + 0.4 + 0.75 + 12.0 + 6.0 + 11.25 \] \[ \lambda_{equipment, \%/1000hrs} = 32.15 \text{ % per 1000 hours} \]

Convert to failures per hour: \( \lambda_{equipment} = \frac{32.15 / 100}{1000} \, \text{failures/hour} = \frac{0.3215}{1000} = 3.215 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{failures/hour} \).

**(i) Failure rate of the equipment:**

The failure rate is \(3.215 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{failures/hour}\) or \(32.15 \, \text{% per 1000 hours}\).

**(ii) Mean time between failure (MTBF):**

MTBF is the reciprocal of the failure rate (assuming a constant failure rate, appropriate for the useful life period).

\[ MTBF = \frac{1}{\lambda_{equipment}} = \frac{1}{3.215 \times 10^{-4}} \, \text{hours/failure} \] \[ MTBF \approx 3109.18 \, \text{hours/failure} \]

**(iii) Reliability for an operating period of 1,000 hours:**

The reliability \(R(t)\) for a time \(t\) is given by \( R(t) = e^{-\lambda_{equipment} t} \).

Here \(t = 1000\) hours. It's easier to use the failure rate per 1000 hours directly.

\[ R(1000) = e^{-(\lambda_{equipment, \%/1000hrs} / 100)} \] \[ R(1000) = e^{-(32.15 / 100)} = e^{-0.3215} \] \[ R(1000) \approx 0.7249 \]

The reliability of the equipment for an operating period of 1,000 hours is approximately 0.7249 or 72.49%.

(c) Effects of humidity on an equipment (2 marks):

  • **Corrosion:** High humidity can accelerate the corrosion of metal parts, including component leads, PCB traces, connectors, and chassis.
  • **Insulation Degradation:** Moisture can reduce the insulation resistance between conductors, potentially leading to leakage currents, short circuits, or malfunction, especially in high-voltage circuits or on PCBs.
  • **Component Damage:** Some components (e.g., electrolytic capacitors, semiconductors) can be adversely affected by moisture absorption or reactions, leading to degraded performance or failure.
  • **Dust Adhesion:** Humidity causes dust particles to stick to surfaces, which can trap moisture and contaminants, exacerbating corrosion and potentially causing tracking or short circuits.

Understanding failure types, reliability modeling, and environmental impacts is crucial for designing and maintaining reliable electronic equipment.

Answer

This question covers visual inspection for faults, a specific soldering method, testing amplifier gain, and using a multimeter to test a diode.

(a) Faults by Visual Inspection and Reflow Soldering (10 marks):

**(i) Two faults revealed by visual inspection:**

  • **Burnt or discolored components:** Indicates overheating or overcurrent (e.g., burnt resistors, swollen capacitors, discolored ICs).
  • **Poor solder joints:** Cold joints (dull, grainy appearance), solder bridges (unwanted connections between adjacent pads), lifted pads or traces, insufficient solder, excessive solder.
  • **Physical damage:** Cracked components, broken wires, damaged connectors, cracks in the PCB laminate, bent pins on ICs or connectors.
  • **Corrosion or contaminants:** Presence of rust, verdigris, or other corrosive residues on component leads or PCB traces.
  • **Loose connections:** Connectors not fully seated, wires detached from terminals.

**(ii) Reflow soldering method description:**

Reflow soldering is a common process used to attach surface-mount components (SMDs) to printed circuit boards (PCBs). It involves using solder paste (a mixture of solder powder and flux) applied to the pads, placing components onto the paste, and then heating the assembly to melt and reflow the solder, creating the connections.

**Description:**

  1. **Solder Paste Application:** Solder paste is accurately applied to the designated pads on the PCB using a stencil and a squeegee, or through jet printing.
  2. **Component Placement:** Electronic components (SMDs) are precisely placed onto the solder paste deposits on the pads using automated pick-and-place machines. The paste's stickiness holds the components in place.
  3. **Preheating:** The entire assembly is slowly heated in a preheating zone of a reflow oven. This evaporates volatile solvents from the solder paste, activates the flux (which cleans the surfaces), and brings the components and PCB to a uniform temperature to minimize thermal shock in the next stage.
  4. **Reflow:** The assembly moves into the reflow zone, where the temperature is rapidly increased above the melting point of the solder alloy. The solder paste melts, and the surface tension of the molten solder pulls the components into alignment with the pads ("self-alignment") and forms reliable solder joints.
  5. **Cooling:** The assembly moves into a cooling zone, where the temperature is gradually reduced. The molten solder solidifies, creating the permanent electrical and mechanical connections. Rapid cooling can lead to thermal stress.

**(ii) Description of how the test is carried out:**

Most digital multimeters have a diode test function (often indicated by a diode symbol on the rotary switch). This function typically applies a small voltage across the diode and measures the resulting voltage drop or indicates open/short circuit.

  1. Set the multimeter to the "diode test" function.
  2. Connect the red lead (positive terminal) of the multimeter to the anode of the diode and the black lead (negative terminal) to the cathode. This is the **forward bias** connection. Visual inspection and testing with basic instruments like multimeters and oscilloscopes are fundamental skills in electronic fault diagnosis and repair, complementing knowledge of circuit operation and component behavior.

(a) State the units for each of the following electrical quantities: (4 marks)

(ii) quantity of electricity;

(iii) magnetomotive force.

(b) Derive, from first principles, the dimensional equation for the pole strength in the c.g.s. electromagnetic units. (8 marks)

(c) Describe "standard" as used in measurements. (4 marks)

(d) Derive the dimensional equation for mechanical energy. (4 marks)

Answer

This question focuses on units and dimensional analysis for various electrical and mechanical quantities.

(a) Units for Electrical Quantities (4 marks):

In the SI system:

  • **(i) Magnetic field strength (H):** Amperes per meter (A/m).
  • **(ii) Quantity of electricity (Charge, Q):** Coulomb (C).
  • **(iii) Magnetomotive force (MMF):** Amperes (A) or Ampere-turns (At).

(b) Dimensional equation for pole strength in c.g.s. electromagnetic units (emu) (8 marks):

In the c.g.s. electromagnetic system (emu), the unit of magnetic pole strength is defined based on the force between two magnetic poles. The force \(F\) between two magnetic poles \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) separated by a distance \(r\) in vacuum is given by \( F = k \frac{p_1 p_2}{r^2} \). In emu, the proportionality constant \(k = 1/\mu_0\) is taken as 1 (since \(\mu_0\) is dimensionless and equal to 1). So, \( F = \frac{p^2}{r^2} \) for \(p_1 = p_2 = p\).

Force \(F\) has dimensions \([MLT^{-2}]\). Distance \(r\) has dimensions \([L]\).

From \( F = \frac{p^2}{L^2} \), we have \( p^2 = F \times L^2 \).

\[ [p^2] = [MLT^{-2}] [L^2] = [ML^3T^{-2}] \]

To find the dimension of \(p\), take the square root:

\[ [p]_{emu} = [M^{1/2}L^{3/2}T^{-1}] \]

This is the dimensional equation for magnetic pole strength in the c.g.s. electromagnetic units.

(c) Definition of "standard" in measurements (4 marks):

As used in measurements, a "standard" is a fundamental reference used to define or realize a unit of measurement for a physical quantity. It represents a specific magnitude of a quantity used for comparison to measure other quantities of the same kind. Standards provide the basis for calibration and ensuring consistency and traceability of measurements within a system or across different locations/times. Examples include the international prototype of the kilogram (historically), the atomic clock (for the second), or a standard resistor (for resistance).

(d) Dimensional equation for mechanical energy (4 marks):

Mechanical energy (like kinetic energy or potential energy) is the capacity to do work. Since energy and work are dimensionally equivalent, we can derive the dimensions of energy from the definition of work or from common energy formulas.

From work \(W = F \times d\), dimensions are \([MLT^{-2}] \times [L] = [ML^2T^{-2}]\).

From kinetic energy \(KE = \frac{1}{2} m v^2\). Mass \(m\) has dimensions \([M]\). Velocity \(v\) has dimensions \([LT^{-1}]\). \[ [\text{Energy}] = [M] \times ([LT^{-1}])^2 \] \[ [\text{Energy}] = [M] \times [L^2T^{-2}] \] \[ [\text{Energy}] = [ML^2T^{-2}] \]

The dimensional equation for mechanical energy is \( [ML^2T^{-2}] \).

This question covers fundamental concepts in units and dimensions, including specialized units like cgs emu, and the definition of measurement standards.

Answer

This question covers reasons for routine maintenance tasks, phases of corrective maintenance, assumptions in fault location, and calculations for a ballistic galvanometer.

(a) Reasons for Routine Maintenance Tasks (6 marks):

Routine maintenance involves scheduled tasks to keep equipment in good working order and prevent failures.

  • **(i) Lubrication:** To reduce friction and wear between moving parts (e.g., in motors, bearings, gears). Proper lubrication prevents overheating, reduces power consumption, minimizes wear, and extends the lifespan of mechanical components.
  • **(ii) Cleaning:** To remove dirt, dust, grease, and contaminants from the equipment surfaces and internal components. Accumulation of dirt can cause overheating, reduce insulation resistance leading to leakage or shorts, interfere with moving parts, and accelerate corrosion. Cleaning ensures proper operation and prevents component damage.

(a) Semiconductor Definitions and p-type Formation (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to semiconductors:**

  • **(I) Covalent bond:** A type of chemical bond formed between atoms by the sharing of valence electrons. In intrinsic semiconductors like silicon or germanium, each atom forms four covalent bonds with its neighboring atoms in a crystal lattice structure.
  • **(II) Drift current:** The movement of charge carriers (electrons and holes) in a semiconductor due to the presence of an electric field. The electric field exerts a force on the carriers, causing them to move in a specific direction (electrons opposite to the field, holes in the direction of the field), resulting in a net current flow.

**(ii) Formation of a p-type semiconductor with a labelled diagram:**

A p-type semiconductor is formed by doping an intrinsic semiconductor (e.g., silicon) with a small amount of trivalent impurity atoms (accepter impurities) like Boron, Gallium, or Indium.

        Silicon Atom (Si)   Boron Atom (B)

        Si      Si        Si       Si
        |       |         |        |

Diagram showing Silicon atoms forming 4 covalent bonds, and one Boron atom in the lattice forming only 3 covalent bonds with Si atoms, leaving a "hole".

**Description:** Silicon is a group IV element with 4 valence electrons. A trivalent impurity atom (group III element) has only 3 valence electrons. When a trivalent atom substitutes a silicon atom in the crystal lattice, it forms covalent bonds with three neighboring silicon atoms. The bond with the fourth silicon atom is incomplete, as the trivalent atom can only share 3 electrons, while the silicon atom has 4 electrons to share. This deficiency of an electron in the covalent bond creates a "hole". These holes are free to move throughout the crystal lattice, acting as positive charge carriers. The trivalent impurity atoms, having accepted an electron to complete three bonds, become fixed negative ions in the lattice. In a p-type semiconductor, holes are the majority carriers, and electrons are the minority carriers.

**Explanation:** Rutherford's model, sometimes called the nuclear model, described the atom as having:

  • A very small, dense, positively charged **nucleus** located at the center of the atom. This nucleus contains most of the atom's mass (attributed to protons and neutrons, although neutrons were discovered later).
  • Negatively charged **electrons** orbiting around the nucleus in definite paths, much like planets orbiting the sun.
  • The atom is mostly **empty space**, as evidenced by the fact that most alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil without deflection.
  • The total positive charge of the nucleus is equal to the total negative charge of the orbiting electrons, making the atom electrically neutral.

This model replaced the earlier plum-pudding model but had limitations, particularly in explaining why orbiting electrons (which are accelerating charges) did not lose energy and spiral into the nucleus according to classical physics.

(c) Semiconductor Diode Specifications and V-I Curve (6 marks):

**(i) Three parameters for semiconductor diode specifications:**

  • **Maximum Forward Current (\(I_F\)):** The maximum average current the diode can safely handle in the forward-biased direction without overheating or damage.
  • **Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) or Maximum Reverse Voltage (\(V_R\)):** The maximum instantaneous reverse voltage the diode can withstand without entering the breakdown region.
  • **Forward Voltage Drop (\(V_F\))**: The typical voltage across the diode when it is conducting a specified forward current (e.g., 0.7V for silicon at a certain current). This might be listed as \(V_{F(typ)}\) at a given \(I_F\).
  • **Maximum Power Dissipation (\(P_D\)):** The maximum power the diode can dissipate safely, usually a product of forward voltage and current, or reverse voltage and leakage current (in breakdown, \(P_D = V_Z \times I_Z\)).
  • **Reverse Saturation Current (\(I_S\) or \(I_R\)):** The small leakage current in the reverse-biased direction (specified at a certain reverse voltage).
  • **Reverse Breakdown Voltage (\(V_{BR}\) or \(V_Z\)):** The reverse voltage at which the diode enters the breakdown region. (Crucial for Zener diodes).
  • **Operating Temperature Range:** The range of ambient or junction temperatures over which the diode is specified to operate reliably.

(a) Figure 1 shows a circuit diagram of a depletion MOSFET amplifier. The MOSFET has a drain saturation current of 4 mA and a pinchoff voltage of -5 V . Determine the: (14 marks)

(i) gate bias voltage, \( \mathrm{V}_{0} \);

(ii) gate-to-source voltage, \( \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{c}} \);

(iii) drain current, \( \mathrm{I}_{5} \);

(iv) drain voltage; \( \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{D}} \).

(b) Derive the expression for the common-base current gain, \( \alpha \), in terms of the common-emitter current gain, \( \beta \). (6 marks)

(c) (i) State the three operating regions of a transistor. (3 marks)

(ii) Draw the circuit diagram of the common-collector configuration of a transistor amplifier. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers the analysis of a depletion MOSFET amplifier, BJT current gain relationships, BJT operating regions, and BJT common-collector configuration.

(a) Depletion MOSFET Amplifier Analysis (14 marks):

Figure 1 shows an N-channel depletion MOSFET amplifier with voltage divider bias and a source resistor. The MOSFET parameters are \(I_{DSS} = 4 \, \mathrm{mA}\) and \(V_p = -5 \, \mathrm{V}\).

From Figure 1: \(V_{DD} = 18 \, \mathrm{V}\), \(R_1 = 2.2 \, M\Omega\), \(R_2 = 1 \, M\Omega\), \(R_D = 3.3 \, k\Omega\), \(R_S = 1.5 \, k\Omega\). Note: The question uses \(V_c\) and \(I_5\) which are likely typos and should refer to \(V_{GS}\) and \(I_D\).

**(i) Gate bias voltage (\(V_G\)):**

The gate voltage is set by the voltage divider \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) from \(V_{DD}\) to ground. Since the gate draws negligible DC current (\(I_G \approx 0\)), there is no voltage drop across \(R_G\). Let's assume \(R_G\) is part of \(R_1, R_2\). The gate voltage \(V_G\) is the voltage at the junction of \(R_1\) and \(R_2\).

\[ V_G = V_{DD} \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \] \[ V_G = 18 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{1 \, M\Omega}{2.2 \, M\Omega + 1 \, M\Omega} = 18 \, \mathrm{V} \times \frac{1}{3.2} \] \[ V_G = \frac{18}{3.2} \, \mathrm{V} = 5.625 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The gate bias voltage is \(V_G = 5.625 \, \mathrm{V}\). The question labels this as \(V_0\), so \(V_0 = 5.625 \, \mathrm{V}\).

**(ii) Gate-to-source voltage (\(V_{GS}\)):**

In a self-biased configuration (which this is, as it has \(R_S\) and the source is not directly grounded or connected to a separate voltage), the source voltage is \(V_S = I_D R_S\). The gate-source voltage is \(V_{GS} = V_G - V_S = V_G - I_D R_S\). We need to find \(V_{GS}\) and \(I_D\) simultaneously using Shockley's equation and the bias equation.

\[ I_D = I_{DSS} \left( 1 - \frac{V_{GS}}{V_p} \right)^2 \]

Substitute \(I_{DSS}=4mA\) and \(V_p=-5V\): \( I_D = 4 \left( 1 - \frac{V_{GS}}{-5} \right)^2 = 4 \left( 1 + \frac{V_{GS}}{5} \right)^2 \). Substitute \(I_D = (V_G - V_{GS}) / R_S\): \( \frac{5.625 - V_{GS}}{1.5k} = 4 \times 10^{-3} \left( 1 + \frac{V_{GS}}{5} \right)^2 \). \[ \frac{5.625 - V_{GS}}{1.5} = 4 \left( \frac{5 + V_{GS}}{5} \right)^2 = 4 \frac{(5 + V_{GS})^2}{25} \] \[ 5.625 - V_{GS} = 1.5 \times \frac{4}{25} (25 + 10V_{GS} + V_{GS}^2) \] \[ 5.625 - V_{GS} = 0.24 (25 + 10V_{GS} + V_{GS}^2) \] \[ 5.625 - V_{GS} = 6 + 2.4V_{GS} + 0.24V_{GS}^2 \] \[ 0.24V_{GS}^2 + 2.4V_{GS} + V_{GS} + 6 - 5.625 = 0 \] \[ 0.24V_{GS}^2 + 3.4V_{GS} + 0.375 = 0 \]

Use the quadratic formula \( V_{GS} = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \), with \(a=0.24\), \(b=3.4\), \(c=0.375\).

\[ V_{GS} = \frac{-3.4 \pm \sqrt{(3.4)^2 - 4(0.24)(0.375)}}{2(0.24)} \] \[ V_{GS} = \frac{-3.4 \pm \sqrt{11.56 - 0.36}}{0.48} = \frac{-3.4 \pm \sqrt{11.2}}{0.48} = \frac{-3.4 \pm 3.3466}{0.48} \]

Two possible solutions:

\[ V_{GS1} = \frac{-3.4 + 3.3466}{0.48} = \frac{-0.0534}{0.48} \approx -0.111 \, V \] \[ V_{GS2} = \frac{-3.4 - 3.3466}{0.48} = \frac{-6.7466}{0.48} \approx -14.055 \, V \]

For an N-channel depletion MOSFET with \(V_p=-5V\), the operating \(V_{GS}\) must be between \(V_p\) (-5V) and the positive bias limit (where the gate-source junction becomes forward-biased, typically +0.7V). \(V_{GS} \approx -0.111V\) is in the depletion region (\(-5V < -0.111V < 0V\)), which is a valid operating point for an N-channel depletion MOSFET. \(V_{GS} \approx -14.055V\) is more negative than \(V_p\), which would mean \(I_D=0\), inconsistent with the circuit biasing. Therefore, the correct \(V_{GS}\) is approximately -0.111 V.

The question uses \(V_c\) for gate-to-source voltage, so \(V_c = V_{GS} \approx -0.111 \, V\).

**(iii) Drain current (\(I_D\)):**

Using the bias equation \(I_D = (V_G - V_{GS}) / R_S\):

\[ I_D = \frac{5.625 \, V - (-0.111 \, V)}{1.5 \, k\Omega} = \frac{5.625 + 0.111}{1.5} \, mA = \frac{5.736}{1.5} \, mA \] \[ I_D \approx 3.824 \, mA \]

Alternatively, using Shockley's equation: \( I_D = 4 \, \mathrm{mA} \left( 1 + \frac{-0.111}{5} \right)^2 = 4 (1 - 0.0222)^2 = 4(0.9778)^2 = 4 \times 0.956 \approx 3.824 \, \mathrm{mA} \). The results match.

The question uses \(I_5\) for drain current, so \(I_5 = I_D \approx 3.824 \, mA\).

**(iv) Drain voltage (\(V_D\)):**

The voltage at the drain is \(V_{DD}\) minus the voltage drop across \(R_D\):

\[ V_D = V_{DD} - I_D R_D = 18 \, \mathrm{V} - (3.824 \, \mathrm{mA}) \times (3.3 \, k\Omega) \] \[ V_D = 18 \, \mathrm{V} - (3.824 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{A}) \times (3.3 \times 10^3 \, \Omega) = 18 \, \mathrm{V} - 12.6192 \, \mathrm{V} \] \[ V_D \approx 5.381 \, \mathrm{V} \]

The drain voltage is approximately 5.381 V.

Check saturation: \(V_{DS} = V_D - V_S = 5.381 - (3.824mA \times 1.5k\Omega) = 5.381 - 5.736 = -0.355V\). This negative \(V_{DS}\) indicates the transistor is in the linear (triode) region, not saturation, which contradicts the assumption made when using Shockley's equation in this bias configuration (usually designed for saturation). There might be an issue with the circuit values or parameters provided if the intention was active region operation.

Assuming the question intended the transistor to be in saturation, and requiring calculations based on standard formulas, the calculated Q-point is \(V_{GS} \approx -0.111V, I_D \approx 3.824mA, V_D \approx 5.381V\), but it operates in the linear region based on these values. Let's state the results as calculated and note the region of operation.

Summary of results (assuming calculations are required despite region inconsistency):

  • **(i) gate bias voltage, \(V_0\):** \( V_G = 5.625 \, V \)
  • **(ii) gate-to-source voltage, \(V_c\):** \( V_{GS} \approx -0.111 \, V \)
  • **(iii) drain current, \(I_5\):** \( I_D \approx 3.824 \, mA \)
  • **(iv) drain voltage; \(V_D\):** \( V_D \approx 5.381 \, V \)

Based on these calculated values, \(V_{DS} = V_D - V_S = 5.381V - (3.824mA \times 1.5k\Omega) = 5.381V - 5.736V = -0.355V\). For saturation, N-channel depletion MOSFET requires \(V_{DS} \ge V_{GS} - V_p = -0.111V - (-5V) = 4.889V\). Since \(V_{DS} < 4.889V\), the transistor is in the linear (triode) region.

(b) Derivation of Common-Base Gain in terms of Common-Emitter Gain (6 marks):

This derivation shows the relationship between the two main BJT current gain parameters, \(\alpha\) (common-base) and \(\beta\) (common-emitter).

Definitions:

  • DC Common-Base Current Gain: \( \alpha_{dc} = \frac{I_C}{I_E} \)
  • DC Common-Emitter Current Gain: \( \beta_{dc} = \frac{I_C}{I_B} \)
  • Relationship between currents: \( I_E = I_B + I_C \)

From \( I_E = I_B + I_C \), express \(I_B\) in terms of \(I_E\) and \(I_C\): \( I_B = I_E - I_C \).

Substitute this expression for \(I_B\) into the definition of \(\beta_{dc}\):

\[ \beta_{dc} = \frac{I_C}{I_E - I_C} \]

Now, divide the numerator and denominator by \(I_E\):

\[ \beta_{dc} = \frac{I_C / I_E}{(I_E - I_C) / I_E} = \frac{I_C / I_E}{I_E/I_E - I_C/I_E} \]

Substitute the definition \( \alpha_{dc} = I_C / I_E \):

\[ \beta_{dc} = \frac{\alpha_{dc}}{1 - \alpha_{dc}} \]

The expression for the common-base current gain (\(\alpha\)) in terms of the common-emitter current gain (\(\beta\)) is derived by rearranging this formula:

\[ \beta (1 - \alpha) = \alpha \] \[ \beta - \beta\alpha = \alpha \] \[ \beta = \alpha + \beta\alpha = \alpha (1 + \beta) \] \[ \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta} \]

The question asks for \(\alpha\) in terms of \(\beta\), so \( \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta} \). The previous question (Q8d July 2014) asked for \(\beta\) in terms of \(\alpha\), which is \( \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \).

(c) Transistor Operating Regions and Common-Collector (6 marks):

**(i) Three operating regions of a transistor (BJT):**

  • **Cutoff Region:** Both emitter-base (EBJ) and collector-base (CBJ) junctions are reverse-biased. The transistor is effectively OFF; collector current is very low (ideally zero). Used as an open switch.
  • **Active Region:** The emitter-base junction (EBJ) is forward-biased, and the collector-base junction (CBJ) is reverse-biased. The transistor acts as an amplifier, where collector current is controlled by the base current (\(I_C = \beta I_B\)).
  • **Saturation Region:** Both emitter-base (EBJ) and collector-base (CBJ) junctions are forward-biased. The transistor is effectively ON; collector current is limited mainly by the external circuit, and \(V_{CE}\) is very low (ideally zero). Used as a closed switch.

Answer

This question focuses on the Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO), electron ballistics within a CRT, capacitor filtering in power supplies, and Zener diode voltage regulation.

(a) Function of CRO Parts (6 marks):

The CRO displays voltage waveforms against time by deflecting an electron beam.

  • **(i) Time base generator (Sweep Generator):** Produces a sawtooth voltage waveform that is applied to the horizontal deflection plates (X-plates). This voltage causes the electron beam to sweep horizontally across the screen at a constant speed from left to right, and then rapidly return to the starting point. This provides the time axis for displaying the input signal waveform.
  • **(ii) Trigger pulse generator (Trigger Circuit):** Synchronizes the start of the horizontal sweep with a specific point (e.g., a rising or falling edge at a particular voltage level) of the input signal (or an external signal). This ensures that the waveform display starts at the same point of the input signal cycle each time, creating a stable, non-moving display on the screen.
  • **(iii) Y-amplifier (Vertical Amplifier):** Amplifies the input signal that is to be displayed on the CRO. This signal is applied to the vertical deflection plates (Y-plates). The amplification allows the CRO to display signals with small amplitudes and provides the user with control over the vertical size (Volts/Div) of the displayed waveform.

(b) Electrostatic CRT Electron Calculations (10 marks):

Calculations for an electron beam accelerated by an anode voltage and passing through deflection plates.

Given:

  • Final anode voltage \(V_a = 2000 \, V\).
  • Deflecting plates length \(l_d = 1.5 \, cm = 0.015 \, m\).
  • Electronic charge \(e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C\).
  • Electron mass \(m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg\).

Assume the electron starts with negligible velocity from the cathode and is accelerated only by \(V_a\).

**(i) Maximum velocity:**

The maximum velocity (\(v\)) is achieved after acceleration by the final anode voltage. Kinetic energy gained equals potential energy lost: \( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = e V_a \).

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2 e V_a}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V)}{9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg}} \] \[ v = \sqrt{\frac{6.4 \times 10^{-16}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{0.70329 \times 10^{15}} = \sqrt{7.0329 \times 10^{14}} \] \[ v \approx 2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s} \]

The maximum velocity of the electron is approximately \(2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s}\).

**(ii) Transit time through the deflecting plates:**

The transit time (\(t_{transit}\)) is the time it takes for the electron to travel the length of the deflection plates at the constant horizontal velocity \(v\) (assuming no deflection voltage or small deflection voltage). We assume the velocity calculated above is the horizontal velocity (\(v_x\)).

\[ t_{transit} = \frac{l_d}{v_x} = \frac{0.015 \, m}{2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s}} \] \[ t_{transit} \approx 0.005656 \times 10^{-7} \, s = 5.656 \times 10^{-11} \, s \]

The transit time through the deflecting plates is approximately \(56.56 \, ps\) (picoseconds).

**(iii) Kinetic energy:**

The kinetic energy (KE) of the electron after acceleration by \(V_a\) is equal to the potential energy \(e V_a\).

\[ KE = e V_a = (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V) = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

The kinetic energy can also be calculated using \( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \).

\[ KE = \frac{1}{2} (9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg) \times (2.652 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s})^2 \] \[ KE = 0.5 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 7.0329 \times 10^{14} \] \[ KE = 4.55 \times 10^{-31} \times 7.0329 \times 10^{14} \approx 32.00 \times 10^{-17} = 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J \]

The kinetic energy of the electron is \(3.2 \times 10^{-16} \, J\).

(c) Capacitors for Power Supply Filters and Zener Regulator (6 marks):

**(i) Properties of capacitors suitable for power supply filters (3 marks):**

Capacitors are used as filters in DC power supplies (after rectification) to smooth out the pulsating DC voltage and reduce ripple. Properties that make them suitable include:

  • **Ability to store electrical charge:** They can store energy during the peaks of the rectifier output voltage and discharge during the valleys, smoothing the output.
  • **Low impedance to AC signals:** Capacitors offer low impedance to the AC ripple component, effectively shunting it away from the load.
  • **High impedance to DC signals:** Ideally, capacitors block DC current, preventing the filter from affecting the DC voltage level significantly (in a shunt filter configuration).
  • **Appropriate Capacitance Value:** The capacitance value must be large enough to effectively filter the lowest ripple frequency. Electrolytic capacitors are often used due to their high capacitance in a relatively small size.
  • **Sufficient Voltage Rating:** The capacitor's voltage rating must be higher than the peak voltage it will be subjected to.

**(ii) Operation of a zener diode voltage regulator with a circuit diagram (3 marks):**

A Zener diode can maintain a stable output voltage across a load despite variations in input voltage or load current, by operating in its reverse breakdown region.

      Vin (Unregulated)
   o------/\/\/\----+----o Vout (Regulated)
          Rs        |    |
                    |    ---
                    |    --- Load RL
                  |>|    |
                  | | Zener|
                  | | Diode|
                  ---    |
                   -     |
                    +----o Ground

**Description:** The circuit consists of a series resistor \(R_S\) and a Zener diode connected in parallel (shunt) with the load \(R_L\). The Zener diode is connected in reverse bias. \(R_S\) limits the current flowing into the Zener and the load. When the input voltage \(V_{in}\) is greater than the Zener breakdown voltage \(V_Z\), the Zener diode enters the breakdown region and maintains a nearly constant voltage \(V_Z\) across its terminals and, therefore, across the parallel load \(R_L\). If \(V_{in}\) increases, the current through \(R_S\) increases; the Zener current \(I_Z\) increases while \(V_L\) remains nearly constant. If the load current \(I_L\) decreases (due to increasing \(R_L\)), \(I_Z\) increases to keep \(I_S\) constant (\(I_S = I_Z + I_L\)), maintaining \(V_L\) constant. Regulation is maintained as long as the Zener stays in breakdown (\(I_Z \ge I_{Z,min}\)) and its maximum current rating is not exceeded (\(I_Z \le I_{Z,max}\)).
