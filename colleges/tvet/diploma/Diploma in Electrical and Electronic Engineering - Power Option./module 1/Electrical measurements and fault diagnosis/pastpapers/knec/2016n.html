(c) A generator system designed for continuous operation fails three times in one year. If the total time to repair is 14 days, determine the: (4 marks)

(i) mean time to repair (MTTR), in days;

(ii) mean time between failure (MTBF), in days.

Answer

This question focuses on equipment reliability, the bathtub curve, and calculations of reliability metrics like MTTR, MTBF, and system reliability.

(a) Factors affecting Reliability and Bathtub Curve (8 marks):

**(i) Two factors that affect equipment reliability:**

  • **Component Quality:** The intrinsic reliability of the individual components used in the equipment. Higher quality components manufactured to tighter specifications generally lead to higher equipment reliability.
  • **Design Robustness:** How well the equipment is designed to withstand expected operating stresses (voltage, current, temperature, vibration, humidity) and variations without failure. Derating components is a design practice to improve reliability.
  • **Manufacturing and Assembly Processes:** The quality of manufacturing and assembly, including soldering, wiring, testing, and quality control, directly impacts the number of initial defects and thus reliability.
  • **Operating Environment:** The conditions under which the equipment operates (temperature, humidity, dust, vibration, power supply stability). Harsh environments can accelerate degradation and cause failures.
  • **Maintenance Quality:** The quality and timeliness of preventive and corrective maintenance. Poor maintenance can introduce new faults or fail to address potential issues.
  • **Usage/Stress Level:** How the equipment is used relative to its design limits (e.g., continuous heavy load vs. intermittent light load).

**(ii) Sketch and explanation of the failure rate curve (Bathtub Curve):**

The bathtub curve illustrates the typical failure rate of a population of components or equipment over its lifespan, composed of three periods:

  • **Infant Mortality (Early Failures):** The failure rate is high initially but decreases rapidly. Caused by manufacturing defects, poor quality components, or installation errors. These are often screened out during burn-in testing.
  • **Useful Life (Random Failures):** The failure rate is relatively constant and low. Failures are random events, often stress-induced (e.g., voltage spikes, transient surges, external shock), not related to age. This is the desired operating period.
  • **Wear-out Period:** The failure rate increases rapidly with time. Caused by aging, wear, fatigue, corrosion, and material degradation. Preventive maintenance or replacement is often scheduled before this phase.

(b) Reliability of Units and Equipment (8 marks):

For components or units connected in series, the failure of any single unit causes the failure of the entire system. The system reliability is the product of the individual unit reliabilities.

Given MTBFs for units in a series system and an operating period \(t = 1000\) hours. The reliability \(R(t)\) for a time \(t\), assuming a constant failure rate (\(\lambda = 1/MTBF\)), is \( R(t) = e^{-\lambda t} = e^{-t/MTBF} \).

From Table 1 (Figure 1):

  • Power supply: MTBF = 30,000 hrs
  • Pre-amplifier: MTBF = 100,000 hrs
  • Oscillator: MTBF = 60,000 hrs
  • Modulator: MTBF = 75,000 hrs
  • Power amplifier: MTBF = 40,000 hrs

**(i) Reliability of each unit for an operating period of 1000 hours:**

  • Power supply: \( R_{PS}(1000) = e^{-1000/30000} = e^{-1/30} \approx e^{-0.0333} \approx 0.9672 \)
  • Pre-amplifier: \( R_{PA}(1000) = e^{-1000/100000} = e^{-0.01} \approx 0.9900 \)
  • Oscillator: \( R_{Osc}(1000) = e^{-1000/60000} = e^{-1/60} \approx e^{-0.0167} \approx 0.9835 \)
  • Modulator: \( R_{Mod}(1000) = e^{-1000/75000} = e^{-1/75} \approx e^{-0.0133} \approx 0.9868 \)
  • Power amplifier: \( R_{PowA}(1000) = e^{-1000/40000} = e^{-1/40} = e^{-0.025} \approx 0.9753 \)

**(ii) Reliability of the equipment for an operating period of 1000 hours:**

For a series system, the system reliability (\(R_{system}\)) is the product of the individual unit reliabilities:

\[ R_{system}(t) = R_{PS}(t) \times R_{PA}(t) \times R_{Osc}(t) \times R_{Mod}(t) \times R_{PowA}(t) \] \[ R_{system}(1000) \approx 0.9672 \times 0.9900 \times 0.9835 \times 0.9868 \times 0.9753 \] \[ R_{system}(1000) \approx 0.9055 \]

Alternatively, calculate the total failure rate for the series system: \( \lambda_{system} = \sum \lambda_i = \sum (1/MTBF_i) \).

\[ \lambda_{system} = \frac{1}{30000} + \frac{1}{100000} + \frac{1}{60000} + \frac{1}{75000} + \frac{1}{40000} \quad (\text{per hour}) \] \[ \lambda_{system} \approx (33.33 + 10 + 16.67 + 13.33 + 25) \times 10^{-6} \quad (\text{per hour}) \] \[ \lambda_{system} \approx 98.33 \times 10^{-6} \quad (\text{per hour}) \] \[ \lambda_{system} \approx 0.09833 \quad (\text{per 1000 hours}) \]

System reliability: \( R_{system}(1000) = e^{-\lambda_{system} \times 1000} = e^{-0.09833} \approx 0.9063 \).

Note: The two methods (multiplying individual reliabilities vs. using total failure rate) yield slightly different results due to rounding. Using the total failure rate calculated from MTBFs is generally more precise for a series system. \( R_{system}(1000) \approx 0.9063 \).

(c) Generator System Reliability Metrics (4 marks):

Given:

  • Operating period = 1 year
  • Number of failures in 1 year = 3
  • Total time to repair = 14 days

Assume 1 year = 365 days.

**(i) Mean time to repair (MTTR), in days:**

MTTR is the total time spent on repair divided by the number of failures.

\[ MTTR = \frac{\text{Total Time to Repair}}{\text{Number of Failures}} = \frac{14 \text{ days}}{3 \text{ failures}} \approx 4.67 \text{ days/failure} \]

**(ii) Mean time between failure (MTBF), in days:**

MTBF is the average time between successive failures for a repairable system. It includes both operating time and repair time. \[ MTBF = \frac{\text{Total Operating Period}}{\text{Number of Failures}} = \frac{1 \text{ year}}{3 \text{ failures}} = \frac{365 \text{ days}}{3 \text{ failures}} \approx 121.67 \text{ days/failure} \]

Alternatively, MTBF can be viewed as \( MTBF = MTTF + MTTR \). Total operating time (uptime) = Total period - Total downtime = 365 days - 14 days = 351 days. \[ MTTF = \frac{\text{Total Uptime}}{\text{Number of Failures}} = \frac{351 \text{ days}}{3 \text{ failures}} = 117 \text{ days} \] \[ MTBF = MTTF + MTTR = 117 \text{ days} + 4.67 \text{ days} = 121.67 \text{ days} \]

Both definitions yield the same result.

Calculating reliability metrics helps in predicting system performance and planning maintenance.

Answer

This question covers different systems of units (MKS vs CGS, ESU, EMU) and factors affecting the precision of Wheatstone bridge measurements.

(a) MKS vs CGS Units and Charge Units Comparison (6 marks):

**(i) Two advantages of MKS over CGS system of units:**

  • **Practical Magnitudes:** The base units (meter, kilogram, second) and many derived units (like Newton, Joule, Watt) have magnitudes that are more convenient for practical engineering applications compared to the CGS units (centimeter, gram, second).
  • **Basis for SI:** The MKS system formed the foundation for the modern International System of Units (SI), which is the internationally adopted standard.
  • **Simplified Formulas:** When extended to electromagnetism (MKSA), it provides a coherent system where electromagnetic equations (like Maxwell's equations) often take a simpler form without awkward constants compared to CGS-ESU or CGS-EMU.

**(ii) Comparison of charge units (cgs e.m.u vs MKS):**

Comparing charge units requires understanding the definitions in each system and the relationship between them, which involves the speed of light (\(c\)). This is a complex derivation typically covered in electromagnetism courses.

In cgs e.m.u, current is a fundamental quantity derived from force between conductors. The unit of current (abampere) is defined such that \(k=1\) in Ampere's force law (\(F = k \frac{I_1 I_2 l}{r}\)). The unit of charge (abcoulomb) is defined as 1 abampere-second.

In MKS (or SI), current (Ampere) is a fundamental quantity. The unit of charge (Coulomb) is defined as 1 Ampere-second.

The relationship between abampere (emu current) and Ampere (SI current) is 1 abampere = 10 Amperes.

The relationship between abcoulomb (emu charge) and Coulomb (SI charge) is 1 abcoulomb = 1 abampere \(\times\) 1 second = 10 Amperes \(\times\) 1 second = 10 Coulombs.

So, one unit of charge in cgs electromagnetic units (1 abcoulomb) is equal to 10 units of charge in MKS/SI units (1 Coulomb). The question states "equal to one unit", which is incorrect based on the standard definitions. It might be asking for the *dimensional* equality or a specific context where a factor might make them numerically equal, but conventionally, 1 abcoulomb = 10 C.

Let's check the dimensions. In CGS-EMU, charge \([Q]_{emu} = [M^{1/2}L^{1/2}]\). In MKS/LMTI, charge \([Q]_{LMTI} = [IT]\). These dimensions are fundamentally different, highlighting the distinct nature of the systems.

Given the likely simplification intended, perhaps it's just a concept check. The ratio of the unit of charge in ESU to the unit of charge in EMU is numerically equal to the speed of light \(c\). 1 statcoulomb \(\approx 3.3356 \times 10^{-10}\) Coulomb. 1 abcoulomb = 10 Coulomb.

Conclusion: One unit of charge in cgs electromagnetic units (abcoulomb) is **not** equal to one unit of charge in MKS/SI units (Coulomb); 1 abcoulomb = 10 Coulombs.

(b) Dimensional Expressions in CGS Units (6 marks):

CGS units (centimeter-gram-second) have two main electromagnetic branches: ESU (electrostatic) and EMU (electromagnetic). The dimensions can be expressed in terms of L, M, T for each branch.

  • **(i) Charge (Q) in e.s.u:** As derived in Q1b of the July 2014 paper: \[ [Q]_{esu} = [M^{1/2}L^{3/2}T^{-1}] \] This is derived from Coulomb's Law assuming \(k=1/\epsilon_0=1\).
  • **(ii) Magnetising Force (H) in e.m.u:** Magnetising force H is related to magnetic flux density B (\(B = \mu H\)). In EMU, permeability of vacuum \(\mu_0\) is dimensionless and taken as 1. H is related to the magnetic field produced by current. For a long solenoid, \(H = NI/l\). In EMU, current \(I\) has dimensions \([M^{1/2}L^{1/2}T^{-1}]\). Length \(l\) has dimensions \([L]\). N is dimensionless. \[ [H]_{emu} = \frac{[I]_{emu}}{[L]} = \frac{[M^{1/2}L^{1/2}T^{-1}]}{[L]} = [M^{1/2}L^{-1/2}T^{-1}] \] Alternatively, using the force between magnetic poles: \(F = k \frac{p_1 p_2}{r^2}\). In EMU, \(k=1/\mu_0=1\). Force \([MLT^{-2}]\), Length \([L]\). Magnetic pole strength \(p\) has dimensions \( [p]_{emu} = [M^{1/2}L^{3/2}T^{-1}] \) (same as charge in ESU). Magnetic field strength \(H\) is force per unit pole strength \(H = F/p\). \[ [H]_{emu} = \frac{[MLT^{-2}]}{[M^{1/2}L^{3/2}T^{-1}]} = [M^{1/2}L^{-1/2}T^{-1}] \] Both derivations yield the same dimensions for H in EMU.

(c) Factors affecting precision of medium resistance measurement with Wheatstone Bridge (6 marks):

This section seems to be a repeat from Question 2(c) of the July 2014 paper. The explanation for each factor is the same:

  • **(i) Temperature effects:** Changes in temperature affect the resistance values of the bridge arms. Non-uniform heating or temperature drift during measurement can lead to incorrect balance points. Using resistors with low temperature coefficients or operating in a temperature-controlled environment minimizes this error.
  • **(ii) Contact resistance:** Resistance introduced at the connections between components and wiring. These small resistances add in series to the bridge arms. While often negligible for high resistances, they can become significant when measuring medium resistances, affecting precision. Using clean terminals and secure connections helps.
  • **(iii) Thermo-electric effects:** Small voltages generated at junctions of dissimilar metals due to temperature differences (Seebeck effect). These e.m.f.s can disturb the null detector's reading, especially when using a sensitive galvanometer. Keeping the bridge at a uniform temperature or using specific switching sequences can help mitigate this.

This question tests understanding of fundamental unit systems and the practical considerations for achieving precision in bridge measurements.

Answer

This question covers oscilloscope measurements of AM waves, calculations for an electrostatic voltmeter, and AC bridge circuits, including derivation and calculation of unknown components.

(a) Measuring Depth of Modulation with CRO (Timebase OFF) and Waveform (6 marks):

When the time base of an oscilloscope is switched off, the spot on the screen moves only in the vertical direction if the signal is applied to the Y-input. For an amplitude modulated (AM) wave, the amplitude of the carrier signal varies according to the modulating signal. Applying the AM wave to the Y-input with the timebase off results in a vertical line whose length corresponds to the peak-to-peak variation of the AM signal's amplitude.

Procedure:

  1. Connect the AM signal source to the vertical input (Y-input) of the oscilloscope.
  2. Ensure the timebase is switched off (horizontal sweep is disabled).
  3. Adjust the vertical gain (Volts/Div) and position controls to center the vertical line and fit it within the screen.
  4. Measure the maximum length of the vertical line (\(2 V_{max}\), corresponding to the peak-to-peak amplitude when the modulating signal is at its peak positive or negative value).
  5. Measure the minimum length of the vertical line (\(2 V_{min}\), corresponding to the peak-to-peak amplitude when the modulating signal is at its most compressed amplitude).

Sketch of the displayed waveform:

The depth of modulation (modulation index, \(m\)) is given by the formula based on \(V_{max}\) and \(V_{min}\) (the instantaneous maximum and minimum amplitudes of the AM wave envelope):

\[ m = \frac{V_{max} - V_{min}}{V_{max} + V_{min}} \]

On the CRO display with timebase off, the length of the vertical line swings between \(2V_{max}\) and \(2V_{min}\). We measure the total maximum vertical deflection \(A_{max} = 2V_{max}\) and the total minimum vertical deflection \(A_{min} = 2V_{min}\). So \(V_{max} = A_{max}/2\) and \(V_{min} = A_{min}/2\).

\[ m = \frac{A_{max}/2 - A_{min}/2}{A_{max}/2 + A_{min}/2} = \frac{(A_{max} - A_{min})/2}{(A_{max} + A_{min})/2} = \frac{A_{max} - A_{min}}{A_{max} + A_{min}} \]

The depth of modulation is calculated using the maximum and minimum lengths (deflections) measured directly from the screen.

(b) Electrostatic Voltmeter Calculation (8 marks):

Electrostatic voltmeters measure voltage by the electrostatic force between charged plates. The torque on the movable plates is balanced by a spring torque.

The energy stored in a capacitor is \( W = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 \). In an electrostatic voltmeter, the change in stored energy with respect to displacement (\(\theta\)) gives the electric torque \(T_e\):

\[ T_e = \frac{dW}{d\theta} = \frac{1}{2} V^2 \frac{dC}{d\theta} \]

The spring provides a restoring torque \(T_s = K_s \theta\), where \(K_s\) is the spring constant. At equilibrium, \(T_e = T_s\):

\[ \frac{1}{2} V^2 \frac{dC}{d\theta} = K_s \theta \]

We are given the total capacitance \(C = 35.4 \theta \, \mathrm{pF} = 35.4 \theta \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{F}\).

The rate of change of capacitance with respect to deflection is \( \frac{dC}{d\theta} = \frac{d}{d\theta} (35.4 \theta \times 10^{-12}) = 35.4 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{F/radian} \).

Note that \(\theta\) must be in radians for the derivative \(dC/d\theta\) to be dimensionally correct in the torque equation which is typically in Nm/radian. The given deflection is 100 degrees. Convert this to radians:

\[ \theta_{FSD} = 100^\circ \times \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} \mathrm{rad} = \frac{100\pi}{180} \mathrm{rad} = \frac{5\pi}{9} \mathrm{rad} \]

At full-scale deflection (FSD), \(V = 10 \, kV = 10 \times 10^3 \, V\) and \(\theta = \frac{5\pi}{9}\) radians.

Substitute these values into the equilibrium equation:

\[ \frac{1}{2} (10 \times 10^3 \, V)^2 \times (35.4 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{F/radian}) = K_s \times \frac{5\pi}{9} \, \mathrm{rad} \] \[ \frac{1}{2} (100 \times 10^6) \times (35.4 \times 10^{-12}) = K_s \times \frac{5\pi}{9} \] \[ 50 \times 10^6 \times 35.4 \times 10^{-12} = K_s \times \frac{5\pi}{9} \] \[ 1770 \times 10^{-6} = K_s \times \frac{5\pi}{9} \] \[ 0.00177 = K_s \times \frac{5\pi}{9} \]

Solve for \(K_s\):

\[ K_s = 0.00177 \times \frac{9}{5\pi} \quad \mathrm{Nm/radian} \] \[ K_s \approx 0.00177 \times \frac{9}{15.708} \approx 0.00177 \times 0.573 \approx 0.001014 \quad \mathrm{Nm/radian} \]

The spring constant is approximately \(1.014 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{Nm/radian}\).

Answer

This question covers common component failures and handling, troubleshooting electronic circuits using voltage measurements and fault effects, and developing a fault location chart for a system based on a block diagram.

(a) Capacitor Failures and Component Handling (6 marks):

**(I) Two factors that may cause premature failure of capacitors:**

  • **Overvoltage:** Applying a voltage exceeding the capacitor's voltage rating can cause the dielectric material to break down, leading to a short circuit.
  • **Overheating:** Operating the capacitor at temperatures above its maximum rated temperature can accelerate degradation of the dielectric and electrolyte (for electrolytics), leading to reduced capacitance, increased ESR, or bulging/venting.
  • **Incorrect Polarity:** Applying reverse voltage to polarized capacitors (like electrolytic or tantalum) can cause chemical reactions leading to damage, leakage, venting, or explosion.
  • **Excessive Ripple Current:** For capacitors used in filtering (especially electrolytics), excessive AC ripple current can cause internal heating, leading to premature aging and failure.
  • **Physical Stress:** Bending leads too sharply, mechanical shock, or vibration can damage internal connections or the case.

**(II) Two precautions when handling and testing electronic components:**

  • **Static Discharge (ESD) Prevention:** Especially important for static-sensitive devices like MOSFETs and ICs. Use grounded wrist straps and work surfaces, handle components by the body, and keep them in anti-static packaging until use.
  • **Proper Tool Usage:** Use appropriate tools and techniques to avoid damaging components or the PCB. Use heat sinks when soldering heat-sensitive components. Use correct desoldering methods.
  • **Power Off Before Testing:** Disconnect power to the circuit before performing resistance or continuity checks with a multimeter to avoid damaging the meter or components.
  • **Component Polarity:** Pay attention to the polarity of polarized components (diodes, electrolytic capacitors, ICs, transistors) during installation and testing.
  • **Voltage/Current Ratings:** Be aware of the voltage and current limitations of components and test equipment to avoid overstressing or damaging them during testing.

(b) Amplifier Fault Analysis (8 marks):

Figure 2 shows a common-emitter amplifier with voltage divider bias. We need to estimate DC voltages at test points TP1, TP2, and TP3 under normal operation and given fault conditions. TP1 is at the collector, TP2 at the base, and TP3 at the emitter.

Assume typical \(V_{BE} = 0.7V\) for silicon, and a reasonable \(\beta\) (e.g., 100) for estimating base voltage if not negligible. Let's first analyze normal operation to get a baseline.

From Figure 2: \(V_{CC} = 12V\), \(R_1 = 33k\Omega\), \(R_2 = 8.2k\Omega\), \(R_C = 3.3k\Omega\), \(R_E = 1k\Omega\).

Normal Operation (Approximate Calculation):

  • Base Voltage \(V_B \approx V_{CC} \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = 12V \times \frac{8.2k}{33k + 8.2k} = 12V \times \frac{8.2}{41.2} \approx 2.398V \). TP2 voltage \(\approx 2.4V\).
  • Emitter Voltage \(V_E = V_B - V_{BE} \approx 2.398V - 0.7V = 1.698V \). TP3 voltage \(\approx 1.7V\).
  • Emitter Current \(I_E = V_E / R_E = 1.698V / 1k\Omega \approx 1.7mA\).
  • Collector Current \(I_C \approx I_E \approx 1.7mA\).
  • Collector Voltage \(V_C = V_{CC} - I_C R_C = 12V - (1.7mA)(3.3k\Omega) = 12V - (1.7 \times 10^{-3})(3.3 \times 10^3) = 12V - 5.61V \approx 6.39V \). TP1 voltage \(\approx 6.4V\).

Normal Voltages: TP1 (\(V_C\)) \(\approx 6.4V\), TP2 (\(V_B\)) \(\approx 2.4V\), TP3 (\(V_E\)) \(\approx 1.7V\).

**(i) Estimate voltages at test points for each fault:**

  • **(I) Resistor \(R_1\) open circuit:**
    • If \(R_1\) opens, the base is biased only by \(R_2\) to ground. The base voltage \(V_B\) will be very low, pulled towards ground through \(R_2\). Ideally, with no base current path from \(V_{CC}\), \(I_B\) approaches zero, and the transistor goes into cutoff. \(V_B\) will be close to 0V (or slightly above due to leakage or \(R_2\) current if it were connected to a negative rail, but here it's to ground).
    • **Effect:** Transistor turns OFF (\(I_C \approx 0\), \(I_E \approx 0\)).
    • **Estimated Voltages:** TP1 (\(V_C\)) \(\approx V_{CC} = 12V\) (no drop across \(R_C\)), TP2 (\(V_B\)) \(\approx 0V\) (pulled down by \(R_2\) to ground), TP3 (\(V_E\)) \(\approx 0V\) (no current through \(R_E\)).
  • **(II) Transistor base-emitter junction short circuit:**
    • If the B-E junction shorts, \(V_{BE}\) is forced to 0V. This prevents the transistor from operating in the active region where \(V_{BE} \approx 0.7V\). Instead, the base and emitter are effectively connected directly.

A thermocouple ammeter measures current by utilizing the heating effect of the current and the Seebeck effect (thermo-electric effect). It can measure both AC and DC currents and is particularly useful for measuring RMS values of non-sinusoidal AC currents.

The main components are a heating element (a resistor), a thermocouple junction, and a sensitive DC moving-coil meter. \[ \theta_{half} = k I_{half}^2 \] \[ \frac{\theta_{FSD}}{2} = k I_{half}^2 \]

Substitute \( \theta_{FSD} = 100k \):

\[ \frac{100k}{2} = k I_{half}^2 \] \[ 50k = k I_{half}^2 \]

Divide by \(k\) (assuming \(k \neq 0\)):

\[ Y \approx \tan \alpha \times L = \frac{v_y}{v_x} \times L = \left(\frac{e V_d l_d}{m d v_x}\right) / v_x \times L = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{m d v_x^2} \]

\[ Y = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{m d (2 e V_a / m)} = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{2 e V_a d} = \frac{V_d l_d L}{2 V_a d} \]

(i) minimum value of \( R_{L} \) that the Zener will tolerate in maintaining \( V_{L} \) constant;

(ii) minimum value of load current;

(iii) maximum value of load current.

Answer

This question deals with DC power supplies, including rectifier characteristics, voltage multiplier circuits, and Zener diode voltage regulators.

(a) DC Power Supply Definitions and Half-wave Rectifier (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to dc power supplies:**

  • **(I) Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV):** The maximum instantaneous voltage that a rectifying diode must withstand in the reverse-biased direction when it is not conducting. It is a critical parameter for selecting diodes for rectifier circuits. Exceeding the PIV can cause diode breakdown and failure.
  • **(II) Ripple Factor:** A measure of the effectiveness of a filter in reducing the AC component (ripple) in the output of a rectifier. It is defined as the ratio of the RMS value of the AC component of the output voltage to the DC (average) value of the output voltage. A lower ripple factor indicates a smoother DC output. \[ \text{Ripple Factor} = \frac{V_{rms(ac)}}{V_{dc}} \]

**(ii) Derivation of dc output voltage for a half-wave diode rectifier feeding a pure resistive load (6 marks):**

A half-wave rectifier with a pure resistive load \(R_L\) conducts current only during the positive half-cycle of the input AC voltage. The diode is assumed ideal (zero forward voltage drop, infinite reverse resistance).

Let the input AC voltage be \( v_{in}(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \).

During the positive half-cycle (\(0 \leq \omega t \leq \pi\)), the diode is forward-biased and acts as a short circuit. The output voltage across the load is equal to the input voltage: \( v_{out}(t) = v_{in}(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \).

During the negative half-cycle (\(\pi \leq \omega t \leq 2\pi\)), the diode is reverse-biased and acts as an open circuit. No current flows through the load, so the output voltage is zero: \( v_{out}(t) = 0 \).

The DC output voltage is the average value of the output voltage waveform over one full cycle (period \(2\pi\)).

\[ V_{dc} = V_{avg} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} v_{out}(t) \, d(\omega t) \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left[ \int_0^{\pi} V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \, d(\omega t) + \int_{\pi}^{2\pi} 0 \, d(\omega t) \right] \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \sin(\theta) \, d\theta \quad (\text{where } \theta = \omega t) \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-\cos(\theta)]_0^{\pi} \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-\cos(\pi) - (-\cos(0))] \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-(-1) - (-1)] = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [1 + 1] = \frac{2 V_{peak}}{2\pi} \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{\pi} \]

The DC output voltage of an ideal half-wave rectifier with a resistive load is the peak AC input voltage divided by \(\pi\).

(b) Full-wave Voltage Doubler Operation (6 marks):

A full-wave voltage doubler is a rectifier circuit that produces a DC output voltage approximately equal to twice the peak value of the input AC voltage. It uses two diodes and two capacitors.

**Operation:**

  • During the **positive half-cycle** of the AC input voltage, diode D1 is forward-biased, and D2 is reverse-biased. Current flows through D1, charging capacitor C1 to the peak voltage \(V_{peak}\) with the polarity shown (top plate positive, bottom plate negative). Once C1 is charged to \(V_{peak}\), D1 becomes reverse-biased.
  • During the **negative half-cycle** of the AC input voltage, diode D2 is forward-biased, and D1 is reverse-biased. Current flows through D2, charging capacitor C2. The voltage across C2 is the sum of the input AC voltage (negative peak) and the voltage already stored on C1 (\(V_{peak}\)). Diode D2 conducts and charges C2 to approximately \(V_{peak}\). As the negative peak of the input voltage is reached, the voltage across C2 becomes approximately \(V_{peak}\) (bottom plate positive, top plate negative relative to the junction point).
  • The output DC voltage is taken across the series combination of capacitors C1 and C2. Since C1 is charged to \(V_{peak}\) and C2 is charged to \(V_{peak}\) (with opposing polarities relative to their junction), the total output DC voltage is \(V_{out} = V_{C1} + V_{C2} = V_{peak} + V_{peak} = 2 V_{peak}\).

This output is a pulsating DC voltage that can be smoothed further using additional filtering. The PIV rating for the diodes in this circuit needs to be at least \(2V_{peak}\).

(c) Zener Diode Shunt Regulator Calculation (4 marks):

The circuit is a Zener diode shunt regulator. The Zener diode maintains a constant voltage \(V_Z\) across the load as long as the input voltage \(V_{in}\) and load resistance \(R_L\) vary within limits that keep the Zener in its breakdown region (i.e., Zener current \(I_Z\) is between \(I_{Z,min}\) and \(I_{Z,max}\)).

Given: \(V_{in} = 20V\), \(R_S = 220\Omega\). Zener diode: \(V_Z = 10V\), \(I_{Z,min} = 5mA\), \(I_{Z,max} = 50mA\). Load is variable resistor \(R_L\).

The voltage across the load \(V_L\) is regulated by the Zener diode, so \(V_L = V_Z = 10V\). The voltage across the series resistor \(R_S\) is \(V_{R_S} = V_{in} - V_L = 20V - 10V = 10V\).

The current through the series resistor \(I_S\) is constant as long as \(V_{in}\) and \(R_S\) are constant:

\[ I_S = \frac{V_{R_S}}{R_S} = \frac{10 \, V}{220 \, \Omega} \approx 0.04545 \, A = 45.45 \, mA \]

The current \(I_S\) splits between the Zener diode (\(I_Z\)) and the load (\(I_L\)): \(I_S = I_Z + I_L\).

For regulation to be maintained, the Zener diode must be in breakdown, meaning \(I_{Z,min} \leq I_Z \leq I_{Z,max}\). The load current \(I_L\) varies as \(R_L\) varies: \(I_L = V_L / R_L = 10V / R_L\).

**(i) Minimum value of \(R_L\) that the Zener will tolerate in maintaining \(V_L\) constant:**

Regulation is maintained as long as \(I_Z \geq I_{Z,min}\). Since \(I_S = I_Z + I_L\), the minimum Zener current corresponds to the maximum load current (\(I_{L,max}\)).

\[ I_Z = I_S - I_L \]

Minimum \(I_Z\) occurs when \(I_L\) is maximum: \( I_{Z,min} = I_S - I_{L,max} \).

\[ I_{L,max} = I_S - I_{Z,min} = 45.45 \, mA - 5 \, mA = 40.45 \, mA \]

The minimum load resistance \(R_{L,min}\) occurs when the load current is maximum:

\[ R_{L,min} = \frac{V_L}{I_{L,max}} = \frac{10 \, V}{40.45 \, mA} = \frac{10 \, V}{40.45 \times 10^{-3} \, A} \approx 247.22 \, \Omega \]

**(ii) Minimum value of load current:**

The minimum load current (\(I_{L,min}\)) occurs when the Zener current is maximum (\(I_{Z,max}\)).

\[ I_{L,min} = I_S - I_{Z,max} = 45.45 \, mA - 50 \, mA = -4.55 \, mA \]

A load current cannot be negative. This result suggests that even with minimum load current (or load removed, \(I_L = 0\)), the maximum current through the Zener \(I_S = 45.45mA\) is within the Zener's \(I_{Z,max} = 50mA\) rating. The minimum load current that still allows regulation is when \(I_Z = I_{Z,max}\). The absolute minimum load current would be 0 (open circuit load). Regulation is maintained as long as the Zener doesn't exceed \(I_{Z,max}\) or go below \(I_{Z,min}\). The lower limit for \(I_L\) is when \(I_Z = I_{Z,max}\).

\[ I_{L,min} = I_S - I_{Z,max} = 45.45 \, mA - 50 \, mA = -4.55 \, mA \]

This indicates that \(I_S\) is never large enough to push the Zener current to its maximum limit, even if \(I_L\) is zero. Thus, the minimum load current for which regulation is maintained is when \(I_Z = I_{Z,min}\). The actual minimum load current the circuit can *handle* while maintaining regulation is when \(I_Z = I_{Z,max}\). However, the calculation shows \(I_S < I_{Z,max}\), so the Zener current will never reach \(I_{Z,max}\). The minimum load current corresponds to maximum \(I_Z\), but since \(I_S\) is limited, \(I_Z\) cannot exceed \(I_S\). The range of \(I_L\) for regulation is from \(I_S - I_{Z,max}\) to \(I_S - I_{Z,min}\). Since \(I_S < I_{Z,max}\), the lower bound is negative, meaning the Zener current never reaches \(I_{Z,max}\) within the operating range. The practical minimum load current is often considered to be 0 (open circuit load). In this case, \(I_Z = I_S = 45.45mA\), which is between \(I_{Z,min}\) and \(I_{Z,max}\), so regulation is maintained. Given the calculation resulted in a negative value, there might be an interpretation issue or the Zener limits are set such that \(I_S\) is within the Zener's operating range regardless of the load. The minimum *required* load current to keep \(I_Z\) below \(I_{Z,max}\) would be \(I_S - I_{Z,max}\). Let's state the minimum load current for which \(I_Z \ge I_{Z,min}\). \[ I_{L,max} = I_S - I_{Z,min} = 40.45 \, mA \] \[ R_{L,min} = 10V / 40.45mA \approx 247.22\Omega \] If \(R_L\) is larger than \(R_{L,min}\), \(I_L\) is smaller than \(I_{L,max}\), and \(I_Z = I_S - I_L\) will be greater than \(I_{Z,min}\). The maximum possible \(R_L\) is infinite, meaning \(I_L = 0\). In this case, \(I_Z = I_S = 45.45mA\). Since \(5mA \le 45.45mA \le 50mA\), the Zener stays in breakdown for all \(R_L \ge 247.22\Omega\). The question asks for the minimum value of load current. This is when \(R_L\) is maximum (ideally infinite or open circuit). \[ I_{L,min} = \frac{V_L}{R_{L,max}} = \frac{10V}{\infty} = 0A \] Regulation is maintained down to \(I_L=0A\) because \(I_S=45.45mA\) is within the Zener's operational range (5mA to 50mA).

**(iii) Maximum value of load current:**

The maximum value of load current (\(I_{L,max}\)) occurs when the Zener current is at its minimum (\(I_{Z,min}\)).

\[ I_{L,max} = I_S - I_{Z,min} = 45.45 \, mA - 5 \, mA = 40.45 \, mA \]

Summary of Zener Regulator Calculations:

  • **(i) minimum value of \(R_L\):** \( R_{L,min} \approx 247.22 \, \Omega \) (for \(I_Z \geq I_{Z,min}\))
  • **(ii) minimum value of load current:** \( I_{L,min} = 0 \, A \) (as \(I_S < I_{Z,max}\))
  • **(iii) maximum value of load current:** \( I_{L,max} = 40.45 \, mA \) (when \(I_Z = I_{Z,min}\))
