Question 1

(a) (i) Describe "standard" with respect to measurements. (6 marks)

(ii) Distinguish between primary and secondary standards.

(b) Derive from first principles, the dimensional equation for magnetic flux density using the LMTI system. (8 marks)

(c) (i) State the derived units for each of the following quantities: (4 marks)

I. density;

II. electric field strength.

(ii) Derive the dimensional equation for momentum. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers fundamental concepts of measurement standards, units, and dimensional analysis.

(a) Measurement Standards (6 marks):

**(i) Describe "standard" with respect to measurements:**

  • A standard, in the context of measurements, is a reference quantity used to define, realize, or reproduce a unit of a physical quantity. It serves as a basis for comparison in the measurement process. Standards ensure that measurements are consistent, accurate, and comparable globally.

**(ii) Distinguish between primary and secondary standards:**

  • **Primary standards:** These are standards of the highest metrological quality. Their values are established directly from the fundamental definitions of the units or by comparison with international standards. They are maintained at national standards laboratories and are not used for routine measurements. They are used to calibrate secondary standards.
  • **Secondary standards:** These are high-precision standards calibrated directly or indirectly against primary standards. They are also maintained by national standards laboratories or accredited calibration laboratories. They are used to calibrate working standards.

(b) Dimensional equation for magnetic flux density using LMTI system (8 marks):

The LMTI system uses Length (L), Mass (M), Time (T), and Electric Current (I) as fundamental dimensions. Magnetic flux density (B) can be dimensionally derived from the force experienced by a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field: \(F = BI l\), where F is force, B is magnetic flux density, I is current, and l is the length of the conductor perpendicular to the field.

Rearrange the formula to solve for B: \( B = \frac{F}{I l} \).

Force (F) has dimensions \([MLT^{-2}]\).

Current (I) has dimensions \([I]\).

Length (l) has dimensions \([L]\).

Substitute these dimensions into the expression for B:

\[ [B] = \frac{[F]}{[I][l]} \] \[ [B] = \frac{[MLT^{-2}]}{[I][L]} \] \[ [B] = [M L^{1-1} T^{-2} I^{-1}] \] \[ [B] = [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \]

The dimensional equation for magnetic flux density in the LMTI system is \( [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \).

(c) Derived units and dimensional equation for momentum (7 marks):

**(i) Derived units for quantities:**

Derived units are combinations of fundamental units.

  • **I. Density:** Density is mass per unit volume (\(\rho = m/V\)). The SI unit of mass is kilogram (kg), and the SI unit of volume is cubic meter (m³).
    Derived unit: kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m³).
  • **II. Electric field strength:** Electric field strength is force per unit charge (\(E = F/Q\)). The SI unit of force is Newton (N), and the SI unit of charge is Coulomb (C).
    Derived unit: Newtons per Coulomb (N/C).
    Alternatively, electric field strength is also voltage per unit distance (\(E = V/d\)). The SI unit of voltage is Volt (V), and the SI unit of distance is meter (m).
    Derived unit: Volts per meter (V/m).

**(ii) Derive the dimensional equation for momentum:**

Momentum (p) is defined as the product of mass (m) and velocity (v): \(p = mv\).

Mass (m) has dimensions \([M]\).

Velocity (v) is distance per unit time, so its dimensions are \([LT^{-1}]\).

Substitute these dimensions into the expression for momentum:

\[ [p] = [m] \times [v] \] \[ [p] = [M] \times [LT^{-1}] \] \[ [p] = [MLT^{-1}] \]

The dimensional equation for momentum is \( [MLT^{-1}] \).

This question tests fundamental concepts in metrology and dimensional analysis, including the definition of units and standards and the derivation of dimensional formulas for physical quantities.

Question 2

(a) Figure 1 shows the circuit diagram of the Maxwell's inductance-capacitance bridge at balance. (10 marks)

(i) Derive the expressions for the unknown components \( \mathrm{R}_{1} \) and \( \mathrm{L}_{1} \).

(ii) Determine the values of \( \mathrm{R}_{1} \) and \( \mathrm{L}_{1} \) from the expressions in (a)(i).

(b) With aid of a labelled block diagram, describe the operation of a digital frequency meter. (10 marks)

(c) The coil of a permanent magnet moving coil instrument has a resistance of \( 5 \Omega \) and requires a current f 15 mA for full-scale deflection. Determine the resistance to be connected in: (5 marks)

(i) parallel to enable the instrument read up to 1 A ;

(ii) series to enable the instrument read up to 15 V .

Answer

This question covers Maxwell's bridge, the operation of a digital frequency meter, and range extension calculations for a moving-coil instrument.

(a) Maxwell's Inductance-Capacitance Bridge (10 marks):

Figure 1 shows Maxwell's bridge used for measuring unknown inductance \(L_1\) and its series resistance \(R_1\) by comparing them with a known capacitance \(C_4\) and known resistances \(R_2, R_3, R_4\).

From Figure 1, the impedances of the arms are:

  • \(Z_1 = R_1 + j\omega L_1\) (Unknown impedance)
  • \(Z_2 = R_2\) (Known non-inductive resistance)
  • \(Z_3 = R_3\) (Known non-inductive resistance)
  • \(Z_4\) is \(R_4\) in parallel with \(C_4\). Its impedance is \( Z_4 = \frac{R_4 \times (1/j\omega C_4)}{R_4 + (1/j\omega C_4)} = \frac{R_4}{1 + j\omega R_4 C_4} \)

At balance, \( Z_1 Z_4 = Z_2 Z_3 \).

\[ (R_1 + j\omega L_1) \left( \frac{R_4}{1 + j\omega R_4 C_4} \right) = R_2 R_3 \] \[ R_1 + j\omega L_1 = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_4} (1 + j\omega R_4 C_4) \] \[ R_1 + j\omega L_1 = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_4} + j\omega \frac{R_2 R_3 R_4 C_4}{R_4} \] \[ R_1 + j\omega L_1 = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_4} + j\omega R_2 R_3 C_4 \]

**(i) Derive the expressions for the unknown components \(R_1\) and \(L_1\):**

Equating the real parts on both sides:

\[ R_1 = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_4} \]

Equating the imaginary parts on both sides:

\[ j\omega L_1 = j\omega R_2 R_3 C_4 \]

Assuming \(\omega \neq 0\), we can cancel \(j\omega\):

\[ L_1 = R_2 R_3 C_4 \]

These are the expressions for \(R_1\) and \(L_1\) at balance.

**(ii) Determine the values of \(R_1\) and \(L_1\) from the expressions in (a)(i):**

To determine the numerical values, the values of \(R_2, R_3, R_4,\) and \(C_4\) are required. These values are not provided in the text of Question 2, but are shown in Figure 1.

From Figure 1:

  • \(R_2 = 300 \, \Omega\)
  • \(R_3 = 700 \, \Omega\)
  • \(R_4 = 1500 \, \Omega\)
  • \(C_4 = 0.8 \, \mu F = 0.8 \times 10^{-6} \, F\)

Calculate \(R_1\):

\[ R_1 = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_4} = \frac{300 \, \Omega \times 700 \, \Omega}{1500 \, \Omega} = \frac{210000}{1500} \, \Omega = 140 \, \Omega \]

Calculate \(L_1\):

\[ L_1 = R_2 R_3 C_4 = 300 \, \Omega \times 700 \, \Omega \times (0.8 \times 10^{-6} \, F) \] \[ L_1 = 210000 \times 0.8 \times 10^{-6} \, H = 168000 \times 10^{-6} \, H = 0.168 \, H \]

The values of the unknown components are \(R_1 = 140 \, \Omega\) and \(L_1 = 0.168 \, H\).

(b) Operation of a Digital Frequency Meter (10 marks):

A digital frequency meter measures the frequency of an input signal by counting the number of cycles or pulses of the input signal within a precisely defined time interval (gate time). The count is then displayed as the frequency.

Block Diagram:

+----------------+    +------------+    +---------+    +-----------+
| Input Signal   |--->| Amplifier/ |--->| Main    |--->| Decade    |---> Digital
| (Unknown Freq) |    | Shaper     |    | Gate    |    | Counters  |     Display
+----------------+    +------------+    +---------+    +-----------+
                            |                 ^                ^
                            |                 |                |
+-----------------+    +----------+    +----+-----+    +-----------+
| Time Base (High|--->| Flip-flop|---->| Gate    |    | Decoder & |
| Stability Osc)  |    | /Divider |     | Control |    | Display   |
+-----------------+    +----------+     +---------+    | Drivers   |
                                                          +-----------+

**Description:**

  • **Input Signal:** The AC or pulse signal whose frequency is to be measured is applied to the input.
  • **Amplifier/Shaper (Schmitt Trigger):** This stage amplifies the input signal (if necessary) and converts it into a clean train of sharp pulses (typically square waves or logic pulses). Each pulse corresponds to one cycle of the input signal.
  • **Main Gate:** An electronic switch (usually a logic gate, like an AND gate) that controls the flow of pulses from the shaper to the counter. It is enabled (opened) for a precise, predetermined time interval by the gate control unit.
  • **Gate Control Unit:** Generates timing signals that open and close the main gate for an accurate duration (the gate time, e.g., 1 ms, 10 ms, 100 ms, 1 s). This unit uses a high-stability crystal oscillator and frequency dividers to produce the required gate time intervals.
  • **Decade Counters:** A series of interconnected electronic counters (typically decimal or BCD counters) that count the number of pulses that pass through the main gate during the gate time. Each counter stage represents a digit.
  • **Decoder & Display Drivers:** Converts the digital count from the counters into a format suitable for the digital display. The display drivers illuminate the segments or pixels of the digital display.
  • **Digital Display:** Shows the final count as a numerical value, which represents the frequency (e.g., if 1000 pulses are counted in 1 ms, the frequency is 1000 pulses/ms = 1,000,000 pulses/s = 1 MHz).

The process starts with resetting the counters to zero. The gate control unit opens the main gate for a precise time. Input pulses pass through the gate and are counted. After the gate time expires, the gate control unit closes the gate, stopping the count. The final count held in the counters is transferred to the display, and the unit prepares for the next measurement cycle.

(c) Moving Coil Instrument Range Extension (5 marks):

To extend the range of a moving coil instrument, a shunt resistor is used for ammeter ranges (parallel connection) and a multiplier resistor is used for voltmeter ranges (series connection).

Given: Coil resistance \(R_m = 5 \, \Omega\). FSD current \(I_m = 15 \, mA = 15 \times 10^{-3} \, A\).

Calculate the voltage across the meter at FSD: \( V_m = I_m R_m = (15 \times 10^{-3} \, A) \times (5 \, \Omega) = 75 \times 10^{-3} \, V = 75 \, mV \).

**(i) Parallel resistance (Shunt) for 1 A range:**

Desired current range \(I = 1 \, A\). Current through shunt \(I_{sh} = I - I_m = 1 \, A - 15 \times 10^{-3} \, A = 1 - 0.015 = 0.985 \, A\). At FSD, the voltage across the shunt equals the voltage across the meter: \(I_{sh} R_{sh} = I_m R_m\).

\[ R_{sh} = \frac{I_m R_m}{I_{sh}} = \frac{V_m}{I - I_m} \] \[ R_{sh} = \frac{75 \times 10^{-3} \, V}{1 \, A - 15 \times 10^{-3} \, A} = \frac{0.075}{0.985} \, \Omega \approx 0.07614 \, \Omega \]

A shunt resistor of approximately \(0.07614 \, \Omega\) is needed in parallel.

**(ii) Series resistance (Multiplier) for 15 V range:**

Desired voltage range \(V = 15 \, V\). Current through the meter and multiplier at FSD is \(I_m = 15 \, mA\). Total resistance \( R_{total} = R_m + R_{mult} \). By Ohm's Law \( V = I_m R_{total} \).

\[ 15 \, V = (15 \times 10^{-3} \, A) \times (5 \, \Omega + R_{mult}) \] \[ \frac{15}{15 \times 10^{-3}} = 5 + R_{mult} \] \[ 1000 = 5 + R_{mult} \] \[ R_{mult} = 1000 - 5 = 995 \, \Omega \]

A multiplier resistor of \(995 \, \Omega\) is needed in series.

This question covers AC bridge analysis, digital instrument operation, and practical meter range extension techniques.

Question 3

(a) (i) Define each of the following with respect to the operation of electrical equipment: (6 marks)

I. reliability;

II. failure;

III. mean time to fail.

(ii) Sketch the reliability and unreliability curves against time and explain their shape. (6 marks)

(b) Two units, X and Y , have MTBFs of 20,000 hours and 100,000 hours respectively. The units are connected in parallel and the resulting system is operated for a period of 1,000 hours. Determine the: (10 marks)

(i) reliability of each unit;

(ii) unreliability of each unit;

(iii) probability of failure of the system;

(iv) system reliability.

Table 1

Answer

This question covers fundamental reliability definitions, reliability and unreliability curves, and calculating reliability for parallel systems based on MTBF.

(a) Reliability Definitions and Curves (12 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to electrical equipment operation:**

  • **I. Reliability:** The probability that an equipment will perform its intended function adequately for a specified period of time under specified operating conditions.
  • **II. Failure:** The termination of the ability of an equipment to perform its required function.
  • **III. Mean time to fail (MTTF):** The average time a non-repairable item is expected to function before it fails. It's also the expected time to the first failure of a repairable system.

**(ii) Sketch of reliability and unreliability curves against time and explanation:**

For equipment operating in its useful life period (constant failure rate \(\lambda\)), reliability \(R(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\) and unreliability \(U(t) = 1 - R(t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}\).

Probability ^
            |
          1 +---------+
            | \       |  Reliability R(t) (Probability of Survival)
            |  \      |
            |   \     |
            |    \    |
          0 +-----+---+---------> Time (t)
              MTTF/MTBF


Probability ^
            |
            |   /-------+
            |  /        | Unreliability U(t) (Probability of Failure)
            | /         |
            |/          |
          0 +-----------+---------> Time (t)
              MTTF/MTBF

(Both curves plotted on the same axis, if intended by the question wording "on the same axis")

Probability ^
            |
          1 +---------+       Reliability R(t)
            | \      /|
            |  \    / |
            |   \  /  |
            |    \/   | Unreliability U(t)
          0 +-----+---+---------> Time (t)
              MTTF/MTBF

**Explanation:**

  • **Reliability Curve:** Starts at 1 (100% probability of working at time t=0) and decreases exponentially towards 0 as time increases. This reflects that the longer a system operates, the higher the probability of failure.
  • **Unreliability Curve:** Starts at 0 (0% probability of failure at time t=0) and increases exponentially towards 1 as time increases. This reflects that the longer a system operates, the higher the probability of having experienced a failure.

The sum of reliability and unreliability at any given time is always 1 (\(R(t) + U(t) = 1\)).

(b) Reliability of Units in Parallel (10 marks):

Two units X and Y are connected in parallel. A parallel system functions if at least one of its units is functioning. The system fails only if *all* units fail. Reliability of a parallel system \(R_{system}\) is calculated from the unreliabilities of the individual units.

Given:

  • MTBF of Unit X = 20,000 hours (\(MTBF_X\))
  • MTBF of Unit Y = 100,000 hours (\(MTBF_Y\))
  • Operating period \(t = 1000\) hours.

Assume constant failure rates: \( \lambda_X = 1/MTBF_X = 1/20000 \, \mathrm{hr}^{-1} \), \( \lambda_Y = 1/MTBF_Y = 1/100000 \, \mathrm{hr}^{-1} \).

**(i) Reliability of each unit for 1,000 hours:**

  • Reliability of Unit X: \( R_X(t) = e^{-\lambda_X t} = e^{-t/MTBF_X} \) \[ R_X(1000) = e^{-1000/20000} = e^{-1/20} = e^{-0.05} \approx 0.9512 \]
  • Reliability of Unit Y: \( R_Y(t) = e^{-\lambda_Y t} = e^{-t/MTBF_Y} \) \[ R_Y(1000) = e^{-1000/100000} = e^{-1/100} = e^{-0.01} \approx 0.9900 \]

**(ii) Unreliability of each unit for 1,000 hours:**

  • Unreliability of Unit X: \( U_X(t) = 1 - R_X(t) \) \[ U_X(1000) = 1 - 0.9512 = 0.0488 \]
  • Unreliability of Unit Y: \( U_Y(t) = 1 - R_Y(t) \) \[ U_Y(1000) = 1 - 0.9900 = 0.0100 \]

**(iii) Probability of failure of the system for 1,000 hours:**

For a parallel system, the system fails only if both units X and Y fail. Assuming the failures of X and Y are independent events, the probability of system failure (unreliability \(U_{system}\)) is the product of the individual unreliabilities.

\[ U_{system}(t) = U_X(t) \times U_Y(t) \] \[ U_{system}(1000) = U_X(1000) \times U_Y(1000) \approx 0.0488 \times 0.0100 \] \[ U_{system}(1000) \approx 0.000488 \]

**(iv) System reliability for 1,000 hours:**

System reliability \(R_{system}(t)\) is \( 1 - U_{system}(t) \).

\[ R_{system}(1000) = 1 - U_{system}(1000) \approx 1 - 0.000488 \] \[ R_{system}(1000) \approx 0.999512 \]

Alternatively, system reliability for parallel units can be calculated as \( R_{system}(t) = 1 - (1 - R_X(t))(1 - R_Y(t)) \). \[ R_{system}(1000) = 1 - (1 - 0.9512)(1 - 0.9900) = 1 - (0.0488)(0.0100) \] \[ R_{system}(1000) = 1 - 0.000488 = 0.999512 \]

Connecting units in parallel significantly increases system reliability compared to individual unit reliabilities, especially if individual unit reliabilities are high.

Question 4

(a) (i) State three activities that may be carried out in an equipment during routine maintenance. (6 marks)

(ii) Explain preventive maintenance.

(b) With aid of labelled block diagrams, distinguish between convergent and divergent arrangements within an electrical system. (6 marks)

(c) An equipment has a failure rate of \( 0.5 \times 10^{-3} \). The average time to repair any fault in the equipment is 2.5 hours. Determine the: (10 marks)

(i) repair rate;

(ii) mean time between failure;

(iii) availability;

(iv) maintainability for a time of 6 hours.

Answer

This question covers routine and preventive maintenance, convergent and divergent system diagrams, and calculations of maintainability, availability, and related metrics.

(a) Routine and Preventive Maintenance (6 marks):

**(i) Three activities during routine maintenance:**

  • Cleaning equipment surfaces and components to remove dust and debris.
  • Lubricating moving parts such as fans, motors, or hinges.
  • Visually inspecting components and connections for signs of damage, wear, or overheating.
  • Checking and tightening electrical connections.
  • Testing basic operational functions and performance parameters.
  • Updating software or firmware if applicable.

**(ii) Explain preventive maintenance:**

Preventive maintenance is a type of planned maintenance performed at predetermined intervals or according to specified criteria (e.g., time-based, usage-based) while the equipment is still functioning normally. The goal is to prevent future failures or performance degradation by identifying and addressing potential issues early on, or by replacing components before they reach the end of their expected lifespan. Examples include scheduled inspections, cleaning, lubrication, adjustments, and periodic replacement of wear-prone parts like filters, belts, or batteries.

(b) Convergent and Divergent Systems (6 marks):

These terms describe how signals or processes flow through a system.

Divergent System:

       Input
         |
         o---+--> Output 1
         |
         +--> Output 2
         |
         +--> Output 3

**Description:** In a divergent system, a single input branches out to produce multiple outputs or feed multiple subsequent stages. Information or energy flows from one point outward to several points. Example: A power supply distributing power to multiple circuits, a single sensor feeding data to several monitoring systems.

Convergent System:

       Input 1 ---+
                  |
       Input 2 ---+--> Output
                  |
       Input 3 ---+

**Description:** In a convergent system, multiple inputs are combined or processed to produce a single output. Information or energy flows from several points inward to a single point. Example: An audio mixer combining signals from multiple microphones into a single output, a control system receiving feedback from multiple sensors to adjust a single actuator, a logic gate combining multiple inputs to produce one output.

(c) Equipment Reliability and Maintainability Calculations (10 marks):

Given information about an equipment's failure rate and average repair time.

Given:

  • Failure rate \( \lambda = 0.5 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{failures/hour} \).
  • Average time to repair (MTTR) = 2.5 hours.

**(i) repair rate:**

The repair rate (\(\mu\)) is the reciprocal of the Mean Time To Repair (MTTR).

\[ \mu = \frac{1}{MTTR} = \frac{1}{2.5 \, \text{hours}} = 0.4 \, \text{repairs/hour} \]

**(ii) mean time between failure:**

MTBF is the reciprocal of the failure rate (\(\lambda\)), assuming a constant failure rate.

\[ MTBF = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{0.5 \times 10^{-3}} \, \text{hours/failure} = \frac{1}{0.0005} \, \text{hours/failure} = 2000 \, \text{hours/failure} \]

**(iii) availability:**

Availability (\(A\)) is the long-term probability that the equipment is operational. \( A = \frac{MTBF}{MTBF + MTTR} \).

\[ A = \frac{2000 \, \text{hours}}{2000 \, \text{hours} + 2.5 \, \text{hours}} = \frac{2000}{2002.5} \] \[ A \approx 0.99875 \]

**(iv) maintainability for a time of 6 hours:**

Maintainability \(M(t)\) is the probability that a failed equipment is repaired within time \(t\). Assuming exponential repair times, \(M(t) = 1 - e^{-t/\text{MTTR}}\) or \(M(t) = 1 - e^{-\mu t}\).

Here, \(t = 6\) hours and \(MTTR = 2.5\) hours (\(\mu = 0.4\) repairs/hour).

\[ M(6) = 1 - e^{-6 \, \text{hours} / 2.5 \, \text{hours}} = 1 - e^{-2.4} \] \[ M(6) \approx 1 - 0.09072 \] \[ M(6) \approx 0.90928 \]

Understanding maintenance strategies, system flow, and quantifying reliability and maintainability are essential for equipment management.

Question 5

(a) (i) State the purpose for each of the following tools: (6 marks)

I. tweezers;

II. solder sucker;

III. crimping tool.

(ii) Outline the procedure of testing a resistor using a digital multimeter. (6 marks)

(b) State two failures that can occur in each of the following components and their possible causes: (6 marks)

(i) ceramic capacitors;

(ii) aluminum electrolytic capacitors.

(c) A sinusoidal voltage waveform displayed on an oscilloscope occupies a peak height of 3 cm . If the vertical sensitivity setting is \( 20 \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{cm} \), determine the r.m.s value of the voltage. (7 marks)

Answer

This question covers common electronic maintenance tools, component testing, typical component failures and their causes, and calculating voltage from oscilloscope display parameters.

(a) Purpose of Tools and Resistor Testing (12 marks):

**(i) Purpose for each tool:**

  • **I. Tweezers:** Small pincer-like tools used for picking up, holding, and positioning small components (like surface-mount devices), wires, or other delicate items in electronic assembly, repair, or handling.
  • **II. Solder sucker (Desoldering pump):** A tool used to remove molten solder from a solder joint by creating a vacuum. Essential for desoldering components from a PCB or cleaning up excess solder.
  • **III. Crimping tool:** A tool used to join a connector to the end of a wire by deforming (crimping) the connector around the wire. This creates a secure electrical and mechanical connection without soldering. Used for making wire terminals or attaching connectors to cables.

**(ii) Procedure of testing a resistor using a digital multimeter (DMM):**

  1. Ensure the resistor is disconnected from the circuit and power is removed. Resistance measurements should ideally be performed on components isolated from the rest of the circuit to avoid measuring parallel paths.
  2. Set the digital multimeter to the resistance measurement function (\(\Omega\)). Select an appropriate range (if manual ranging) that is higher than the expected resistance value. Most DMMs have auto-ranging.
  3. Touch the probes of the multimeter firmly to the two leads of the resistor.
  4. Read the resistance value displayed on the DMM screen. Compare this reading to the resistor's marked value (using color codes or markings, considering tolerance).
  5. A resistor is considered good if the measured value is within its specified tolerance range of the marked value. An open resistor will show a very high or "OL" (Over Limit) reading. A shorted resistor will show a very low resistance, close to 0 \(\Omega\) (this is rare for standard resistors unless physically damaged or burnt).

(b) Common Component Failures and Causes (6 marks):

**(i) Ceramic capacitors:**

  • **Short Circuit:** The dielectric material breaks down. Possible causes: excessive voltage (exceeding voltage rating), physical stress (cracking the ceramic), manufacturing defect.
  • **Reduced Capacitance:** Degradation of the dielectric layer. Possible causes: aging, temperature stress, voltage stress.
  • **Physical cracking:** Can lead to open circuits or unpredictable behavior. Possible causes: thermal shock (rapid temperature changes during soldering), excessive mechanical stress, physical impact.

**(ii) Aluminum electrolytic capacitors:**

  • **Open Circuit:** Loss of electrolyte or internal connection failure. Possible causes: drying out of the electrolyte (common with age and high temperature), corrosion of leads, internal fuse activation (some types), physical damage. Often accompanied by bulging or venting.
  • **Short Circuit:** Dielectric breakdown. Possible causes: reverse voltage applied (polarized type), excessive ripple current causing overheating, overvoltage, manufacturing defect.
  • **Increased Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR):** Electrolyte degradation increases internal resistance. Possible causes: aging, overheating, excessive ripple current. Leads to reduced filtering effectiveness and can cause circuit malfunctions.

(c) Oscilloscope Voltage Measurement (7 marks):

An oscilloscope displays voltage on its vertical axis. The vertical sensitivity setting determines how many volts are represented by each division on the screen's vertical graticule.

Given:

  • Peak height of sinusoidal waveform displayed = 3 cm.
  • Vertical sensitivity = \(20 \, \mathrm{V/cm}\).

The peak height measured is from the center (zero axis) to the peak of the waveform. This represents the peak voltage (\(V_{peak}\)).

\[ V_{peak} = \text{Peak height (in cm)} \times \text{Vertical Sensitivity (V/cm)} \] \[ V_{peak} = 3 \, \mathrm{cm} \times 20 \, \mathrm{V/cm} = 60 \, \mathrm{V} \]

For a sinusoidal waveform, the RMS value (\(V_{rms}\)) is related to the peak value by \( V_{rms} = \frac{V_{peak}}{\sqrt{2}} \).

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{60 \, V}{\sqrt{2}} \approx \frac{60}{1.4142} \, V \approx 42.426 \, V \]

The RMS value of the voltage is approximately 42.426 V.

This question covers practical electronic maintenance skills, knowledge of component failures, and interpreting measurements from an oscilloscope.

Question 6

(a) (i) Define each of the following with respect to semi conductors: (4 marks)

I. hole;

II. forbidden gap;

(a) Semiconductor Definitions and Avalanche Breakdown (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to semiconductors:**

  • **I. Hole:** A vacancy or absence of an electron in a covalent bond within the crystal structure of a semiconductor. It behaves as if it were a positive charge carrier with a mobility. Holes are the majority carriers in p-type semiconductors and minority carriers in n-type semiconductors.
  • **II. Forbidden gap:** The range of energy levels that electrons cannot occupy in a solid material according to quantum mechanics. It is the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. The size of the forbidden gap (\(E_g\)) determines whether a material is a conductor, semiconductor, or insulator.
  • **III. Dour atom:** This term is likely a typo in the OCR. Based on the context of doping semiconductors (making them extrinsic), the intended term is probably **Donor atom**. A donor atom is an impurity atom added to an intrinsic semiconductor that has one or more valence electrons more than the semiconductor atom (e.g., a pentavalent impurity in silicon). These extra electrons are loosely bound and become free electrons, contributing to conduction.

**(ii) Explain avalanche breakdown with respect to p-n junctions (6 marks):**

Avalanche breakdown is a mechanism of conduction that can occur in a reverse-biased p-n junction when the reverse voltage becomes sufficiently high (at or above the breakdown voltage \(V_{BR}\)).

**Explanation:** When a reverse bias voltage is applied, a strong electric field exists across the depletion region. Minority carriers (electrons on the p-side, holes on the n-side) that are thermally generated are accelerated by this field. As they gain energy, they collide with atoms in the crystal lattice. If the energy gained is sufficient, these collisions can break covalent bonds, generating new electron-hole pairs (impact ionization). The newly created carriers are also accelerated by the field and cause further impact ionization collisions, leading to a cumulative multiplication effect (avalanche effect). When the reverse voltage reaches \(V_{BR}\), the avalanche process becomes self-sustaining, causing a rapid and significant increase in the reverse current.

Answer

This question covers thermionic emission concepts, calculations for electron beam deflection in an electrostatic CRT, and the operation and waveforms of a full-wave bridge rectifier.

(a) Thermionic Emission Definitions (4 marks):

  • **(i) Space charge:** The cloud of electrons emitted from a heated cathode in a vacuum tube that accumulates near the cathode surface. This negative charge cloud repels subsequently emitted electrons and limits the current flow to the anode for a given anode voltage.
  • **(ii) Work function:** The minimum amount of energy required for an electron to escape from the surface of a solid material into the surrounding vacuum. This energy is typically measured in electron volts (eV) and is a property of the material.

(b) Electrostatic CRT Deflection Calculations (10 marks):

Calculations for an electron beam accelerated by the accelerating potential and deflected by the voltage applied to the deflecting plates.

Given:

  • Deflecting plates length \(l_d = 1.5 \, cm = 0.015 \, m\).
  • Deflecting plates separation \(d = 6 \, mm = 0.006 \, m\).
  • Screen distance from center of plates \(L = 55 \, cm = 0.55 \, m\).
  • Deflecting potential \(V_d = 150 \, V\).
  • Accelerating potential \(V_a = 2.5 \, kV = 2500 \, V\).
  • Electron charge \(e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C\).
  • Electron mass \(m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg\).

Assume the electron starts with zero initial velocity.

**(i) Velocity of electrons:**

Horizontal velocity (\(v_x\)) after acceleration by \(V_a\): \( \frac{1}{2} m v_x^2 = e V_a \).

\[ v_x = \sqrt{\frac{2 e V_a}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2500 \, V)}{9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg}} \] \[ v_x = \sqrt{\frac{8 \times 10^{-16}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{0.87912 \times 10^{15}} = \sqrt{8.7912 \times 10^{14}} \] \[ v_x \approx 2.965 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s} \]

**(ii) Amount of deflection:**

This refers to the vertical deflection on the screen (Y). The formula for total deflection is \( Y = \frac{V_d l_d L}{2 V_a d} \).

\[ Y = \frac{150 \, V \times 0.015 \, m \times 0.55 \, m}{2 \times 2500 \, V \times 0.006 \, m} \] \[ Y = \frac{150 \times 0.015 \times 0.55}{2 \times 2500 \times 0.006} \, m = \frac{1.2375}{30} \, m = 0.04125 \, m \]

Convert to cm: \( Y = 0.04125 \, m \times 100 \, cm/m = 4.125 \, cm \).

**(iii) Deflection sensitivity of the tube:**

Sensitivity (\(S\)) is the deflection per unit deflection voltage (\(S = Y / V_d\)). Express in mm/V.

\[ Y = 4.125 \, cm = 41.25 \, mm \] \[ S = \frac{41.25 \, mm}{150 \, V} = 0.275 \, \mathrm{mm/V} \]

**(iv) Deflection factor of the tube:**

Deflection factor (\(D\)) is the reciprocal of sensitivity (\(D = 1/S\)). Express in V/mm.

\[ D = \frac{1}{S} = \frac{150 \, V}{41.25 \, mm} \approx 3.636 \, \mathrm{V/mm} \]

This question covers fundamental semiconductor definitions, atomic models, diode characteristics, and calculations related to current and power in a semiconductor material.

(a) Semiconductor Definitions (4 marks):

  • **(i) Ionization potential:** The minimum energy required to remove an electron from an atom. In semiconductors, this relates to the energy needed to break a covalent bond and create a free electron and a hole, or to ionize a donor/acceptor impurity atom. (Same as Q6a(i) July 2017)
  • **(ii) Covalent bonding:** A type of chemical bond where atoms share valence electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration.   • **(iii) Doping:** The process of intentionally adding impurity atoms (either donor or acceptor impurities) to an intrinsic (pure) semiconductor material to change its electrical conductivity and create either n-type or p-type semiconductors.

(b) Rutherford's atomic model (4 marks):

Rutherford's atomic model, based on alpha particle scattering experiments, proposed that an atom consists of:

  • A tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center.
  • Electrons orbiting the nucleus at a relatively large distance.
  • The atom is mostly empty space.
  • The positive charge of the nucleus equals the total negative charge of the electrons, making the atom neutral.
