Question 1

(a) (i) Describe the following standards with respect to measurements: (4 marks)

(I) secondary;

(II) working.

(ii) A standard cell has a voltage rating of 1.018500 V and internal resistance of \( 500 \Omega \). If the insulation resistance between its terminals is \( 5 \mathrm{M} \Omega \), determine the: (4 marks)

(I) current drain due to insulation resistance;

(II) difference between the rated voltage and the drop due to internal resistance.

(b) Derive the dimensional equation of "work done" in MLT system of units. (4 marks)

(c) A voltmeter having a sensitivity of \( 1,000 \Omega / \mathrm{V} \) reads 100 V on its 150 V scale when connected across an unknown resistor in series with a milliammeter. Neglecting the milliammeter resistance, determine the percentage error due to the loading effect of the voltmeter when the milliammeter reads 5 mA . (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers standards of measurement, calculation involving a standard cell, dimensional analysis for work, and voltmeter loading effect calculation.

(a) Standards of Measurement and Standard Cell (8 marks):

**(i) Standards of measurement descriptions:**

  • **(I) Secondary Standards:** These are standards whose values are determined by comparing them with primary standards. They are maintained by national standards laboratories and are used for calibrating working standards. They are highly accurate but less accurate than primary standards.
  • **(II) Working Standards:** These are standards used in everyday laboratory measurements and calibrations. They are calibrated against secondary standards. They are less accurate than secondary standards but are practical for routine use.

**(ii) Standard cell calculations:**

A standard cell is designed to provide a very stable and precise reference voltage. However, it has some internal resistance and insulation resistance, which can cause a slight voltage drop under load or current drain.

Given:

  • Rated voltage (\(V_{rated}\)) = 1.018500 V
  • Internal resistance (\(R_{int}\)) = \(500 \Omega\)
  • Insulation resistance (\(R_{ins}\)) = \(5 \, M\Omega = 5 \times 10^6 \, \Omega\)

**(I) Current drain due to insulation resistance:**

Insulation resistance is in parallel with the load (or effectively across the terminals if no load is connected). The rated voltage is across this insulation resistance, causing a small leakage current.

\[ I_{leakage} = \frac{V_{rated}}{R_{ins}} = \frac{1.018500 \, V}{5 \times 10^6 \, \Omega} = 0.2037 \times 10^{-6} \, A = 0.2037 \, \mu A \]

The current drain due to insulation resistance is \(0.2037 \, \mu A\).

**(II) Difference between the rated voltage and the drop due to internal resistance:**

The voltage drop due to internal resistance occurs when there is current flowing *out* of the cell. This current is the sum of any load current and the leakage current calculated above. If no external load is connected, the only current flowing out is the leakage current \(I_{leakage}\).

\[ V_{drop} = I_{leakage} \times R_{int} = (0.2037 \times 10^{-6} \, A) \times (500 \, \Omega) \] \[ V_{drop} = 101.85 \times 10^{-6} \, V = 0.10185 \, mV \]

The terminal voltage of the cell, with only the insulation resistance as load, is \( V_{terminal} = V_{rated} - V_{drop} = 1.018500 \, V - 0.00010185 \, V = 1.01839815 \, V \).

The difference between the rated voltage and the drop due to internal resistance is \( V_{drop} = 0.10185 \, mV \).

(b) Dimensional equation of "work done" in MLT system (4 marks):

Work is defined as Force times Distance (\(W = F \times d\)). In the MLT system, Force (F) has dimensions of Mass (M) times Acceleration (L/T²). So, the dimensions of force are \([MLT^{-2}]\).

\[ [\text{Work}] = [\text{Force}] \times [\text{Distance}] \] \[ [\text{Work}] = [MLT^{-2}] \times [L] \] \[ [\text{Work}] = [ML^2T^{-2}] \]

The dimensional equation for work done is \( [ML^2T^{-2}] \).

(c) Percentage error due to voltmeter loading effect (8 marks):

When a voltmeter is connected in parallel with a resistor to measure its voltage, its internal resistance reduces the total resistance of the parallel combination, affecting the current distribution in the circuit and thus the measured voltage (loading effect).

Given:

  • Voltmeter Sensitivity = \(1000 \Omega/V\)
  • Voltmeter Scale = 150 V
  • Voltmeter Reading (\(V_m\)) = 100 V
  • Milliammeter Reading (\(I_m\)) = 5 mA = \(5 \times 10^{-3} \, A\)
  • Milliammeter resistance is neglected.

Internal resistance of the voltmeter (\(R_v\)) = Sensitivity \(\times\) Full Scale Voltage = \(1000 \, \Omega/V \times 150 \, V = 150,000 \, \Omega = 150 \, k\Omega\).

The milliammeter measures the total current entering the parallel combination of the unknown resistor \(R_x\) and the voltmeter. This total current is \(I_m = 5 \, mA\). The voltmeter measures the voltage \(V_m = 100 \, V\) across this parallel combination.

The apparent resistance (\(R_{apparent}\)) being measured is the resistance of the parallel combination of \(R_x\) and \(R_v\), calculated from the meter readings:

\[ R_{apparent} = \frac{V_m}{I_m} = \frac{100 \, V}{5 \times 10^{-3} \, A} = \frac{100}{0.005} \, \Omega = 20,000 \, \Omega = 20 \, k\Omega \]

This \(R_{apparent}\) is the equivalent resistance of \(R_x\) in parallel with \(R_v\).

\[ R_{apparent} = \frac{R_x R_v}{R_x + R_v} \]

Solve for the actual unknown resistance \(R_x\):

\[ 20 \, k\Omega = \frac{R_x \times 150 \, k\Omega}{R_x + 150 \, k\Omega} \] \[ 20 (R_x + 150) = 150 R_x \quad (\text{dropping } k\Omega) \] \[ 20 R_x + 3000 = 150 R_x \] \[ 3000 = 130 R_x \] \[ R_x = \frac{3000}{130} = \frac{300}{13} \approx 23.077 \, k\Omega \]

The percentage error due to loading is typically calculated as the difference between the actual value and the measured (apparent) value, relative to the actual value.

\[ \% \text{Error} = \frac{R_x - R_{apparent}}{R_x} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Error} = \frac{23.077 \, k\Omega - 20 \, k\Omega}{23.077 \, k\Omega} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Error} = \frac{3.077}{23.077} \times 100\% \approx 13.33\% \]

The voltmeter loading effect causes the measured resistance to be 13.33% lower than the actual resistance.

This question tests knowledge of measurement standards, basic electrical calculations involving standard sources and internal resistances, dimensional analysis, and the practical impact of instrument loading on measurement accuracy.

Question 2

(a) (i) State one source of error in wattmeters when used in measurements. (6 marks)

(ii) With the aid of a labelled block diagram, describe the heterodyne method of frequency measurement.

(b) A solenoid, 1.2 m long, is uniformly wound with 800 turns. A search coil of mean diameter 30 mm , wound with 50 turns, is placed at the centre of the solenoid and connected to a ballistic galvanometer. The total resistance of the search-coil circuit is \( 2000 \Omega \). When a current of 5 A through the solenoid is reversed, the deflection on the galvanometer scale is 85 divisions. (14 marks)

Taking \( \mu_{0}=4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{H} / \mathrm{m} \), determine the ballistic constant of the galvanometer in coulombs/unit deflection.

Answer

This question covers sources of error in wattmeters, a method of frequency measurement, and magnetic flux measurement using a ballistic galvanometer.

(a) Wattmeter Error and Heterodyne Frequency Measurement (6 marks):

**(i) One source of error in wattmeters:**

  • **Pressure coil inductance error:** In AC circuits, the pressure coil (voltage coil) of a wattmeter is designed to be as resistive as possible, but it always has some inductance. This inductance causes a small phase shift between the voltage across the pressure coil and the voltage of the circuit, leading to an error in the power measurement, especially at low power factors or high frequencies.
  • Other errors include: eddy current errors, stray magnetic field errors, friction in the moving system, temperature errors, errors due to connections (connecting pressure coil on load or supply side).

**(ii) Heterodyne method of frequency measurement with diagram:**

The heterodyne method (or beat frequency method) measures an unknown frequency by mixing it with a known, adjustable frequency from a variable frequency oscillator (VFO). When the two frequencies are equal, a "zero beat" condition is detected.

+-----------------+    +-------+    +---------------+    +-----------+
|   Input Signal  |--->| Mixer |--->| Low Pass Filter |--->| Amplifier |
| (Unknown freq)  |    +-------+    +---------------+    +-----------+
+-----------------+        ^                                  |
                           |                                  |
+---------------------+    |    +-----------------+    +-----------------+
| Variable Frequency  |----+----| Frequency Meter/|--->| Zero Beat       |
| Oscillator (VFO)    |         | Calibrated Dial |    | Indicator (e.g.,|
| (Known freq)        |         +-----------------+    | Headphones/Meter)|
+---------------------+                                  +-----------------+

**Description:**

  • **Input Signal:** The unknown frequency (\(f_{unknown}\)) is applied as one input to the mixer.
  • **Variable Frequency Oscillator (VFO):** Generates a signal with a known frequency (\(f_{VFO}\)) that can be precisely adjusted over a range. This frequency is applied as the second input to the mixer.
  • **Mixer:** This is a non-linear circuit that combines the two input frequencies (\(f_{unknown}\) and \(f_{VFO}\)). Due to the non-linearity, the output of the mixer contains signals at the sum (\(f_{unknown} + f_{VFO}\)) and difference (\(|f_{unknown} - f_{VFO}|\)) of the input frequencies, as well as the original frequencies and harmonics.
  • **Low Pass Filter:** Filters out the sum frequency and higher frequency components, passing only the difference frequency (\(f_{beat} = |f_{unknown} - f_{VFO}|\)).
  • **Amplifier:** Amplifies the beat frequency signal.
  • **Zero Beat Indicator:** A detector (like headphones, a meter, or an oscilloscope) used to indicate when the beat frequency is zero or very low.

The VFO frequency (\(f_{VFO}\)) is adjusted until the beat frequency (\(f_{beat}\)) goes to zero or a minimum detectable level (the "zero beat"). When this occurs, the unknown frequency \(f_{unknown}\) is equal to the VFO frequency \(f_{VFO}\). The unknown frequency is then read from the calibrated dial of the VFO.

(b) Magnetic Flux Measurement and Ballistic Constant (14 marks):

This problem involves calculating the ballistic constant of a galvanometer used with a search coil to measure the change in magnetic flux produced by reversing the current in a solenoid.

Given:

  • Solenoid: Length \(l = 1.2 \, m\), \(N_{sol} = 800\) turns.
  • Search coil: Mean diameter \(D_s = 30 \, mm = 0.03 \, m\). Radius \(r_s = D_s/2 = 0.015 \, m\). Mean area \(A_s = \pi r_s^2 = \pi (0.015 \, m)^2 = \pi \times 2.25 \times 10^{-4} \, m^2 \). \(N_s = 50\) turns.
  • Search-coil circuit total resistance \(R_{total} = 2000 \, \Omega\).
  • Current in solenoid \(I = 5 \, A\), which is reversed (change is \(2I\)).
  • Galvanometer deflection \(\theta = 85\) divisions.
  • Permeability of free space \( \mu_{0}=4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{H/m} \).

The magnetic field \(H\) at the center of a long solenoid is \( H = \frac{N_{sol} I}{l} \). The flux density \(B\) inside the solenoid (assuming air core, i.e., permeability \(\mu_0\)) is \( B = \mu_0 H = \mu_0 \frac{N_{sol} I}{l} \).

\[ B = \mu_0 \frac{N_{sol} I}{l} \] \[ B = (4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \mathrm{H/m}) \times \frac{800 \text{ turns} \times 5 \, \mathrm{A}}{1.2 \, \mathrm{m}} \] \[ B = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times \frac{4000}{1.2} \quad \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \] \[ B = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 3333.33 \quad \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \] \[ B \approx 13333.32\pi \times 10^{-7} \approx 0.001333\pi \approx 0.004189 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \]

When the current in the solenoid is reversed, the flux density changes from \(B\) to \(-B\), a total change of \( \Delta B = 2B \).

\[ \Delta B = 2 \times (0.004189 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2}) \approx 0.008378 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2} \]

The change in flux through the search coil is \( \Delta\Phi = \Delta B \times A_s \).

\[ \Delta\Phi = (0.008378 \, \mathrm{Wb/m^2}) \times (\pi \times 2.25 \times 10^{-4} \, \mathrm{m}^2) \] \[ \Delta\Phi \approx 0.008378 \times 3.14159 \times 2.25 \times 10^{-4} \, \mathrm{Wb} \] \[ \Delta\Phi \approx 5.923 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb} \]

The total change in flux linkage (\(\Delta\Psi\)) with the search coil is the change in flux multiplied by the number of turns in the search coil:

\[ \Delta\Psi = N_s \times \Delta\Phi = 50 \text{ turns} \times (5.923 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb}) \] \[ \Delta\Psi = 296.15 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns} \approx 296.15 \, \mu\mathrm{Wb-turns} \]

When this change in flux linkage occurs, it induces a voltage in the search coil. The integral of this voltage over time gives the total charge \(Q\) that flows through the ballistic galvanometer circuit:

\[ Q = \frac{\Delta\Psi}{R_{total}} \] \[ Q = \frac{296.15 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{Wb-turns}}{2000 \, \Omega} \approx 0.148075 \times 10^{-6} \, C = 0.148075 \, \mu C \]

The ballistic galvanometer deflection \(\theta\) is proportional to the charge \(Q\): \(Q = K \theta\), where \(K\) is the ballistic constant in C/division.

\[ K = \frac{Q}{\theta} \] \[ K = \frac{0.148075 \times 10^{-6} \, C}{85 \text{ divisions}} \] \[ K \approx 0.001742 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{C/division} = 1.742 \times 10^{-9} \, \mathrm{C/division} \]

The ballistic constant of the galvanometer is approximately \(1.742 \, \mathrm{nC/division}\).

This question involves understanding errors in power measurement, a specific method for frequency measurement, and performing calculations for magnetic flux density and galvanometer calibration.

Question 3

(a) (i) Define reliability with respect to engineering systems. (6 marks)

(ii) Sketch, on the same axis, the curves showing the manufacturing cost against reliability and explain the shape of each curve.

(b) Outline the procedure of assessing the reliability of an equipment. (8 marks)

(c) In a test of 400 diodes, 5 failed over a 1,000 hour period. For the diodes, determine the: (6 marks)

(i) failure rate;

(ii) mean time to fail.

Answer

This question focuses on equipment reliability, the relationship between cost and reliability, the process of reliability assessment, and calculating reliability metrics from test data.

(a) Reliability Definition and Cost Curves (6 marks):

**(i) Definition of reliability:**

  • Reliability, with respect to engineering systems, is the probability that the system will perform its intended function without failure for a specified period of time under specified operating conditions. It quantifies the likelihood of successful operation over time.

**(ii) Sketch and explanation of manufacturing cost vs. reliability:**

The manufacturing cost generally increases significantly as the required reliability increases. To achieve higher reliability, manufacturers must use higher quality components, implement more stringent manufacturing processes, perform more thorough testing and quality control checks, and potentially employ redundancy in the design. These measures all add to the cost of production.

Manufacturing Cost
      ^
      |
      |      /
      |     /
      |    /
      |   /
      |  /
      +----------------> Reliability
      0%               100%

The curve showing manufacturing cost versus reliability typically slopes upwards and is convex. This means that achieving small improvements in reliability becomes increasingly expensive as the reliability level gets higher, especially when approaching 100% reliability, where costs can rise very sharply.

Note: The question asks for "manufacturing cost against reliability" and "each curve". This implies there might be other cost curves expected here, such as design and development cost or the total cost curve (including maintenance), which is commonly shown alongside manufacturing cost when discussing the economics of reliability (as seen in Q4b of July 2014 paper). Assuming the question might implicitly expect the total cost perspective or at least one other cost like design/development, the explanation would be similar – these costs also increase with target reliability.

(b) Procedure of assessing the reliability of an equipment (8 marks):

Reliability assessment is a process to estimate or predict the reliability of an equipment based on data and analysis. The procedure can involve several steps:

  1. **Define System Boundaries and Function:** Clearly define what constitutes the equipment and its intended function, as well as what is considered a failure.
  2. **Identify Stress Levels and Operating Conditions:** Specify the environmental (temperature, humidity, vibration) and operational (voltage, current, duty cycle) conditions under which the equipment will operate.
  3. **Collect Data:** Gather data on component failure rates (from databases, manufacturer specifications, or historical data) or conduct reliability testing on prototype or production units. Record times to failure for individual components or the system.
  4. **Perform Reliability Modeling:** Use reliability block diagrams or other models to represent the system structure (series, parallel, standby redundancy) and how component failures affect system failure.
  5. **Calculate Reliability Metrics:** Based on the collected data and the system model, calculate key reliability metrics such as component failure rates (\(\lambda\)), Mean Time To Failure (MTTF), Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF), and system reliability \(R(t)\) for a specified time \(t\).
  6. **Identify Failure Modes and Effects (FMEA):** Analyze potential ways the equipment can fail and the consequences of these failures. This helps identify critical components or single points of failure.
  7. **Perform Statistical Analysis:** Use statistical methods to analyze failure data, determine the failure rate distribution (e.g., exponential, Weibull), and calculate confidence intervals for reliability estimates.
  8. **Predict System Reliability:** Based on the component reliabilities and the system model, predict the overall reliability of the equipment.
  9. **Report and Document:** Document the reliability assessment process, results, assumptions, and any limitations.

(c) Reliability Calculation from Test Data (6 marks):

In a test of 400 diodes, 5 failed over a period of 1000 hours. This data can be used to calculate the failure rate and MTTF/MTBF for this population of diodes, assuming they are non-repairable or this is the first failure data.

Given:

  • Number of units tested \(N = 400\)
  • Test duration \(T = 1000\) hours
  • Number of failures \(f = 5\)

Total operating time (device-hours) = \(N \times T = 400 \times 1000 = 400,000\) device-hours.

**(i) Failure rate (\(\lambda\)):**

Failure rate is the number of failures per unit of operating time.

\[ \lambda = \frac{\text{Number of Failures}}{\text{Total Operating Time}} = \frac{5 \text{ failures}}{400,000 \text{ device-hours}} \] \[ \lambda = 12.5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ failures/hour} \]

Often expressed per 1000 hours:

\[ \lambda = 12.5 \times 10^{-6} \times 1000 = 0.0125 \text{ failures per 1000 hours} \]

**(ii) Mean time to fail (MTTF):**

MTTF is the average time a unit is expected to function before failure. For a constant failure rate period, MTTF is the reciprocal of the failure rate.

\[ MTTF = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{\text{Total Operating Time}}{\text{Number of Failures}} \] \[ MTTF = \frac{400,000 \text{ hours}}{5 \text{ failures}} = 80,000 \text{ hours/failure} \]

The MTTF for the diodes is 80,000 hours.

Reliability is a key aspect of engineering systems, evaluated through data collection, modeling, and calculation of relevant metrics like failure rate and MTTF/MTBF.

Question 4

(a) (i) State any two possible causes of open circuit fault in carbon composition resistors. (6 marks)

(ii) Figure 1 shows a schematic block diagram of a d.c motor speed control system. Explain the effect of an open circuit fault in the feedback line and how it can be verified.

(b) With the aid of a labelled block diagram incorporating a signal generator and an oscilloscope, describe a test to determine the gain of a faulty audio amplifier. (8 marks)

(c) In an electrical system, the probability \( (\mathrm{P}_{\mathrm{t}}) \) of the system being returned to working condition within a time of 4 hours is \( 86.5 \% \). (6 marks)

Determine the:

(i) mean time to repair;

(ii) failure rate.

Answer

This question covers fault diagnosis in resistors and control systems, testing electronic amplifier gain, and calculating maintainability metrics.

(a) Resistor Faults and Feedback Control System (6 marks):

**(i) Two possible causes of open circuit fault in carbon composition resistors:**

  • **Physical stress:** Excessive bending or pulling of the leads where they connect to the carbon body can cause the internal connection to break.
  • **Overheating:** Prolonged exposure to excessive current or temperature can degrade the carbon material or the connection between the carbon element and the end caps/leads, leading to an open circuit.
  • **Corrosion:** Environmental factors like moisture and contaminants can corrode the leads or end caps, causing the connection to become intermittent or open over time.

**(ii) Effect and verification of an open circuit in the feedback line of a DC motor speed control system:**

Figure 1 shows a closed-loop speed control system where the tachogenerator measures the actual motor speed and provides a feedback signal to the comparator. The comparator compares the feedback signal (representing actual speed) with the input speed reference signal (representing desired speed).

**Effect of open circuit in feedback line:**

If the feedback line from the tachogenerator opens, the comparator receives no feedback signal (or a zero signal, depending on implementation). The comparator will sense a large difference between the input speed reference (desired speed) and the feedback signal (perceived as zero). This large error signal will be amplified by the controller and power amplifier, causing the motor to receive maximum voltage and drive the motor speed to its highest possible value (limited by the power supply and motor characteristics), irrespective of the input speed reference setting. The control loop becomes effectively open, and the system loses regulation.

**How it can be verified:**

  • Measure the voltage signal output from the tachogenerator (the signal in the feedback line). This voltage should be proportional to the actual motor speed. If the line is open, the voltage at the comparator input end of the feedback line will be zero or floating, while the tachogenerator output terminal (if measured directly) might show a voltage depending on the motor speed (if the tachogenerator itself is working).
  • Alternatively, with the system running and the motor spinning at high speed, vary the input speed reference setting. If the motor speed does not change in response to changes in the reference, it indicates the feedback loop is broken or ineffective.
  • Check for continuity of the feedback wiring using a multimeter (with power off).

(b) Testing Audio Amplifier Gain (8 marks):

To determine the voltage gain of an audio amplifier, a known AC signal is applied to its input, and the resulting AC signal at the output is measured. The gain is the ratio of the output voltage to the input voltage.

Block Diagram Set-up:

+-----------------+     +---------------+     +-----------------+
| Signal Generator|---->| Audio         |---->| Oscilloscope    |
| (Known signal)  |     | Amplifier     |     | (Measure V_out) |
+-----------------+     | (Under Test)  |     +-----------------+
    (Measure V_in)      +---------------+
      ^
      |
      +---------------- Measure V_in here

**Procedure:**

  1. **Set up Equipment:** Connect the signal generator output to the input of the audio amplifier. Connect the amplifier's output to the input of the oscilloscope. Ensure all equipment is properly grounded.
  2. **Configure Signal Generator:** Set the signal generator to produce a sinusoidal waveform at a suitable frequency within the amplifier's operating range (e.g., 1 kHz for audio). Set the output amplitude to a small level that is within the amplifier's linear operating range (check specifications if available, or start low and increase gradually to avoid clipping).
  3. **Measure Input Voltage:** Use the oscilloscope to measure the peak-to-peak or RMS voltage of the signal directly at the amplifier's input terminals. Record this value as \(V_{in}\). Adjust the oscilloscope's vertical sensitivity (Volts/Div) and horizontal timebase for a clear view of the waveform.
  4. **Measure Output Voltage:** Move the oscilloscope probe to the amplifier's output terminals. Measure the peak-to-peak or RMS voltage of the output signal. Record this value as \(V_{out}\). Ensure the output waveform is not distorted (clipped), which would indicate the amplifier is overloaded or faulty. If clipping occurs, reduce the input voltage.
  5. **Calculate Gain:** The voltage gain (\(A_v\)) is the ratio of the output voltage to the input voltage. Use consistent units (both peak-to-peak or both RMS). \[ A_v = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \] The gain can also be expressed in decibels (dB): \( A_v(dB) = 20 \log_{10} (A_v) \).
  6. **Repeat (Optional):** Repeat the measurement at different frequencies within the amplifier's bandwidth to assess its frequency response.

(c) Maintainability Calculations (6 marks):

The probability of a system being returned to a working condition within a specified time \(t\) is a measure of its maintainability \(M(t)\). Maintainability is often modeled by an exponential function \(M(t) = 1 - e^{-t/\text{MTTR}}\) or \(M(t) = 1 - e^{-\mu t}\), where MTTR is the Mean Time To Repair, and \(\mu = 1/\text{MTTR}\) is the repair rate.

Given: Probability \(P(t) = M(t) = 86.5\% = 0.865\) within a time \(t = 4\) hours.

The formula is \( M(t) = 1 - e^{-t/\text{MTTR}} \).

\[ 0.865 = 1 - e^{-4/\text{MTTR}} \] \[ e^{-4/\text{MTTR}} = 1 - 0.865 = 0.135 \]

Take the natural logarithm of both sides:

\[ \ln(e^{-4/\text{MTTR}}) = \ln(0.135) \] \[ -4/\text{MTTR} = \ln(0.135) \] \[ -4/\text{MTTR} \approx -2.000 \] \[ 4/\text{MTTR} \approx 2.000 \]

**(i) Mean time to repair (MTTR):**

\[ \text{MTTR} = \frac{4}{2.000} = 2.0 \, \text{hours} \]

**(ii) Failure rate:**

The question asks for "failure rate" in the context of maintainability. Failure rate (\(\lambda\)) is a measure of reliability, not maintainability. Maintainability relates to how quickly a system can be repaired *after* failure. There is no direct way to determine the failure rate (\(\lambda\)) solely from maintainability data (\(M(t)\)). The relationship is within Availability \(A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)\) or \(A = \mu / (\mu + \lambda)\), where \(MTBF = 1/\lambda\) and \(\mu = 1/\text{MTTR}\). To find \(\lambda\), we would need either the MTBF or the Availability. Assuming the question might contain a typo and meant to ask for the *repair rate* (\(\mu\)) instead of failure rate, we can calculate the repair rate:

\[ \text{Repair Rate } (\mu) = \frac{1}{\text{MTTR}} = \frac{1}{2.0 \, \text{hours}} = 0.5 \, \text{repairs/hour} \]
Assuming the question intended to ask for the repair rate, it is 0.5 repairs/hour. If it genuinely intended failure rate, the information provided is insufficient.

Analyzing control system faults, testing amplifier characteristics, and quantifying maintainability through metrics like MTTR and repair rate are important aspects of electrical and electronic engineering practice.

Question 5

(a) (i) Distinguish between instrumental errors and environmental errors with respect to measurements. (6 marks)

(ii) With the aid of a circuit diagram, describe how a full-wave rectifier instrument is used to measure the r.m.s value of an a.c voltage. (6 marks)

(b) (i) With the aid of a phasor diagram, show that the power measured by the twowattmeter method in a 3-phase system gives the total power in the system. Assume the wattmeters are connected in the red and blue phases. (8 marks)

(ii) In the measurement in b (i), the readings on each wattmeter is 5.2 kW and -1.7 kW . Determine the: (4 marks)

(I) total power;

(II) power factor.

Answer

This question covers types of measurement errors, operation of rectifier instruments for AC voltage measurement, and the two-wattmeter method for three-phase power measurement.

(a) Measurement Errors and Rectifier Voltmeter (12 marks):

**(i) Distinction between instrumental and environmental errors:**

  • **Instrumental Errors:** Errors inherent to the measuring instrument itself, arising from its design, manufacturing, calibration, or physical condition. They are related to the instrument's performance limitations (e.g., friction in bearings, inaccurate scale marking, calibration drift).
  • **Environmental Errors:** Errors caused by external conditions and influences from the surroundings, such as changes in temperature, humidity, pressure, vibrations, or external electric and magnetic fields. These conditions affect the instrument's components or the properties of the object being measured.

**(ii) Operation of a full-wave rectifier instrument for AC voltage measurement with diagram (6 marks):**

A moving-coil instrument measures the average value of current. To measure AC voltage, a rectifier circuit is used to convert the AC voltage into a pulsating DC voltage, which drives a current through the moving-coil meter proportional to the average value of the rectified AC voltage. A full-wave rectifier utilizes both positive and negative half-cycles of the input AC waveform.

**Description:**

The instrument consists of a series multiplier resistor (\(R_s\)), a full-wave bridge rectifier (typically four diodes), and a DC moving-coil meter. The AC voltage to be measured is applied across the series combination of \(R_s\) and the bridge rectifier circuit. The bridge rectifier converts the input AC voltage into a pulsating DC voltage across its output terminals. This pulsating DC voltage causes a unidirectional current to flow through the moving-coil meter. The deflection of the meter is proportional to the average value of this current, which in turn is proportional to the average value of the rectified AC voltage.

The scale of the instrument is typically calibrated to read the RMS value of a sine wave, based on the relationship between the average and RMS values for a pure sine wave (e.g., \(V_{RMS} \approx 1.11 V_{Avg}\) for a full-wave rectified sine wave). The multiplier resistor \(R_s\) is used to limit the current through the meter and extend the voltage range, similar to how it's used in a DC voltmeter.

(b) Two-Wattmeter Method for 3-Phase Power (12 marks):

The two-wattmeter method uses two wattmeters to measure the total power in a 3-phase, 3-wire system (balanced or unbalanced load). The current coil of each wattmeter is inserted in series with one line (say, R and B phases), and the voltage coil is connected between that line and the third line (Y phase).

**(i) Phasor diagram and proof that total power is sum of readings:**

Assume a balanced, star-connected load with phase voltages \(V_R, V_Y, V_B\) and phase currents \(I_R, I_Y, I_B\), with the current lagging the voltage by an angle \(\phi\). Line voltages \(V_{RY}, V_{YB}, V_{BR}\).

         I_R
    V_R /   \ V_Y   <-- Phase Voltages (120 deg apart)
       /     \
      /       \
     +---------+
     |   Load  |
     +---------+
      \       /
       \     /
    V_B \   / I_B
         V_B

Wattmeter 1 (W1) in R phase measures current \(I_R\) and voltage \(V_{RY} = V_R - V_Y\). Wattmeter 2 (W2) in B phase measures current \(I_B\) and voltage \(V_{BY} = V_B - V_Y\). (Connections R-Y and B-Y). In some diagrams, it's R-Y and B-Y, or R-B and Y-B. The OCR says "red and blue phases", common connections are R-Y and B-Y or R-B and Y-B. Let's assume R-Y and B-Y.

Using phasor algebra, it can be shown that \(V_{RY}\) leads \(V_R\) by 30°, and \(V_{BY}\) lags \(V_B\) by 30° (for a standard Y connection). The angle between \(I_R\) and \(V_{RY}\) is \( (30^\circ - \phi) \). The angle between \(I_B\) and \(V_{BY}\) is \( (30^\circ + \phi) \).

  • \( P_1 = V_{RY} I_R \cos(30^\circ - \phi) \)
  • \( P_2 = V_{BY} I_B \cos(30^\circ + \phi) \)

For a balanced load, \(|V_{RY}| = |V_{BY}| = V_L\), and \(|I_R| = |I_B| = I_L\). So:

  • \( P_1 = V_L I_L \cos(30^\circ - \phi) \)
  • \( P_2 = V_L I_L \cos(30^\circ + \phi) \)

Sum of readings: \( P_1 + P_2 = V_L I_L [\cos(30^\circ - \phi) + \cos(30^\circ + \phi)] \).

Using the identity \( \cos(A-B) + \cos(A+B) = 2 \cos A \cos B \):

\[ P_1 + P_2 = V_L I_L [2 \cos 30^\circ \cos \phi] = V_L I_L [2 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \cos \phi] = \sqrt{3} V_L I_L \cos \phi \]

This is the total power in a balanced 3-phase system.

For unbalanced loads, instantaneous power \( p_R = v_R i_R \), \( p_Y = v_Y i_Y \), \( p_B = v_B i_B \). Total instantaneous power \( p_{total} = p_R + p_Y + p_B \). The instantaneous power measured by W1 is \( p_1 = v_{RY} i_R \). The instantaneous power measured by W2 is \( p_2 = v_{BY} i_B \). Using Kirchhoff's Current Law at point Y (\(i_R + i_Y + i_B = 0 \implies i_Y = -(i_R + i_B)\)) and line voltage relations, it can be shown that \( p_1 + p_2 = p_R + p_Y + p_B = p_{total} \). Since the average of the sum equals the sum of the averages, \( P_1 + P_2 = P_{total} \) for both balanced and unbalanced 3-wire loads.

  • **(II) Power factor:** Using \( \tan \phi = \sqrt{3} \frac{P_1 - P_2}{P_1 + P_2} \): \[ \tan \phi = \sqrt{3} \frac{5.2 - (-1.7)}{5.2 + (-1.7)} = \sqrt{3} \frac{6.9}{3.5} \approx 1.732 \times 1.9714 \approx 3.413 \] \[ \phi = \arctan(3.413) \approx 73.65^\circ \] Power factor \( \cos \phi = \cos(73.65^\circ) \approx 0.2815 \). **(I) Insulator:**

**(II) Semiconductor:**

(b) Thermionic Emission and CRT Calculations (14 marks):

**(i) Explanation of thermionic emission as applied to metals (4 marks):**

Thermionic emission is the release of electrons from the surface of a heated metal. Metals have many free electrons in their conduction band. These electrons are held within the metal by a potential barrier at the surface. When the metal is heated to a sufficiently high temperature, some of the free electrons gain enough thermal energy to overcome this surface barrier (work function) and escape from the metal into the surrounding space (vacuum or gas). The higher the temperature and the lower the work function of the metal, the greater the rate of thermionic emission.

**(ii) Electrostatic CRT Calculations (10 marks):**

Calculations for electron beam deflection in an electrostatic CRT involve the velocity of electrons accelerated by the anode voltage and their deflection by the electric field between the deflection plates.

Given:

  • Final anode voltage \(V_a = 2 \, kV = 2000 \, V\).
  • Deflection plates: Length \(l_d = 2 \, cm = 0.02 \, m\), separation \(d = 5 \, mm = 0.005 \, m\).
  • Screen distance from center of plates \(L = 30 \, cm = 0.3 \, m\).
  • Deflection voltage \(V_d = 100 \, V\).
  • Electronic charge \(e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, C\) (using the value from the 2023j paper for consistency, though 1.6e-19C is common).
  • Electron mass \(m = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, kg\) (using the value from the 2023j paper for consistency).

First, calculate the velocity (\(v_x\)) of the electrons after being accelerated by the anode voltage \(V_a\). The kinetic energy gained equals the potential energy lost: \( \frac{1}{2} m v_x^2 = e V_a \).

\[ v_x = \sqrt{\frac{2 e V_a}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, C) \times (2000 \, V)}{9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, kg}} \] \[ v_x = \sqrt{\frac{6.408 \times 10^{-16}}{9.109 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{0.70359 \times 10^{15}} = \sqrt{7.0359 \times 10^{14}} \] \[ v_x \approx 2.6525 \times 10^7 \, \mathrm{m/s} \]

While passing through the deflection plates, the electron experiences a constant electric field in the y-direction \( E_y = V_d / d \). This causes an acceleration \( a_y = \frac{e E_y}{m} = \frac{e V_d}{m d} \). The time spent between the plates is \( t_d = l_d / v_x \).

The vertical velocity gained is \( v_y = a_y t_d = \frac{e V_d}{m d} \times \frac{l_d}{v_x} \).

The vertical displacement as the electron leaves the plates is \( y_1 = \frac{1}{2} a_y t_d^2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{e V_d}{m d} \left(\frac{l_d}{v_x}\right)^2 \).

After leaving the plates, the electron travels in a straight line at an angle \(\alpha\) to the horizontal, where \( \tan \alpha = v_y / v_x \). This line appears to originate from the center of the deflection plates (at \(x = l_d/2\)). The total deflection on the screen (at \(x = L + l_d/2\)) is \(Y\).

\[ Y = \tan \alpha \times L + y_1 \]

A simpler formula for the total deflection \(Y\) on the screen is often used, assuming \(y_1\) is negligible compared to the deflection caused by the angle \( \tan \alpha \times L \):

\[ Y \approx \tan \alpha \times L = \frac{v_y}{v_x} \times L = \left(\frac{e V_d l_d}{m d v_x}\right) / v_x \times L = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{m d v_x^2} \]

Substitute \( v_x^2 = \frac{2 e V_a}{m} \):

\[ Y = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{m d (2 e V_a / m)} = \frac{e V_d l_d L}{2 e V_a d} = \frac{V_d l_d L}{2 V_a d} \]

**(I) Maximum deflection, in cm:**

Using the formula for \(Y\):

\[ Y = \frac{100 \, V \times 0.02 \, m \times 0.3 \, m}{2 \times 2000 \, V \times 0.005 \, m} = \frac{100 \times 0.02 \times 0.3}{2 \times 2000 \times 0.005} \, m \] \[ Y = \frac{0.6}{20} \, m = 0.03 \, m \]

Convert to cm: \( Y = 0.03 \, m \times 100 \, cm/m = 3 \, cm \).

**(II) Sensitivity of CRT, in \( \mathrm{mm} / \mathrm{V} \):**

Sensitivity is the deflection per unit deflection voltage (\(S = Y / V_d\)). Use the deflection in mm.

\[ Y = 3 \, cm = 30 \, mm \] \[ S = \frac{Y}{V_d} = \frac{30 \, mm}{100 \, V} = 0.3 \, \mathrm{mm/V} \]

**(III) Deflection factor, in \( \mathrm{V} / \mathrm{mm} \):**

Deflection factor is the reciprocal of sensitivity (\(D = 1/S\)).

\[ D = \frac{V_d}{Y} = \frac{100 \, V}{30 \, mm} = \frac{10}{3} \, \mathrm{V/mm} \approx 3.33 \, \mathrm{V/mm} \]

This question deals with DC power supplies, including rectifier characteristics, voltage multiplier circuits, and Zener diode voltage regulators.

(a) DC Power Supply Definitions and Half-wave Rectifier (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to dc power supplies:**

  • **(I) Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV):** The maximum instantaneous voltage that a rectifying diode must withstand in the reverse-biased direction when it is not conducting. It is a critical parameter for selecting diodes for rectifier circuits. Exceeding the PIV can cause diode breakdown and failure.
  • **(II) Ripple Factor:** A measure of the effectiveness of a filter in reducing the AC component (ripple) in the output of a rectifier. It is defined as the ratio of the RMS value of the AC component of the output voltage to the DC (average) value of the output voltage. A lower ripple factor indicates a smoother DC output. \[ \text{Ripple Factor} = \frac{V_{rms(ac)}}{V_{dc}} \]

**(ii) Derivation of dc output voltage for a half-wave diode rectifier feeding a pure resistive load (6 marks):**

       Input AC
   o-----~ ~ -----+----o Output
   |               |
  [~] Vin(t)      [\] Diode (Ideal)
   |               |
   o---------------+----o Load RL
                   |
                 Ground

A half-wave rectifier with a pure resistive load \(R_L\) conducts current only during the positive half-cycle of the input AC voltage. The diode is assumed ideal (zero forward voltage drop, infinite reverse resistance).

Let the input AC voltage be \( v_{in}(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \).

During the positive half-cycle (\(0 \leq \omega t \leq \pi\)), the diode is forward-biased and acts as a short circuit. The output voltage across the load is equal to the input voltage: \( v_{out}(t) = v_{in}(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \).

During the negative half-cycle (\(\pi \leq \omega t \leq 2\pi\)), the diode is reverse-biased and acts as an open circuit. No current flows through the load, so the output voltage is zero: \( v_{out}(t) = 0 \).

The DC output voltage is the average value of the output voltage waveform over one full cycle (period \(2\pi\)).

\[ V_{dc} = V_{avg} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} v_{out}(t) \, d(\omega t) \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left[ \int_0^{\pi} V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) \, d(\omega t) + \int_{\pi}^{2\pi} 0 \, d(\omega t) \right] \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \sin(\theta) \, d\theta \quad (\text{where } \theta = \omega t) \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-\cos(\theta)]_0^{\pi} \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-\cos(\pi) - (-\cos(0))] \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{2\pi} [-(-1) - (-1)] = \frac{2 V_{peak}}{2\pi} \] \[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_{peak}}{\pi} \]

The DC output voltage of an ideal half-wave rectifier with a resistive load is the peak AC input voltage divided by \(\pi\).

(b) Full-wave Voltage Doubler Operation (6 marks):

AC Input o-------+------[\]----o + Output
               |       D1     |
               [+] C1 [-]     [+] C2 [-]
               |       D2     |
AC Input o-------+------[/]----o - Output
                         (Opposite direction diode symbol)

A full-wave voltage doubler is a rectifier circuit that produces a DC output voltage approximately equal to twice the peak value of the input AC voltage. It uses two diodes and two capacitors.

**Operation:**

  • During the **positive half-cycle** of the AC input voltage, diode D1 is forward-biased, and D2 is reverse-biased. Current flows through D1, charging capacitor C1 to the peak voltage \(V_{peak}\) with the polarity shown (top plate positive, bottom plate negative). Once C1 is charged to \(V_{peak}\), D1 becomes reverse-biased.
  • During the **negative half-cycle** of the AC input voltage, diode D2 is forward-biased, and D1 is reverse-biased. Current flows through D2, charging capacitor C2. The voltage across C2 is the sum of the input AC voltage (negative peak) and the voltage already stored on C1 (\(V_{peak}\)). Diode D2 conducts and charges C2 to approximately \(V_{peak}\). As the negative peak of the input voltage is reached, the voltage across C2 becomes approximately \(V_{peak}\) (bottom plate positive, top plate negative relative to the junction point).
  • The output DC voltage is taken across the series combination of capacitors C1 and C2. Since C1 is charged to \(V_{peak}\) and C2 is charged to \(V_{peak}\) (with opposing polarities relative to their junction), the total output DC voltage is \(V_{out} = V_{C1} + V_{C2} = V_{peak} + V_{peak} = 2 V_{peak}\).

This output is a pulsating DC voltage that can be smoothed further using additional filtering. The PIV rating for the diodes in this circuit needs to be at least \(2V_{peak}\).

(c) Zener Diode Shunt Regulator Calculation (4 marks):

The circuit is a Zener diode shunt regulator. The Zener diode maintains a constant voltage \(V_Z\) across the load as long as the input voltage \(V_{in}\) and load resistance \(R_L\) vary within limits that keep the Zener in its breakdown region (i.e., Zener current \(I_Z\) is between \(I_{Z,min}\) and \(I_{Z,max}\)).

Given: \(V_{in} = 20V\), \(R_S = 220\Omega\). Zener diode: \(V_Z = 10V\), \(I_{Z,min} = 5mA\), \(I_{Z,max} = 50mA\). Load is variable resistor \(R_L\).

The voltage across the load \(V_L\) is regulated by the Zener diode, so \(V_L = V_Z = 10V\). The voltage across the series resistor \(R_S\) is \(V_{R_S} = V_{in} - V_L = 20V - 10V = 10V\).

The current through the series resistor \(I_S\) is constant as long as \(V_{in}\) and \(R_S\) are constant:

\[ I_S = \frac{V_{R_S}}{R_S} = \frac{10 \, V}{220 \, \Omega} \approx 0.04545 \, A = 45.45 \, mA \]

The current \(I_S\) splits between the Zener diode (\(I_Z\)) and the load (\(I_L\)): \(I_S = I_Z + I_L\).

For regulation to be maintained, the Zener diode must be in the breakdown region, meaning \(I_{Z,min} \leq I_Z \leq I_{Z,max}\). The load current \(I_L\) varies as \(R_L\) varies: \(I_L = V_L / R_L = 10V / R_L\).

**(i) Minimum value of \(R_L\) that the Zener will tolerate in maintaining \(V_L\) constant:**

Regulation is maintained as long as \(I_Z \geq I_{Z,min}\). Since \(I_S = I_Z + I_L\), the minimum Zener current corresponds to the maximum load current (\(I_{L,max}\)).

\[ I_Z = I_S - I_L \]

Minimum \(I_Z\) occurs when \(I_L\) is maximum: \( I_{Z,min} = I_S - I_{L,max} \).

\[ I_{L,max} = I_S - I_{Z,min} = 45.45 \, mA - 5 \, mA = 40.45 \, mA \]

The minimum load resistance \(R_{L,min}\) occurs when the load current is maximum:

\[ R_{L,min} = \frac{V_L}{I_{L,max}} = \frac{10 \, V}{40.45 \, mA} = \frac{10 \, V}{40.45 \times 10^{-3} \, A} \approx 247.22 \, \Omega \]

**(ii) Minimum value of load current:**

The minimum load current (\(I_{L,min}\)) corresponds to the condition where the Zener current is at its maximum (\(I_{Z,max}\)). However, the calculated \(I_S\) (45.45mA) is less than \(I_{Z,max}\) (50mA). This means the series resistor \(R_S\) limits the total current to a value below the Zener's maximum rating. Therefore, the Zener current will never reach \(I_{Z,max}\) in this circuit, even when the load current is minimum (i.e., load is open circuit, \(I_L = 0\)).

Regulation is maintained as long as \(I_Z \ge I_{Z,min}\). When \(I_L\) is at its minimum possible value (0A for an open circuit), \(I_Z = I_S = 45.45mA\). Since \(5mA \le 45.45mA \le 50mA\), regulation is maintained even with the load removed.

The minimum value of load current for which regulation is maintained is when \(I_Z\) is maximum. But since \(I_S\) is the maximum possible Zener current (\(I_S < I_{Z,max}\)), the lowest load current is when Zener takes all \(I_S\) except \(I_{Z,min}\). The *range* of load current for regulation is from \(I_S - I_{Z,max}\) to \(I_S - I_{Z,min}\). \(I_{L,min} = I_S - I_{Z,max} = 45.45mA - 50mA = -4.55mA\). This negative value implies that the Zener *could* handle even more current if \(I_S\) was higher. The *actual* minimum load current is when \(I_Z\) is just above \(I_{Z,min}\), which means \(I_L\) is just below \(I_{L,max}\). The theoretical minimum load current is 0A (open circuit load). Since \(I_S = 45.45mA\) is between \(I_{Z,min}=5mA\) and \(I_{Z,max}=50mA\), the Zener stays in breakdown even with no load. Thus, the minimum load current for which regulation holds is 0A.

\[ I_{L,min} = 0 \, A \]

**(iii) Maximum value of load current:**

The maximum value of load current (\(I_{L,max}\)) occurs when the Zener current is at its minimum (\(I_{Z,min}\)).

\[ I_{L,max} = I_S - I_{Z,min} = 45.45 \, mA - 5 \, mA = 40.45 \, mA \]

Summary of Zener Regulator Calculations:

  • **(i) minimum value of \(R_L\):** \( R_{L,min} \approx 247.22 \, \Omega \) (This occurs at max load current)
  • **(ii) minimum value of load current:** \( I_{L,min} = 0 \, A \) (Regulation holds down to no load)
  • **(iii) maximum value of load current:** \( I_{L,max} = 40.45 \, mA \) (This occurs at min Zener current)
