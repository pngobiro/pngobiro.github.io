Answer

This question covers thermocouple ammeters, magnetic measurements using a permeameter, and error analysis for a voltmeter.

(a) Thermocouple Ammeters (10 marks):

**(i) Advantage and disadvantage of indirect-contact vs. direct-contact thermocouple ammeters:**

  • **Indirect-contact (Advantage):** The heater and thermocouple are electrically insulated, allowing for measurement of high AC voltages across the heater without affecting the DC measuring circuit. This provides isolation.
  • **Indirect-contact (Disadvantage):** The thermal coupling is less efficient than direct contact, potentially making the instrument less sensitive or slower to respond to current changes.
  • **Direct-contact (Advantage):** More efficient thermal transfer between the heater and thermocouple, leading to higher sensitivity and faster response time.
  • **Direct-contact (Disadvantage):** The thermocouple is electrically connected to the heating element circuit. This limits its use primarily to low AC voltages across the heater and can introduce errors if the DC measuring circuit is not properly isolated or has high impedance.

**(ii) Operation of the direct-contact thermocouple ammeter with circuit diagram:**

         |    +-------------+    |

         |           | Junction (hot junction)

**Description:** In a direct-contact thermocouple ammeter, the heating element through which the current to be measured flows is integrated directly into the hot junction of the thermocouple. The AC or DC current (\(I\)) heats the junction due to \(I^2R\) losses. The temperature difference between this hot junction and the cold junctions (typically at the terminals of the DC meter) generates a proportional DC voltage (thermo-electric e.m.f. or Seebeck effect). This DC voltage drives a current through the sensitive DC moving-coil meter, causing a deflection. The meter scale is calibrated to indicate the RMS value of the current. This type has better thermal transfer but is electrically connected to the circuit being measured, which can be a limitation.

If coil X measures flux linkage in the specimen, \( \Delta\Psi_X = N_X \Delta\Phi_{specimen} = N_X (2 \Phi_{specimen}) \). Assuming it measures the total flux change (\(2\Phi\)), \( \Delta\Psi_X = N_X (2 \Phi_{specimen}) \).

We can calculate \(H\) in the air gap using \(B_{air} = \mu_0 H_{air}\). In a well-designed permeameter, \(H\) in the specimen is approximately equal to \(H_{air}\) in the closely coupled search coil region, adjusted for leakage. Let's assume there's a standard formula relating the magnetic field H to the current reversal and deflection of Coil Y. This is unlikely.

MMF \( = N_{mag} I \). This MMF drives the flux. \(MMF = \int H \cdot dl = H_{specimen} l_{specimen} + H_{air} l_{air} + ...\). This requires knowing the lengths and areas, which are not given.

Let's assume there's a relationship between \(H\) and the current reversal measured by Coil Y. Coil Y measures flux change in air. \( \Delta\Psi_Y = N_Y (2 \Phi_{air}) \). \( \Phi_{air} = B_{air} A_Y = \mu_0 H_{air} A_Y \). \( \Delta\Psi_Y = N_Y (2 \mu_0 H_{air} A_Y) \). \( H_{air} = \frac{\Delta\Psi_Y}{2 N_Y \mu_0 A_Y} \). In some permeameter designs, the magnetizing force \(H\) in the specimen is assumed to be approximately equal to the magnetizing force in a small air gap specifically designed for measurement (which Coil Y might be measuring). Let's assume \(H_{specimen} \approx H_{air}\).

\[ H_{specimen} \approx 19.89 \times 10^3 \, \mathrm{A/m} \]

\[ \mu_r = \frac{0.25 \, \mathrm{Wb/m}^2}{(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \mathrm{H/m}) \times (19.89 \times 10^3 \, \mathrm{A/m})} \] \[ \mu_r = \frac{0.25}{4\pi \times 19.89 \times 10^{-4}} = \frac{0.25}{249.8 \times 10^{-4}} = \frac{0.25}{0.02498} \] \[ \mu_r \approx 10.008 \]

(c) Voltmeter Percentage Limiting Error (5 marks):

Limiting error is the guaranteed maximum deviation from the true value, usually specified as a percentage of the full-scale reading.

Given:

  • Voltmeter range = 0 - 150 V (Full Scale \(V_{FS} = 150 \, V\))
  • Guaranteed accuracy = \( \pm 1\% \) of full-scale reading.
  • Reading \(V_{reading} = 75 \, V\).

Calculate the maximum absolute error based on the full scale:

\[ \Delta V_{absolute, max} = 1\% \text{ of } V_{FS} = 0.01 \times 150 \, V = 1.5 \, V \]

This means any reading on the 150V scale could potentially be off by up to \( \pm 1.5 \, V \).

The percentage limiting error for a specific reading is the maximum absolute error divided by that reading, multiplied by 100%:

\[ \% \text{Limiting Error at } 75V = \frac{\Delta V_{absolute, max}}{V_{reading}} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Limiting Error at } 75V = \frac{1.5 \, V}{75 \, V} \times 100\% \] \[ \% \text{Limiting Error at } 75V = 0.02 \times 100\% = 2\% \]

The percentage limiting error at a reading of 75 V is 2%.

Answer

This question covers reliability concepts, factors affecting reliability, design features for high availability, and calculations of reliability metrics for a system based on component data.

(a) Redundancy and Vibration Effects on Reliability (8 marks):

**(i) Definition of redundancy with respect to reliability:**

  • Redundancy is the inclusion of duplicate or alternative components or subsystems that can take over the function of a primary element if it fails. This increases the overall system reliability because the system can continue to operate even if a single component fails, as long as a redundant path or component is available.

**(ii) Effects of mechanical vibrations and shock on reliability and minimization:**

  • **1. Effects:**
    • **Fatigue:** Repeated stress from vibration can cause metal parts (like leads, solder joints, traces) to weaken and eventually fracture.
    • **Loose Connections:** Vibrations can cause connectors to become loose, leading to intermittent operation or complete open circuits.
    • **Component Damage:** Sensitive components or those with mechanical parts can be damaged by excessive shock or prolonged vibration.
    • **Wire Chafing:** Wires rubbing against each other or sharp edges can wear through insulation, causing short circuits.
  • **II. Ways of minimizing the effects:**
    • **Mounting and Isolation:** Mounting the equipment on vibration-absorbing mounts or pads to isolate it from the source of vibration.
    • **Robust Design and Construction:** Using robust connectors, providing strain relief for wires and components, securing components firmly to the PCB and chassis, using conformal coatings on PCBs, and selecting components rated for the expected vibration/shock levels.
    • **Routing and Support:** Routing wires carefully and providing adequate support to prevent chafing and excessive movement.

(b) Design Features for High Availability (6 marks):

Availability depends on both reliability (low failure rate) and maintainability (short repair time). Designers can incorporate features to improve both aspects.

Five features a designer should consider for high availability:

  • **Modularity:** Designing the system as a collection of independent, easily replaceable modules. This simplifies fault isolation and speeds up repair by allowing replacement of a faulty module rather than repairing a component on a complex board.
  • **Accessibility:** Ensuring critical components and test points are easily accessible for testing, removal, and replacement without extensive disassembly.
  • **Built-In Test (BIT) / Diagnostics:** Including internal circuitry or software that can perform self-tests, identify faults, and indicate the location of the fault (e.g., via LEDs, display codes, or diagnostic interfaces).
  • **Standardization:** Using standard, off-the-shelf components and connectors where possible, which are readily available for replacement and whose characteristics are well known.
  • **Error Reporting/Logging:** Designing the equipment to detect and log errors or failures, providing valuable information for diagnosis.
  • **Redundancy:** Implementing redundant subsystems for critical functions (as discussed in a(i)), allowing the system to continue operating after a failure (improves reliability, thus availability).
  • **Simplified Interfaces:** Designing clear and simple user and maintenance interfaces.

Answer

This question covers measuring the depth of modulation using a CRO, characteristics and calculations for ammeter shunts and voltmeter multipliers, and the theoretical basis for wattmeter measurement in AC circuits.

(a) Measurement of Depth of Modulation with CRO (Timebase OFF) and Diagram (8 marks):

When the oscilloscope timebase is switched off, the horizontal deflection is disabled. Applying a signal to the vertical input (Y-input) causes the spot to move only up and down. For an amplitude modulated (AM) wave, the amplitude of the carrier varies according to the modulating signal. The resulting display is a vertical line whose length varies over time, tracing the maximum and minimum amplitudes of the AM envelope.

Amplitude ^
          |
  (A_max) |-----------
          |          |
          |          |  <-- Vertical line length varies
          |          |
  (A_min) |-----------
          +--------------> (No Time Axis)

**Explanation:** Connect the AM signal to the Y-input of the oscilloscope with the timebase switched off. The display will be a vertical line that oscillates in length. The maximum length of this line corresponds to the peak-to-peak amplitude of the modulated wave when the envelope is at its maximum (\(A_{max}\)), and the minimum length corresponds to the peak-to-peak amplitude when the envelope is at its minimum (\(A_{min}\)). The depth of modulation (modulation index, \(m\)) is calculated from these measured values:

\[ m = \frac{A_{max} - A_{min}}{A_{max} + A_{min}} \]

Where \(A_{max}\) is the maximum vertical deflection (peak-to-peak) and \(A_{min}\) is the minimum vertical deflection (peak-to-peak) observed on the screen. This is based on the relationship \(A_{max} = 2V_{max}\) and \(A_{min} = 2V_{min}\), where \(V_{max}\) and \(V_{min}\) are the maximum and minimum instantaneous amplitudes of the AM envelope measured from the zero axis.

(b) Meter Range Extension Calculations (12 marks):

To extend the range of a moving coil instrument, shunts are used for ammeters (connected in parallel) and multipliers are used for voltmeters (connected in series).

Given: Moving coil instrument coil resistance \(R_m = 4 \, \Omega\). FSD current \(I_m = 20 \, mA = 20 \times 10^{-3} \, A\).

**(i) Three desirable characteristics of shunts:**

  • **Low temperature coefficient of resistance:** The shunt resistance should not change significantly with temperature to maintain accuracy across different operating temperatures. Manganin is commonly used.
  • **Stable over time:** The resistance value should remain constant over the lifespan of the instrument.
  • **Low resistance:** The shunt must have a low resistance value to divert the majority of the current and minimize the voltage drop across the ammeter.
  • **Non-inductive:** For AC measurements (though this is a DC instrument question, shunts are also used for AC meters), the shunt should ideally be non-inductive.

**(ii) Resistors required for range extension:**

Calculate the voltage across the meter at FSD: \( V_m = I_m R_m = (20 \times 10^{-3} \, A) \times (4 \, \Omega) = 80 \times 10^{-3} \, V = 80 \, mV \).

I. To read up to 1 A (Ammeter): A shunt resistor \(R_{sh}\) is needed in parallel with the meter.

Total current \(I = 1 \, A\). Current through shunt \(I_{sh} = I - I_m = 1 \, A - 20 \times 10^{-3} \, A = 1 - 0.02 = 0.98 \, A\).

Voltage across shunt \(V_{sh} = I_{sh} R_{sh}\). At FSD, \(V_{sh} = V_m\). \[ I_m R_m = I_{sh} R_{sh} \] \[ 80 \, mV = 0.98 \, A \times R_{sh} \] \[ R_{sh} = \frac{80 \times 10^{-3} \, V}{0.98 \, A} \approx 0.08163 \, \Omega \]

A shunt resistor of approximately \(0.08163 \, \Omega\) is required for the 1A range.

II. To read up to 15 V (Voltmeter): A multiplier resistor \(R_{mult}\) is needed in series with the meter.

Total voltage \(V = 15 \, V\). The current through the meter and multiplier at FSD is \(I_m = 20 \, mA\).

The total resistance of the voltmeter (meter + multiplier) is \( R_{total} = R_m + R_{mult} \).

By Ohm's Law, \( V = I_m \times R_{total} \).

\[ 15 \, V = (20 \times 10^{-3} \, A) \times (R_m + R_{mult}) \] \[ 15 = 0.02 \times (4 + R_{mult}) \] \[ \frac{15}{0.02} = 4 + R_{mult} \] \[ 750 = 4 + R_{mult} \] \[ R_{mult} = 750 - 4 = 746 \, \Omega \]

A multiplier resistor of \(746 \, \Omega\) is required for the 15V range.

(c) Average Power vs. Wattmeter Reading Proof (5 marks):

For an AC circuit with instantaneous voltage \(v(t)\) and instantaneous current \(i(t)\) lagging by an angle \(\phi\), the instantaneous power is \(p(t) = v(t) i(t)\).

Let \(v(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t)\) and \(i(t) = I_{peak} \sin(\omega t - \phi)\).

Instantaneous power \( p(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t) I_{peak} \sin(\omega t - \phi) \).

Using the identity \( \sin A \sin B = \frac{1}{2} [\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)] \):

\[ p(t) = V_{peak} I_{peak} \frac{1}{2} [\cos(\omega t - (\omega t - \phi)) - \cos(\omega t + (\omega t - \phi))] \] \[ p(t) = \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} [\cos(\phi) - \cos(2\omega t - \phi)] \] \[ p(t) = \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(\phi) - \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(2\omega t - \phi) \]

The average power over one cycle is the integral of the instantaneous power over one period (\(T = 2\pi/\omega\)) divided by the period:

\[ P_{avg} = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T p(t) dt = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi/\omega} \left[ \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(\phi) - \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(2\omega t - \phi) \right] dt \] \[ P_{avg} = \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(\phi) \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T dt - \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \cos(2\omega t - \phi) dt \]

The integral of \(\cos(2\omega t - \phi)\) over a full cycle (or integer multiple of cycles) of \(2\omega t\) is zero. So the second term's average is zero.

\[ P_{avg} = \frac{V_{peak} I_{peak}}{2} \cos(\phi) \times 1 - 0 \] \[ P_{avg} = \frac{V_{peak}}{\sqrt{2}} \frac{I_{peak}}{\sqrt{2}} \cos(\phi) = V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos(\phi) \]

A wattmeter measures the average power in an AC circuit. The torque produced in a wattmeter is proportional to the product of the RMS voltage of the pressure coil, the RMS current of the current coil, and the cosine of the phase angle between them. If the wattmeter is connected correctly, the voltage across the pressure coil is the circuit voltage \(V\) and the current through the current coil is the circuit current \(I\), and the torque is proportional to \(V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos \phi\). The deflection of the pointer is proportional to the average torque. Therefore, the wattmeter reading is equal to the average power over one cycle, \( V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos \phi \).

(a) Definitions and Standards of Measurement (12 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to electrical measurements:**

  • **I. Standard:** A standard is a fundamental reference used to define, realize, or reproduce a unit of measurement. It provides a consistent magnitude of a physical quantity for comparison. Examples include the standard kilogram or the atomic clock.
  • **II. Derived unit:** A unit of measurement for a physical quantity that is defined as a combination of fundamental units. Example: The unit of force (Newton) is derived from mass, length, and time units (kg⋅m/s²).

**(ii) Description of standards of measurement:**

  • **I. International Standard:** These are the highest accuracy standards, maintained at international metrology laboratories (like BIPM). They are based on the fundamental definitions of SI units and serve as the ultimate reference for all measurements globally. They are used to calibrate national standards.
  • **II. Primary Standard:** These are standards of the highest metrological quality available at national standards laboratories. Their values are established directly from the fundamental definitions of the units or by comparison with international standards. They are used to calibrate secondary standards but are not used for routine measurements.

(b) Dimensional Analysis of \(e = Blv\) in LMTI System (8 marks):

We need to show that the dimensions on both sides of the equation \(e = Blv\) are the same in the LMTI system (Length L, Mass M, Time T, Current I).

Left-hand side: \(e\) is e.m.f. induced in a conductor. e.m.f. is a voltage, which is energy per unit charge. Energy has dimensions \([ML^2T^{-2}]\). Charge has dimensions \([IT]\).

\[ [e] = \frac{[\text{Energy}]}{[\text{Charge}]} = \frac{[ML^2T^{-2}]}{[IT]} = [ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}] \]

Right-hand side: \(B\) is flux density, \(l\) is length, \(v\) is velocity.

Magnetic flux density \(B\). From the force on a conductor in a magnetic field \(F = BI l\), \(B = F / (Il)\). Force \(F\) has dimensions \([MLT^{-2}]\). Current \(I\) is \([I]\). Length \(l\) is \([L]\).

\[ [B] = \frac{[MLT^{-2}]}{[I][L]} = [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \]

Length \(l\) has dimensions \([L]\).

Velocity \(v\) has dimensions \([LT^{-1}]\).

Now, multiply the dimensions of \(B, l, v\):

\[ [Blv] = [B] \times [l] \times [v] \] \[ [Blv] = [MT^{-2}I^{-1}] \times [L] \times [LT^{-1}] \] \[ [Blv] = [M^1 L^{1+1} T^{-2-1} I^{-1}] = [ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}] \]

LHS dimensions: \([ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}]\).

RHS dimensions: \([ML^2T^{-3}I^{-1}]\).

Since the dimensions on both sides are the same, the equation \(e = Blv\) is dimensionally correct.

Figure 2 shows a typical linear regulated DC power supply consisting of a transformer, rectifier, filter capacitor (C1), IC regulator, and output capacitor (C2).

**(i) Diode D1 open circuit:**

  • D1 is one of the diodes in the bridge rectifier. If D1 opens, the bridge rectifier will effectively become a half-wave rectifier circuit (using D2 and D3, or D4 depending on connection).
  • **Effect:** The output of the bridge will be pulsating DC from only half of the AC cycle. The ripple frequency will be the same as the input frequency (e.g., 50 Hz or 60 Hz) instead of double (e.g., 100 Hz or 120 Hz). The average voltage after the rectifier will be lower.
  • **Symptom:** The voltage across C1 will have a much larger ripple amplitude and a lower average value. The IC regulator might fail to maintain a stable output voltage if its input voltage drops too low during parts of the cycle, resulting in a regulated output with significant ripple or a lower average value.

**(ii) Capacitor C1 short circuit:**

  • C1 is the main filter capacitor after the rectifier. If it shorts out, it creates a very low resistance path across the output of the rectifier.
  • **Effect:** This will essentially short-circuit the rectifier's output terminals. A large current will flow from the transformer secondary through the rectifier diodes and C1. This large current will likely blow a fuse (if present) or damage the transformer secondary winding and/or the rectifier diodes.   • **Symptom:** No output voltage from the entire power supply. Blown fuse, possible smell of burning, rectifier diodes or transformer may be hot or damaged.

**(iii) Terminal 3 of the IC regulator open to ground:**

For fixed regulators (like 78xx), it's directly connected to ground. For adjustable regulators (like LM317), it's connected to ground via a resistor.

The internal feedback loop requires this terminal to be at ground potential relative to the output voltage regulation. Without a stable ground reference, the regulator cannot function correctly.
  • **Symptom:** The output voltage will likely be unregulated and much higher than the rated voltage, potentially rising close to the input voltage (\(V_{in}\) to the IC). This can damage the connected load. Or, the regulator might simply shut down or produce unstable output depending on the IC design.

**(iv) Capacitor C2 open circuit:**

  • C2 is the output filter capacitor after the IC regulator. Its function is to smooth out any remaining high-frequency ripple or transient voltages from the regulator output and improve load transient response.
  • **Effect:** If C2 is open, the filtering of high-frequency components at the output is reduced or eliminated. The regulator's transient response to sudden changes in load current might also be degraded.
  • **Symptom:** The output DC voltage may appear stable for steady loads, but closer inspection with an oscilloscope will reveal more high-frequency noise or ripple, and potentially larger voltage dips or overshoots when the load current changes rapidly. The DC voltage level should remain correct.

(a) Semiconductor Definitions and p-type Formation (10 marks):

**(i) Definitions with respect to semiconductors:**

  • **(I) Covalent bond:** A type of chemical bond formed between atoms by the sharing of valence electrons. In intrinsic semiconductors like silicon or germanium, each atom forms four covalent bonds with its neighboring atoms in a crystal lattice structure.
  • **(II) Drift current:** The movement of charge carriers (electrons and holes) in a semiconductor due to the presence of an electric field. The electric field exerts a force on the carriers, causing them to move in a specific direction (electrons opposite to the field, holes in the direction of the field), resulting in a net current flow.

**(ii) Formation of a p-type semiconductor with a labelled diagram:**

A p-type semiconductor is formed by doping an intrinsic semiconductor (e.g., silicon) with a small amount of trivalent impurity atoms (accepter impurities) like Boron, Gallium, or Indium.

        Silicon Atom (Si)   Boron Atom (B)

**Description:** Silicon is a group IV element with 4 valence electrons. A trivalent impurity atom (group III element) has only 3 valence electrons. When a trivalent atom substitutes a silicon atom in the crystal lattice, it forms covalent bonds with three neighboring silicon atoms. The bond with the fourth silicon atom is incomplete, as the trivalent atom can only share 3 electrons, while the silicon atom has 4 electrons to share. This deficiency of an electron in the covalent bond creates a "hole". These holes are free to move throughout the crystal lattice, acting as positive charge carriers. The trivalent impurity atoms, having accepted an electron to complete three bonds, become fixed negative ions in the lattice. In a p-type semiconductor, holes are the majority carriers, and electrons are the minority carriers.

(b) Rutherford's atomic structure (4 marks):

Ernest Rutherford proposed a model of the atom based on the results of his gold foil scattering experiment (Geiger-Marsden experiment).

**Explanation:** Rutherford's model, sometimes called the nuclear model, described the atom as having:

  • A very small, dense, positively charged **nucleus** located at the center of the atom. This nucleus contains most of the atom's mass (attributed to protons and neutrons, although neutrons were discovered later).
  • Negatively charged **electrons** orbiting around the nucleus in definite paths, much like planets orbiting the sun.
  • The atom is mostly **empty space**, as evidenced by the fact that most alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil without deflection.
  • The total positive charge of the nucleus is equal to the total negative charge of the orbiting electrons, making the atom electrically neutral.

This model replaced the earlier plum-pudding model but had limitations, particularly in explaining why orbiting electrons (which are accelerating charges) did not lose energy and spiral into the nucleus according to classical physics.

  • **Reverse Breakdown Voltage (\(V_{BR}\) or \(V_Z\)):** The reverse voltage at which the diode enters the breakdown region. (Crucial for Zener diodes).
  • **Operating Temperature Range:** The range of ambient or junction temperatures over which the diode is specified to operate reliably.

(a) Disadvantages of Field Effect Transistors (FETs) compared to Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) (4 marks):

  • **Sensitivity to Electrostatic Discharge (ESD):** Especially MOSFETs, the thin gate oxide is easily damaged by static electricity, requiring careful handling. While BJTs can also be damaged by ESD, MOSFETs are generally more sensitive.
  • **Lower Transconductance:** FETs typically have lower transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_D / \Delta V_{GS}\)) compared to the transconductance (\(g_m = \Delta I_C / \Delta V_{BE}\) or \(g_m = I_C/V_T\)) of BJTs for the same operating current. This means that for the same change in input voltage, a BJT can provide a larger change in output current.
  • **Limited Current Driving Capability:** Power BJTs can generally handle larger currents and powers compared to FETs of similar size, making BJTs often preferred for high-power switching or amplification applications.
  • **Switching Speed (sometimes):** Although MOSFETs can switch very fast, BJTs sometimes offer faster switching speeds in specific high-current applications due to lower ON-resistance limitations in some power FETs.

(b) Operation of a P-channel Junction Field Effect Transistor (JFET) with Diagram (7 marks):

A P-channel JFET consists of a P-type semiconductor channel with N-type regions diffused or implanted on opposite sides to form the gate. The operation relies on varying the effective width of the channel using a gate-source voltage to control current flow between the drain and source.

**Principle of Operation:**

For proper operation, the gate-source PN junction is typically reverse-biased. For a P-channel JFET, this means applying a positive voltage to the gate relative to the source (\(V_{GS} > 0\)). The drain is connected to a negative voltage relative to the source (\(V_{DS} < 0\)) to cause holes to flow from source to drain.

  • A negative voltage \(V_{DS}\) is applied between the drain and source. This causes holes (majority carriers in the P-channel) to flow from the source (positive terminal) through the channel to the drain (negative terminal), constituting the drain current \(I_D\). The current direction is usually shown as flowing from drain to source (conventional current, positive direction).
  • A positive voltage \(V_{GS}\) is applied between the gate and source. This reverse-biases the PN junction between the N-type gate and the P-channel.
  • The reverse bias voltage creates a depletion region within the P-channel, depleted of free charge carriers. This narrows the conductive path of the channel.
  • As the positive \(V_{GS}\) voltage is increased (made more positive), the reverse bias across the gate-channel junction increases, widening the depletion region and further constricting the channel.
  • This increases the channel resistance and decreases the drain current \(I_D\) for a given \(V_{DS}\).
  • If \(V_{GS}\) is made sufficiently positive, the depletion regions can meet, closing off the channel and reducing \(I_D\) to zero. This \(V_{GS}\) voltage is called the pinch-off voltage (\(V_p\)), which is positive for a P-channel JFET.

The P-channel JFET acts as a voltage-controlled resistor or current source, where the positive \(V_{GS}\) controls the negative \(I_D\) (or positive conventional \(I_D\) flowing S to D).
