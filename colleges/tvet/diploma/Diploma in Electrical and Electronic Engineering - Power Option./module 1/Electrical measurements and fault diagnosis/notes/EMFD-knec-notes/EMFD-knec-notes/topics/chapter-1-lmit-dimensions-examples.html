⚛️ Rationalised MKS System (LMTI) and Examples

Dimensions of Electrical and Magnetic Quantities in Rationalised MKS System(LMTI)

The dimensions of the various quantities in the Rationalised System are based on FOUR fundamental quantities and their dimensions. These are: metre [M], kilogramme [K], second [S] (or time [T]), and ampere (or current) [I]. The use of ampere as the fourth fundamental unit was recommended by the IEC in July, 1950 and was subsequently adopted universally. The main feature of the system is that it does away with the use of either permittivity or permeability which do not appear in any of the dimensions.

The current itself being the rate of electric charge (charge per unit time), can be dimensionally expressed as

$$[\mathrm{I}]=\frac{[\mathrm{Q}]}{[\mathrm{T}]} \quad \text { or } \quad[\mathrm{Q}]=[\mathrm{I}][\mathrm{T}]$$

Electromotive force or potential difference in MKS

Since potential difference = work per unit charge

$$\begin{aligned} [\mathrm{PD}] & =[\mathrm{V}]=\frac{[\mathrm{W}]}{[\mathrm{Q}]} \\ & =\frac{\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]}{[\mathrm{Q}]} \\ & =\left\lceil\mathrm{M}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right\rceil \end{aligned}$$

substituting for the dimensions of [Q].

Electric flux

This is dimensionally equal to the charge producing it, _

or

$$[\psi]=[\mathrm{Q}]=[\mathrm{I}][\mathrm{T}]=[\mathrm{I} \mathrm{~T}]$$

Example 2.1 : Prove that the following equation is dimensionally correct :

$e=B l v$

where $e=e . m . f$. in volts, $B=$ flux density
$l=$ length, $\quad v=$ velocity

Solution : On right hand side,

$$\begin{aligned} [\mathrm{B}]&=\frac{[\phi]}{[\mathrm{A}]}=\frac{\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right]}{\left[\mathrm{L}^{2}\right]}=\left[\mathrm{MT}^{-2} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right]\\ [l]&=[\mathrm{L}] \quad \text { and }[\mathrm{v}]=\frac{[\mathrm{L}]}{[\mathrm{T}]}=\left[\mathrm{LT}^{-1}\right] \end{aligned}$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{B}][\mathrm{ll}][\mathrm{v}]=\left[\mathrm{MT}^{-2} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right][\mathrm{L}]\left[\mathrm{LT}^{-1}\right]=\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right]$$

These are dimensions of right hand side.

$$\begin{aligned} [\mathrm{e}] & =\frac{[\mathrm{W}]}{[\mathrm{Q}]}=\frac{\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]}{[\mathrm{TI}]} \\ & =\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right] \end{aligned}$$

These are dimensions of left hand side, same as that of right hand side.

This proves that the equation is dimensionally correct.

Example 2.2: The expression for the mean torque of an electrodynamometer type wattmeter may be written as,

$T \propto M^{p} E^{q} Z^{t}$

where $M=$ Mutual inductance between fixed and moving coils
$E=$ Applied voltage, $\quad Z=$ Impedance of load circuit
Determine the values of $p, q$, and $t$ performing the dimensional analysis.

Solution : Let $K$ is the constant of proportionality in the given equation which is dimensionless.

$$\therefore \quad T=K M^{P} E^{q} Z^{t}$$

Dimensions of L.H.S. are,

$$[\mathrm{T}]=[\text { Force }] \times[\text { Distance }]=\left[M L T^{-2}\right][\mathrm{L}]=\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]$$

On R.H.S. using LMTI system of units,

$$\begin{aligned} [\mathrm{M}] & =[\mathrm{L}] \\ & =\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]\\ [\mathrm{E}] & =\frac{[\mathrm{W}]}{[\mathrm{Q}]}=\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right]\\ [\mathrm{Z}] & =[\mathrm{R}]=\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right] \end{aligned} \quad \text {... as both are inductances}$$

The impedance is measured in ohms hence having same dimensions as that of resistance.

$$\begin{aligned} \therefore \quad \text { R.H.S. } & =\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]^{p}\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-1}\right]^{q}\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]^{t} \\ & =\left[\mathrm{M}^{p+q+t} \mathrm{~L}^{2 p+2 q+2 t} \mathrm{~T}^{-2 p-3 q-3 t} \mathrm{I}^{-2 p-q-2 t}\right] \end{aligned}$$

Equating the dimensions of L.H.S. and R.H.S. and hence the power of M, L, T and I on both sides, we get

$$\begin{aligned} p+q+t & =1\\ 2p+2q+2t & =2\\ -2p-3q-3t & =-2\\ -2p-q-2t&=0 \end{aligned}$$

Adding equation (2) and (3), $-q-t=0$ i.e. $t=-q$
Adding equation (2) and (4), $q=2$ so $t=-2$
Using in equation (1), $p=1$

Thus

$$p=1, q=2, t=-2$$

Hence the expression can be written as,

$$\mathrm{T} \propto \mathrm{ME}^{2} Z^{-2} \propto \frac{\mathrm{ME}^{2}}{\mathrm{Z}^{2}}$$

Example 2.4 : In deriving equation for resistance in Hay's bridge, the following expression is obtained,

$$R=\frac{\omega^{2} R_{1} R_{2} R_{3} C^{2}}{1+\omega^{2} R_{2}^{2} C}$$

Find whether the equation is dimensionally correct or not. In case there is an error, find the error and correct the equation accordingly.

Solution : In M.K.S.A. rationalised system,

$$\begin{aligned} [\mathrm{R}] & =\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{2}\right]\\ [\mathrm{C}] & =\left[\mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{~L}^{-2} \mathrm{~T}^{4} \mathrm{I}^{2}\right]\\ [\omega] & =\text { cycles second }=\left[\mathrm{T}^{-1}\right] \end{aligned} \quad \text{... as cycles is dimensionless}$$

Hence substituting in the right hand side,

$$\text { R.H.S. }=\frac{\left[\mathrm{T}^{-1}\right]^{2}\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]^{3}\left[\mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{~L}^{-2} \mathrm{~T}^{4} \mathrm{I}^{2}\right]^{2}}{1+\left[\mathrm{T}^{-1}\right]^{2}\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]^{2}\left[\mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{~L}^{-2} \mathrm{~T}^{4} \mathrm{I}^{2}\right]}=\frac{\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]}{1+\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-4} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]}$$

While $\quad[\text { L.H.S. }]=[\mathrm{R}]=\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-3} \mathrm{I}^{-2}\right]$

Thus dimensionally equation is not correct. It can be seen that numerator dimensions of R.H.S. are same as the dimensions of L.H.S. Hence to satisfy the equation dimensionally, denominator of R.H.S. must be dimensionless. The term ' 1 ' is dimensionless. So to have other term in the denominator of R.H.S. dimensionless, it must be multiplied by [$\mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{~L}^{-2} \mathrm{~T}^{4} \mathrm{I}^{2}$]. These are the dimensions of capacitor C. This indicates that the term $\omega^{2} \mathrm{R}_{2}^{2} \mathrm{C}$ must be multiplied by one more C to satisfy the equation dimensionally,

$$R=\frac{\omega^{2} R_{1} R_{2} R_{3} C^{2}}{1+\omega^{2} C^{2} R_{2}^{2}}$$
