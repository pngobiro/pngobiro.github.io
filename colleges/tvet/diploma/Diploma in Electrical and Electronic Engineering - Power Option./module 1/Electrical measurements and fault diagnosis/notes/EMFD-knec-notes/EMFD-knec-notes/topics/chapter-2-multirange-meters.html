multirange ammeter

The range of the basic d.c. ammeter can be extended by using number of shunts and a selector switch. Such ammeter is called multirange ammeter. The aryton shunt is used

Example : Design a multirange d.c. milliammeter with a basic meter having a resistance $75 \Omega$ and full scale deflection for the current of 2 mA . The required ranges are $0-10 m A, 0-50 m A$ and $0-100 m A$.

Solution : The first range is $0-10 \mathrm{~mA}$,

While
$\mathrm{I}_{1}=10 \mathrm{~mA}$
$\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}}=2 \mathrm{~mA} \quad$ and $\quad \mathrm{R}_{\mathrm{m}} \hat{2}^{6} 75 \Omega$

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{R}_{1}&=\frac{\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}} \mathrm{R}_{\mathrm{m}}}{\left(\mathrm{I}_{1}-\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}}\right)}=\frac{2 \times 75}{(10 \times 2)}\\ &=18.75 \Omega \end{aligned}$$

The second range is $0-50 \mathrm{~mA}$,

$$\begin{aligned} \therefore \quad \mathrm{I}_{2}&=50 \mathrm{~mA}\\ \mathrm{R}_{2}&=\frac{\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}} \mathrm{R}_{\mathrm{m}}}{\left(\mathrm{I}_{2}-\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}}\right)}=\frac{2 \times 75}{(50-2)}\\ &=3.125 \Omega \end{aligned}$$

The third range is $0-100 \mathrm{~mA}$,

$$\begin{aligned} \therefore \quad \mathrm{I}_{3}&=100 \mathrm{~mA}\\ \mathrm{R}_{3}&=\frac{\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}} \mathrm{R}_{\mathrm{m}}}{\left(\mathrm{I}_{3}-\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{m}}\right)}=\frac{2 \times 75}{(100-2)}=1.53 \Omega \end{aligned}$$

Multirange voltmeters:

The range of the basic d.c. voltmeter can be extended by using number of multipliers clnd a selector switch. Such a meter is called multirange voltmeter

The $\mathrm{R}_{1}, \mathrm{R}_{2}, \mathrm{R}_{3}$ and $\mathrm{R}_{4}$ are the four series multipliers. When connected in series with the meter, they can give four different voltage ranges as $V_{1}, V_{2}, V_{3}$ and $V_{4}$. The selector switch $S$ is multiposition switch by which the required multiplier can be selected in the circuit.

The mathematical analysis of basic d.c. voltmeter is equally applicable for such multirange voltmeter. Thus,

$$R_{1}=\frac{V_{1}}{I_{m}}-R_{m} \wedge^{\left\langle B_{2}=\right.} \frac{V_{2}}{I_{m}}-R_{m} \quad \text { and so on. }$$
