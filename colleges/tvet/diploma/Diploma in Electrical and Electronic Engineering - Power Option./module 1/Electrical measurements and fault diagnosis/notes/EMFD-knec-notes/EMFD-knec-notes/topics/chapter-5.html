Chapter 5: DC Voltage and Current Measurement

🔌 Types of Electrical Instruments

Electrical indicating instruments are divided into two types:

  • • Analog instruments - output is the deflection of pointer
  • • Digital instruments - output is in decimal form

The action of all ammeters and voltmeters, with the exception of electrostatic type of instruments, depends upon a deflecting torque produced by an electric current.

💻 Advantages of Digital Meters

  • • More accurate than analog multimeters
  • • Reduce reading and interpolation errors
  • • Auto-polarity function prevents connection problems
  • • Eliminate parallax errors
  • • No moving parts, free from wear and shock failures
  • • Increased reading speed
  • • Zero adjustment not required
  • • Digital output suitable for further processing

📊 Galvanometers and Measurements

The heart of most analog meters is a device called a galvanometer. Current flow through a galvanometer produces a proportional needle deflection. This movement of coil is called D'Arsonval movement.

Galvanometer as Ammeter

A galvanometer can be made into an ammeter by placing it in parallel with a small resistance R, called the shunt resistance. The shunt resistance can be calculated as:

$R_{sh} = \frac{R_m}{m-1}$ where $m = \frac{I}{I_m}$

Where:

  • • $R_m$ = Internal resistance of coil
  • • $I_m$ = Full scale deflection current
  • • $m$ = Multiplying power of the shunt

⚡ Loading Effects

When measuring current, connect ammeter in series:

  • • Any ammeter has some resistance r
  • • Current in presence of ammeter is $I = \frac{V}{R+r}$
  • • Current without the ammeter would be $I = \frac{V}{R}$

To minimize error, ammeter resistance r must be very small. An ideal ammeter would have zero resistance.

📝

Key Points

  • •

    Digital meters offer numerous advantages over analog meters in terms of accuracy and ease of use.

  • •

    Galvanometers can be converted to ammeters using shunt resistances.

  • •

    Loading effects must be considered when making measurements in circuits.

  • •

    Ideal measuring instruments should have minimal impact on the circuit being measured.
