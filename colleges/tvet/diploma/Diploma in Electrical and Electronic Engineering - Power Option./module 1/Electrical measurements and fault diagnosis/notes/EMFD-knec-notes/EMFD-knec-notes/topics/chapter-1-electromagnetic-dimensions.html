Instead of using $\varepsilon$ as a fourth unit, $\mu$ is used as a fourth unit in electromagnetic system of units. Let us derive the dimensions of some electromagnetic units interms of L, M, T and $\mu$.

1. Pole strength (m) :

The force between the two magnetic poles is given by Coulomb's law as,

$$\mathrm{F}=\frac{\mathrm{m}_{1} \mathrm{~m}_{2}}{\mu \mathrm{d}^{2}}$$

where $\quad \mathrm{d}=$ Distance between the two poles

$$\begin{aligned} \therefore \quad \left[\mathrm{MLT}^{-2}\right] & =\frac{\left[\mathrm{m}^{2}\right]}{[\mu]\left[\mathrm{L}^{2}\right]} \\ \therefore \quad \left[\mathrm{m}^{2}\right] & =\left[\mu \mathrm{M}^{3} \mathrm{~T}^{2}\right] \\ \therefore \quad [\mathrm{m}] & =\left[\mu^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{3}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-1}\right] \end{aligned}$$

2. Magnetic field strength ($\mathbf{H}$) :

It is the force exerted of a unit pole.

$$H=\frac{F}{m}$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{H}]=\frac{\left[M \mathrm{MLT}^{-2}\right]}{\left[\mu^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{3}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-1}\right]}=\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-1}\right]$$

3. Current (I) :

The magnetising force at the centre of a loop having radius $r$ is given by,

$$H=\frac{2 \pi I}{r} \text { where } I \text { is in e.m. system of units. }$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{I}]=[\mathrm{H}][\mathrm{r}]=[\mathrm{H}][\mathrm{L}]$$
$$=\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-1}\right]$$

4. Charge (Q) :

The charge is product of current and time.

$$Q=I t$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{Q}]=[\mathrm{I}][\mathrm{T}]=\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]$$

5. Potential difference (E):

It is the work done per unit charge.

$$E=\frac{W}{Q}$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{E}]=\frac{\left[\mathrm{ML}^{2} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]}{\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]}=\left[\mu^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{3}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]$$

6. Capacitance (C) :

It is the ratio of charge to the potential difference.

$$C=\frac{Q}{E}$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{C}]=\frac{\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]}{\left[\mu^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{3}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]}=\partial^{\gamma^{-1}} \mathrm{~L}^{-1} \mathrm{~T}^{2}\right]$$

7. Resistance ( R ) :

It is the ratio of e.m.f. to the current.

$$\mathrm{R}=\frac{\mathrm{E}}{\mathrm{I}}$$
$$\therefore \quad [\mathrm{R}]=\frac{\left[\mu^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{3}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-2}\right]}{\left[\mu^{-\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{M}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~L}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathrm{~T}^{-1}\right]}=\left[\mu \mathrm{LT}^{-1}\right]$$

8. Inductance (L) :

It is the ratio of e.m.f. and the corresponding rate of change of current.

$$\mathrm{L}=\frac{\mathrm{E}}{\mathrm{dI} / \mathrm{dt}}$$
$$\begin{aligned} \therefore \quad [\mathrm{L}] & =\frac{[\mathrm{E}]}{\frac{[\mathrm{I}]}{[\mathrm{T}]}} \text { but } \frac{[\mathrm{E}]}{[\mathrm{I}]}=[\mathrm{R}] \text { dimensionally } \\ \therefore \quad [\mathrm{L}] & =[\mathrm{R}][\mathrm{T}]=\left[\mu \mathrm{LT}^{-1}\right][\mathrm{T}]=[\mu \mathrm{L}] \end{aligned}$$
