(a) (i) Define the following terms as applied to radioactive decay:

(I) half life; (1 mark)

(II) background radiation. (1 mark)

(ii) Describe two sources of background radiation. (4 marks)

(b) With the aid of a labelled diagram, explain the production of X-rays in an X-ray tube. (6 marks)

(c) (i) State two properties of acids. (2 marks)

(ii) An atom has a mass number of 35 and a charge number (Atomic Number) of 24:

Note: Atomic number 24 is Chromium (Cr), its most common isotopes have mass numbers 50, 52, 53, 54. Mass number 35 is unusual for Z=24. Let's proceed with the given numbers Z=24, A=35.

(I) determine the numbers of protons and neutrons in the atom; (2 marks)

(II) draw the electronic structure of the atom. (2 marks)

(d) A light ray travelling through air strikes a glass interface at an incidence angle of $30^{\circ}$. If the refractive index of the glass is 1.55, determine the angle of refraction. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers radioactivity basics, X-ray production, acid properties, atomic structure, and basic optics (Snell's Law).

(a) Radioactive Decay Terms and Sources:

(i) Definitions:

  • (I) Half-life ($T_{1/2}$): The time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a given sample to undergo radioactive decay, or the time for the activity of the sample to decrease to half its initial value.
  • (II) Background Radiation: The low level of ionizing radiation naturally present in the environment from various sources (cosmic, terrestrial, internal) and artificial sources, independent of any specific source being considered.

(ii) Two Sources of Background Radiation:

  • Cosmic Rays: High-energy particles originating from outer space, primarily the sun and distant galaxies. Their intensity increases with altitude.
  • Terrestrial Radiation: Radiation emitted by naturally occurring radioactive isotopes found in rocks, soil, and building materials. Common isotopes include those of Uranium, Thorium, Potassium-40, and Radon gas (which emanates from the ground).
  • Internal Radiation: Radioactive isotopes naturally present within living organisms, including the human body (e.g., Potassium-40, Carbon-14), ingested or inhaled from the environment.
  • Artificial Sources: Man-made contributions like medical X-rays, nuclear medicine procedures, fallout from past nuclear weapons testing, emissions from nuclear power plants, and some consumer products (e.g., smoke detectors).
  • (Describe any two sources).

(b) Production of X-rays in an X-ray Tube:

Explanation:

  1. Thermionic Emission: A filament (part of the cathode) is heated by a low voltage supply, causing it to emit electrons.
  2. Acceleration: A high voltage (kV range) is applied between the cathode (-) and the anode (+), accelerating the electrons to high speeds across the vacuum inside the glass tube.
  3. Collision & Deceleration: The high-speed electrons collide with a metal target (anode, typically tungsten).
  4. X-ray Production: Upon collision, the electrons rapidly decelerate and lose kinetic energy. A small fraction (~1%) of this energy is converted into X-ray photons (via Bremsstrahlung and Characteristic emission), while the majority (~99%) becomes heat in the target.
  5. Cooling & Shielding: The anode requires efficient cooling (e.g., fins, oil, rotation) to dissipate heat. Lead shielding contains stray radiation.

(c) Acids and Atomic Structure:

(i) Properties of Acids:

  • Sour taste.
  • Turn blue litmus paper red.
  • React with active metals to produce hydrogen gas.
  • React with bases (neutralization) to form salt and water.
  • React with carbonates to produce carbon dioxide.
  • Have pH < 7.
  • (State any two)

(ii) Atom Structure (Z=24, A=35):

(I) Protons and Neutrons:

  • Number of Protons = Atomic Number (Z) = 24.
  • Number of Neutrons = Mass Number (A) - Atomic Number (Z) = 35 - 24 = 11.

The atom has 24 protons and 11 neutrons.

This combination (24p, 11n) results in an extremely neutron-deficient isotope ($^{35}$Cr) which would be highly unstable and likely does not exist naturally. Standard Chromium isotopes are $^{50}$Cr, $^{52}$Cr, $^{53}$Cr, $^{54}$Cr.

(II) Electronic Structure (Configuration):

Number of electrons in a neutral atom = Number of protons = 24.

Electron configuration for Chromium (Z=24) - based on standard rules (including exception):

  • Shell 1 (n=1): 2 electrons ($1s^2$)
  • Shell 2 (n=2): 8 electrons ($2s^2 2p^6$)
  • Shell 3 (n=3): 13 electrons ($3s^2 3p^6 3d^5$) - Exception for stability
  • Shell 4 (n=4): 1 electron ($4s^1$) - Exception for stability

Configuration: **2, 8, 13, 1**. (Detailed: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6 3d^5 4s^1$)

(d) Refraction Calculation:

Given: Medium 1 = Air ($n_1 \approx 1.00$), Medium 2 = Glass ($n_2 = 1.55$).

Angle of incidence ($i$ or $\theta_1$) = $30^{\circ}$.

Find angle of refraction ($r$ or $\theta_2$).

Using Snell's Law: $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$.

$1.00 \times \sin(30^{\circ}) = 1.55 \times \sin(r)$.

$\sin(30^{\circ}) = 0.5$.

$1.00 \times 0.5 = 1.55 \sin(r)$.

$\sin(r) = 0.5 / 1.55 \approx 0.3226$.

$r = \arcsin(0.3226) \approx 18.81^{\circ}$.

The angle of refraction is approximately $18.8^{\circ}$.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Distinguish between the following types of waves, giving one example for each:

(i) mechanical and electromagnetic waves; (3 marks)

(ii) transverse and longitudinal waves. (3 marks)

(b) (i) Explain the term damping as applied to vibrations. (2 marks)

(ii) With the aid of sketches, distinguish between under damped and over damped vibrations. (4 marks)

(c) Two military boats are 240 m apart in a sea. A water wave traverses the sea such that when one boat is on a crest, the other is on a trough, with two other crests between the boats. The boats move up and down, 30 times per minute. Determine the:

(i) wavelength of the water wave; (4 marks)

(ii) speed of the wave in m/s. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Define the term homologous series as applied to organic compounds. (1 mark)

(ii) State four homologous series. (4 marks)

Answer

This question deals with classification of waves, damped oscillations, wave properties calculations, and homologous series in organic chemistry.

(a) Wave Classification:

(i) Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic Waves:

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a physical medium (solid, liquid, or gas) for propagation. Energy is transferred through the oscillation of particles of the medium. Example: Sound waves, water waves, waves on a string.
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require a medium; can travel through a vacuum. Consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. Travel at the speed of light in vacuum. Example: Light waves, radio waves, X-rays, microwaves.

(ii) Transverse vs. Longitudinal Waves:

  • Transverse Waves: Particle oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of wave energy propagation. Exhibit crests and troughs. Example: Light waves, waves on a rope shaken up and down.
  • Longitudinal Waves: Particle oscillations are parallel to the direction of wave energy propagation. Consist of compressions and rarefactions. Example: Sound waves in air.

(b) Damping:

(i) Damping Explanation: Damping is the process by which the amplitude of oscillations in a system gradually decreases over time due to energy dissipation caused by resistive forces, such as friction or air resistance.

(ii) Underdamped vs. Overdamped Sketches:

Distinction:

  • Underdamped: The system oscillates, but the amplitude decreases exponentially with time. It overshoots the equilibrium position before settling.
  • Overdamped: The system returns slowly to equilibrium without oscillating or overshooting. Damping is very strong.

(c) Water Wave Calculation:

Scenario: Boat 1 on Crest, Boat 2 on Trough. Distance = 240 m. Two other crests between them.

Visualisation: C (Boat1) ... C ... C ... T (Boat2)

The distance between consecutive crests is one wavelength ($\lambda$).

The distance between a crest and the next trough is $\lambda/2$.

Pattern: Crest - Trough ($\lambda/2$) - Crest ($\lambda/2$) - Trough ($\lambda/2$) - Crest ($\lambda/2$) ...

Distance from Crest 1 to Crest 2 = $\lambda$.

Distance from Crest 1 to Crest 3 = $2\lambda$.

Distance from Crest 1 to Trough after Crest 3 = $2\lambda + \lambda/2 = 2.5\lambda$.

The distance between Boat 1 (Crest) and Boat 2 (Trough), with 2 crests in between, covers 2 full wavelengths plus half a wavelength.

Distance = $\lambda + \lambda + \lambda/2 = 2.5 \lambda$.

$2.5 \lambda = 240$ m.

(i) Wavelength ($\lambda$):

$\lambda = 240 / 2.5 = 2400 / 25 = 96$ m.

The wavelength is 96 m.

Boats move up and down 30 times per minute. This gives the frequency (f).

Frequency (f) = 30 cycles / 60 seconds = 0.5 Hz.

(ii) Speed of the wave (v):

$v = f \lambda$.

$v = 0.5 \text{ Hz} \times 96 \text{ m} = 48 \text{ m/s}$.

The speed of the wave is 48 m/s.

(d) Homologous Series:

(i) Definition: A homologous series is a series of organic compounds having the same functional group and similar chemical properties, in which successive members differ by a constant relative molecular mass, typically corresponding to a $CH_2$ group.

(ii) Four Homologous Series:

  • Alkanes ($C_n H_{2n+2}$)
  • Alkenes ($C_n H_{2n}$)
  • Alkynes ($C_n H_{2n-2}$)
  • Alcohols ($C_n H_{2n+1} OH$)
  • Carboxylic Acids ($C_{n-1} H_{2n-1} COOH$ or $R-COOH$)
  • Aldehydes ($R-CHO$)
  • Ketones ($R-CO-R'$)
  • (State any four)

The question covers wave types, damping, wave speed calculations, and basic organic chemistry definitions.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Define the following terms and state their S.I units:

(i) momentum; (2 marks)

(ii) kinetic energy. (2 marks)

(b) A truck of mass of 8 tonnes travelling at $20 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$ collides with a stationary van of mass 4 tonnes. Determine the loss of kinetic energy if the:

(i) two vehicles move together after impact; (4 marks)

(ii) truck comes to rest on impact. (Assume perfectly inelastic impact where truck stops, van moves?) (3 marks)

Note: Part (b)(ii) seems physically unlikely unless the van is much heavier or anchored. Interpreting as: calculate KE loss assuming *only* the truck stops and the van moves off.

(c) (i) State the principle of conservation of energy. (2 marks)

(ii) Figure 1 shows a system of coplanar forces.

By constructing a polygon of the forces, determine the magnitude and direction of the resultant force. (7 marks)

Answer

This question covers definitions of momentum and kinetic energy, calculations involving inelastic collisions and energy loss, the principle of energy conservation, and finding the resultant of forces graphically.

(a) Definitions and Units:

(i) Momentum (p): A measure of the quantity of motion of an object. It is the product of the object's mass (m) and its velocity (v). It is a vector quantity. $p = mv$. SI Unit: kilogram meter per second (kg m/s).

(ii) Kinetic Energy (KE): The energy possessed by an object due to its motion. It depends on the object's mass (m) and speed (v). It is a scalar quantity. $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. SI Unit: Joule (J).

(b) Collision Calculation:

Given: Mass of truck ($m_t$) = 8 tonnes = 8000 kg.

Initial velocity of truck ($u_t$) = 20 m/s.

Mass of van ($m_v$) = 4 tonnes = 4000 kg.

Initial velocity of van ($u_v$) = 0 m/s (stationary).

Initial Total Kinetic Energy ($KE_i$):

$KE_i = KE_{truck} + KE_{van} = \frac{1}{2}m_t u_t^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_v u_v^2$.

$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}(8000)(20)^2 + \frac{1}{2}(4000)(0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(8000)(400) + 0 = 1,600,000$ J = 1.6 MJ.

(i) Vehicles Move Together (Perfectly Inelastic Collision):

Use conservation of momentum to find common final velocity ($v_f$).

$m_t u_t + m_v u_v = (m_t + m_v) v_f$.

$(8000)(20) + (4000)(0) = (8000 + 4000) v_f$.

$160000 = 12000 v_f$.

$v_f = 160000 / 12000 = 160 / 12 = 40 / 3 \approx 13.33$ m/s.

Final Total Kinetic Energy ($KE_f$):

$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(m_t + m_v) v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}(12000)(40/3)^2$.

$KE_f = 6000 \times (1600/9) = 3,200,000 / 3 \approx 1,066,667$ J $\approx$ 1.07 MJ.

Loss of Kinetic Energy ($\Delta KE$) = $KE_i - KE_f$.

$\Delta KE = 1,600,000 - 1,066,667 = 533,333$ J $\approx$ 0.533 MJ.

(ii) Truck Comes to Rest ($v_t = 0$):

This requires external forces or a different type of interaction. Assuming conservation of momentum still applies to the truck-van system *instantaneously* during collision, leading to the truck stopping requires the van to move very fast, possibly violating physical constraints. Let's assume momentum conservation applies to the system, find van's velocity if truck stops.

$m_t u_t + m_v u_v = m_t v_t + m_v v_v$.

$(8000)(20) + (4000)(0) = (8000)(0) + (4000) v_v$.

$160000 = 4000 v_v$.

$v_v = 160000 / 4000 = 40$ m/s.

Final Total Kinetic Energy ($KE'_f$) = $KE_{truck} + KE_{van} = 0 + \frac{1}{2}m_v v_v^2$.

$KE'_f = \frac{1}{2}(4000)(40)^2 = 2000 \times 1600 = 3,200,000$ J = 3.2 MJ.

Loss of Kinetic Energy ($\Delta KE'$) = $KE_i - KE'_f$.

$\Delta KE' = 1,600,000 - 3,200,000 = -1,600,000$ J = -1.6 MJ.

A negative loss means KE *increased*, which is impossible in this type of collision without external energy input (like an explosion). This scenario as interpreted violates energy principles or requires significant external work/force not accounted for. The question part (ii) is likely flawed.

Assuming the question meant "How much energy was dissipated *by the truck* as it came to rest?", the answer would be its initial KE = 1.6 MJ. But "loss of kinetic energy" usually refers to the system.

(c) Energy Conservation and Force Polygon:

(i) Principle of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another or transferred from one system to another. The total energy in an isolated system remains constant.

(ii) Resultant Force by Polygon Construction:

Forces: $F_1=10$N @ 30°, $F_2=15$N @ 120°, $F_3=20$N @ 225°.

Method: Draw forces head-to-tail using a chosen scale (e.g., 1 cm = 2 N) and protractor.

  1. Draw origin O.
  2. Draw vector OA representing $F_1$ (length 5 cm at 30° from horizontal).
  3. From point A, draw vector AB representing $F_2$ (length 7.5 cm at 120° from horizontal).
  4. From point B, draw vector BC representing $F_3$ (length 10 cm at 225° from horizontal).
  5. The resultant force $\vec{R}$ is the vector drawn from the origin O to the final point C ($\vec{R} = \vec{OC}$).
  6. Measure the length of OC and convert back using the scale to find the magnitude of R.
  7. Measure the angle that OC makes with the horizontal (positive x-axis) to find the direction of R.

Calculation (for verification):

$F_{1x} = 10 \cos 30 = 8.66$

$F_{1y} = 10 \sin 30 = 5.00$

$F_{2x} = 15 \cos 120 = -7.50$

$F_{2y} = 15 \sin 120 = 12.99$

$F_{3x} = 20 \cos 225 = -14.14$

$F_{3y} = 20 \sin 225 = -14.14$

$R_x = 8.66 - 7.50 - 14.14 = -12.98$ N

$R_y = 5.00 + 12.99 - 14.14 = 3.85$ N

$R = \sqrt{(-12.98)^2 + (3.85)^2} = \sqrt{168.48 + 14.82} = \sqrt{183.3} \approx 13.54$ N.

Angle $\phi$ with negative x-axis: $\tan\phi = |R_y/R_x| = 3.85/12.98 \approx 0.2966$. $\phi \approx 16.5^{\circ}$.

Direction $\theta = 180^{\circ} - \phi = 180 - 16.5 = 163.5^{\circ}$ from positive x-axis.

Graphical construction should yield Magnitude $\approx 13.5$ N, Direction $\approx 163.5^{\circ}$.

The question covers momentum, energy, collisions, and graphical vector addition.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Explain the function of an engine governor. (1 mark)

(ii) List three types of engine governors. (3 marks)

(b) (i) State the principles of Archimedes. (1 mark)

(ii) Explain the functions of the following components of a hydraulic coupling:

(I) shell; (1 mark)

(II) impeller; (1 mark)

(III) turbine. (1 mark)

(c) With the aid of a labelled Temperature-Entropy (T-S) diagram, outline the processes of an ideal carnot cycle for steam. (6 marks)

(d) A body of mass 0.4 kg is attached to a shaft. The distance from the shaft axis to the centre of gravity of the body is 50 mm. Determine the centrifugal force on the body when the shaft rotates at 1000 rev/min. (6 marks)

Answer

This question covers engine governors, Archimedes' principle, hydraulic couplings, the Carnot cycle on a T-S diagram, and centrifugal force calculation.

(a) Engine Governor:

(i) Function: An engine governor automatically controls the mean speed of an engine by regulating the supply of fuel (or working fluid) in response to changes in load, maintaining the speed within specified limits.

(ii) Types:

  • Centrifugal Governors (e.g., Watt, Porter, Proell, Hartnell)
  • Inertia Governors
  • Electronic Governors
  • (List any three specific types or categories)

(b) Archimedes' Principle and Hydraulic Coupling:

(i) Archimedes' Principle: When a body is wholly or partially immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force (upthrust) equal in magnitude to the weight of the fluid displaced by the body.

(ii) Hydraulic Coupling Components:

  • (I) Shell (Casing): The sealed housing that contains the working fluid (usually oil) and encloses the impeller and turbine, preventing leakage and contamination.
  • (II) Impeller (Pump): Connected to the input/driving shaft. Its vanes accelerate the working fluid outwards centrifugally as it rotates, imparting kinetic energy to the fluid.
  • (III) Turbine (Runner): Connected to the output/driven shaft. Its vanes receive the high-velocity fluid from the impeller. The impact and redirection of the fluid exerts a torque on the turbine, causing the output shaft to rotate.

(c) Carnot Cycle for Steam on T-S Diagram:

Outline of Processes:

  1. Isothermal Heat Addition (1-2): Saturated liquid water at pressure $P_1$ and high temperature $T_h$ absorbs heat ($Q_{in}$) from the high-temperature reservoir and vaporizes completely at constant temperature $T_h$ to become saturated steam (state 2). Entropy increases.
  2. Reversible Adiabatic (Isentropic) Expansion (2-3): The saturated steam expands adiabatically (e.g., in an ideal turbine) doing work. No heat is exchanged ($Q=0$), entropy remains constant ($S_3=S_2$). Temperature drops to the low temperature $T_l$. The steam becomes a wet mixture (state 3).
  3. Isothermal Heat Rejection (3-4): The wet steam mixture has heat ($Q_{out}$) rejected from it to the low-temperature reservoir at constant temperature $T_l$ (e.g., in a condenser), partially condensing it to a wetter state (state 4). Entropy decreases.
  4. Reversible Adiabatic (Isentropic) Compression (4-1): The wet steam mixture (state 4) is compressed adiabatically (e.g., by an ideal pump) back to the initial pressure $P_1$. No heat is exchanged ($Q=0$), entropy remains constant ($S_1=S_4$). Temperature rises back to $T_h$, and the substance becomes saturated liquid (state 1), completing the cycle.

(d) Centrifugal Force Calculation:

Given: Mass (m) = 0.4 kg.

Radius (r) = 50 mm = 0.05 m.

Rotational Speed (N) = 1000 rev/min.

Convert N to angular velocity $\omega$ in rad/s:

$\omega = N \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 1000 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = \frac{100\pi}{3}$ rad/s.

$\omega \approx 104.72$ rad/s.

Centrifugal Force ($F_c$) = $m \omega^2 r$.

$F_c = (0.4 \text{ kg}) \times (\frac{100\pi}{3} \text{ rad/s})^2 \times (0.05 \text{ m})$.

$F_c = 0.4 \times \frac{10000 \pi^2}{9} \times 0.05$.

$F_c = (0.4 \times 10000 \times 0.05 / 9) \times \pi^2 = (200/9) \times \pi^2$.

$F_c \approx 22.22 \times (9.8696) \approx 219.3$ N.

Centrifugal force $\approx 219$ N.

The question covers governors, fluid mechanics, thermodynamics cycles, and rotational dynamics.

(b) With the aid of a diagram, explain 'magnetic fringing' as applied to magnetic circuits. (4 marks)

(c) Describe the operation of the permanent magnet moving coil indicating instrument. (5 marks)

(d) With the aid of a circuit diagram, explain how a permanent magnet moving coil instrument can be converted to a multi-range ammeter. (5 marks)

Answer

This question covers magnetic-electric circuit analogies, magnetic fringing, PMMC instrument operation, and multi-range ammeter design.

(a) Electrical Analogy of Magnetic Quantities:

See completed table in the question section above.

(b) Magnetic Fringing:

Explanation: Magnetic fringing refers to the **bulging or spreading out** of magnetic flux lines as they cross an air gap in a magnetic circuit. Air has a much higher reluctance (lower permeability) than the ferromagnetic core material. Because flux lines tend to repel each other and prefer the path of least reluctance, they spread out into the area surrounding the gap instead of crossing straight across. This spreading means the effective cross-sectional area of the flux path in the gap is larger than the physical area of the core face, and the flux density within the gap is reduced compared to what it would be without fringing. Fringing effects are more pronounced for wider air gaps.

(c) Operation of Permanent Magnet Moving Coil (PMMC) Instrument:

Principle: Based on the motor effect - a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force.

Operation:

  1. A lightweight rectangular coil, wound on an aluminium former, is pivoted to rotate freely within the strong, radial magnetic field produced by a permanent magnet and soft iron pole pieces/core.
  2. The current to be measured (or a current proportional to the voltage) is passed through the coil via two spiral hairsprings.
  3. The current flowing through the coil sides interacts with the magnetic field, producing forces ($F=BIlN$) according to Fleming's Left-Hand Rule. These forces create a deflecting torque ($T_d$) that rotates the coil.
  4. Since the field is radial and uniform, the deflecting torque is directly proportional to the current ($T_d \propto I$).
  5. The hairsprings provide a controlling torque ($T_c$) that opposes the deflection and is proportional to the angle of rotation ($T_c \propto \theta$).
  6. The coil rotates until the deflecting torque equals the controlling torque ($T_d = T_c$), so $I \propto \theta$. The pointer attached to the coil thus moves over a uniformly calibrated (linear) scale.
  7. The aluminium former provides eddy current damping, making the pointer settle quickly. PMMC instruments respond only to DC.

(d) Multi-Range Ammeter Conversion:

A PMMC instrument (galvanometer) with FSD current $I_m$ and internal resistance $R_m$ can be converted into an ammeter by connecting a low-resistance **shunt resistor** ($R_{sh}$) in parallel with the meter movement. To create multiple ranges, several shunts can be selected using a switch.

Explanation (Universal Shunt):

  1. A set of shunt resistors ($R_{sh1}, R_{sh2}, R_{sh3}$, etc.) are connected in series to form a shunt chain across the meter movement ($R_m$).
  2. A multi-position switch selects the point where the total input current (I) enters the shunt chain.
  3. When the switch is at a particular range setting, the total current I divides between the meter movement (carrying $I_m$) and the effective shunt path.
  4. For each range, the value of the effective shunt resistance ($R_{sh\_eff}$) is calculated such that when the total input current I corresponds to the desired full-scale range, the current through the meter movement is exactly $I_m$.
  5. The voltage across the meter ($V_m = I_m R_m$) must equal the voltage across the effective shunt ($V_{sh} = I_{sh} R_{sh\_eff}$), where $I_{sh} = I - I_m$.
  6. By selecting different tap points with the switch, different total currents (ranges) correspond to the same meter FSD current ($I_m$), allowing multiple ranges from a single meter movement and shunt chain. The Ayrton shunt provides make-before-break switching, protecting the meter.

The question covers magnetic circuit analogies, fringing effect, PMMC operation, and multi-range ammeter design using shunts.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(b) The resistance of a coil of nickel wire at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is $100 \Omega$. The temperature of the wire is increased and the resistance rises to $130 \Omega$. If the temperature coefficient of resistance of nickel is $0.006 /{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, determine the temperature of the wire when its resistance is $130 \Omega$. (4 marks)

(c) Explain three safety precautions to be taken when charging secondary cells. (6 marks)

(d) Ten 1.5 V cells, each having an internal resistance of $0.1 \Omega$ are connected in series to a load of $14 \Omega$. Determine the:

Answer

This question covers definitions of resistivity and temperature coefficient, resistance calculation with temperature change, battery charging safety, and analysis of cells connected in series.

(a) Electrical Material Terms:

(i) Resistivity ($\rho$): An intrinsic property of a material that measures how strongly it resists the flow of electric current. It is defined as the resistance (R) of a conductor of unit length (L) and unit cross-sectional area (A). $R = \rho L / A \implies \rho = RA/L$. Unit: Ohm-meter ($\Omega \cdot m$).

(ii) Temperature Coefficient of Resistance ($\alpha$): A measure of the fractional change in resistance of a material per degree Celsius (or Kelvin) change in temperature, relative to its resistance at a specified reference temperature. $\alpha = \frac{R_T - R_{ref}}{R_{ref}(T - T_{ref})}$. Unit: per degree Celsius (/$^{\circ}$C) or per Kelvin (/K).

(b) Resistance Change Calculation:

Given: Resistance at $T_1 = 20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_{20}$) = 100 $\Omega$.

Final Resistance ($R_T$) = 130 $\Omega$.

Temperature coefficient at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($\alpha_{20}$) = $0.006 /{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

Find the final temperature ($T$).

Use the formula: $R_T = R_{20} [1 + \alpha_{20} (T - T_1)]$.

$130 = 100 [1 + 0.006 (T - 20)]$.

$130 / 100 = 1 + 0.006 (T - 20)$.

$1.3 = 1 + 0.006 (T - 20)$.

$1.3 - 1 = 0.006 (T - 20)$.

$0.3 = 0.006 (T - 20)$.

$T - 20 = 0.3 / 0.006 = 300 / 6 = 50$.

$T = 50 + 20 = 70^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

The temperature of the wire is $70^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

(c) Safety Precautions for Charging Secondary Cells:

  1. Good Ventilation: Charge in a well-ventilated area to disperse potentially explosive gases (like hydrogen released from lead-acid batteries) safely.
  2. Correct Polarity: Always connect the charger positive (+) terminal to the battery positive (+) terminal and charger negative (-) to battery negative (-). Incorrect connection can damage the battery and charger.
  3. Avoid Sparks/Flames: Keep sparks, flames, and smoking materials away from charging batteries, especially lead-acid types, due to the risk of hydrogen gas ignition.
  4. Use Correct Charger: Use a charger specifically designed for the battery type (e.g., lead-acid, Li-ion) and voltage/capacity rating. Using an incorrect charger can lead to overheating, damage, or fire.
  5. Monitor Temperature: Avoid charging batteries when they are excessively hot or cold. Discontinue charging if the battery becomes abnormally hot.

(d) Cells in Series Circuit Calculation:

Given: Number of cells (N) = 10.

EMF per cell ($E_{cell}$) = 1.5 V.

Internal resistance per cell ($r_{cell}$) = 0.1 $\Omega$.

Load resistance ($R_L$) = 14 $\Omega$.

Cells connected in series.

Total EMF ($E_{total}$) = $N \times E_{cell} = 10 \times 1.5 = 15 V$.

Total internal resistance ($r_{total}$) = $N \times r_{cell} = 10 \times 0.1 = 1.0 \Omega$.

Total circuit resistance ($R_{circuit}$) = $R_L + r_{total} = 14 + 1.0 = 15 \Omega$.

(i) Current flowing in the circuit (I):

$I = E_{total} / R_{circuit} = 15 V / 15 \Omega = 1.0 A$.

(ii) Potential difference at the battery terminals ($V_T$):

$V_T$ (Terminal Voltage) = $E_{total} - I \times r_{total}$.

$V_T = 15 V - (1.0 A \times 1.0 \Omega) = 15 - 1.0 = 14.0 V$.

(Alternatively, $V_T = I \times R_L = 1.0 A \times 14 \Omega = 14.0 V$).

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) State three:

(i) methods of varying the capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor; (3 marks)

(ii) applications of transformers. (3 marks)

(b) With the aid of a labelled circuit diagram, describe the transformer short-circuit test. (8 marks)

(c) Two capacitors are connected in series across a $110 \mathrm{~V}$ d.c supply. If the capacitors have capacitance $10 \mu \mathrm{F}$ and $100 \mu \mathrm{F}$, determine the:

(i) total circuit capacitance; (2 marks)

(ii) charge in the circuit; (2 marks)

(iii) energy stored by the $100 \mu \mathrm{F}$ capacitor. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers factors affecting capacitance, transformer applications and testing, and analysis of series capacitor circuits.

(a) Capacitance Variation and Transformer Applications:

(i) Methods of Varying Capacitance (Parallel Plate):

  • Changing Overlapping Area (A): Sliding one set of plates relative to another changes the effective area A (used in variable tuning capacitors). $C \propto A$.
  • Changing Distance (d): Altering the separation between the plates (less common for variable capacitors). $C \propto 1/d$.
  • Changing Dielectric Material ($\epsilon_r$): Inserting or adjusting the position of a dielectric material between the plates (used in trimmer capacitors). $C \propto \epsilon_r$.

(ii) Applications of Transformers:

  • Power Transmission/Distribution: Stepping up voltage for efficient long-distance transmission and stepping down for distribution and consumer use.
  • Electronics Power Supplies: Stepping down mains voltage for low-voltage electronic circuits (often followed by rectification and smoothing).
  • Impedance Matching: Matching the impedance of a source to a load for maximum power transfer (e.g., audio amplifier to speaker).
  • Electrical Isolation: Providing electrical separation between circuits for safety or noise reduction (using a 1:1 ratio transformer).
  • Instrumentation: Current Transformers (CTs) and Potential Transformers (PTs) for measuring high currents and voltages safely.

(b) Transformer Short-Circuit Test:

Purpose: To determine the equivalent series impedance ($R_{eq}, X_{eq}$) and the full-load copper losses ($P_{cu}$) of a transformer.

Procedure:

  1. The Low Voltage (LV) winding is short-circuited using a thick conductor or ammeter.
  2. A low, variable AC voltage (typically 5-10% of rated voltage) at rated frequency is applied to the High Voltage (HV) winding.
  3. Instruments are connected to the HV side: An Ammeter (A) in series, a Voltmeter (V) in parallel, and a Wattmeter (W) to measure input voltage ($V_{sc}$), current ($I_{sc}$), and power ($W_{sc}$ or $P_{sc}$).
  4. The applied voltage ($V_{sc}$) is gradually increased until the ammeter ($I_{sc}$) reads the rated full-load current for the HV winding.
  5. Readings of $V_{sc}$, $I_{sc}$, and $P_{sc}$ are taken at this rated current.
Explanation: Since the applied voltage is very low, the magnetic flux in the core is small, and the core losses (hysteresis and eddy current) are negligible ($P_i \approx 0$). Therefore, the wattmeter reading ($P_{sc}$) represents almost entirely the full-load copper losses ($P_{cu}$) in both windings (referred to the HV side). Calculations:
  • Full Load Copper Loss $P_{cu} \approx P_{sc}$.
  • Equivalent Impedance referred to HV side ($Z_{eq,HV}$) = $V_{sc} / I_{sc}$.
  • Equivalent Resistance referred to HV side ($R_{eq,HV}$) = $P_{sc} / I_{sc}^2$.
  • Equivalent Reactance referred to HV side ($X_{eq,HV}$) = $\sqrt{Z_{eq,HV}^2 - R_{eq,HV}^2}$.

(c) Series Capacitor Circuit Calculation:

Given: $C_1 = 10 \mu F$, $C_2 = 100 \mu F$. Connected in series.

Supply voltage ($V_S$) = 110 V DC.

Note: For DC supply, capacitors act as open circuits once charged. Charge calculation assumes charging process.

(i) Total Circuit Capacitance ($C_{eq}$):

$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{100}$ (working in $\mu F$).

$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{10 + 1}{100} = \frac{11}{100}$.

$C_{eq} = \frac{100}{11} \mu F \approx 9.09 \mu F$.

(ii) Charge in the Circuit (Q):

The total charge stored in the series combination after charging is complete.

$Q = C_{eq} \times V_S = (\frac{100}{11} \times 10^{-6} \text{ F}) \times 110 \text{ V}$.

$Q = \frac{100 \times 110}{11} \times 10^{-6} = 100 \times 10 \times 10^{-6} = 1000 \times 10^{-6}$ C = 1000 $\mu$C (or 1 mC).

The charge on each capacitor in series is the same: $Q_1 = Q_2 = Q = 1000 \mu C$.

(iii) Energy stored by the $100 \mu \mathrm{F}$ capacitor ($E_2$):

Energy $E = Q^2 / (2C)$.

$E_2 = Q_2^2 / (2 C_2) = (1000 \times 10^{-6} C)^2 / (2 \times 100 \times 10^{-6} F)$.

$E_2 = (10^{-3})^2 / (200 \times 10^{-6}) = 10^{-6} / (2 \times 10^{-4}) = 0.5 \times 10^{-2}$ J = 0.005 J (or 5 mJ).

(Alternatively, find $V_2 = Q_2/C_2 = (1000 \mu C) / (100 \mu F) = 10 V$. Then $E_2 = \frac{1}{2} C_2 V_2^2 = \frac{1}{2} (100 \times 10^{-6})(10)^2 = 50 \times 10^{-6} \times 100 = 5000 \times 10^{-6} = 0.005 J$).

Answer

This question covers the definition of EMF, analysis of a series RC circuit, and analysis of a series-parallel DC circuit (requires missing information).

(a) Electromotive Force (EMF):

Definition: Electromotive force (EMF) is the energy provided by a source (like a battery or generator) per unit charge passing through it. It represents the total voltage or potential difference the source can produce when no current is flowing (open circuit voltage). It is the driving force that tends to cause current flow in a circuit.

SI Unit: Volt (V), which is equivalent to Joule per Coulomb (J/C).

(b) Series RC Circuit Analysis:

Given: Capacitive Reactance ($X_C$) = $30 \Omega$, Resistance (R) = $40 \Omega$. Circuit current (I) = 4.6 A, Frequency (f) = 50 Hz.

(i) Supply Voltage (V):

First find the total impedance (Z) of the series circuit.

$Z = \sqrt{R^2 + X_C^2}$ (Magnitude).

$Z = \sqrt{40^2 + 30^2} = \sqrt{1600 + 900} = \sqrt{2500} = 50 \Omega$.

Supply Voltage (V) = Current (I) $\times$ Impedance (Z).

$V = 4.6 A \times 50 \Omega = 230 V$.

(ii) Circuit Power Factor (PF):

$PF = \cos \phi = R / Z$.

$PF = 40 \Omega / 50 \Omega = 0.8$.

Since the circuit contains capacitance, the current leads the voltage. Power Factor = 0.8 leading.

(iii) Capacitance of the Capacitor (C):

$X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}$.

$30 \Omega = \frac{1}{2\pi (50 Hz) C} = \frac{1}{100\pi C}$.

$C = \frac{1}{100\pi \times 30} = \frac{1}{3000\pi}$.

$C \approx \frac{1}{9424.8} \approx 1.061 \times 10^{-4}$ F.

$C \approx 106.1 \times 10^{-6}$ F or 106.1 $\mu$F.

(c) DC Series-Parallel Circuit Analysis (Fig 2):

Cannot solve without the supply voltage (V). Assuming a value V allows demonstrating the method.

Given: Supply Current ($I_{total}$) = 4.5 A. $R_1 = 6\Omega$, $R_2 = 12\Omega$. $R_X$ is unknown. Supply Voltage V is unknown.

Method if V was known:

(i) Total Resistance ($R_{total}$):

$R_{total} = V / I_{total}$. (Requires V).

(ii) Value of Resistor $R_X$:

First find the equivalent resistance of the parallel part ($R_P$):

$R_P = (R_1 \times R_2) / (R_1 + R_2) = (6 \times 12) / (6 + 12) = 72 / 18 = 4 \Omega$.

Total resistance $R_{total} = R_X + R_P = R_X + 4$.

So, $R_X = R_{total} - 4$. (Requires $R_{total}$, which requires V).

(iii) Power dissipated by the $6 \Omega$ resistor ($P_6$):

Need the current through the $6 \Omega$ resistor ($I_6$). Use the current divider rule:

$I_6 = I_{total} \times \frac{R_{other}}{R_{other} + R_6} = 4.5 A \times \frac{12}{12 + 6} = 4.5 \times \frac{12}{18} = 4.5 \times \frac{2}{3} = 3 A$.

Power $P_6 = I_6^2 \times R_1 = (3 A)^2 \times 6 \Omega = 9 \times 6 = 54 W$.
