Question 1 (20 marks)

An aqueous solution of zinc sulphate is electrolysed using platinum electrodes as shown in the setup of Figure 1.

(a) (i) write an ionic equation for the reaction taking place at electrode A; (1 mark)

(ii) identify electrode B; (1 mark)

(iii) explain the observations at the electrode B if a copper plate was used instead of platinum electrode; (1 mark)

(iv) draw one set of apparatus that would be used instead of the graduated syringe. (1 mark)

(b) 0.22 g of metal P are deposited by electrolysis when a current of 0.06 amperes flows for 99 minutes (P=184, F=96,500 C). Determine:

(i) the number of moles of electrons passed; (2 marks)

(ii) the number of moles of metal P deposited; (2 marks)

(iii) the charge on the ion of metal P. (2 marks)

(c) Figure 2 below shows the arrangements of apparatus that could be used to prepare ethene gas:

(i) name the reagent A and state its two functions in this reaction; (3 marks)

(ii) explain why sand granules are put in the reaction flask; (1 mark)

(iii) name the alkali used in flask K and state its function; (2 marks)

(iv) explain why it is preferable to use a sand bath instead of heating the reaction flask directly with a bunsen burner. (1 mark)

Note: The MMD OCR seems to have duplicated part (c) in the source text. It is included once here.

Answer

This question covers electrolysis of aqueous solutions, Faraday's laws calculations, and the preparation of ethene gas.

(a) Electrolysis of Zinc Sulphate ($ZnSO_4(aq)$):

Ions present: $Zn^{2+}(aq)$, $SO_4^{2-}(aq)$, $H^+(aq)$, $OH^-(aq)$ (from water ionization).

(i) Reaction at Electrode A (Anode - Positive):

$SO_4^{2-}$ and $OH^-$ migrate to the anode. $OH^-$ ions are discharged preferentially over $SO_4^{2-}$ ions (unless the electrode is reactive, but platinum is inert).

Ionic Equation: $4OH^-(aq) \longrightarrow 2H_2O(l) + O_2(g) + 4e^-$

Observation: Bubbles of a colorless gas (oxygen) are produced.

(ii) Identity of Electrode B (Cathode - Negative):

Electrode B is the Cathode.

(iii) Observation at Cathode B if Copper Plate Used:

$Zn^{2+}$ and $H^+$ migrate to the cathode. Comparing standard reduction potentials: $Zn^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow Zn$ ($E^0 = -0.76V$), $2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2$ ($E^0 = 0.00V$). Hydrogen ions ($H^+$) are more easily reduced than Zinc ions ($Zn^{2+}$). Therefore, hydrogen gas will be produced, regardless of whether the cathode is platinum or copper (as copper is also less reactive than hydrogen).

Observation: Bubbles of a colorless gas (hydrogen) are produced. $2H^+(aq) + 2e^- \longrightarrow H_2(g)$

(iv) Apparatus instead of Syringe:

An inverted measuring cylinder or gas burette filled with water can be placed over electrode B to collect and measure the volume of hydrogen gas produced.

(b) Electrolysis Calculation:

Given: mass $m=0.22$ g, current $I=0.06$ A, time $t=99$ minutes = $99 \times 60 = 5940$ s, Molar mass $M=184$ g/mol, Faraday constant $F=96500$ C/mol.

(i) Moles of electrons passed:

Quantity of charge $Q = I \times t = 0.06 \text{ A} \times 5940 \text{ s} = 356.4 \text{ C}$.

Moles of electrons = $Q / F = 356.4 \text{ C} / 96500 \text{ C/mol} \approx 0.00369 \text{ moles}$.

(ii) Moles of metal P deposited:

Moles of P = mass / Molar mass = $0.22 \text{ g} / 184 \text{ g/mol} \approx 0.001196 \text{ moles}$.

(iii) Charge on the ion of metal P:

Let the charge on the ion be $+z$. The deposition reaction is $P^{z+} + ze^- \longrightarrow P$.

From stoichiometry, $\frac{\text{moles of electrons}}{\text{moles of P deposited}} = \frac{z}{1}$.

$z = \frac{0.00369 \text{ moles}}{0.001196 \text{ moles}} \approx 3.085$

Since the charge must be an integer, the charge on the ion of metal P is +3.

(c) Preparation of Ethene:

This reaction involves the dehydration of ethanol ($C_2H_5OH$) to form ethene ($C_2H_4$).

(i) Reagent A and Functions:

Reagent A is concentrated Sulphuric acid ($H_2SO_4$).

Functions:

  1. Dehydrating agent: Removes a molecule of water from ethanol.
  2. Catalyst: Speeds up the dehydration reaction (typically heated to around 170°C).

(ii) Sand Granules:

Sand granules (or porcelain chips) are added to ensure smooth boiling of the liquid mixture (ethanol and conc. acid) and prevent bumping, which can occur due to superheating.

(iii) Alkali in Flask K and Function:

Alkali used: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution or Potassium hydroxide (KOH) solution.

Function: To absorb (scrub) acidic impurities produced during the reaction, such as sulphur dioxide ($SO_2$) from the reduction of sulphuric acid or carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) from excessive oxidation/charring.

(iv) Preference for Sand Bath:

A sand bath provides more uniform and controlled heating compared to direct heating with a Bunsen burner. This helps maintain the reaction temperature within the optimal range (around 170°C for ethene production) and minimizes side reactions like the formation of diethyl ether (at lower temperatures, ~140°C) or charring (at excessively high temperatures).

The question covers key aspects of electrolysis and a standard organic preparation method.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Sketch a graph of displacement (y-axis) against time of a wave of amplitude 1.0 cm and frequency 4.0 Hz over a time interval of 1.0 s. (4 marks)

(b) Describe with the aid of a diagram how you would produce stationary waves on a string. (5 marks)

(c) Figure 3 shows a series of wave fronts, one wavelength apart, approaching a narrow slit in a ripple tank.

(i) explain what is meant by a wave front; (1 mark)

(ii) add three more wave fronts to each diagram (a) and (b) shown in figure 3 to show what happens as the wave fronts pass through the slits. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Define Simple Harmonic Motion; (1 mark)

(ii) Identify the general conditions of Simple Harmonic Motion; (2 marks)

(iii) State the meaning of the following terms as applied to Simple Harmonic Motion: (3 marks)

I amplitude;

II periodic time;

III frequency.

Answer

This question explores wave properties, stationary waves, diffraction, and simple harmonic motion.

(a) Displacement-Time Graph:

Given: Amplitude A = 1.0 cm, Frequency f = 4.0 Hz.

Period T = 1/f = 1/4.0 = 0.25 s.

The graph is a sine or cosine wave. Assuming it starts at y=0 at t=0 and moves positive (sine wave): $y = A \sin(2\pi f t) = 1.0 \sin(8\pi t)$.

Over 1.0 s, there will be $1.0 / 0.25 = 4$ complete cycles.

(b) Production of Stationary Waves on a String:

A stationary wave (or standing wave) is formed by the superposition (interference) of two progressive waves of the same frequency, amplitude, and speed travelling in opposite directions.

Method:

  1. Fix a string or elastic cord at one end (P).
  2. Attach the other end (Q) to a vibrator (e.g., driven by a signal generator) which oscillates perpendicularly to the string.
  3. Adjust the frequency of the vibrator and/or the tension in the string.
  4. Waves travel from the vibrator (Q) towards the fixed end (P), reflect off P with a phase change of 180°, and travel back towards Q.
  5. The incident waves from Q and the reflected waves from P interfere.
  6. At specific frequencies (resonant frequencies), the interference results in a stable pattern of nodes (points of zero amplitude) and antinodes (points of maximum amplitude). This pattern is the stationary wave.

(c) Wave Fronts and Diffraction:

(i) Wave Front:

A wave front is an imaginary line or surface connecting points of a wave that are in the same phase of oscillation (e.g., points that are all at a crest or all at a trough simultaneously).

(ii) Wave Fronts After Slits:

Explanation:

  • In (a) with the wide slit, the wave fronts pass through largely undisturbed, with only slight curving (diffraction) at the edges.
  • In (b) with the narrow slit (width comparable to the wavelength), the slit acts like a point source according to Huygens' principle, and the wave fronts spread out significantly in semi-circular patterns. This phenomenon is called diffraction.

(d) Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM):

(i) Definition:

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a specific type of periodic motion or oscillation where the restoring force (and hence acceleration) is directly proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium position and is always directed towards that equilibrium position.

Mathematically: $F = -kx$ or $a = -\omega^2 x$, where $k$ and $\omega^2$ are positive constants.

(ii) General Conditions:

  • A stable equilibrium position must exist.
  • There must be a restoring force acting on the object when it is displaced from equilibrium.
  • The restoring force must be directly proportional to the displacement ($F \propto x$).
  • The restoring force must always act in the opposite direction to the displacement (towards equilibrium).
  • Effects of friction/damping are negligible for ideal SHM.

(iii) Terminology:

  • Amplitude (A): The maximum displacement of the oscillating object from its equilibrium position.
  • Periodic Time (T): The time taken for one complete oscillation or cycle.
  • Frequency (f): The number of complete oscillations or cycles that occur per unit time (usually per second). It is the reciprocal of the periodic time ($f = 1/T$). Unit: Hertz (Hz).

The question covers fundamental concepts of wave behavior and simple harmonic motion.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Define the following:

(i) potential energy; (1 mark)

(ii) kinetic energy; (1 mark)

(iii) the law of conservation of energy. (2 marks)

(b) A pile of mass $\frac{3}{4}$ tonne can just support a stationary mass of 40 tonne without subsidence. The mass is removed and the pile is driven to a greater depth by blows of a 2 tonne hammer dropping on the top of the pile from a height of 1.22 m. The hammer does not rebound from the top of the pile. Calculate the:

Assumptions: 1 tonne = 1000 kg. "Support a mass of 40 tonne" implies the ground resistance equals the weight of 40 tonnes. g = 9.81 m/s².

(i) penetration per blow, assuming that the ground resistance is constant; (6 marks)

(ii) energy lost per blow, and; (4 marks)

(iii) efficiency of the operation. (4 marks)

Answer

This question involves definitions of energy and its conservation, followed by a calculation involving impact, work, and energy in a pile driving scenario.

(a) Energy Definitions:

(i) Potential Energy (PE): Energy possessed by an object by virtue of its position or configuration. Examples include gravitational potential energy (due to height) and elastic potential energy (due to deformation).

(ii) Kinetic Energy (KE): Energy possessed by an object by virtue of its motion. For an object of mass $m$ moving with velocity $v$, $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

(iii) Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another or transferred from one system to another. The total energy in an isolated system remains constant.

(b) Pile Driver Calculation:

Given:

  • Mass of pile ($m_p$) = $\frac{3}{4}$ tonne = 750 kg
  • Mass of hammer ($m_h$) = 2 tonne = 2000 kg
  • Drop height (h) = 1.22 m
  • Mass supported by pile = 40 tonne = 40000 kg
  • Ground resistance (R) = Weight of 40 tonnes = $40000 \times g = 40000 \times 9.81 = 392400$ N
  • Impact is inelastic (hammer does not rebound, they move together initially).

Step 1: Velocity of hammer just before impact ($u_h$).

Using conservation of energy (PE to KE): $m_h g h = \frac{1}{2} m_h u_h^2$.

$u_h = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 1.22} = \sqrt{23.9364} \approx 4.892 \text{ m/s}$.

Step 2: Velocity of hammer and pile immediately after impact ($v_{common}$).

Using conservation of linear momentum for the inelastic collision:

$m_h u_h + m_p (0) = (m_h + m_p) v_{common}$

$2000 \times 4.892 = (2000 + 750) v_{common}$

$9784 = 2750 v_{common}$

$v_{common} = 9784 / 2750 \approx 3.558 \text{ m/s}$.

Step 3: Penetration (d).

Use the work-energy principle during penetration. The initial kinetic energy of the combined hammer and pile is dissipated by the net work done against resistance and gravity.

Initial KE of system = $\frac{1}{2}(m_h + m_p) v_{common}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2750)(3.558)^2 \approx 17394 \text{ J}$.

Work done by gravity during penetration = $(m_h + m_p) g d = (2750)(9.81)d \approx 26978 d$.

Work done against ground resistance = $R \times d = 392400 d$.

Net work done = Change in KE

Work done by gravity - Work done against resistance = $KE_{final} - KE_{initial}$

$26978 d - 392400 d = 0 - 17394$

$-365422 d = -17394$

$d = 17394 / 365422 \approx 0.0476 \text{ m}$.

(i) Penetration per blow $\approx 0.0476$ m or 4.76 cm.

(ii) Energy lost per blow:

This usually refers to the energy lost *during the impact* due to inelastic collision.

Initial KE of hammer = $\frac{1}{2} m_h u_h^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2000)(4.892)^2 \approx 23936 \text{ J}$.

Initial KE of pile = 0 J.

Total KE before impact = 23936 J.

Total KE immediately after impact = $\frac{1}{2}(m_h + m_p) v_{common}^2 \approx 17394 \text{ J}$.

Energy lost during impact = KE before - KE after = $23936 - 17394 = 6542 \text{ J}$.

Alternatively, if "energy lost per blow" means the total energy dissipated against ground resistance, it would be $W_{resistance} = R \times d = 392400 \times 0.0476 \approx 18678$ J. However, loss during impact is the standard interpretation.

Energy lost per blow (during impact) $\approx 6542$ J.

(iii) Efficiency of the operation:

Efficiency ($\eta$) = (Useful work done) / (Energy input)

Energy input = Potential energy of the hammer before dropping = $m_h g h = 23936$ J.

Useful work done = Work done in penetrating the pile against the ground resistance = Energy available immediately after impact (which is dissipated during penetration) = $17394$ J.

Efficiency $\eta = \frac{17394}{23936} \approx 0.7267$

Efficiency $\approx 72.7\%$.

The calculation involves applying principles of energy conservation, momentum conservation, and the work-energy theorem.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Three forces of 2P, 3P and 4P act along the three sides of an equilateral triangle of side 100 mm taken in order. Find the magnitude and position of the resultant force. (10 marks)

(b) A 4 Mg body, A, travelling at 18 m/s runs into the back of another 6 Mg body, B, travelling at 7 m/s in the same direction. Calculate the:

Assumption: Mg = Megagram = $10^6$ g = 1000 kg = 1 tonne.

(i) momentum of each vehicle before impact; (4 marks)

(ii) final velocity of the bodies after impact assuming the bodies remain locked together. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves finding the resultant of forces acting along the sides of a triangle and calculating momentum and velocity in an inelastic collision.

(a) Resultant Force on Equilateral Triangle:

Let the vertices be 1, 2, 3. Side length L = 100 mm = 0.1 m. Forces: $F_1=2P$ (along side 1-2), $F_2=3P$ (along side 2-3), $F_3=4P$ (along side 3-1). Let side 1-2 lie along the positive x-axis, with vertex 1 at the origin (0,0).

Coordinates: Vertex 1=(0,0), Vertex 2=(0.1, 0), Vertex 3=(0.1 * cos(60°), 0.1 * sin(60°)) = (0.05, 0.1*√3/2) = (0.05, 0.0866).

Resolve forces into components:

  • $F_1 = 2P \hat{i} + 0 \hat{j}$ (Acts along +x axis)
  • $F_2$: Acts from (0.1, 0) to (0.05, 0.0866). Angle with +x axis is $120^{\circ}$. $F_{2x} = 3P \cos(120^{\circ}) = 3P(-0.5) = -1.5P$ $F_{2y} = 3P \sin(120^{\circ}) = 3P(\sqrt{3}/2) \approx 2.598P$
  • $F_3$: Acts from (0.05, 0.0866) to (0,0). Angle with +x axis is $240^{\circ}$ (or $-120^{\circ}$). $F_{3x} = 4P \cos(240^{\circ}) = 4P(-0.5) = -2.0P$ $F_{3y} = 4P \sin(240^{\circ}) = 4P(-\sqrt{3}/2) \approx -3.464P$

Resultant force components:

$R_x = F_{1x} + F_{2x} + F_{3x} = 2P - 1.5P - 2.0P = -1.5P$

$R_y = F_{1y} + F_{2y} + F_{3y} = 0 + 2.598P - 3.464P = -0.866P$ (which is $-P\sqrt{3}/2$)

Magnitude of Resultant (R):

$R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} = \sqrt{(-1.5P)^2 + (-0.866P)^2}$

$R = \sqrt{2.25 P^2 + 0.75 P^2} = \sqrt{3.00 P^2} = P\sqrt{3}$

Magnitude $R = P\sqrt{3} \approx 1.732 P$.

Position of Resultant Force (Line of Action):

We use Varignon's Theorem (Principle of Moments). Calculate the sum of moments of the original forces about the origin (Vertex 1) and equate it to the moment of the resultant about the origin.

Moment of $F_1$ about origin = 0 (passes through origin).

Moment of $F_2$ about origin: Line passes through (0.1, 0) at 120°. Perpendicular distance from origin: $d_2 = 0.1 \times \sin(120^{\circ}) = 0.1 \times \sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.0866$ m. Moment $M_2 = F_2 \times d_2 = 3P \times 0.0866 \approx 0.2598 P$ (Anticlockwise = positive).

Moment of $F_3$ about origin = 0 (passes through origin).

Total Moment $M_{total} = 0.2598 P$.

Let the resultant force R act along a line whose perpendicular distance from the origin is $d$.

Moment of Resultant $M_R = R \times d = (P\sqrt{3}) \times d$.

$M_{total} = M_R \implies 0.2598 P = (P\sqrt{3}) d$.

$d = 0.2598 / \sqrt{3} = 0.2598 / 1.732 \approx 0.15$ m.

Direction of Resultant: The angle $\theta$ with the positive x-axis.

$\tan \phi = |R_y / R_x| = |-0.866P / -1.5P| = 0.5773$. $\phi = 30^{\circ}$.

Since both $R_x$ and $R_y$ are negative, the angle is in the third quadrant.

$\theta = 180^{\circ} + 30^{\circ} = 210^{\circ}$ (or $-150^{\circ}$).

The resultant is $P\sqrt{3}$ acting at $210^{\circ}$ to the direction of the 2P force, along a line passing 0.15 m perpendicularly from the origin.

(b) Collision Calculation:

Given:

  • Mass of body A ($m_A$) = 4 Mg = 4000 kg
  • Initial velocity of A ($u_A$) = 18 m/s
  • Mass of body B ($m_B$) = 6 Mg = 6000 kg
  • Initial velocity of B ($u_B$) = 7 m/s (same direction)
  • Collision is inelastic (bodies lock together).

(i) Momentum before impact:

Momentum of A ($p_A$) = $m_A u_A = 4000 \text{ kg} \times 18 \text{ m/s} = 72000 \text{ kg m/s}$.

Momentum of B ($p_B$) = $m_B u_B = 6000 \text{ kg} \times 7 \text{ m/s} = 42000 \text{ kg m/s}$.

(ii) Final velocity after impact ($v_{final}$):

Using conservation of linear momentum:

Total momentum before = Total momentum after

$p_A + p_B = (m_A + m_B) v_{final}$

$72000 + 42000 = (4000 + 6000) v_{final}$

$114000 = 10000 v_{final}$

$v_{final} = 114000 / 10000 = 11.4 \text{ m/s}$.

The final common velocity of the locked bodies is 11.4 m/s in the original direction.

The question combines vector addition of forces and application of the conservation of momentum principle.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Define a magnet. (1 mark)

(ii) State three properties of magnetic lines of force. (3 marks)

(b) State three differences between electric and magnetic fields. (6 marks)

(c) Sketch the magnetic field pattern associated with a solenoid connected to a battery and wound on an iron bar. Show the direction of the field. (4 marks)

(d) Draw a dry leclanché cell and label the essential parts. (6 marks)

Answer

This question focuses on fundamental concepts of magnetism, comparison of electric and magnetic fields, field patterns, and primary cell construction.

(a) Magnet and Lines of Force:

(i) Magnet Definition: A magnet is an object or material that produces a magnetic field. This field is invisible but is responsible for the most notable property of a magnet: a force that pulls on other ferromagnetic materials, such as iron, and attracts or repels other magnets.

(ii) Properties of Magnetic Lines of Force:

  • They form closed loops, emerging from the north pole and entering the south pole externally, and continuing from south to north internally within the magnet.
  • They never intersect each other.
  • The density of the lines (how close they are together) indicates the strength of the magnetic field; denser lines mean a stronger field.
  • The tangent to a field line at any point gives the direction of the magnetic field (or the force on a hypothetical north pole) at that point.
  • They tend to shorten their length (like stretched elastic bands) and repel each other sideways.
  • (Any three valid properties)

(b) Differences Between Electric (E) and Magnetic (B) Fields:

FeatureElectric Field (E)Magnetic Field (B)
SourceStatic electric charges; Time-varying magnetic fields.Moving electric charges (currents); Time-varying electric fields; Intrinsic magnetic moments (e.g., electron spin).

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Explain three sources of error that may occur with an electrical measurement instrument. (6 marks)

(b) Figure 4 shows two resistors, A and B, having resistances $10 \Omega$ and $15 \Omega$ respectively, connected in parallel across a battery of four cells in series. Each cell has an e.m.f. of 2 V and an internal resistance of $0.2 \Omega$. Calculate the:

(i) p.d. between battery terminals P Q; (Assume P and Q are the terminals *of the battery*, not just across the parallel resistors) (5 marks)

(ii) current through each resistor, and; (4 marks)

(iii) total power dissipated in the resistors. (5 marks)

Answer

This question addresses errors in electrical measurements and involves DC circuit analysis of a series-parallel circuit with internal resistance.

(a) Sources of Error in Electrical Instruments:

  • Systematic Errors: These are errors inherent in the instrument or the measurement system, causing readings to be consistently off in one direction.
    • Instrumental Errors: Due to defects in manufacturing, calibration issues, or wear and tear (e.g., incorrect zero setting, non-linear scale, faulty components).
    • Loading Errors: The act of connecting the instrument alters the circuit conditions, causing the measured value to differ from the true value (e.g., voltmeter drawing current, ammeter having resistance).
    • Environmental Errors: Changes in temperature, humidity, pressure, or external magnetic/electric fields affecting the instrument's performance.
  • Random Errors: Unpredictable fluctuations in readings due to various uncontrollable factors.
    • Observational Errors: Parallax error when reading analog scales, estimation errors between scale divisions.
    • Noise: Electrical noise from internal components or external interference affecting sensitive electronic instruments.
    • Fluctuations: Minor variations in supply voltage or component values during measurement.
  • Gross Errors (Mistakes): Human errors in reading the instrument, recording data incorrectly, using the instrument improperly (e.g., wrong range selection), or calculation mistakes.

(Explain any three distinct sources).

(b) Circuit Analysis (Fig 4):

Battery details: 4 cells in series.

  • Total EMF ($E_{total}$) = $4 \times 2 \text{ V} = 8 \text{ V}$.
  • Total internal resistance ($r_{total}$) = $4 \times 0.2 \Omega = 0.8 \Omega$.

External circuit: Resistor A ($R_A = 10 \Omega$) in parallel with Resistor B ($R_B = 15 \Omega$).

Equivalent external resistance ($R_{ext}$):

$\frac{1}{R_{ext}} = \frac{1}{R_A} + \frac{1}{R_B} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{15} = \frac{3 + 2}{30} = \frac{5}{30} = \frac{1}{6}$

$R_{ext} = 6 \Omega$.

Total circuit resistance ($R_{circuit}$) = $R_{ext} + r_{total} = 6 \Omega + 0.8 \Omega = 6.8 \Omega$.

Total current drawn from the battery ($I_{total}$):

$I_{total} = \frac{E_{total}}{R_{circuit}} = \frac{8 \text{ V}}{6.8 \Omega} \approx 1.1765 \text{ A}$.

(i) P.D. between battery terminals P Q ($V_{PQ}$):

The potential difference across the battery terminals (terminal voltage) is the total EMF minus the voltage drop across the internal resistance OR the voltage across the external circuit.

$V_{PQ} = E_{total} - I_{total} \times r_{total} = 8 - (1.1765 \times 0.8) = 8 - 0.9412 = 7.0588 \text{ V}$.

Alternatively: $V_{PQ} = I_{total} \times R_{ext} = 1.1765 \times 6 = 7.059 \text{ V}$.

$V_{PQ} \approx 7.06 \text{ V}$.

(ii) Current through each resistor:

The voltage across the parallel combination (across both $R_A$ and $R_B$) is $V_{PQ}$.

Current through $R_A$ ($I_A$): $I_A = \frac{V_{PQ}}{R_A} = \frac{7.059}{10} \approx 0.706 \text{ A}$.

Current through $R_B$ ($I_B$): $I_B = \frac{V_{PQ}}{R_B} = \frac{7.059}{15} \approx 0.471 \text{ A}$.

(Check: $I_A + I_B \approx 0.706 + 0.471 = 1.177 \text{ A}$, which matches $I_{total}$).

(iii) Total power dissipated in the resistors ($P_{ext}$):

This is the power dissipated in the external parallel combination ($R_{ext}$).

Method 1: $P_{ext} = V_{PQ} \times I_{total} = 7.059 \times 1.1765 \approx 8.305 \text{ W}$.

Method 2: $P_{ext} = I_{total}^2 \times R_{ext} = (1.1765)^2 \times 6 \approx 1.384 \times 6 \approx 8.305 \text{ W}$.

Method 3: Sum of power in individual resistors: $P_A = I_A^2 R_A = (0.7059)^2 \times 10 \approx 0.4983 \times 10 \approx 4.983 \text{ W}$. $P_B = I_B^2 R_B = (0.4706)^2 \times 15 \approx 0.2215 \times 15 \approx 3.322 \text{ W}$. $P_{total} = P_A + P_B = 4.983 + 3.322 = 8.305 \text{ W}$.

Total power dissipated $\approx 8.31 \text{ W}$.

The analysis involves combining series and parallel resistances, accounting for internal resistance, and applying Ohm's law and power formulas.
