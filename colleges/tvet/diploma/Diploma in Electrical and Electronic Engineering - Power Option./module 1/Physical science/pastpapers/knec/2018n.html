Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) State:

(i) two properties of x-rays; (2 marks)

(ii) three applications of electrolysis. (3 marks)

(b) Explain the forces acting in the nucleus of an atom. (4 marks)

(c) Differentiate between 'lattice' and 'hydration' energy changes when ionic compound is dissolved in polar water. (4 marks)

(d) Write:

(i) balanced chemical equation for complete combustion of methane gas. (2 marks)

(ii) the molecular formula for the following hydrocarbons: (5 marks)

(I) propene;

(II) ethane;

(III) ethyne.

Answer

This question covers X-rays, electrolysis, nuclear forces, solution chemistry, and basic organic chemistry nomenclature.

(a) (i) Properties of X-rays:

  • They travel in straight lines.
  • They are highly penetrating electromagnetic radiation.
  • They are not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (they are uncharged).
  • They cause ionization in gases.
  • They affect photographic plates.
  • They cause fluorescence in certain materials (e.g., zinc sulphide).
  • They exhibit wave properties like diffraction and interference (though requires special conditions due to short wavelength).
  • They carry energy and momentum.
  • (Any two valid properties)

(a) (ii) Applications of Electrolysis:

  • Electroplating: Coating a thin layer of one metal onto another (e.g., chromium plating).
  • Extraction of reactive metals: Such as sodium, potassium, aluminium from their molten ores/compounds.
  • Purification of metals: Refining metals like copper to high purity.
  • Manufacture of chemicals: Production of sodium hydroxide, chlorine, hydrogen gas from brine (NaCl solution).
  • Anodizing: Thickening the protective oxide layer on metals like aluminium.
  • Electrotyping: Making duplicate printing plates.
  • (Any three valid applications)

(b) Forces acting in the nucleus of an atom:

The nucleus contains protons (positively charged) and neutrons (neutral). The primary forces are:

  • Strong Nuclear Force: This is a very strong, short-range attractive force that acts between nucleons (protons and neutrons). It overcomes the electrostatic repulsion between protons and holds the nucleus together. Its range is typically limited to the size of the nucleus (about $10^{-15}$ m).
  • Electrostatic Force (Coulomb Force): This is a repulsive force acting between the positively charged protons. It is weaker than the strong nuclear force at short distances within the nucleus but has an infinite range. It tends to push the protons apart, destabilizing larger nuclei.
  • Weak Nuclear Force: This force is responsible for certain types of radioactive decay, such as beta decay, where a neutron can change into a proton (or vice versa). It is much weaker than the strong force and has an even shorter range.
  • Gravitational Force: Although present between nucleons due to their mass, this force is extremely weak compared to the other nuclear forces and is generally negligible in nuclear physics calculations.

The stability of a nucleus depends on the balance between the attractive strong nuclear force and the repulsive electrostatic force.

(c) Lattice Energy vs. Hydration Energy:

When an ionic compound dissolves in a polar solvent like water, two main energy changes occur:

  • Lattice Energy: This is the energy *required* (endothermic process, energy absorbed) to completely separate one mole of a solid ionic compound into its gaseous constituent ions. It represents the strength of the ionic bonds holding the crystal lattice together. A high lattice energy means strong ionic bonds.
    Example: $NaCl(s) \rightarrow Na^+(g) + Cl^-(g) \quad \Delta H_{lattice} > 0$
  • Hydration Energy: This is the energy *released* (exothermic process) when one mole of gaseous ions becomes hydrated (dissolved) in water. Polar water molecules surround the ions, forming ion-dipole interactions. The positive ends (H atoms) of water molecules surround anions, and the negative ends (O atom) surround cations. This process stabilizes the ions in solution.
    Example: $Na^+(g) + Cl^-(g) \xrightarrow{H_2O} Na^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq) \quad \Delta H_{hydration} < 0$

The overall enthalpy change of solution ($\Delta H_{solution}$) depends on the balance between these two: $\Delta H_{solution} = \Delta H_{lattice} + \Delta H_{hydration}$. If hydration energy released is greater than lattice energy absorbed, the dissolution is exothermic. If lattice energy is greater, it's endothermic.

(d) (i) Balanced chemical equation for complete combustion of methane gas:

Methane ($CH_4$) reacts with oxygen ($O_2$) to produce carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) and water ($H_2O$).

Balanced Equation: $CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l)$ (or (g) if steam)

(d) (ii) Molecular formula for hydrocarbons:

  • (I) Propene: An alkene with 3 carbon atoms. General formula $C_n H_{2n}$. Molecular formula: $C_3H_6$
  • (II) Ethane: An alkane with 2 carbon atoms. General formula $C_n H_{2n+2}$. Molecular formula: $C_2H_6$
  • (III) Ethyne: An alkyne with 2 carbon atoms. General formula $C_n H_{2n-2}$. Molecular formula: $C_2H_2$

This question tests fundamental concepts in physics (waves, radioactivity), chemistry (electrolysis, bonding, solutions, organic nomenclature), and nuclear physics.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) State the laws of refraction of light. (2 marks)

(b) A light ray passes from glass to water striking the glass-water interface at an angle of $26.3^{\circ}$ with the normal. The refractive indices of glass and water are 1.5 and 1.33 respectively.

Determine:

(i) refractive index of water with respect to glass; (2 marks)

(ii) angle of refraction; (3 marks)

(iii) critical angle for glass-water interface. (3 marks)

(c) With aid of a diagram, describe energy changes when a pendulum bob is displaced from equilibrium position released to swing freely. (5 marks)

(d) (i) State two precautions taken to minimize loss of heat by calorimeter to the surrounding. (2 marks)

(d) (ii) A metallic material of mass 5 kg absorbs 184 kJ of heat energy causing its temperature to rise from $10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to $90^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Determine its specific heat capacity. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers optics (refraction), mechanics (energy in SHM), and thermal physics (calorimetry).

(a) Laws of Refraction of Light (Snell's Laws):

  1. The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal to the interface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  2. The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence ($i$) to the sine of the angle of refraction ($r$) is a constant for a given pair of media and for light of a given wavelength. This constant is the refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first medium ($n_{12}$). Mathematically: $\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = n_{12} = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$, where $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the absolute refractive indices of the first and second media, respectively.

(b) Refraction from Glass to Water:

Given:

  • Medium 1: Glass ($n_1 = n_g = 1.5$)
  • Medium 2: Water ($n_2 = n_w = 1.33$)
  • Angle of incidence in glass ($\theta_1 = i$) = $26.3^{\circ}$

(i) Refractive index of water with respect to glass ($n_{gw}$):

$n_{gw} = \frac{n_w}{n_g} = \frac{1.33}{1.5}$

$n_{gw} \approx 0.8867$

(ii) Angle of refraction ($\theta_2 = r$):

Using Snell's Law: $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$

$n_g \sin i = n_w \sin r$

$1.5 \times \sin(26.3^{\circ}) = 1.33 \times \sin r$

$\sin r = \frac{1.5 \times \sin(26.3^{\circ})}{1.33}$

$\sin(26.3^{\circ}) \approx 0.4431$

$\sin r = \frac{1.5 \times 0.4431}{1.33} \approx \frac{0.66465}{1.33} \approx 0.4997$

$r = \arcsin(0.4997) \approx 29.98^{\circ}$ (Approximately $30.0^{\circ}$)

(iii) Critical angle for glass-water interface ($\theta_c$):

Critical angle occurs when light travels from a denser medium (glass) to a less dense medium (water) and the angle of refraction is $90^{\circ}$.

$n_g \sin \theta_c = n_w \sin(90^{\circ})$

$1.5 \sin \theta_c = 1.33 \times 1$

$\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.33}{1.5} = n_{gw} \approx 0.8867$

$\theta_c = \arcsin(0.8867) \approx 62.46^{\circ}$

(c) Energy Changes in a Simple Pendulum:

When a simple pendulum bob is displaced from its equilibrium position (C) to an extreme position (A or B) and released, it swings freely under gravity (ignoring air resistance).

  • At Extreme Positions (A and B): The bob is momentarily at rest. Its height above the equilibrium position is maximum. Therefore, its Potential Energy (PE) is maximum, and its Kinetic Energy (KE) is zero.
  • Moving from Extreme to Equilibrium (A to C or B to C): As the bob swings downwards, its height decreases, and its speed increases. PE is converted into KE.
  • At Equilibrium Position (C): The bob is at its lowest point (minimum height, minimum PE - often taken as zero reference). Its speed is maximum. Therefore, its KE is maximum, and its PE is minimum (or zero).
  • Moving from Equilibrium to Extreme (C to B or C to A): As the bob swings upwards, its height increases, and its speed decreases. KE is converted back into PE.

Throughout the swing, the total mechanical energy (TME = PE + KE) remains constant, assuming no energy loss due to friction or air resistance. Energy continuously transforms between potential and kinetic forms.

(d) (i) Precautions to Minimize Heat Loss in Calorimetry:

  • Insulation: Use a well-lagged calorimeter (e.g., polished outer surface to reduce radiation, placed inside an insulating jacket, sometimes with trapped air or vacuum).
  • Lid: Cover the calorimeter with an insulating lid (with holes for stirrer and thermometer) to minimize heat loss by convection and evaporation.
  • Stirring: Stir the contents gently and continuously to ensure uniform temperature distribution, minimizing localized temperature differences that drive heat loss.
  • Temperature Range: Keep the temperature change relatively small and conduct the experiment quickly to reduce the time available for heat exchange with the surroundings.
  • Polished Surfaces: Inner and outer surfaces of the calorimeter are often polished to minimize heat loss/gain by radiation.
  • (Any two valid precautions)

(d) (ii) Specific Heat Capacity Calculation:

Given:

  • Mass (m) = 5 kg
  • Heat absorbed (Q) = 184 kJ = $184 \times 10^3$ J
  • Initial temperature ($T_1$) = $10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Final temperature ($T_2$) = $90^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Temperature change ($\Delta T$) = $T_2 - T_1 = 90 - 10 = 80^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (or 80 K)

Formula: $Q = mc\Delta T$, where c is the specific heat capacity.

$c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T}$

$c = \frac{184 \times 10^3 \text{ J}}{5 \text{ kg} \times 80 \text{ K}}$

$c = \frac{184000}{400} \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$

$c = 460 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$

The specific heat capacity of the metallic material is 460 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

This question covers power transmission couplings, impact mechanics, engine governors, and work/power calculations.

(a) (i) Shaft Coupling:

A shaft coupling is a mechanical device used to connect the ends of two shafts together for the purpose of transmitting power. Couplings allow for connection/disconnection for maintenance, accommodate some misalignment between shafts, and can sometimes provide overload protection or damp vibrations.

(a) (ii) Factors to consider when choosing a shaft coupling:

  • Amount of Torque/Power to be transmitted: The coupling must be strong enough.
  • Type and Amount of Misalignment: (Angular, parallel, axial) - Different couplings handle different misalignments (e.g., rigid, flexible, universal joints).
  • Operating Speed (RPM): Balancing becomes critical at high speeds.
  • Operating Environment: Temperature, corrosion, space constraints.
  • Vibration Damping Requirements: Some couplings absorb shock/vibration.
  • Maintenance Requirements: Ease of installation/removal, lubrication needs.
  • Cost and Availability.
  • (Any two valid factors)

(b) Collision and Stopping:

Given:

  • Mass 1 ($m_1$) = 200 kg
  • Initial velocity of $m_1$ ($u_1$) = 60 m/s
  • Mass 2 ($m_2$) = 120 kg
  • Initial velocity of $m_2$ ($u_2$) = 0 m/s (stationary)
  • Stopping time for $m_1$ ($\Delta t$) = 3 s
  • Final velocity of $m_1$ ($v_1$) = 0 m/s (after 3s)

Addressing the ambiguity in (b)(i): If "initial common velocity" refers to an inelastic collision where they stick together, we'd use conservation of momentum: $m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v_{common}$. $200(60) + 120(0) = (200+120) v_{common} \implies 12000 = 320 v_{common} \implies v_{common} = 12000/320 = 37.5 \text{ m/s}$. However, this doesn't fit the stopping description well. (b) (i) Initial Velocity (Ambiguous):

As noted, the term "initial common velocity" is problematic here. The initial velocity of the body being stopped (m1) just before the 3-second interval is 60 m/s. If an inelastic collision occurred first, the common velocity would be 37.5 m/s. Without clarification, this part cannot be answered definitively as intended.

(b) (ii) Average Impact Force (Stopping Force on $m_1$):

We use the impulse-momentum theorem: Impulse = Change in Momentum

Average Force ($F_{avg}$) $\times$ time interval ($\Delta t$) = final momentum ($m_1 v_1$) - initial momentum ($m_1 u_1$)

$F_{avg} \times 3 = (200 \times 0) - (200 \times 60)$

$3 F_{avg} = -12000$

$F_{avg} = \frac{-12000}{3} = -4000 \text{ N}$

The magnitude of the average impact force required to stop the 200 kg body is 4000 N. The negative sign indicates the force opposes the initial motion.

(c) (i) Inertia vs. Centrifugal Governors:

  • Advantages of Inertia Governor over Centrifugal:
    1. More sensitive: Responds more quickly to changes in load because it utilizes both centrifugal force and the inertia force due to angular acceleration/deceleration.
    2. Can be designed to be isochronous (maintain constant speed regardless of load) more easily.
  • Disadvantage of Inertia Governor over Centrifugal:
    1. More complex construction and potentially less robust.
    2. Balancing can be more difficult.

(c) (ii) Principle of Operation of Centrifugal Governors:

A centrifugal governor uses rotating masses (flyballs) connected via arms to a central spindle driven by the engine. As the engine speed increases, the centrifugal force acting on the flyballs ($F_c = m \omega^2 r$) increases, causing them to move outwards and upwards. This movement is transmitted through linkages to a sleeve, which slides along the spindle. The sleeve's movement operates a throttle valve controlling the fuel/steam supply to the engine.

  • If engine speed increases (due to reduced load), flyballs move out, sleeve moves up, throttle closes slightly, reducing fuel supply and bringing speed down.
  • If engine speed decreases (due to increased load), flyballs move in, sleeve moves down, throttle opens further, increasing fuel supply and bringing speed up.
The governor thus attempts to maintain a near-constant engine speed despite load variations by regulating the fuel supply based on speed changes detected via centrifugal force.

(d) Crane Lifting a Car:

Given:

  • Mass (m) = 1100 kg
  • Height (h) = 4 m
  • Time (t) = 6 s
  • Gravitational force per kg = 9.8 N/kg
  • Constant speed implies acceleration = 0.

(i) Work done by the lifting force:

Since speed is constant, the lifting force ($F_{lift}$) equals the weight of the car.

Weight ($W$) = mass $\times$ gravitational force per kg = $1100 \text{ kg} \times 9.8 \text{ N/kg} = 10780 \text{ N}$

Work Done = Force $\times$ Distance = $F_{lift} \times h$

Work Done = $10780 \text{ N} \times 4 \text{ m} = 43120 \text{ J}$ (or 43.12 kJ)

(ii) Power of the crane:

Power = $\frac{\text{Work Done}}{\text{Time}}$

Power = $\frac{43120 \text{ J}}{6 \text{ s}} \approx 7186.67 \text{ W}$

Power $\approx 7.19 \text{ kW}$

Calculations cover couplings, ambiguous impact, governors, work and power.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) State:

(i) the parallelogram law of coplanar forces; (2 marks)

(ii) two conditions for static equilibrium of coplanar forces. (2 marks)

(b) (i) Differentiate between centrifugal and centripetal forces. (2 marks)

(b) (ii) A centrifuge machine is used to separate cream from milk of mass 20 kg in a container fitted 80 cm from the centre of rotation. The machine makes one rotation in 4 seconds.

Determine the:

(I) angular velocity; (3 marks)

(II) centrifugal force. (3 marks)

(c) State the following laws of thermodynamics: (4 marks)

(i) first law;

(ii) second law.

(d) Figure 1, shows a horizontal pipe with a narrow section. A fluid of density $800 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}$ flowing through the pipe passes the narrow section at a speed of $4 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$.

Determine the:

(i) speed of fluid in the pipe; (2 marks)

(ii) pressure difference due to the narrow section. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers statics (forces, equilibrium), dynamics (circular motion), thermodynamics laws, and fluid dynamics (continuity, Bernoulli).

(a) (i) Parallelogram Law of Coplanar Forces:

If two coplanar forces, acting at a point, are represented in magnitude and direction by the two adjacent sides of a parallelogram drawn from that point, then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the diagonal of the parallelogram passing through that same point.

(a) (ii) Conditions for Static Equilibrium of Coplanar Forces:

  1. The vector sum of all forces acting on the body must be zero. (This ensures no translational acceleration). Mathematically, $\sum \vec{F} = 0$, which implies $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$ for components.
  2. The algebraic sum of the moments of all forces about any point in the plane must be zero. (This ensures no rotational acceleration). Mathematically, $\sum M = 0$.

(b) (i) Centrifugal vs. Centripetal Force:

  • Centripetal Force: This is the *real* force acting towards the center of the circular path, required to cause an object to move in a circle (i.e., to provide the centripetal acceleration). It's provided by an external agent (e.g., tension in a string, gravity, friction).
  • Centrifugal Force: This is an *apparent* or *fictitious* force experienced by an observer in a rotating (non-inertial) frame of reference. It appears to act outwards from the center of rotation. It's an inertial force, not a real force caused by interaction, but arises from the inertia of the object resisting the change in direction required by circular motion.

(b) (ii) Centrifuge Machine:

Given:

  • Mass (m) = 20 kg (Note: this is mass of milk+cream, force acts on this)
  • Radius (r) = 80 cm = 0.8 m
  • Time for one rotation (Period, T) = 4 s

(I) Angular velocity ($\omega$):

Angular velocity $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$

$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2} \text{ rad/s}$

$\omega \approx 1.57 \text{ rad/s}$

(II) Centrifugal force ($F_c$):

Centrifugal force $F_c = m \omega^2 r$

$F_c = 20 \times (\frac{\pi}{2})^2 \times 0.8$

$F_c = 20 \times \frac{\pi^2}{4} \times 0.8$

$F_c = 5 \times \pi^2 \times 0.8 = 4 \pi^2 \text{ N}$

$F_c \approx 4 \times (3.14159)^2 \approx 4 \times 9.8696 \approx 39.48 \text{ N}$

(c) Laws of Thermodynamics:

(i) First Law of Thermodynamics (Conservation of Energy):

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. In a thermodynamic process, the change in internal energy ($\Delta U$) of a system is equal to the heat added to the system ($Q$) minus the work done by the system ($W$). Mathematically: $\Delta U = Q - W$.

(ii) Second Law of Thermodynamics (Entropy and Direction of Processes):

This law defines the direction of spontaneous processes and the concept of entropy. It can be stated in several ways:

  • (Clausius Statement): Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body without external work being done.
  • (Kelvin-Planck Statement): It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a single reservoir and the performance of an equivalent amount of work. (i.e., no heat engine can be 100% efficient).
  • (Entropy Statement): The total entropy of an isolated system can only increase over time, or remain constant in ideal cases where the system is in a steady state or undergoing a reversible process. Entropy is a measure of disorder or randomness.

(d) Fluid Flow in Pipe:

Given:

  • Density ($\rho$) = $800 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}$
  • Wide section diameter ($D_1$) = 10 cm = 0.1 m $\implies$ Radius $r_1 = 0.05$ m
  • Narrow section diameter ($D_2$) = 5 cm = 0.05 m $\implies$ Radius $r_2 = 0.025$ m
  • Speed in narrow section ($v_2$) = $4 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$

(i) Speed of fluid in the wide pipe ($v_1$):

Using the equation of continuity (conservation of mass flow rate): $A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$

Area $A = \pi r^2$

$A_1 = \pi (0.05)^2 = 0.0025\pi \text{ m}^2$

$A_2 = \pi (0.025)^2 = 0.000625\pi \text{ m}^2$

$(0.0025\pi) v_1 = (0.000625\pi) \times 4$

$0.0025 v_1 = 0.0025$

$v_1 = 1 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$

(ii) Pressure difference ($\Delta P = P_1 - P_2$):

Using Bernoulli's equation for a horizontal pipe ($h_1 = h_2$):

$P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g h_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g h_2$

$P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2$

$P_1 - P_2 = \frac{1}{2}\rho (v_2^2 - v_1^2)$

$\Delta P = \frac{1}{2} \times 800 \times (4^2 - 1^2)$

$\Delta P = 400 \times (16 - 1)$

$\Delta P = 400 \times 15 = 6000 \text{ Pa}$ (or 6 kPa)

The pressure difference between the wide and narrow sections is 6000 Pa.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) State three disadvantages of dry Leclanché cells. (3 marks)

(b) Describe the following methods of charging a battery:

(i) Booster charging; (3 marks)

(ii) Trickle charging. (3 marks)

(c) Figure 2, shows series-parallel arrangement of 5 identical cells connected across a 5 ohm resistor. Each cell has an internal resistance of 0.15 ohm and an e.m.f of 2.2 V. Using superposition theorem, determine the current through the 5 ohms resistor. (11 marks)

Answer

This question deals with primary cells, battery charging methods, and DC circuit analysis using the superposition theorem.

(a) Disadvantages of Dry Leclanché Cells:

  • Voltage Drop on Load: The terminal voltage drops significantly when supplying current, especially higher currents, due to relatively high internal resistance.
  • Not Rechargeable: It is a primary cell and cannot be reliably recharged once depleted.
  • Polarization: Accumulation of hydrogen gas at the carbon electrode increases internal resistance and reduces voltage (though manganese dioxide acts as a depolarizer, it's not perfectly efficient).
  • Limited Shelf Life: Chemical reactions continue slowly even when not in use, leading to self-discharge and potential leakage.
  • Leakage Risk: The zinc casing can corrode, especially after discharge, leading to leakage of corrosive electrolyte (ammonium chloride paste).
  • Poor Low-Temperature Performance: Efficiency decreases significantly at low temperatures.
  • (Any three valid disadvantages)

(b) Battery Charging Methods:

(i) Booster Charging:

This method involves charging a battery at a relatively high current for a short period. It is typically used to quickly recharge a significantly discharged battery (like a car battery) to a usable state, but not necessarily to full capacity. It often requires careful monitoring to avoid overheating or damaging the battery due to the high current.

(ii) Trickle Charging:

This method involves charging a battery at a very low current, often just enough to counteract its natural self-discharge rate. It is used to maintain a fully charged battery at its optimal charge level over long periods of inactivity or storage. It helps prevent sulfation (in lead-acid batteries) and keeps the battery ready for use without overcharging.

(c) Superposition Theorem Application:

The circuit consists of two parallel branches connected to the $R_L = 5 \Omega$ resistor.

  • Branch 1 (Left): 3 cells in series. Total EMF $E_1 = 3 \times 2.2 = 6.6$ V. Total internal resistance $r_1 = 3 \times 0.15 = 0.45 \Omega$.
  • Branch 2 (Right): 2 cells in series. Total EMF $E_2 = 2 \times 2.2 = 4.4$ V. Total internal resistance $r_2 = 2 \times 0.15 = 0.30 \Omega$.

We need to find the current $I_L$ through the $5 \Omega$ resistor using superposition.

Step 1: Consider Source $E_1$ acting alone (Short-circuit $E_2$).

The internal resistance $r_2$ (0.30 $\Omega$) is now in parallel with $R_L$ (5 $\Omega$).

$R_{p1} = \frac{r_2 \times R_L}{r_2 + R_L} = \frac{0.30 \times 5}{0.30 + 5} = \frac{1.5}{5.3} \approx 0.283 \Omega$.

Total resistance seen by $E_1$ is $R_{T1} = r_1 + R_{p1} = 0.45 + 0.283 = 0.733 \Omega$.

Total current from $E_1$ is $I_{T1} = \frac{E_1}{R_{T1}} = \frac{6.6}{0.733} \approx 9.004 \text{ A}$.

Using the current divider rule to find the current through $R_L$ due to $E_1$ (let's call it $I_{L1}$), flowing downwards:

$I_{L1} = I_{T1} \times \frac{r_2}{r_2 + R_L} = 9.004 \times \frac{0.30}{0.30 + 5} = 9.004 \times \frac{0.3}{5.3} \approx 9.004 \times 0.0566 \approx 0.510 \text{ A}$ (downwards)

Step 2: Consider Source $E_2$ acting alone (Short-circuit $E_1$).

The internal resistance $r_1$ (0.45 $\Omega$) is now in parallel with $R_L$ (5 $\Omega$).

$R_{p2} = \frac{r_1 \times R_L}{r_1 + R_L} = \frac{0.45 \times 5}{0.45 + 5} = \frac{2.25}{5.45} \approx 0.413 \Omega$.

Total resistance seen by $E_2$ is $R_{T2} = r_2 + R_{p2} = 0.30 + 0.413 = 0.713 \Omega$.

Total current from $E_2$ is $I_{T2} = \frac{E_2}{R_{T2}} = \frac{4.4}{0.713} \approx 6.171 \text{ A}$.

Using the current divider rule to find the current through $R_L$ due to $E_2$ (let's call it $I_{L2}$), flowing downwards:

$I_{L2} = I_{T2} \times \frac{r_1}{r_1 + R_L} = 6.171 \times \frac{0.45}{0.45 + 5} = 6.171 \times \frac{0.45}{5.45} \approx 6.171 \times 0.0826 \approx 0.509 \text{ A}$ (downwards)

Step 3: Combine the results.

Both $I_{L1}$ and $I_{L2}$ flow downwards through the $5 \Omega$ resistor.

Total current $I_L = I_{L1} + I_{L2} = 0.510 + 0.509 = 1.019 \text{ A}$.

Using the superposition theorem, the current through the 5 ohms resistor is approximately 1.019 A (flowing downwards).

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Define temperature coefficient of resistance of a conductor. (2 marks)

(a) (ii) Explain how temperature change affects electrical conductivity of a material. (3 marks)

(b) The resistance of a copper coil at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is 200 ohms. Determine the resistance of the coil when its temperature rises to $45^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. (Temperature coefficient of resistance of copper is $0.0043 /{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ at $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$) (5 marks)

(c) (i) Explain the term 'loading effect' of a voltmeter. (2 marks)

(c) (ii) State how loading effect in C(i):

(I) alters voltmeter reading; (1 mark)

(II) can be minimized. (1 mark)

(d) Figure 3 shows a voltmeter circuit connection. The voltmeter has a full-scale-deflection of 50 V and a sensitivity of $2.4 \mathrm{k} \Omega / \mathrm{V}$. Determine the:

(i) Voltage across the $60 \mathrm{k} \Omega$ resistor when voltmeter is not connected; (3 marks)

(ii) Voltage indicated by the voltmeter. (3 marks)

Answer

This question explores the relationship between resistance and temperature, and the practical considerations of voltmeter measurements (loading effect).

(a) (i) Temperature Coefficient of Resistance ($\alpha$):

The temperature coefficient of resistance of a conductor is defined as the fractional change in resistance per degree Celsius (or Kelvin) change in temperature, usually referred to a specific base temperature (often $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ or $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$). It quantifies how much the resistance of a material changes with temperature.

Mathematically: $\alpha_{T_0} = \frac{R_T - R_{T_0}}{R_{T_0}(T - T_0)}$, where $R_T$ is resistance at temp T, $R_{T_0}$ is resistance at base temp $T_0$.

(a) (ii) Effect of Temperature Change on Conductivity:

  • Conductors (Metals): As temperature increases, the atoms/ions in the metallic lattice vibrate more vigorously. This increased thermal vibration impedes the flow of free electrons (charge carriers), increasing the frequency of collisions. Consequently, resistance increases, and electrical conductivity ($\sigma = 1/\rho$, where $\rho$ is resistivity) *decreases* with increasing temperature. They have a positive temperature coefficient of resistance.
  • Semiconductors and Insulators: As temperature increases, more electrons gain enough thermal energy to jump from the valence band to the conduction band (or break free from bonds), increasing the number of available charge carriers (electrons and holes). Although lattice vibrations also increase, the effect of increased carrier concentration usually dominates. Therefore, resistance *decreases*, and conductivity *increases* with increasing temperature. They generally have a negative temperature coefficient of resistance.

(b) Resistance Calculation for Copper Coil:

Given:

  • Resistance at $T_1 = 20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_{20}$) = $200 \Omega$
  • Temperature coefficient at $T_0 = 0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($\alpha_0$) = $0.0043 /{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Final temperature ($T_2$) = $45^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$

We use the formula relating resistance at different temperatures based on $\alpha_0$: $R_T = R_0 (1 + \alpha_0 T)$

First, find the resistance at $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_0$) using the resistance at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$:

$R_{20} = R_0 (1 + \alpha_0 \times 20)$

$200 = R_0 (1 + 0.0043 \times 20)$

$200 = R_0 (1 + 0.086)$

$200 = R_0 (1.086)$

$R_0 = \frac{200}{1.086} \approx 184.16 \Omega$

Now, find the resistance at $T_2 = 45^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_{45}$):

$R_{45} = R_0 (1 + \alpha_0 \times 45)$

$R_{45} = 184.16 (1 + 0.0043 \times 45)$

$R_{45} = 184.16 (1 + 0.1935)$

$R_{45} = 184.16 (1.1935) \approx 219.81 \Omega$

Alternatively, using the formula $R_2 = R_1 \frac{1 + \alpha_0 T_2}{1 + \alpha_0 T_1}$:

$R_{45} = R_{20} \frac{1 + \alpha_0 (45)}{1 + \alpha_0 (20)} = 200 \times \frac{1 + 0.0043 \times 45}{1 + 0.0043 \times 20}$

$R_{45} = 200 \times \frac{1 + 0.1935}{1 + 0.086} = 200 \times \frac{1.1935}{1.086} \approx 200 \times 1.099 \approx 219.8 \Omega$

The resistance at $45^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is approximately $219.8 \Omega$.

(c) (i) Loading Effect of a Voltmeter:

The loading effect occurs when connecting a voltmeter across a component in a circuit changes the circuit's conditions (specifically, the equivalent resistance across which the voltage is measured), causing the measured voltage to be different from the actual voltage present before the voltmeter was connected. This happens because a practical voltmeter has a finite internal resistance and draws some current from the circuit.

(c) (ii) Alteration and Minimization:

(I) Alteration: The voltmeter, having finite resistance, provides an additional path for current when connected in parallel. This reduces the equivalent resistance of the parallel combination compared to the component's resistance alone. This change in resistance alters the voltage distribution in the circuit, typically causing the voltmeter to read a *lower* voltage than the actual voltage across the component when the meter is absent.

(II) Minimization: The loading effect can be minimized by using a voltmeter with a very high internal resistance compared to the resistance of the component across which the voltage is being measured. Ideally, an infinite internal resistance would eliminate the loading effect entirely. Digital voltmeters (DVMs) generally have much higher input resistance than analog voltmeters, thus minimizing loading.

(d) Voltmeter Circuit Analysis:

Given:

  • Supply Voltage ($V_S$) = 100 V
  • Resistor 1 ($R_1$) = $40 \mathrm{k} \Omega = 40000 \Omega$
  • Resistor 2 ($R_2$) = $60 \mathrm{k} \Omega = 60000 \Omega$
  • Voltmeter FSD = 50 V
  • Voltmeter Sensitivity (S) = $2.4 \mathrm{k} \Omega / \mathrm{V} = 2400 \Omega / \mathrm{V}$

(i) Voltage across $R_2$ (60 k$\Omega$) without voltmeter:

This is a simple voltage divider circuit.

$V_{R2} = V_S \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$

$V_{R2} = 100 \times \frac{60000}{40000 + 60000} = 100 \times \frac{60000}{100000}$

$V_{R2} = 100 \times 0.6 = 60 \text{ V}$

(ii) Voltage indicated by the voltmeter:

First, find the voltmeter's internal resistance ($R_V$). Note: Sensitivity is often specified for the FSD range. A common interpretation is $R_V = \text{Sensitivity} \times \text{FSD Range}$.

$R_V = S \times \text{FSD} = (2400 \Omega / \mathrm{V}) \times 50 \text{ V} = 120000 \Omega = 120 \mathrm{k} \Omega$.

When the voltmeter is connected across $R_2$, $R_V$ is in parallel with $R_2$.

Equivalent resistance of the parallel combination ($R_p$):

$R_p = \frac{R_2 \times R_V}{R_2 + R_V} = \frac{60000 \times 120000}{60000 + 120000} = \frac{7.2 \times 10^9}{180000} = 40000 \Omega = 40 \mathrm{k} \Omega$.

Now, the voltage across this parallel combination (which is what the voltmeter reads) is found using the voltage divider rule with $R_1$ and $R_p$.

$V_{indicated} = V_S \times \frac{R_p}{R_1 + R_p}$

$V_{indicated} = 100 \times \frac{40000}{40000 + 40000} = 100 \times \frac{40000}{80000}$

$V_{indicated} = 100 \times 0.5 = 50 \text{ V}$

The voltmeter indicates 50 V.

Note that the calculated voltage (60V) without the meter is higher than the meter's FSD (50V). The meter correctly reads 50V, which is its maximum reading in this case, demonstrating significant loading effect (true voltage was 60V, read voltage is 50V).

Calculations confirm the temperature dependence of resistance and demonstrate the loading effect of a voltmeter.

Magnetic flux density is defined as the amount of magnetic flux ($\Phi$) passing perpendicularly through a unit area (A). The SI unit for magnetic flux density is the Tesla (T), where $1 \text{ T} = 1 \text{ Weber per square meter } (Wb/m^2)$.

Mathematically: $B = \frac{\Phi}{A}$

(b) Magnetic Circuit Analysis (with assumptions):

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Explain the term resistance matching. (2 marks)

(a) (ii) Describe how transformer is used as a resistance matching device. (3 marks)

(b) With the aid of a circuit diagram, describe the construction of an auto-transformer. (5 marks)

(c) Figure 5 shows a series-parallel capacitor circuit. The capacitors X, Y and Z are identical and the total equivalent capacitance of the circuit is $2.4 \mu F$.

Determine the:

(i) Value of capacitor X; (6 marks)

(ii) Voltage across capacitor Y. (4 marks)

Answer

This question covers impedance matching using transformers, auto-transformer construction, and analysis of capacitor networks.

(a) (i) Resistance Matching (Impedance Matching):

Resistance matching (more generally, impedance matching) is the practice of designing the input impedance of an electrical load or the output impedance of its corresponding signal source to maximize the power transfer or minimize signal reflection. For maximum power transfer between a source and a load, the load resistance (or impedance) should be equal to the complex conjugate of the source resistance (or impedance). In simpler DC or purely resistive AC circuits, this means making the load resistance equal to the source resistance.

(a) (ii) Transformer for Resistance Matching:

A transformer can change the apparent impedance of a load as seen by the source. The impedance ratio of an ideal transformer is related to the square of its turns ratio ($n = N_s/N_p$). If a load resistance $R_L$ is connected to the secondary winding, the impedance seen looking into the primary winding ($Z_p$) is given by:

$Z_p = \left(\frac{N_p}{N_s}\right)^2 R_L = \left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 R_L$

By choosing the appropriate turns ratio ($N_p/N_s$), the transformer can make the load impedance $R_L$ *appear* to the source as a different impedance $Z_p$. This allows matching the load to the source impedance for maximum power transfer. For example, matching a low-impedance speaker to a high-impedance amplifier output.

(b) Auto-transformer Construction:

An auto-transformer consists of a single continuous winding wound on a laminated iron core. Unlike a conventional two-winding transformer, the primary and secondary circuits share a common part of the winding.

  • Winding: A single coil of insulated copper wire wound around the core.
  • Core: Made of laminated silicon steel to reduce eddy currents and hysteresis losses.
  • Terminals: There are at least three terminals. Two terminals (A and B) connect to the input supply (primary voltage $V_p$) across the entire winding ($N_p$ turns). One of these terminals (B) is common to both input and output. The third terminal (C) is a tap on the winding, and the output (secondary voltage $V_s$) is taken between the common terminal (B) and the tap (C) ($N_s$ turns).
  • Operation: Voltage is induced in the winding by the alternating primary current. The voltage per turn is approximately constant along the winding. By tapping the winding at different points, different output voltages can be obtained (either step-up or step-down relative to the input, depending on whether the output uses more or fewer turns than the input). Part of the power is transferred conductively (due to the shared winding) and part inductively.

(c) Series-Parallel Capacitor Circuit:

Given:

  • Capacitors X, Y, Z are identical. Let their capacitance be C.
  • Total equivalent capacitance ($C_{eq}$) = $2.4 \mu F$
  • Supply Voltage ($V_S$) = 100 V

Circuit configuration: Y and Z are in parallel, and this combination is in series with X.

(i) Value of capacitor X (which is C):

Capacitance of the parallel combination of Y and Z ($C_{YZ}$):

$C_{YZ} = C_Y + C_Z = C + C = 2C$

This parallel combination ($C_{YZ}$) is in series with X ($C_X = C$).

The total equivalent capacitance ($C_{eq}$) is given by:

$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_X} + \frac{1}{C_{YZ}}$

$\frac{1}{2.4} = \frac{1}{C} + \frac{1}{2C}$

Find a common denominator for the right side:

$\frac{1}{2.4} = \frac{2}{2C} + \frac{1}{2C} = \frac{3}{2C}$

Now solve for C:

$2C = 3 \times 2.4$

$2C = 7.2$

$C = \frac{7.2}{2} = 3.6 \mu F$

Since X, Y, and Z are identical, the value of capacitor X is $C_X = C = 3.6 \mu F$.

(ii) Voltage across capacitor Y ($V_Y$):

First, find the total charge supplied by the source ($Q_{total}$).

$Q_{total} = C_{eq} \times V_S = 2.4 \mu F \times 100 V = 240 \mu C$

In a series circuit, the charge is the same on each series component. So, the charge on capacitor X ($Q_X$) is $240 \mu C$, and the charge on the parallel combination YZ ($Q_{YZ}$) is also $240 \mu C$.

The voltage across the parallel combination ($V_{YZ}$) is:

$V_{YZ} = \frac{Q_{YZ}}{C_{YZ}} = \frac{240 \mu C}{2C} = \frac{240 \mu C}{2 \times 3.6 \mu F} = \frac{240}{7.2} \approx 33.33 \text{ V}$

Since Y and Z are in parallel, the voltage across Y is the same as the voltage across Z, which is equal to the voltage across the parallel combination.

$V_Y = V_Z = V_{YZ} \approx 33.33 \text{ V}$

Alternatively, find voltage across X: $V_X = Q_X / C_X = 240 \mu C / 3.6 \mu F \approx 66.67$ V. Then $V_{YZ} = V_S - V_X = 100 - 66.67 = 33.33$ V. So $V_Y = 33.33$ V.

Capacitor X has a value of 3.6 $\mu$F. The voltage across capacitor Y is approximately 33.33 V.
