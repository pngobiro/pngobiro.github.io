Answer

This question tests understanding of periodic trends based on ionic configurations and radii, and concepts related to homologous series in organic chemistry.

(a) Analysis of Elements from Table 1:

(i) Atomic Numbers:

  • P: Ion P$^{2+}$ has configuration 2.8.8 (18 electrons). Since it lost 2 electrons, the neutral atom P had $18 + 2 = 20$ electrons. Atomic Number of P = 20 (Calcium).
  • S: Ion S$^{3+}$ has configuration 2.8 (10 electrons). Since it lost 3 electrons, the neutral atom S had $10 + 3 = 13$ electrons. Atomic Number of S = 13 (Aluminium).

(ii) Most Reactive Metallic Element:

Metallic reactivity increases down a group (easier to lose electrons) and decreases across a period. We look for elements that form positive ions easily, typically Group 1 or 2 metals with large atomic radii.

  • R forms R$^{+}$ (Group 1) with configuration 2.8.8, meaning neutral R is 2.8.8.1 (Atomic number 19, Potassium). Atomic radius 0.231 mm.
  • V forms V$^{+}$ (Group 1) with configuration 2.8, meaning neutral V is 2.8.1 (Atomic number 11, Sodium). Atomic radius 0.186 mm.
  • W forms W$^{+}$ (Group 1) with configuration 2., meaning neutral W is 2.1 (Atomic number 3, Lithium). Atomic radius 0.156 mm.
  • P forms P$^{2+}$ (Group 2) with configuration 2.8.8, meaning neutral P is 2.8.8.2 (Atomic number 20, Calcium). Atomic radius 0.197 mm.
  • U forms U$^{2+}$ (Group 2) with configuration 2.8, meaning neutral U is 2.8.2 (Atomic number 12, Magnesium). Atomic radius 0.160 mm.

Comparing the Group 1 metals (R, V, W) and Group 2 metals (P, U), reactivity generally increases with atomic radius within a group. R (Potassium) has the largest atomic radius (0.231 mm) among these metals. Therefore, R is likely the most reactive metallic element.

Explanation: Element R has the largest atomic radius and readily loses its single valence electron to form a stable ion, indicating high metallic reactivity.

(iii) Elements Reacting with Cold Water:

Highly reactive metals, particularly Group 1 metals (except Lithium sometimes reacts slowly) and some Group 2 metals (Calcium, Strontium, Barium), react with cold water.

From our analysis: R (Potassium), V (Sodium), and P (Calcium) would react with cold water. (W (Lithium) might react slowly).

Selected elements: R, V, P.

(iv) Elements from the Same Group:

Group 1: R (K), V (Na), W (Li). Select any three: R, V, W.

Group 2: P (Ca), U (Mg). Only two listed.

Group 17 (Halogens, forming X$^-$ ions): Q (likely F, 2.8 config for ion means 2.7 neutral, At No 9), X (likely Cl, 2.8.8 config for ion means 2.8.7 neutral, At No 17). Only two listed.

(v) Chemical Formulas:

  • S and Oxygen: S forms S$^{3+}$ (Aluminium). Oxygen typically forms O$^{2-}$. Formula: $S_2O_3$ (Aluminium oxide).
  • V and X: V forms V$^{+}$ (Sodium). X forms X$^{-}$ (likely Chlorine). Formula: $VX$ (Sodium chloride - NaCl).

(b) Homologous Series:

(i) Definition:

A homologous series is a series of organic compounds with a general formula, usually varying by a single parameter such as the length of a carbon chain. Successive members differ by a common structural unit (typically a $CH_2$ group).

(ii) Characteristics:

  • All members can be represented by a single general formula (e.g., $C_n H_{2n+2}$ for alkanes).
  • Each successive member differs from the next by a $CH_2$ group.
  • They show a gradual change in physical properties (like boiling point, density) as the molar mass increases.
  • They have similar chemical properties due to the presence of the same functional group.
  • They can be prepared by similar general methods.
  • (Any three valid characteristics)

(iii) Variation in Physical State:

The physical state (gas, liquid, solid) at room temperature depends primarily on the strength of intermolecular forces (Van der Waals forces) between molecules. As the number of carbon atoms (and molar mass) increases within a homologous series, the size and surface area of the molecules increase. This leads to stronger Van der Waals forces between molecules. Consequently, more energy is required to overcome these forces, resulting in higher melting and boiling points. Lower members with weaker forces are gases, intermediate members are liquids, and higher members with strong forces are solids at room temperature. The table shows this trend for alkanes ($C_1-C_4$ are gases, $C_5-C_6$ are liquids).

(iv) Isomers of C$_{4}$H$_{10}$ (Butane):

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.

  1. Butane (n-butane): Straight chain.
  2. 2-Methylpropane (isobutane): Branched chain.

The question effectively links electronic configuration to periodic properties and covers basic concepts and trends in organic chemistry.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) State two uses of X-rays in medicine. (2 marks)

(b) Figure 1 shows the features of an X-ray tube.

(i) name the parts labelled A, B and D; (3 marks)

(ii) name the suitable material for the part labelled C; (1 mark)

(iii) explain how X-rays are produced in this tube? (3 marks)

(iv) state why it is necessary to have oil cooling the anode; (1 mark)

(c) The accelerating potential in certain X-ray tube is 15 kV. Determine the maximum frequency of the emitted X-rays. (Take: charge on an electron, e = $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C, Plank's constant, h = $6.62 \times 10^{-34}$ J s). (3 marks)

(d) (i) Identify the possible radiations in each of the following nuclear reactions. (3 marks)

I ${ }_{6}^{12} \mathrm{C}^{*} \longrightarrow { }_{6}^{12} \mathrm{C}$ +radiation

II ${ }_{88}^{226} \mathrm{Ra} \longrightarrow { }_{86}^{222} \mathrm{Rn}$ + radiation

III ${ }_{6}^{14} \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{\beta} { }_{7}^{14} \mathrm{N}$ + radiation

(ii) A sample of a radioactive substance has $8.12 \times 10^{20}$ atoms. The half-life of the substance is 21 minutes. Determine the number of atoms remaining undecayed after 84 minutes. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers the properties, production, and applications of X-rays, along with concepts of nuclear decay and half-life.

(a) Uses of X-rays in Medicine:

  • Diagnostic Imaging (Radiography): To view bones, detect fractures, locate foreign objects, diagnose conditions like pneumonia or blocked intestines.
  • Computed Tomography (CT Scan): Uses X-rays to create detailed cross-sectional images of the body.
  • Fluoroscopy: Real-time X-ray imaging used to observe movement within the body (e.g., during barium swallows or catheter placement).
  • Radiotherapy (Cancer Treatment): High-energy X-rays are used to destroy cancerous cells.
  • (Any two valid uses)

(b) X-ray Tube Analysis (Fig 1):

(i) Naming Parts:

  • A: Filament (part of the Cathode assembly) - Source of electrons.
  • B: Anode (Target) - Metal target struck by electrons.
  • D: Lead Shielding - Absorbs stray X-rays for safety.

(ii) Material for Part C (Target Face within Anode):

Part C is the specific target material embedded in the anode (B). It must have a high melting point (to withstand heat) and a high atomic number (for efficient X-ray production). Suitable material: **Tungsten** (or sometimes Molybdenum).

(iii) X-ray Production:

  1. The filament (A) is heated by a low voltage supply, causing thermionic emission of electrons.
  2. A high potential difference (accelerating voltage, typically tens of kV) is applied between the cathode (containing the filament) and the anode (B).
  3. Electrons are accelerated at high speed across the vacuum towards the anode target (C).
  4. When these high-energy electrons strike the target metal, they rapidly decelerate and lose kinetic energy. Most (~99%) of this energy is converted into heat, but a small fraction (~1%) is converted into X-ray photons through two main processes:
    • Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation): Electrons decelerate as they are deflected by the nuclei of the target atoms, emitting a continuous spectrum of X-rays.
    • Characteristic Radiation: Incident electrons knock out inner-shell electrons of target atoms. When outer-shell electrons drop down to fill these vacancies, specific energy (characteristic) X-rays are emitted, forming sharp peaks on the spectrum.

(iv) Oil Cooling for Anode:

The process of X-ray production is highly inefficient, with most of the kinetic energy of the incident electrons converted into heat at the anode target. Oil cooling (often circulating through the anode structure) is necessary to dissipate this large amount of heat and prevent the target from melting or being damaged.

(v) Purpose of Part D (Lead Shielding):

X-rays are produced in various directions from the target. The lead shielding absorbs stray X-rays, protecting operators and patients from unnecessary radiation exposure and ensuring that X-rays emerge only through a specific window towards the intended target.

(c) Maximum Frequency of Emitted X-rays:

The maximum energy of an emitted X-ray photon ($hf_{max}$) corresponds to the case where an incident electron loses all its kinetic energy in a single interaction. The kinetic energy gained by an electron accelerated through potential V is $E_k = eV$.

Therefore, $hf_{max} = eV$.

$f_{max} = \frac{eV}{h}$

Given: $V = 15 \text{ kV} = 15 \times 10^3 \text{ V}$, $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$, $h = 6.62 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$.

$f_{max} = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (15 \times 10^3 \text{ V})}{6.62 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}}$

$f_{max} = \frac{2.4 \times 10^{-15}}{6.62 \times 10^{-34}} \text{ Hz}$

$f_{max} \approx 3.625 \times 10^{18} \text{ Hz}$.

(d) Nuclear Reactions and Half-Life:

(i) Identifying Radiations:

  • I ${ }_{6}^{12} \mathrm{C}^{*} \longrightarrow { }_{6}^{12} \mathrm{C}$ + radiation: The nucleus goes from an excited state (*) to a ground state without changing atomic or mass number. This releases energy as **Gamma (γ) radiation**.
  • II ${ }_{88}^{226} \mathrm{Ra} \longrightarrow { }_{86}^{222} \mathrm{Rn}$ + radiation: Mass number decreases by 4, atomic number decreases by 2. This corresponds to the emission of an **Alpha (α) particle** (${ }_{2}^{4}He$).
  • III ${ }_{6}^{14} \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{\beta} { }_{7}^{14} \mathrm{N}$ + radiation: Mass number remains the same, atomic number increases by 1. This indicates **Beta-minus (β⁻) decay** (emission of an electron ${ }_{-1}^{0}e$ and an antineutrino). The "radiation" includes the beta particle.

(ii) Radioactive Decay Calculation:

Given: Initial number of atoms ($N_0$) = $8.12 \times 10^{20}$, Half-life ($T_{1/2}$) = 21 minutes, Time elapsed ($t$) = 84 minutes.

Number of half-lives ($n$) = $t / T_{1/2} = 84 / 21 = 4$.

Number of atoms remaining ($N$) after $n$ half-lives is given by: $N = N_0 \times (\frac{1}{2})^n$.

$N = (8.12 \times 10^{20}) \times (\frac{1}{2})^4$

$N = (8.12 \times 10^{20}) \times (\frac{1}{16})$

$N = \frac{8.12}{16} \times 10^{20} = 0.5075 \times 10^{20}$

$N = 5.075 \times 10^{19}$ atoms.

The number of atoms remaining undecayed after 84 minutes is $5.075 \times 10^{19}$.

The question assesses knowledge of X-ray tubes, fundamental decay processes, and half-life calculations.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) An immersion heater rated 2.5 kW is immersed into a liquid of mass 2 kg contained in a calorimeter of heat capacity $450 J K^{-1}$. If the heater is switched on for 5 minutes, the temperature of the liquid rises from $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to $70^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Determine the specific heat capacity of the liquid. (5 marks)

(b) Define the term "specific latent heat of vaporization" of a substance. (2 marks)

(c) Figure 2 shows a simplified diagram of a domestic refrigerator. A volatile liquid circulates through the capillary tube under the action of compression pump.

(i) Give the reason why a volatile liquid is used. (1 mark)

(ii) Explain how the volatile liquid is made to vaporize in the cooling compartment and condense in the cooling fins. (3 marks)

(iii) Explain how cooling takes place in the refrigerator. (2 marks)

(iv) Explain the purpose of the double wall. (1 mark)

(d) Steam of mass 4.0 g at $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is passed into water of mass 450 g at $10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. The final temperature of the mixture rises to $T^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, and the container carrying temperature absorbs negligible heat:

(i) derive an expression for the heat lost by the steam as it condensed to water at temperature $T^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$; (2 marks)

(ii) derive an expression for the heat gained by the water; (1 mark)

(iii) determine the value of T; (3 marks)

Take: specific Latent heat of vaporization of steam = $2260 \mathrm{kJkg}^{-1}$

specific heat capacity of water = $4200 \mathrm{Jkg}^{-1} \mathrm{K}^{-1}$ (Note: MMD OCR had $41.00$, corrected to standard 4200)

Answer

This question deals with calorimetry involving electrical heating, phase changes (latent heat), the principles of refrigeration, and mixing calculations.

(a) Specific Heat Capacity from Immersion Heater:

Given:

  • Power of heater (P) = 2.5 kW = 2500 W (J/s)
  • Mass of liquid ($m_l$) = 2 kg
  • Heat capacity of calorimeter ($C_{cal}$) = 450 J K$^{-1}$
  • Time (t) = 5 minutes = $5 \times 60 = 300$ s
  • Initial temperature ($T_1$) = $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Final temperature ($T_2$) = $70^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Temperature change ($\Delta T$) = $T_2 - T_1 = 70 - 20 = 50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (or 50 K)

Heat supplied by heater ($Q_{supplied}$) = Power $\times$ Time = $2500 \text{ J/s} \times 300 \text{ s} = 750000 \text{ J}$.

Heat absorbed by liquid ($Q_{liquid}$) = $m_l \times c_l \times \Delta T = 2 \times c_l \times 50 = 100 c_l$, where $c_l$ is the specific heat capacity of the liquid.

Heat absorbed by calorimeter ($Q_{cal}$) = $C_{cal} \times \Delta T = 450 \times 50 = 22500 \text{ J}$.

Assuming no heat loss: Heat supplied = Heat absorbed by liquid + Heat absorbed by calorimeter.

$Q_{supplied} = Q_{liquid} + Q_{cal}$

$750000 = 100 c_l + 22500$

$100 c_l = 750000 - 22500 = 727500$

$c_l = 727500 / 100 = 7275 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$.

The specific heat capacity of the liquid is $7275 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$.

(b) Specific Latent Heat of Vaporization ($L_v$):

The specific latent heat of vaporization of a substance is the quantity of heat energy required to change a unit mass (e.g., 1 kg) of the substance from the liquid state to the gaseous state (vapor) at its boiling point without any change in temperature.

(c) Domestic Refrigerator:

(i) Reason for Using Volatile Liquid (Refrigerant):

A volatile liquid is used because it evaporates easily at low temperatures and pressures (absorbing latent heat) and condenses back to liquid at higher temperatures and pressures (releasing latent heat). This property allows it to efficiently transfer heat from the cold interior to the warmer exterior.

(ii) Vaporization and Condensation:

  • Vaporization (in Evaporator/Cooling Compartment): The liquid refrigerant flows through a narrow capillary tube or expansion valve, causing a significant drop in pressure as it enters the wider tubes of the evaporator inside the cooling compartment. At this low pressure, the liquid boils and vaporizes even at low temperatures, absorbing its latent heat of vaporization from the surroundings (the inside of the fridge), thus cooling the compartment.
  • Condensation (in Condenser/Cooling Fins): The low-pressure vapor is drawn into the compressor, which increases its pressure and temperature significantly. This hot, high-pressure vapor then flows into the condenser coils (cooling fins) located outside the refrigerator, usually at the back. Here, the vapor is at a higher temperature than the surrounding air. As it loses heat to the air, it condenses back into a high-pressure liquid, releasing its latent heat of vaporization to the surroundings.

(iii) How Cooling Takes Place:

Cooling occurs primarily in the evaporator section inside the refrigerator compartment. As the volatile refrigerant liquid turns into a gas (vaporizes) within the evaporator coils at low pressure, it absorbs the necessary latent heat of vaporization from the air and contents inside the fridge. This removal of heat lowers the temperature of the compartment.

(iv) Purpose of Double Wall:

The double wall, usually filled with insulating material (like foam), minimizes heat transfer by conduction and convection between the warmer outside environment and the cold interior of the refrigerator. This reduces the rate at which heat leaks into the fridge, improving efficiency and reducing the workload on the refrigeration cycle.

(d) Steam and Water Mixing:

Given:

  • Mass of steam ($m_s$) = 4.0 g = 0.004 kg
  • Initial temp of steam = $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Mass of water ($m_w$) = 450 g = 0.450 kg
  • Initial temp of water ($T_w$) = $10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Final temperature of mixture = $T^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • $L_v$ (steam) = $2260 \text{ kJ/kg} = 2.26 \times 10^6 \text{ J/kg}$
  • $c_w$ (water) = $4200 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$
  • Container absorbs negligible heat.

(i) Expression for Heat Lost by Steam ($Q_{lost}$):

Heat lost occurs in two stages:

  1. Condensation at $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$: $Q_1 = m_s \times L_v$
  2. Cooling of condensed water from $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to $T^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$: $Q_2 = m_s \times c_w \times (100 - T)$
Total Heat Lost $Q_{lost} = Q_1 + Q_2 = m_s L_v + m_s c_w (100 - T)$

$Q_{lost} = (0.004 \times 2.26 \times 10^6) + (0.004 \times 4200 \times (100 - T))$

$Q_{lost} = 9040 + 16.8(100 - T)$ Joules

(ii) Expression for Heat Gained by Water ($Q_{gained}$):

Heat gained by the initial water as it warms from $10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to $T^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$:

$Q_{gained} = m_w \times c_w \times (T - T_w)$

$Q_{gained} = 0.450 \times 4200 \times (T - 10)$

$Q_{gained} = 1890(T - 10)$ Joules

(iii) Determine Value of T:

Assuming no heat loss to surroundings: Heat Lost = Heat Gained.

$Q_{lost} = Q_{gained}$

$9040 + 16.8(100 - T) = 1890(T - 10)$

$9040 + 1680 - 16.8T = 1890T - 18900$

$10720 - 16.8T = 1890T - 18900$

Collect T terms on one side and constants on the other:

$10720 + 18900 = 1890T + 16.8T$

$29620 = 1906.8 T$

$T = 29620 / 1906.8 \approx 15.534^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$

The final temperature of the mixture is approximately $15.5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

The question combines principles of electrical heating, thermodynamics of refrigeration, and heat transfer calculations involving phase changes.

Question 4 (20 marks)

A pile driver of mass 300 kg is used to drive a pile of mass 500 kg vertically into the ground. The pile driver falls freely through a distance of 5.0 m. Rebounding is negligible. (Correction: Q4 in OCR seems a duplicate of Q4(b) from July 2014, but with different numbers and slightly different wording. Let's solve this version.) Determine:

Assumptions: g = 9.81 m/s². Ground resistance (R) is constant. Rebounding is negligible means it's an inelastic collision.

(a) the velocity of the driver immediately before impact; (4 marks)

(b) the velocity of the pile immediately after the impact; (Assume pile and driver move together) (4 marks)

(c) the depth of penetration of the pile after impact given that the ground resisting force is constant and equal to 115 kN; (8 marks)

(d) the time taken for the penetration. (4 marks)

Answer

This question involves calculations related to impact, energy, momentum, and kinematics in a pile driving scenario.

Given:

  • Mass of pile driver (hammer, $m_h$) = 300 kg
  • Mass of pile ($m_p$) = 500 kg
  • Drop height (h) = 5.0 m
  • Ground resistance (R) = 115 kN = $115 \times 10^3$ N
  • Collision is inelastic (rebounding negligible).
  • Assume $g = 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$.

(a) Velocity of driver immediately before impact ($u_h$):

Using conservation of energy (PE to KE): $m_h g h = \frac{1}{2} m_h u_h^2$.

$u_h = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 5.0} = \sqrt{98.1} \approx 9.9045 \text{ m/s}$.

Velocity before impact $\approx 9.90$ m/s.

(b) Velocity of pile immediately after impact ($v_{common}$):

Since the collision is inelastic, the hammer and pile move together immediately after impact. Use conservation of linear momentum:

$m_h u_h + m_p (0) = (m_h + m_p) v_{common}$

$300 \times 9.9045 = (300 + 500) v_{common}$

$2971.35 = 800 v_{common}$

$v_{common} = 2971.35 / 800 \approx 3.714 \text{ m/s}$.

Velocity immediately after impact $\approx 3.71$ m/s.

(c) Depth of penetration (d):

Use the work-energy principle during penetration. The initial kinetic energy of the combined system is dissipated by the net work done against resistance and gravity as it penetrates a distance $d$.

Initial KE of system ($KE_i$) = $\frac{1}{2}(m_h + m_p) v_{common}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(800)(3.714)^2 \approx \frac{1}{2}(800)(13.794) \approx 5517.6 \text{ J}$.

Forces acting during penetration:

  • Downward force (Weight): $W_{total} = (m_h + m_p)g = 800 \times 9.81 = 7848$ N.
  • Upward force (Resistance): $R = 115000$ N.

Net upward force (retarding force) $F_{net} = R - W_{total} = 115000 - 7848 = 107152$ N.

Work done by the net retarding force = Change in KE.

$F_{net} \times d = KE_i - KE_{final}$ (where $KE_{final} = 0$)

$107152 \times d = 5517.6 - 0$

$d = 5517.6 / 107152 \approx 0.05149 \text{ m}$.

Depth of penetration $\approx 0.0515$ m or 5.15 cm.

(d) Time taken for penetration (t):

We can use impulse-momentum or kinematics.

Using kinematics: First find the deceleration ($a$) caused by the net retarding force.

$F_{net} = (m_h + m_p) a$

$a = F_{net} / (m_h + m_p) = -107152 / 800 \approx -133.94 \text{ m/s}^2$ (Negative sign indicates deceleration).

Using $v = u + at$, where $v=0$ (final velocity), $u=v_{common} \approx 3.714$ m/s.

$0 = 3.714 - 133.94 t$

$133.94 t = 3.714$

$t = 3.714 / 133.94 \approx 0.0277 \text{ s}$.

Alternatively, using $d = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)t$:

$0.05149 = \frac{1}{2}(3.714 + 0) t$

$t = (2 \times 0.05149) / 3.714 = 0.10298 / 3.714 \approx 0.0277 \text{ s}$.

Time taken for penetration $\approx 0.0277$ seconds.

The analysis involves applying principles of energy conservation, momentum conservation, work-energy theorem, and kinematics.

Answer

This question differentiates between flywheels and governors, defines governor characteristics, and involves calculations from tensile test data.

(a) Flywheel vs. Speed Governor:

FeatureFlywheelSpeed Governor
FunctionMinimizes speed fluctuations *during each cycle* of operation due to variations in engine turning moment or load torque within the cycle. Stores and releases energy.Controls the *mean speed* of the engine over a period by regulating the fuel/working fluid supply when the *external load* changes. Maintains speed within set limits.
Operation PrincipleInertia - stores kinetic energy when supply > demand, releases it when demand > supply.Senses speed change (usually via centrifugal force) and adjusts fuel supply accordingly.
LocationMounted on the main crankshaft.Driven by the engine (e.g., via gears from camshaft/crankshaft) but controls the throttle/fuel mechanism.
Control ActionControls cyclic fluctuations. Does not control mean speed over long term.Controls mean speed based on load changes. Does not control cyclic fluctuations.

(b) Governor Characteristics:

(i) Sensitivity: The sensitivity of a governor is a measure of its responsiveness to a small change in speed. A governor is said to be sensitive if its sleeve moves significantly for a small fractional change in speed. It is often defined as the ratio of the range of speed to the mean equilibrium speed. A smaller range for a given sleeve lift means higher sensitivity.

(ii) Stability: A governor is stable if, for each speed within its operating range, there is a definite and unique configuration (i.e., sleeve position and flyball radius). If the speed increases, the flyballs should move to a new radius and remain there; they shouldn't oscillate indefinitely (hunt). Stability requires the controlling force to increase at a faster rate than the centrifugal force as the radius of rotation increases.

(iii) Isochronous: An isochronous governor is one with infinite sensitivity. This means the equilibrium speed is constant for all radii of rotation of the flyballs within the working range (i.e., the speed does not change at all from no-load to full-load). While theoretically ideal for perfect speed control, purely isochronous governors tend to be unstable and hunt (oscillate between fully open and fully closed throttle positions).

(c) Tensile Test Calculations:

Given:

  • Original diameter ($D_0$) = 12.5 mm $\implies$ Radius $r_0 = 6.25$ mm
  • Original gauge length ($L_0$) = 200 mm
  • Final length ($L_f$) = 257 mm
  • Diameter at fracture ($D_f$) = 7.85 mm $\implies$ Radius $r_f = 3.925$ mm
  • Load at yield point ($P_y$) = 34575 N
  • Maximum load ($P_{max}$) = 49023 N

Calculate original cross-sectional area ($A_0$):

$A_0 = \pi r_0^2 = \pi (6.25 \text{ mm})^2 = \pi (39.0625) \approx 122.718 \text{ mm}^2$.

Calculate area at fracture ($A_f$):

$A_f = \pi r_f^2 = \pi (3.925 \text{ mm})^2 = \pi (15.4056) \approx 48.396 \text{ mm}^2$.

(i) Tensile Strength (Ultimate Tensile Strength, UTS):

UTS = Maximum Load / Original Area

UTS = $49023 \text{ N} / 122.718 \text{ mm}^2 \approx 399.47 \text{ N/mm}^2$ (or MPa).

Tensile Strength $\approx 399.5$ MPa.

(ii) Stress at Yield Point (Yield Strength):

Yield Stress ($\sigma_y$) = Load at Yield Point / Original Area

$\sigma_y = 34575 \text{ N} / 122.718 \text{ mm}^2 \approx 281.74 \text{ N/mm}^2$ (or MPa).

Stress at Yield Point $\approx 281.7$ MPa.

(iii) Percentage Reduction in Area (%RA):

%RA = $\frac{A_0 - A_f}{A_0} \times 100\%$

%RA = $\frac{122.718 - 48.396}{122.718} \times 100\% = \frac{74.322}{122.718} \times 100\% \approx 0.6056 \times 100\%$.

% Reduction in Area $\approx 60.6\%$.

(iv) Percentage Elongation (%El):

%El = $\frac{L_f - L_0}{L_0} \times 100\%$

%El = $\frac{257 - 200}{200} \times 100\% = \frac{57}{200} \times 100\% = 0.285 \times 100\%$.

% Elongation $\approx 28.5\%$.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) State the meaning of each of the following;

(i) ohmic conductors; (1 mark)

(ii) electric power; (1 mark)

(iii) electrical energy. (1 mark)

(b) Two resistors are connected in series across a 24 V supply and a current of 3 A flows in the circuit. If one of the resistors has a resistance of 2 $\Omega$, determine;

(i) the value of the other resistor; (3 marks)

(ii) the p.d across the 2 $\Omega$ resistor; (2 marks)

(iii) the amount of energy consumed if the circuit is connected for 50 hours. (3 marks)

(c) Figure 3 shows a direct current circuit.

Determine:

(i) the battery voltage V and the total resistance of the circuit; (4 marks)

(ii) the values of resistors R$_{1}$, R$_{2}$ and R$_{3}$ given that the p.d across R$_{1}$, R$_{2}$ and R$_{3}$ are 5 V, 2 V and 6 V respectively. (5 marks)

Answer

This question covers basic DC circuit definitions and calculations involving series circuits and Ohm's Law.

(a) Definitions:

(i) Ohmic Conductors: Conductors that obey Ohm's Law, meaning the current flowing through them is directly proportional to the voltage applied across them, provided physical conditions (like temperature) remain constant. Their resistance is constant regardless of the applied voltage or current. Examples include most metals at constant temperature.

(ii) Electric Power (P): The rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted into another form (like heat, light, mechanical work). It is measured in Watts (W) or Joules per second (J/s). Formulas: $P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R$.

(iii) Electrical Energy (E or W): The total work done or energy transferred by an electric current over a period of time. It is the product of power and time. Measured in Joules (J) or kilowatt-hours (kWh). Formulas: $E = P \times t = VIt = I^2Rt = (V^2/R)t$.

(b) Series Circuit Calculation:

Given: Supply Voltage ($V_S$) = 24 V, Current (I) = 3 A, Resistor 1 ($R_1$) = 2 $\Omega$. Let the other resistor be $R_2$.

(i) Value of the other resistor ($R_2$):

Total resistance ($R_{total}$) = $V_S / I = 24 \text{ V} / 3 \text{ A} = 8 \Omega$.

In a series circuit, $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2$.

$8 \Omega = 2 \Omega + R_2$

$R_2 = 8 - 2 = 6 \Omega$.

The value of the other resistor is 6 $\Omega$.

(ii) P.D. across the 2 $\Omega$ resistor ($V_1$):

Using Ohm's Law: $V_1 = I \times R_1 = 3 \text{ A} \times 2 \Omega = 6 \text{ V}$.

The p.d. across the 2 $\Omega$ resistor is 6 V.

(iii) Energy consumed in 50 hours:

Time (t) = 50 hours = $50 \times 3600 = 180000$ seconds.

Total power consumed by the circuit ($P_{total}$) = $V_S \times I = 24 \text{ V} \times 3 \text{ A} = 72 \text{ W}$.

Energy consumed (E) = $P_{total} \times t = 72 \text{ W} \times 180000 \text{ s} = 12,960,000 \text{ J}$.

Alternatively, in kWh: $P_{total} = 72 \text{ W} = 0.072 \text{ kW}$.

$E = 0.072 \text{ kW} \times 50 \text{ h} = 3.6 \text{ kWh}$.

Energy consumed is 12,960,000 J or 3.6 kWh.

(c) Circuit Analysis (Fig 3):

Given: Total current ($I_{total}$) = 1.5 A. Resistors $R_1, R_2, R_3$ are in series.

Voltage across $R_1$ ($V_1$) = 5 V.

Voltage across $R_2$ ($V_2$) = 2 V.

Voltage across $R_3$ ($V_3$) = 6 V.

(i) Battery voltage (V) and Total resistance ($R_{total}$):

In a series circuit, the total voltage is the sum of individual voltages.

Battery voltage $V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = 5 \text{ V} + 2 \text{ V} + 6 \text{ V} = 13 \text{ V}$.

Total resistance of the circuit ($R_{total}$) = $V / I_{total} = 13 \text{ V} / 1.5 \text{ A} \approx 8.667 \Omega$.

Battery Voltage = 13 V, Total Resistance $\approx 8.67 \Omega$.

(ii) Values of resistors $R_1, R_2, R_3$:

The current through each resistor in series is the total current, $I_{total} = 1.5$ A.

$R_1 = V_1 / I_{total} = 5 \text{ V} / 1.5 \text{ A} \approx 3.333 \Omega$.

$R_2 = V_2 / I_{total} = 2 \text{ V} / 1.5 \text{ A} \approx 1.333 \Omega$.

$R_3 = V_3 / I_{total} = 6 \text{ V} / 1.5 \text{ A} = 4.0 \Omega$.

(Check: $R_1 + R_2 + R_3 \approx 3.333 + 1.333 + 4.0 = 8.666 \Omega$, which matches $R_{total}$).

$R_1 \approx 3.33 \Omega$, $R_2 \approx 1.33 \Omega$, $R_3 = 4.0 \Omega$.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Name five quantities that a cathode ray oscilloscope (CRO) is capable of measuring. (5 marks)

(b) State three other instruments which also measure various quantities. Indicate the quantities measured by each. (6 marks)

(c) Explain the principle of operation of a single phase transformer. (9 marks)

Answer

This question asks about the measurement capabilities of a CRO, other measuring instruments, and the fundamental operating principle of a transformer.

(a) Quantities Measurable by a CRO:

A Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO) is a versatile instrument primarily used for displaying and analyzing electrical waveforms. It can measure:

  • Voltage (DC and AC): By measuring the vertical deflection of the trace against the calibrated Y-axis (Volts/Division). Peak voltage, peak-to-peak voltage, and RMS voltage (for sinusoidal waves) can be determined.
  • Time Period: By measuring the horizontal length of one complete cycle of a waveform against the calibrated X-axis (Time/Division).
  • Frequency: Calculated as the reciprocal of the measured time period ($f = 1/T$).
  • Phase Difference: By displaying two waveforms simultaneously (using dual channels) or using Lissajous figures, the phase relationship between two signals can be determined.
  • Waveform Shape/Analysis: Visual inspection of the displayed waveform allows identification of its shape (sine, square, triangle, etc.) and detection of distortion, noise, or other anomalies.
  • Rise Time / Fall Time: For pulse waveforms, the time taken for the signal to rise or fall between specified levels (e.g., 10% to 90%) can be measured.
  • DC Component / AC Component: By switching the input coupling, the presence and magnitude of DC offset or the AC component can be observed.
  • (Any five valid quantities)

(b) Other Measuring Instruments and Quantities:

  • Ammeter: Measures electric current (flow of charge). Unit: Ampere (A).
  • Voltmeter: Measures electric potential difference (voltage) between two points. Unit: Volt (V).
  • Ohmmeter: Measures electrical resistance. Unit: Ohm ($\Omega$).
  • Wattmeter: Measures electrical power (rate of energy transfer). Unit: Watt (W).
  • Frequency Meter: Measures the frequency of an AC signal. Unit: Hertz (Hz).
  • Multimeter: A versatile instrument combining the functions of an ammeter, voltmeter, and ohmmeter (and sometimes others like capacitance or frequency measurement).
  • Energy Meter (kWh meter): Measures the total electrical energy consumed over time. Unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh).

(c) Principle of Operation of a Single Phase Transformer:

A single-phase transformer operates on the principle of **mutual induction** between two electrically isolated but magnetically coupled coils.

  1. Primary Winding Excitation: An alternating voltage ($V_p$) is applied to the primary winding (a coil with $N_p$ turns wound on a laminated iron core).
  2. Alternating Magnetic Flux Production: The alternating voltage drives an alternating current ($I_p$, primarily the magnetizing current $I_m$ on no-load) through the primary winding. According to Ampere's law, this alternating current produces an alternating magnetic flux ($\Phi$) within the iron core.
  3. Flux Linkage: The laminated iron core provides a low reluctance path, concentrating and guiding almost all of this alternating magnetic flux so that it links with the secondary winding (a coil with $N_s$ turns wound on the same core).
  4. Induced EMF in Secondary (Mutual Induction): According to Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction, the changing magnetic flux ($\frac{d\Phi}{dt}$) linking the secondary winding induces an alternating electromotive force (EMF or voltage, $E_s$) across its terminals. The magnitude of this induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of flux and the number of turns in the secondary winding ($E_s = -N_s \frac{d\Phi}{dt}$).
  5. Induced EMF in Primary (Self-Induction): The same changing flux also links the primary winding itself, inducing a back EMF ($E_p$) according to Faraday's Law ($E_p = -N_p \frac{d\Phi}{dt}$). In an ideal transformer, this back EMF is equal and opposite to the applied primary voltage ($V_p \approx E_p$).
  6. Voltage Transformation Ratio: Since the flux ($\Phi$) and its rate of change ($\frac{d\Phi}{dt}$) are common to both windings (ideally), the ratio of the induced EMFs is equal to the ratio of the number of turns: $\frac{E_s}{E_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$. For an ideal transformer, $E_s \approx V_s$ (secondary terminal voltage on no-load) and $E_p \approx V_p$. Therefore, $\frac{V_s}{V_p} \approx \frac{N_s}{N_p} = n$ (turns ratio). This allows the transformer to step voltage up ($N_s > N_p$) or step voltage down ($N_s < N_p$).
  7. Power Transfer: When a load is connected to the secondary, a secondary current ($I_s$) flows. This current creates its own MMF, which tends to oppose the original flux. To maintain the flux (and thus the back EMF $E_p$ to balance $V_p$), the primary draws an additional load current ($I'_p$) from the supply such that the primary load MMF ($N_p I'_p$) counteracts the secondary MMF ($N_s I_s$). In an ideal transformer, input power equals output power ($V_p I_p \approx V_s I_s$), leading to the inverse current ratio: $\frac{I_p}{I_s} \approx \frac{N_s}{N_p}$.

Thus, a transformer transfers electrical energy from the primary circuit to the secondary circuit via a changing magnetic field, allowing voltage and current levels to be changed while (ideally) keeping the frequency constant.

The question covers the practical use of CROs, common electrical instruments, and the fundamental theory behind transformer operation based on mutual induction.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) (i) State three ways in which the capacitance of a capacitor can be varied. (3 marks)

(ii) Name three types of capacitors. (3 marks)

(b) A capacitor of 20 $\mu$F charged to 500 V is connected in parallel with another of 10 $\mu$F capacitance charged to 200 V. Determine the energy loss. (8 marks)

(c) Differentiate between permanent and temporary magnets. (6 marks)

Answer

This question covers capacitor properties, energy storage and redistribution in capacitor circuits, and the difference between permanent and temporary magnets.

(a) Capacitance Variation and Types:

(i) Varying Capacitance (Parallel Plate Capacitor Formula: $C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d}$):

  • Varying the Overlapping Plate Area (A): Increasing the area of overlap between the plates increases capacitance, while decreasing the area decreases it. This is used in variable air capacitors (tuning capacitors).
  • Varying the Distance Between Plates (d): Decreasing the separation distance increases capacitance, while increasing the distance decreases it. This method is less common for variable capacitors but affects fixed capacitance design.
  • Varying the Dielectric Material ($\varepsilon_r$): Changing the dielectric material between the plates alters the relative permittivity ($\varepsilon_r$). Inserting a dielectric with a higher $\varepsilon_r$ increases capacitance. Removing it decreases capacitance. Some trimmer capacitors work by adjusting the position of a dielectric material.

(ii) Types of Capacitors:

  • Electrolytic Capacitors: (e.g., Aluminium, Tantalum) - Polarized, high capacitance values, used for filtering, smoothing in DC circuits.
  • Ceramic Capacitors: (e.g., Disc, Multilayer) - Non-polarized, wide range of values, good frequency characteristics, used for coupling, decoupling, timing.
  • Film Capacitors: (e.g., Polyester (Mylar), Polypropylene, Polystyrene) - Non-polarized, stable, good insulation, used in filters, timing circuits, audio applications.
  • Variable Capacitors: (e.g., Air-gap tuning capacitors, Trimmer capacitors) - Capacitance can be adjusted mechanically. Used in tuning circuits (radios), oscillators.
  • Mica Capacitors: (Silver Mica) - Non-polarized, very stable, low loss, high precision, used in RF circuits, filters.
  • Paper Capacitors: (Older type, often oil-impregnated) - Non-polarized, used in power applications, motor start.
  • (Any three distinct types)

(b) Energy Loss When Connecting Charged Capacitors:

Given:

  • $C_1 = 20 \mu F = 20 \times 10^{-6} F$, $V_1 = 500 V$
  • $C_2 = 10 \mu F = 10 \times 10^{-6} F$, $V_2 = 200 V$
Connected in parallel. Assume positive plate to positive plate connection.

Step 1: Calculate initial charges and energies.

$Q_1 = C_1 V_1 = (20 \times 10^{-6}) \times 500 = 0.01 C$.

$Q_2 = C_2 V_2 = (10 \times 10^{-6}) \times 200 = 0.002 C$.

Initial Energy ($E_i$) = $E_1 + E_2 = \frac{1}{2}C_1 V_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}C_2 V_2^2$

$E_1 = \frac{1}{2}(20 \times 10^{-6})(500)^2 = (10 \times 10^{-6})(250000) = 2.5 J$.

$E_2 = \frac{1}{2}(10 \times 10^{-6})(200)^2 = (5 \times 10^{-6})(40000) = 0.2 J$.

$E_i = 2.5 + 0.2 = 2.7 J$.

Step 2: Calculate conditions after connecting in parallel.

Total charge ($Q_{total}$) = $Q_1 + Q_2 = 0.01 + 0.002 = 0.012 C$ (Charge is conserved).

Total capacitance ($C_{total}$) = $C_1 + C_2 = 20 \mu F + 10 \mu F = 30 \mu F = 30 \times 10^{-6} F$.

Final common voltage ($V_f$) across the parallel combination:

$V_f = Q_{total} / C_{total} = 0.012 C / (30 \times 10^{-6} F) = 400 V$.

Step 3: Calculate final energy.

Final Energy ($E_f$) = $\frac{1}{2} C_{total} V_f^2$

$E_f = \frac{1}{2} (30 \times 10^{-6}) (400)^2 = (15 \times 10^{-6}) (160000) = 2.4 J$.

Step 4: Calculate energy loss.

Energy Loss ($\Delta E$) = Initial Energy - Final Energy

$\Delta E = E_i - E_f = 2.7 J - 2.4 J = 0.3 J$.

The energy loss is 0.3 J. (This energy is typically dissipated as heat in the connecting wires and as electromagnetic radiation during the charge redistribution).
