Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) (i) State three properties of X-rays. (3 marks)

(ii) Describe the production of X-rays in an X-ray tube. (4 marks)

(b) An object moving with a simple harmonic motion has an amplitude of 0.02 m and a frequency of 20 Hz. Determine the maximum acceleration. (4 marks)

(c) The atomic notation of element is ${ }_{18}^{40} \mathrm{X}$. Draw the electronic configuration of the atom. (3 marks)

(d) Sodium sulphate is formed by reacting $25 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$ of dilute sulphuric acid solution with $40 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$ of 0.4 M sodium hydroxide solution. The chemical reaction equation is given by:

$\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4(\mathrm{aq})}+2 \mathrm{NaOH}_{(\mathrm{aq})} \rightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4(\mathrm{aq})}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{(l)}$

Determine the:

(i) moles of sodium hydroxide; (2 marks)

(ii) moles of sulphuric acid; (2 marks)

(iii) molarity of sulphuric acid. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers properties and production of X-rays, kinematics of SHM, atomic structure, and stoichiometry involving solution molarity.

(a) X-rays:

(i) Properties of X-rays:

  • Highly penetrating electromagnetic radiation.
  • Travel in straight lines at the speed of light in vacuum.
  • Not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (uncharged).
  • Cause ionization in gases.
  • Affect photographic film/plates.
  • Cause fluorescence in certain materials.
  • Have very short wavelengths (typically 0.01 to 10 nanometers).
  • (State any three)

(ii) Production of X-rays in an X-ray Tube:

X-rays are produced in an evacuated glass tube containing a cathode and an anode.

  1. Electron Emission: The cathode contains a filament (usually tungsten) which is heated by a low-voltage supply. This heating causes thermionic emission, releasing electrons.
  2. Electron Acceleration: A very high potential difference (accelerating voltage, typically tens or hundreds of kilovolts) is applied between the cathode (-) and the anode (+).
  3. Collision with Target: The high voltage accelerates the emitted electrons to very high speeds across the vacuum towards the anode, which contains a metal target (usually tungsten or molybdenum, embedded in a copper block for cooling).
  4. X-ray Generation: When these high-energy electrons strike the metal target, they rapidly decelerate and interact with the target atoms. Most (~99%) of their kinetic energy is converted into heat, but a small fraction (~1%) is converted into X-ray photons through:
    • Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation): Electrons slowing down near the target nuclei emit a continuous spectrum of X-rays.
    • Characteristic Radiation: Incident electrons knock out inner-shell electrons of target atoms; when outer-shell electrons drop down to fill the vacancies, X-rays with specific energies (characteristic of the target material) are emitted.
  5. Cooling: Due to the immense heat generated, the anode target requires efficient cooling (e.g., using circulating oil or water, or a rotating anode).

(b) SHM Maximum Acceleration:

Given: Amplitude (A) = 0.02 m, Frequency (f) = 20 Hz.

Angular frequency ($\omega$) = $2\pi f = 2\pi \times 20 = 40\pi$ rad/s.

Acceleration in SHM is given by $a = -\omega^2 x$, where x is displacement.

Maximum acceleration ($a_{max}$) occurs at the maximum displacement, i.e., when $x = \pm A$.

Magnitude of maximum acceleration $|a_{max}| = \omega^2 A$.

$a_{max} = (40\pi)^2 \times 0.02 = (1600 \pi^2) \times 0.02$.

$a_{max} = 32 \pi^2 \text{ m/s}^2$.

$a_{max} \approx 32 \times (9.8696) \approx 315.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Maximum acceleration $\approx 316 \text{ m/s}^2$.

(c) Electronic Configuration of ${ }_{18}^{40} \mathrm{X}$:

The notation ${ }_{Z}^{A} \mathrm{X}$ means: A = Mass Number = 40, Z = Atomic Number = 18.

Atomic number (Z) = Number of protons = Number of electrons (in a neutral atom).

Element X has 18 electrons.

Electronic configuration shows the arrangement of electrons in shells (energy levels).

  • 1st Shell (n=1): Max 2 electrons -> $1s^2$
  • 2nd Shell (n=2): Max 8 electrons -> $2s^2 2p^6$
  • 3rd Shell (n=3): Remaining electrons $18 - 2 - 8 = 8$ electrons -> $3s^2 3p^6$

Electronic Configuration: **2, 8, 8** (or $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6$).

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) State the effect of each of the following on the speed of sound:

(i) density of the medium; (2 marks)

(ii) temperature of the medium. (2 marks)

(b) A plane progressive wave is governed by the expression $y=0.08 \sin (36 t-9 x)$ metres. Determine the:

(i) amplitude; (1 mark)

(ii) wavelength; (2 marks)

(iii) frequency; (2 marks)

(iv) speed of the wave. (1 mark)

(c) (i) Define each of the following:

(I) heat capacity; (1 mark)

(II) latent heat. (1 mark)

(ii) Explain each of the following with respect to heat:

(I) absolute scale; (2 marks)

(II) black body radiation. (2 marks)

(d) With the aid of energy level diagram, describe exothermic reaction with respect to thermo-chemistry. (4 marks)

Answer

This question covers factors affecting the speed of sound, analysis of a wave equation, definitions in thermal physics, and concepts of absolute temperature, black body radiation, and reaction energetics.

(a) Effect on Speed of Sound:

The speed of sound ($v$) in a medium generally depends on its elasticity (Bulk Modulus, B, or Young's Modulus, E) and its density ($\rho$). For fluids: $v = \sqrt{B/\rho}$. For solids: $v = \sqrt{E/\rho}$.

(i) Density of the Medium ($\rho$): Generally, for media with similar elastic properties, the speed of sound is **inversely proportional to the square root of the density**. Higher density means slower sound speed ($v \propto 1/\sqrt{\rho}$), because denser materials have more inertia, making it harder for particles to oscillate and pass on the disturbance quickly.

(ii) Temperature of the Medium (T): For gases, the speed of sound is directly proportional to the square root of the absolute temperature ($v \propto \sqrt{T}$). As temperature increases, gas molecules move faster, leading to quicker transmission of the sound disturbance. In solids and liquids, the effect is more complex as both elasticity and density can change with temperature, but generally, speed increases slightly with temperature in liquids and can increase or decrease in solids depending on the material.

(b) Wave Equation Analysis:

Given: $y = 0.08 \sin(36t - 9x)$ metres.

Compare with standard form $y = A \sin(\omega t - kx)$.

(i) Amplitude (A): $A = 0.08$ m.

(ii) Wavelength ($\lambda$): Wave number $k = 9$. Since $k = 2\pi/\lambda$, then $\lambda = 2\pi/k = 2\pi/9$ metres ($\approx 0.698$ m).

(iii) Frequency (f): Angular frequency $\omega = 36$. Since $\omega = 2\pi f$, then $f = \omega/(2\pi) = 36/(2\pi) = 18/\pi$ Hz ($\approx 5.73$ Hz).

(iv) Speed of the wave (v): $v = f\lambda = (18/\pi) \times (2\pi/9) = (18 \times 2\pi) / (9\pi) = 36\pi / 9\pi = 4$ m/s.

(Alternatively, $v = \omega/k = 36 / 9 = 4$ m/s).

(c) Thermal Physics Definitions and Concepts:

(i) Definitions:

  • (I) Heat Capacity (C): The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of an entire object or system by one degree Celsius (or Kelvin). Unit: J/K or J/°C.
  • (II) Latent Heat (L): The heat energy absorbed or released during a phase change (like melting, freezing, boiling, condensation) of a substance at a constant temperature and pressure. Specific Latent Heat (per unit mass) unit: J/kg.

(ii) Explanations:

  • (I) Absolute Scale (Kelvin Scale): A thermodynamic temperature scale where zero Kelvin (0 K) represents absolute zero, the theoretical temperature at which particles have minimum possible thermal energy (minimal vibration). Temperature intervals on the Kelvin scale are the same size as on the Celsius scale (1 K change = 1 °C change). $K = °C + 273.15$. It is called absolute because it's independent of the properties of any specific substance.
  • (II) Black Body Radiation: A black body is an idealized object that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation, regardless of frequency or angle of incidence. When in thermal equilibrium, it also emits radiation (called black body radiation) with a characteristic continuous spectrum and intensity that depends only on its absolute temperature. The spectrum follows Planck's law, and the total power radiated follows the Stefan-Boltzmann law ($P \propto T^4$). Real objects approximate black bodies to varying degrees.

(d) Exothermic Reaction Energy Level Diagram:

Description:

  1. The diagram plots the potential energy or enthalpy (H) of the reacting system against the progress of the reaction (reaction coordinate).
  2. The reactants start at a certain energy level ($H_{reactants}$).
  3. To react, the reactants must overcome an energy barrier called the Activation Energy ($E_a$), reaching a high-energy transition state (peak of the curve).
  4. In an **exothermic** reaction, the products are at a *lower* energy level ($H_{products}$) than the reactants.
  5. The difference in energy between products and reactants ($H_{products} - H_{reactants}$) is the enthalpy change of the reaction ($\Delta H$).
  6. For an exothermic reaction, $H_{products} < H_{reactants}$, so $\Delta H$ is **negative**, indicating that energy is released to the surroundings during the reaction.

The question assesses understanding of sound propagation, wave equations, thermal definitions, and reaction energetics.

Answer

This question covers power, work, force, static equilibrium of forces, Pitot tubes, and material testing.

(a) Power, Work, Force:

(i) Power Definition: Power is the rate at which work is done or the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. Unit: Watt (W).

(ii) Calculation:

Given: Height (h) = 160 m, Time (t) = 20 s, Energy expended (Work done, W) = 80 kJ = 80000 J.

(I) Power (P):

$P = \text{Work Done} / \text{Time} = 80000 \text{ J} / 20 \text{ s} = 4000 \text{ W}$ (or 4 kW).

(II) Force (F):

Assuming the energy expended is primarily work done against gravity by the lifting force F.

Work Done = Force $\times$ Distance (Height).

$W = F \times h$.

$80000 \text{ J} = F \times 160 \text{ m}$.

$F = 80000 / 160 = 500$ N.

(b) Equilibrium and Forces:

(i) Equilibrium of Coplanar Forces: A body is in static equilibrium under the action of a system of coplanar forces if it has no tendency to accelerate, either linearly or rotationally. This requires two conditions to be met:

  1. The vector sum of all forces acting on the body must be zero ($\sum \vec{F} = 0$, ensuring no linear acceleration).
  2. The algebraic sum of the moments of all forces about any point in the plane must be zero ($\sum M = 0$, ensuring no angular acceleration).

$F_{1x} = 10 \cos(0) = 10.0$

$F_{1y} = 10 \sin(0) = 0.0$

$F_{2x} = 15 \cos(60) = 15(0.5) = 7.5$

$F_{2y} = 15 \sin(60) = 15(\sqrt{3}/2) \approx 12.99$

$F_{3x} = 20 \cos(135) = 20(-\sqrt{2}/2) \approx -14.14$

$F_{3y} = 20 \sin(135) = 20(\sqrt{2}/2) \approx 14.14$

$F_{4x} = F \cos(270) = 0.0$

$F_{4y} = F \sin(270) = F(-1) = -F$

$\sum F_x = 10.0 + 7.5 - 14.14 + 0 = 0$

$3.36 = 0$. This indicates an error in the problem statement or diagram, as the horizontal forces do not balance without F having a horizontal component. Assuming the forces *as drawn* are meant to be in equilibrium despite the diagram potentially being slightly inaccurate.

Let's proceed assuming equilibrium is intended. The calculation $3.36=0$ shows the system *cannot* be in equilibrium as drawn with F purely vertical. However, if we must find F for equilibrium based on vertical balance only:

$\sum F_y = 0.0 + 12.99 + 14.14 - F = 0$

If F is required to balance the system, it needs both x and y components. Let the balancing force be $\vec{F}_{balance}$. $R_x = 3.36$, $R_y = 27.13$. $\vec{F}_{balance}$ must be equal and opposite to the resultant of the first three forces. $\vec{F}_{balance} = - (3.36 \hat{i} + 27.13 \hat{j})$. This does not match the force F shown at 270°. There is likely an error in the question setup.

(i) Demerits:

  • Can easily get clogged by suspended particles in the fluid.
  • Measures velocity only at a single point, may not represent average velocity accurately without traversing.
  • Accuracy affected by turbulence.
  • Pressure difference measured is often small, requiring sensitive manometers/transducers.

(d) Destructive Tensile Test Outline:

A destructive tensile test measures a material's response to a gradually applied pulling (tensile) force until fracture.

  1. A standardized specimen (dog-bone shape) of the material is prepared with known dimensions (cross-sectional area, gauge length).
  2. The specimen is mounted securely in the grips of a tensile testing machine (e.g., Universal Testing Machine).
  3. An extensometer (or strain gauge) is attached to the gauge length to accurately measure elongation.
  4. A tensile load is applied gradually and increased at a controlled rate.
  5. Simultaneously, the applied load (Force) and the corresponding elongation (change in gauge length) are recorded continuously until the specimen breaks (fractures).
  6. Data is typically plotted as a Load-Extension or Stress-Strain curve, from which properties like Yield Strength, Ultimate Tensile Strength, Modulus of Elasticity, %Elongation, and %Reduction in Area can be determined.

The test is 'destructive' because the specimen is permanently deformed and ultimately broken.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Explain plasticity of a material. (2 marks)

(ii) Draw a labelled Hooke's Law curve for an elastic material. (Show Elastic Limit, Yield Point) (4 marks)

(b) (i) State two forces acting on a body moving at a constant speed in a circular path. (2 marks)

(ii) The speed of a motor shaft increases uniformly from zero to 280 revolutions per minute in 4 seconds. Determine the final:

(I) angular velocity; (2 marks)

(II) angular acceleration. (3 marks)

(c) Figure 2 shows a centrifugal type governor.

(i) Identify the type of the governor. (Specific type might be ambiguous from generic diagram) (1 mark)

(ii) Name the parts labelled A and B. (2 marks)

(iii) Explain how the governor controls engine speed when the load on the engine reduces. (4 marks)

Answer

This question covers material properties (plasticity, elasticity), rotational kinematics, and the operation of centrifugal governors.

(a) Material Properties:

(i) Plasticity: Plasticity is the ability of a solid material to undergo permanent deformation (a non-reversible change in shape) in response to applied forces without fracturing. Once the applied stress exceeds the material's elastic limit (or yield strength), plastic deformation occurs, and the material does not return to its original shape when the load is removed.

(ii) Hooke's Law Curve (Stress-Strain Curve for Elastic Material):

The curve shows stress ($\sigma$) on the y-axis versus strain ($\epsilon$) on the x-axis. For an elastic material obeying Hooke's Law up to the proportional limit, the initial part of the curve is a straight line through the origin ($\sigma = E\epsilon$, where E is Young's Modulus). The elastic limit marks the point beyond which permanent deformation occurs. The yield point marks the onset of significant plastic flow.

(b) Circular Motion and Rotational Kinematics:

(i) Forces on Body in Uniform Circular Motion:

  1. Centripetal Force: A net inward force acting towards the center of the circular path, responsible for changing the direction of the velocity vector and causing the circular motion ($F_c = mv^2/r$).
  2. **(Other Forces Balancing in Tangential/Vertical Direction):** While centripetal force is the net force radially, other forces might be acting. For example, if it's a car on a flat track, friction provides the centripetal force, while weight and normal force balance vertically. If it's a planet orbiting the sun, gravity provides the centripetal force. The question is slightly ambiguous, but the *required net force* is the centripetal force. If considering individual forces, it depends on the situation (e.g., tension, gravity, normal force, friction). Stating **Centripetal Force** and **Tangential Force (which is zero for constant speed)** is a possible interpretation. Or just **Centripetal Force** as the key resultant force.

Likely intended answers: 1. Centripetal force. 2. The force(s) providing the centripetal force (e.g., Tension, Friction, Gravity).

(ii) Motor Shaft Acceleration:

Given: Initial angular speed ($\omega_0$) = 0 rad/s.

Final angular speed (N) = 280 rev/min.

Time (t) = 4 seconds.

(I) Final Angular Velocity ($\omega_f$):

Convert N from rpm to rad/s.

$\omega_f = N \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 280 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = \frac{560\pi}{60} = \frac{28\pi}{3}$ rad/s.

$\omega_f \approx 29.32$ rad/s.

(II) Angular Acceleration ($\alpha$):

Assuming uniform acceleration: $\omega_f = \omega_0 + \alpha t$.

$\frac{28\pi}{3} = 0 + \alpha (4)$.

$\alpha = \frac{28\pi}{3 \times 4} = \frac{7\pi}{3}$ rad/s².

$\alpha \approx 7.33$ rad/s².

(c) Centrifugal Governor (Fig 2):

(i) Type of Governor: The diagram shows rotating flyballs on pivoted arms connected to a central sleeve. Without details like a central dead weight or springs, it most closely resembles a **Watt Governor** (the simplest type) or could be a generic representation of a centrifugal governor.

(ii) Parts Labelled A and B:

  • A: Flyball (or Governor Ball / Rotating Mass)
  • B: Sleeve

(iii) Control Action When Load Reduces:

  1. If the load on the engine reduces, the engine momentarily tends to speed up.
  2. This increases the rotational speed ($\omega$) of the governor spindle and the flyballs (A).
  3. The increased speed causes a larger outward centrifugal force ($F_c = m\omega^2 r$) on the flyballs.
  4. This larger force overcomes the inward controlling force (gravity/spring), causing the flyballs (A) to move outwards and upwards to a larger radius of rotation.
  5. The upward movement of the arms lifts the sleeve (B) on the governor spindle.
  6. The upward movement of the sleeve is transmitted through linkages to the engine's throttle valve.
  7. The throttle valve moves towards the closed position, reducing the supply of fuel (or steam) to the engine.
  8. The reduced fuel supply decreases the engine's power output, causing its speed to decrease back towards the desired equilibrium speed.

The question covers material behavior, circular motion concepts, rotational kinematics, and the operational principle of centrifugal governors.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Define each of the following electrical quantities:

(i) potential difference; (1 mark)

(ii) current. (1 mark)

(b) A 18 Watt electric light bulb is supplied by a 12 V solar battery rated 700 Ah. Determine the:

(i) current flowing through the bulb; (2 marks)

(ii) resistance of the bulb element; (2 marks)

(iii) supply duration. (Assuming battery can supply rated voltage until fully discharged) (3 marks)

(c) (i) Figure 3 shows construction diagram of Lead-acid cell. Name the parts labelled A, B, C and D. (4 marks)

(ii) Explain each of the following as used in electric cells:

(I) local action; (2 marks)

(II) polarization. (2 marks)

(d) Four cells generating an e.m.f of 2.2 V and having internal resistance of $0.9 \Omega$ each are connected in parallel. Determine the:

(i) total internal resistance; (1 mark)

(ii) voltage across the cells terminals when supplying a current of 0.6 A. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers basic electrical definitions, circuit calculations involving power and energy storage (battery capacity), lead-acid cell construction, cell defects, and analysis of parallel cell connections.

(a) Electrical Quantity Definitions:

(i) Potential Difference (Voltage): The potential difference (p.d.) between two points in an electric circuit is the work done per unit charge in moving charge between those two points. It represents the electrical 'pressure' driving the current. Unit: Volt (V).

(ii) Current (I): Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit. $I = dQ/dt$. Unit: Ampere (A).

(b) Light Bulb and Battery Calculation:

Given: Bulb Power (P) = 18 W, Supply Voltage (V) = 12 V, Battery Capacity = 700 Ah.

(i) Current flowing through the bulb (I):

Using $P = VI$.

$18 W = 12 V \times I$.

$I = 18 / 12 = 1.5 A$.

(ii) Resistance of the bulb element (R):

Using Ohm's Law $V = IR$ or $P = V^2/R$ or $P = I^2R$.

$R = V / I = 12 V / 1.5 A = 8 \Omega$.

(Check: $P = I^2R = (1.5)^2 \times 8 = 2.25 \times 8 = 18 W$. $P = V^2/R = 12^2 / 8 = 144 / 8 = 18 W$. Consistent).

(iii) Supply Duration (Time, t):

Battery Capacity = 700 Ah (Ampere-hours). This means the battery can supply a current of 700 A for 1 hour, or 1 A for 700 hours, or 1.5 A for t hours, etc., until discharged.

Capacity (Ah) = Current (A) $\times$ Time (h).

$700 \text{ Ah} = 1.5 \text{ A} \times t \text{ (hours)}$.

$t = 700 / 1.5 \approx 466.67$ hours.

The battery can supply the bulb for approximately 466.7 hours.

(c) Lead-Acid Cell and Defects:

(i) Naming Parts (Fig 3):

(Based on standard Lead-Acid cell construction):

  • A: Positive Plate (typically Lead Dioxide, $PbO_2$, on a lead grid)
  • B: Negative Plate (typically Spongy Lead, $Pb$, on a lead grid)
  • C: Separator (porous insulating material)
  • D: Electrolyte (dilute Sulphuric Acid, $H_2SO_4$)

(ii) Cell Defects Explanation:

  • (I) Local Action: A defect occurring primarily in primary cells (like simple Zinc-Carbon cells) where impurities (like iron or carbon) within the zinc electrode form tiny local electrochemical cells with the zinc itself in the presence of the electrolyte. This causes the zinc electrode to be consumed (corroded) even when the cell is not supplying external current, reducing the cell's shelf life and capacity.
  • (II) Polarization: A defect occurring mainly in primary cells where reaction products accumulate at one or both electrodes during discharge, hindering the main cell reaction and increasing internal resistance. A common example is the formation of hydrogen gas bubbles at the positive (carbon) electrode in a simple cell, which insulates the electrode and reduces the cell's voltage. Depolarizers (like Manganese Dioxide in Leclanché cells) are added to react with these products and reduce polarization.

(d) Parallel Cell Connection:

Given: 4 identical cells in parallel.

Each cell: EMF ($E_{cell}$) = 2.2 V, Internal resistance ($r_{cell}$) = 0.9 $\Omega$.

Load current ($I_L$) = 0.6 A.

(i) Total Internal Resistance ($r_{total}$):

For N identical cells in parallel, the total internal resistance is $r_{total} = r_{cell} / N$.

$r_{total} = 0.9 \Omega / 4 = 0.225 \Omega$.

(ii) Voltage across the cells terminals ($V_T$):

The total EMF of identical cells in parallel ($E_{total}$) is the same as the EMF of a single cell.

$E_{total} = E_{cell} = 2.2 V$.

The terminal voltage $V_T$ when supplying current $I_L$ is:

$V_T = E_{total} - I_L \times r_{total}$.

$V_T = 2.2 V - (0.6 A \times 0.225 \Omega)$.

$V_T = 2.2 - 0.135 = 2.065 V$.

The voltage across the battery terminals is 2.065 V.

The question covers fundamental electrical concepts, battery calculations, cell construction, defects, and analysis of parallel connections.

Question 6 (20 marks)

Answer

This question covers instrument safety, wattmeter connection, temperature coefficient of resistance, superposition theorem, and DC circuit analysis.

(a) Instrument Safety and Wattmeter Connection:

(i) Safety Practices with Instruments:

  • Select Correct Range: Always start with the highest range and reduce it as necessary to get a clear reading, avoiding overloading/damaging the instrument.
  • Correct Connection: Connect instruments correctly (Ammeters in series, Voltmeters in parallel) with proper polarity for DC measurements.
  • Isolate Before Connecting/Disconnecting: De-energize the circuit before connecting or disconnecting instruments, especially ammeters or in high-voltage circuits.
  • Use Insulated Probes/Tools: Ensure test leads and probes are properly insulated and in good condition.
  • Check Instrument Rating: Ensure the instrument's voltage/current ratings are suitable for the circuit being measured.
  • (State any two)

(ii) Wattmeter Connection Diagram:

A wattmeter measures power (P=VIcos$\phi$). It has two coils:

  • Current Coil (CC): Low resistance, connected in SERIES with the load to sense current (I).
  • Potential Coil (PC) or Voltage Coil (VC): High resistance, connected in PARALLEL across the load to sense voltage (V).

Labels: Supply (L, N terminals), Current Coil (CC) in series with Load, Potential Coil (PC) in parallel with Load, Load, Polarity markings (+/- or *) on coils (important for correct reading, usually common terminals connected together on supply side).

(b) Temperature Coefficient of Resistance:

(i) Explanation: The temperature coefficient of resistance ($\alpha$) quantifies how much the resistance of a material changes for each degree Celsius (or Kelvin) change in temperature, relative to its resistance at a specific reference temperature (e.g., $0^{\circ}$C or $20^{\circ}$C). A positive $\alpha$ (most metals) means resistance increases with temperature, while a negative $\alpha$ (semiconductors) means resistance decreases.

(ii) Resistance Calculation:

Given: Resistance at $T_1 = 50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_{50}$) = 30 $\Omega$.

Temperature coefficient at $T_0 = 0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($\alpha_0$) = $0.0038 /{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

Find resistance at $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ($R_0$).

Use the formula $R_T = R_0 (1 + \alpha_0 T)$.

$R_{50} = R_0 (1 + \alpha_0 \times 50)$.

$30 = R_0 (1 + 0.0038 \times 50)$.

$30 = R_0 (1 + 0.19)$.

$30 = R_0 (1.19)$.

$R_0 = 30 / 1.19 \approx 25.21 \Omega$.

The resistance at $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is approximately 25.21 $\Omega$.

(c) Superposition Theorem (DC Circuits):

In any linear DC network containing multiple independent voltage or current sources, the current flowing through (or the voltage across) any element is the algebraic sum of the currents (or voltages) produced by each independent source acting alone, with all other independent voltage sources replaced by short circuits and all other independent current sources replaced by open circuits.

(d) Circuit Analysis using Superposition (Fig 4):

Circuit: Left loop (12V, $R_1=4\Omega$, $R_c=8\Omega$), Right loop (6V, $R_2=6\Omega$, $R_c=8\Omega$). $R_c$ is common.

Find currents in branches: $I_{R1}$ (through 4$\Omega$), $I_{R2}$ (through 6$\Omega$), $I_{Rc}$ (through 8$\Omega$).

Step 1: Consider 12V source alone (6V source shorted).

The 8$\Omega$ and 6$\Omega$ resistors are in parallel.

$R_{p1} = (8 \times 6) / (8 + 6) = 48 / 14 = 24/7 \approx 3.429 \Omega$.

Total resistance $R_{T1} = 4 + R_{p1} = 4 + 24/7 = (28+24)/7 = 52/7 \approx 7.429 \Omega$.

Total current from 12V source ($I'_{R1}$) = $12 / R_{T1} = 12 / (52/7) = 84/52 = 21/13 \approx 1.615 A$ (rightwards).

Using current divider for $I'_{Rc}$ (current in 8$\Omega$, downwards):

$I'_{Rc} = I'_{R1} \times \frac{6}{8+6} = (21/13) \times (6/14) = (21/13) \times (3/7) = 9/13 \approx 0.692 A$ (downwards).

Current in 6$\Omega$ ($I'_{R2}$, downwards): $I'_{R2} = I'_{R1} - I'_{Rc} = (21/13) - (9/13) = 12/13 \approx 0.923 A$ (downwards through short).

Step 2: Consider 6V source alone (12V source shorted).

The 4$\Omega$ and 8$\Omega$ resistors are in parallel.

$R_{p2} = (4 \times 8) / (4 + 8) = 32 / 12 = 8/3 \approx 2.667 \Omega$.

Total resistance $R_{T2} = 6 + R_{p2} = 6 + 8/3 = (18+8)/3 = 26/3 \approx 8.667 \Omega$.

Total current from 6V source ($I''_{R2}$) = $6 / R_{T2} = 6 / (26/3) = 18/26 = 9/13 \approx 0.692 A$ (leftwards/upwards).

Using current divider for $I''_{Rc}$ (current in 8$\Omega$, upwards):

$I''_{Rc} = I''_{R2} \times \frac{4}{4+8} = (9/13) \times (4/12) = (9/13) \times (1/3) = 3/13 \approx 0.231 A$ (upwards).

Current in 4$\Omega$ ($I''_{R1}$, upwards): $I''_{R1} = I''_{R2} - I''_{Rc} = (9/13) - (3/13) = 6/13 \approx 0.462 A$ (upwards through short).

Step 3: Combine results. Assume positive directions: Right for $I_{R1}$, Left for $I_{R2}$, Down for $I_{Rc}$.

Current in 4$\Omega$ ($I_{R1}$) = $I'_{R1}$ (right) - $I''_{R1}$ (left/up through short which translates to left) $= (21/13) - (6/13) = 15/13 \approx 1.154 A$ (rightwards).

Current in 6$\Omega$ ($I_{R2}$) = $I''_{R2}$ (left) - $I'_{R2}$ (right/down through short translates to left) $= (9/13) - (12/13) = -3/13 \approx -0.231 A$. Negative sign means actual flow is opposite to assumed leftwards, i.e., **0.231 A rightwards**. (Let's recheck directions).

Let's define directions more clearly: $I_{R1}$ = right, $I_{R2}$ = left, $I_{Rc}$ = down.

$I_{R1} = I'_{R1} \text{(right)} + I''_{R1} \text{(up through short -> means right in original)} = (21/13) + (6/13) = 27/13 \approx 2.077 A$.

$I_{R2} = I'_{R2} \text{(down through short -> means left in original)} + I''_{R2} \text{(left)} = (12/13) + (9/13) = 21/13 \approx 1.615 A$.

$I_{Rc} = I'_{Rc} \text{(down)} - I''_{Rc} \text{(up)} = (9/13) - (3/13) = 6/13 \approx 0.462 A$.

Branch Currents: $I_{4\Omega} \approx 2.08 A$ (right), $I_{6\Omega} \approx 1.62 A$ (left), $I_{8\Omega} \approx 0.46 A$ (down).

Double-checking directions is crucial in superposition.

(i) Faraday's Laws of Electromagnetic Induction:

  1. Whenever the magnetic flux linking a circuit changes, an EMF is induced in the circuit.
  2. The magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of the magnetic flux linkage ($E \propto d(N\Phi)/dt$).

Inductance relates flux linkage ($N\Phi$) to the current (I) producing it, assuming linearity.

$L = (N\Phi) / I$.

$L = (1600 \times 30 \times 10^{-3} \text{ Wb}) / 1.5 \text{ A}$.

$L = (48 \text{ Wb}) / 1.5 \text{ A} = 32$ H.

(II) Average e.m.f induced ($E_{avg}$):

Induced EMF $E = -L (di/dt)$. For average EMF, use average rate of change of current.

Average rate of change of current = $\Delta I / \Delta t = (I_2 - I_1) / \Delta t = (0 - 1.5) / 0.2 = -1.5 / 0.2 = -7.5$ A/s.

$E_{avg} = -L \times (\Delta I / \Delta t) = -(32 H) \times (-7.5 A/s) = +240$ V.

Alternatively, use Faraday's Law: $E_{avg} = -N (\Delta\Phi / \Delta t)$.

$\Delta\Phi = 0 - (30 \times 10^{-3}) = -30 \times 10^{-3}$ Wb.

$E_{avg} = -N (\Delta\Phi / \Delta t) = -1600 \times (-30 \times 10^{-3} / 0.2)$.

$E_{avg} = -1600 \times (-0.15) = +240$ V.

Answer

This question covers parts of AC generators, AC waveform analysis, transformer losses and efficiency derivation, and analysis of capacitor networks.

(a) Parts of AC Generators (Alternators):

  • Stator: The stationary part containing the armature winding where the output voltage is induced.
  • Rotor: The rotating part containing the field winding (or permanent magnets) which produces the magnetic field.
  • Field Winding: Coils on the rotor supplied with DC current (excitation) to create the magnetic poles.
  • Armature Winding: Coils in the stator slots where the EMF is induced by the rotating magnetic field cutting across them.
  • Slip Rings: Conductive rings mounted on the rotor shaft used to supply DC current to the rotating field winding (in most large alternators).
  • Brushes: Stationary contacts (usually carbon) that press against the slip rings to conduct DC current to the field winding.
  • Shaft, Bearings, Frame/Housing, Cooling System.

(b) AC Waveform Analysis (Fig 6):

From the graph:

  • Peak Voltage ($V_m$) = 8 V.
  • Time for one complete cycle (Period, T) = 20 ms.

(i) Periodic Time (T): $T = 20 \text{ ms} = 20 \times 10^{-3}$ s = 0.02 s.

(ii) Frequency (f): $f = 1 / T = 1 / 0.02 \text{ s} = 50$ Hz.

(c) Transformer Losses and Efficiency:

(i) Two Transformer Losses:

  • Copper Losses ($I^2R$ losses): Heat dissipated in the primary and secondary winding resistances due to current flow.
  • Iron Losses (Core Losses): Losses in the core due to alternating magnetic flux, comprising:
    • Hysteresis Loss
    • Eddy Current Loss

(ii) Efficiency Derivation:

Efficiency ($\eta$) of any device is defined as the ratio of useful power output ($P_{out}$) to the total power input ($P_{in}$).

$\eta = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}}$.

The total power input must supply both the useful output power and the power lost within the transformer ($P_{losses}$).

$P_{in} = P_{out} + P_{losses}$.

Substitute this into the efficiency formula:

$\eta = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{out} + P_{losses}}$.

To express it in the desired form, divide both numerator and denominator by $P_{in}$:

$\eta = \frac{P_{out}/P_{in}}{P_{in}/P_{in}} = P_{out}/P_{in}$. (Doesn't help).

Let's rearrange the relationship $P_{in} = P_{out} + P_{losses}$ to $P_{out} = P_{in} - P_{losses}$.

Substitute this into the efficiency formula:

$\eta = \frac{P_{in} - P_{losses}}{P_{in}} = \frac{P_{in}}{P_{in}} - \frac{P_{losses}}{P_{in}} = 1 - \frac{P_{losses}}{P_{in}}$.

Therefore, Efficiency $= 1 - \frac{\text{losses}}{\text{input power}}$. (Shown)

(d) Capacitor Circuit Analysis (Fig 7):

Given: $V_S = 100V$, $C_1 = 4\mu F$, $C_2 = 6\mu F$, $C_3 = 6\mu F$.

Configuration: $C_1$ in series with the parallel combination of $C_2$ and $C_3$.

(i) Equivalent Circuit Capacitance ($C_{eq}$):

Step 1: Find equivalent capacitance of parallel $C_2$ and $C_3$ ($C_{23}$).

$C_{23} = C_2 + C_3 = 6 \mu F + 6 \mu F = 12 \mu F$.

Step 2: Find equivalent capacitance of $C_1$ in series with $C_{23}$.

$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_{23}} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{12}$.

$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{3 + 1}{12} = \frac{4}{12} = \frac{1}{3}$.

$C_{eq} = 3 \mu F$.

(ii) Voltage across each $6 \mu \mathrm{~F}$ capacitor ($V_2$ and $V_3$):

The voltage across the parallel combination ($V_{23}$) is the same as the voltage across $C_2$ and $C_3$ ($V_2 = V_3 = V_{23}$).

First find the total charge ($Q_{total}$) drawn from the supply.

$Q_{total} = C_{eq} \times V_S = (3 \mu F) \times 100 V = 300 \mu C$.

In a series circuit, the charge on each series component is the same. So, the charge on $C_1$ is $Q_1 = 300 \mu C$, and the charge on the parallel combination $C_{23}$ is $Q_{23} = 300 \mu C$.

Now find the voltage across the parallel combination $C_{23}$:

$V_{23} = Q_{23} / C_{23} = (300 \mu C) / (12 \mu F) = 25 V$.

Since $C_2$ and $C_3$ are in parallel, the voltage across each is $V_{23}$.

$V_2 = V_3 = V_{23} = 25 V$.

The voltage across each $6 \mu F$ capacitor is 25 V.

(Check: Voltage across $C_1$, $V_1 = Q_1 / C_1 = 300 \mu C / 4 \mu F = 75 V$. $V_1 + V_{23} = 75 V + 25 V = 100 V = V_S$).
