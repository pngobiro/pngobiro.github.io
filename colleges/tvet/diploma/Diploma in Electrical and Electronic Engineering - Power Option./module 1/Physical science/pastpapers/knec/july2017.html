Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) (i) State Faraday's laws of electrolysis. (2 marks)

(ii) A calorimeter of heat capacity $80 \mathrm{JK}^{-1}$ contains water of mass 0.1 kg and a coil of 5 ohms totally immersed in the water. The coil is connected in parallel with a copper voltameter having copper electrodes and a resistance of 7 ohms. When the arrangement is connected as a circuit, 0.66 grammes of copper is deposited in 40 minutes. Determine the temperature rise of the calorimeter in the same time. (8 marks)

Take: Specific heat capacity of water $= 4200 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kgK}$

Mass of copper deposited per coulomb (Electrochemical Equivalent, z) $= 3.3 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{C}$.

(b) $1 \mathrm{~m}^{3}$ of air, initially at $110 \mathrm{kN} / \mathrm{m}^{2}$ and $15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, is compressed according to the law $\mathrm{PV}^{1.3}=$ constant, in a cylinder to a final pressure of $1.4 \mathrm{MN} / \mathrm{m}^{2}$.

Taking R for air $= 287 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kgK}$ and $\mathrm{Cp}=1005 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kgK}$, determine the:

(i) volume and temperature of the air at the end of the compression; (4 marks)

(ii) workdone in compressing the air; (2 marks)

(iii) change in internal energy; (2 marks)

(iv) heat exchange through the cylinder walls, stating the direction of heat flow. (2 marks)

Answer

This question involves principles of electrolysis and calorimetry combined with electrical circuits, and thermodynamics of gas compression.

(a) Electrolysis and Calorimetry:

(i) Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis:

  • First Law: The mass (m) of a substance liberated or deposited at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electric charge (Q) passed through the electrolyte ($m \propto Q$ or $m = zQ$, where z is the electrochemical equivalent).
  • Second Law: When the same quantity of electric charge (Q) is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of the substances liberated or deposited are directly proportional to their respective chemical equivalent weights ($m \propto E$).

(ii) Temperature Rise Calculation:

Given:

  • Heat capacity of calorimeter ($C_{cal}$) = 80 J/K
  • Mass of water ($m_w$) = 0.1 kg
  • Resistance of coil ($R_{coil}$) = 5 $\Omega$
  • Resistance of voltameter ($R_{volt}$) = 7 $\Omega$
  • Mass of copper deposited ($m_{Cu}$) = 0.66 g = $0.66 \times 10^{-3}$ kg
  • Time (t) = 40 minutes = $40 \times 60 = 2400$ s
  • Specific heat capacity of water ($c_w$) = 4200 J/kgK
  • Electrochemical equivalent of copper (z) = $3.3 \times 10^{-7}$ kg/C

Step 1: Find current through voltameter ($I_{volt}$).

From Faraday's First Law, $m_{Cu} = z \times Q = z \times I_{volt} \times t$.

$0.66 \times 10^{-3} \text{ kg} = (3.3 \times 10^{-7} \text{ kg/C}) \times I_{volt} \times 2400 \text{ s}$

$I_{volt} = \frac{0.66 \times 10^{-3}}{(3.3 \times 10^{-7}) \times 2400} = \frac{0.66 \times 10^{-3}}{7.92 \times 10^{-4}} = \frac{0.66}{0.792} \approx 0.8333 \text{ A}$.

Step 2: Find voltage across parallel combination (V).

The coil and voltameter are in parallel. Voltage across voltameter $V = I_{volt} \times R_{volt}$.

$V = 0.8333 \text{ A} \times 7 \Omega = 5.833 \text{ V}$.

This is also the voltage across the coil.

Step 3: Find current through the coil ($I_{coil}$).

$I_{coil} = V / R_{coil} = 5.833 \text{ V} / 5 \Omega = 1.1666 \text{ A}$.

Step 4: Find heat generated by the coil ($Q_{coil}$).

Heat generated = Power $\times$ Time = $V \times I_{coil} \times t$ or $I_{coil}^2 R_{coil} t$.

$Q_{coil} = 5.833 \text{ V} \times 1.1666 \text{ A} \times 2400 \text{ s} \approx 16332 \text{ J}$.

(Using $I_{coil}^2 R_{coil} t = (1.1666)^2 \times 5 \times 2400 \approx 1.361 \times 5 \times 2400 \approx 16332$ J).

Step 5: Calculate temperature rise ($\Delta T$).

Heat generated by coil = Heat absorbed by water + Heat absorbed by calorimeter.

$Q_{coil} = (m_w c_w \Delta T) + (C_{cal} \Delta T) = (m_w c_w + C_{cal}) \Delta T$.

Heat capacity of water = $m_w c_w = 0.1 \text{ kg} \times 4200 \text{ J/kgK} = 420 \text{ J/K}$.

Total heat capacity of system = $420 \text{ J/K} + 80 \text{ J/K} = 500 \text{ J/K}$.

$16332 \text{ J} = (500 \text{ J/K}) \times \Delta T$.

$\Delta T = 16332 / 500 = 32.664 \text{ K}$.

The temperature rise is approximately **32.7 K** (or 32.7 °C).

(b) Polytropic Compression of Air:

Given: $P_1 = 110 \text{ kN/m}^2 = 110 \times 10^3 \text{ Pa}$, $V_1 = 1 \text{ m}^3$, $T_1 = 15^{\circ} \mathrm{C} = 15 + 273.15 = 288.15 \text{ K}$.

$P_2 = 1.4 \text{ MN/m}^2 = 1.4 \times 10^6 \text{ Pa}$. Polytropic index $n = 1.3$.

$R = 287 \text{ J/kgK}$, $C_p = 1005 \text{ J/kgK}$.

For air, $\gamma = C_p / C_v$. We need $C_v = C_p - R = 1005 - 287 = 718 \text{ J/kgK}$.

$\gamma = 1005 / 718 \approx 1.4$.

(i) Final Volume ($V_2$) and Temperature ($T_2$):

Use the polytropic relation $P_1 V_1^n = P_2 V_2^n$.

$(110 \times 10^3)(1)^{1.3} = (1.4 \times 10^6) V_2^{1.3}$

$V_2^{1.3} = \frac{110 \times 10^3}{1.4 \times 10^6} = \frac{110}{1400} \approx 0.07857$.

$V_2 = (0.07857)^{1/1.3} \approx (0.07857)^{0.7692} \approx 0.155 \text{ m}^3$.

Use the combined gas law relation for polytropic process: $\frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2}$ OR $\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)^{(n-1)/n}$.

Using the second form: $\frac{n-1}{n} = \frac{1.3-1}{1.3} = \frac{0.3}{1.3} \approx 0.2308$.

$\frac{T_2}{288.15} = \left(\frac{1.4 \times 10^6}{110 \times 10^3}\right)^{0.2308} = \left(\frac{1400}{110}\right)^{0.2308} \approx (12.727)^{0.2308} \approx 1.856$.

$T_2 = 288.15 \times 1.856 \approx 534.8 \text{ K}$.

($T_2 \approx 534.8 - 273.15 \approx 261.7^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$).

Final Volume $V_2 \approx 0.155 \text{ m}^3$, Final Temperature $T_2 \approx 534.8 \text{ K}$.

(ii) Work Done (W) in Compression:

For a polytropic process, $W = \frac{P_1 V_1 - P_2 V_2}{n-1}$.

$W = \frac{(110 \times 10^3 \times 1) - (1.4 \times 10^6 \times 0.155)}{1.3 - 1}$

$W = \frac{110000 - 217000}{0.3} = \frac{-107000}{0.3} \approx -356667 \text{ J}$.

Work done *on* the air is positive, work done *by* the air is negative. Work done *in compressing* the air is $356.7$ kJ.

(iii) Change in Internal Energy ($\Delta U$):

First find mass of air (m): $m = \frac{P_1 V_1}{R T_1} = \frac{110 \times 10^3 \times 1}{287 \times 288.15} \approx \frac{110000}{82699} \approx 1.33$ kg.

$\Delta U = m C_v (T_2 - T_1)$

$\Delta U = 1.33 \text{ kg} \times 718 \text{ J/kgK} \times (534.8 - 288.15) \text{ K}$

$\Delta U = 1.33 \times 718 \times 246.65 \approx 235345 \text{ J}$.

Change in internal energy $\approx 235.3$ kJ (Increase).

(iv) Heat Exchange (Q):

Using the non-flow energy equation (First Law of Thermodynamics): $\Delta U = Q - W$ (where W is work done *by* the gas).

Here, work is done *on* the gas, so $W_{on} = -W = 356.7$ kJ.

Let $Q$ be heat added *to* the gas. $\Delta U = Q + W_{on}$ is incorrect form.

Use $\Delta U = Q - W_{by\_gas}$.

$235.3 \text{ kJ} = Q - (-356.7 \text{ kJ})$ This doesn't seem right. Let's use $Q = \Delta U + W_{by\_gas}$.

$Q = 235.3 \text{ kJ} + (-356.7 \text{ kJ}) = -121.4 \text{ kJ}$.

The negative sign indicates heat is removed from the system (heat flows *out* of the cylinder walls).

Heat exchanged $\approx -121.4$ kJ. Direction: **Out of the cylinder**.

The question involves combining electrical, thermal and electrochemical principles, and applying thermodynamic process equations.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Distinguish between transverse and longitudinal waves and state one example of each. (4 marks)

(ii) Illustrate the following types of damped vibrations: (3 marks)

(I) critically damped;

(II) under damped;

(III) over damped.

(b) The displacement y of a plane progressive wave is given by $y = 10^{-4} \sin(200 \pi t - 0.5 \pi x)$ where 'x' and 'y' are in metres and 't' in seconds. Determine the:

(i) amplitude; (1 mark)

(ii) wave length; (2 marks)

(iii) velocity; (2 marks)

(iv) phase difference between two points one metre apart. (3 marks)

(c) A particle moves with simple harmonic motion between two points one metre apart. The frequency of the oscillation is 4 Hz. Determine the:

Note: "between two points one metre apart" implies the total distance between extreme points is 1m.

(i) periodic time for the oscillation; (1 mark)

(ii) maximum velocity of the particle; (2 marks)

(iii) acceleration of the particle when it is 300 mm from one end of the motion. (2 marks)

Answer

This question covers wave types, damped oscillations, wave equation analysis, and simple harmonic motion calculations.

(a) Waves and Damping:

(i) Transverse vs. Longitudinal Waves:

  • Transverse Waves: Oscillations of particles are perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation (energy transfer). Example: Light waves, waves on a string.
  • Longitudinal Waves: Oscillations of particles are parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Example: Sound waves in air.

(ii) Damped Vibrations Illustrations:

Explanation of Sketch:

  • Underdamped: Amplitude decreases exponentially over time, but oscillations still occur. System returns to equilibrium relatively quickly but overshoots.
  • Critically Damped: Amplitude decays to zero in the minimum possible time without any oscillation (no overshoot). Often desired in systems like car suspensions or meter pointers.
  • Overdamped: Amplitude decays to zero more slowly than critically damped, also without oscillation. System is sluggish in returning to equilibrium.

(b) Wave Equation Analysis:

Given: $y = 10^{-4} \sin(200 \pi t - 0.5 \pi x)$. Standard form: $y = A \sin(\omega t - kx)$.

(i) Amplitude (A):

$A = 10^{-4}$ m.

(ii) Wavelength ($\lambda$):

Wave number $k = 0.5 \pi$. Since $k = 2\pi / \lambda$:

$0.5 \pi = 2\pi / \lambda \implies \lambda = 2\pi / (0.5 \pi) = 4$ m.

(iii) Velocity (v):

Angular frequency $\omega = 200 \pi$. Since $\omega = 2\pi f$, frequency $f = \omega / (2\pi) = 200\pi / (2\pi) = 100$ Hz.

$v = f \lambda = 100 \text{ Hz} \times 4 \text{ m} = 400 \text{ m/s}$.

(Alternatively, $v = \omega / k = 200\pi / (0.5\pi) = 400 \text{ m/s}$).

(iv) Phase Difference ($\Delta \phi$) for $\Delta x = 1$ m:

The phase of the wave is $\phi(x, t) = \omega t - kx$. The phase difference between two points $x_1$ and $x_2$ at the same time $t$ is:

$\Delta \phi = \phi(x_2, t) - \phi(x_1, t) = (\omega t - kx_2) - (\omega t - kx_1) = -k(x_2 - x_1) = -k \Delta x$.

The magnitude of the phase difference is $|-k \Delta x| = k \Delta x$.

$\Delta \phi = k \times \Delta x = (0.5 \pi \text{ rad/m}) \times (1 \text{ m}) = 0.5 \pi$ radians.

Phase difference = $\pi/2$ radians (or $90^{\circ}$).

(c) Simple Harmonic Motion Calculation:

Given: Motion between two points 1 m apart. This distance represents twice the amplitude ($2A = 1$ m). Frequency ($f$) = 4 Hz.

Amplitude ($A$) = $1 \text{ m} / 2 = 0.5 \text{ m}$.

(i) Periodic Time (T):

$T = 1 / f = 1 / 4 = 0.25$ s.

(ii) Maximum Velocity ($v_{max}$):

Angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi \times 4 = 8\pi$ rad/s.

Maximum velocity occurs at the equilibrium position ($x=0$) and is given by $v_{max} = A \omega$.

$v_{max} = 0.5 \text{ m} \times 8\pi \text{ rad/s} = 4\pi \text{ m/s}$.

$v_{max} \approx 12.57 \text{ m/s}$.

(iii) Acceleration (a) at 300 mm from one end:

Distance from one end = 300 mm = 0.3 m.

The equilibrium position is at the center, 0.5 m from either end.

Displacement from equilibrium ($x$) = Distance from center = Amplitude - Distance from nearest end.

$x = 0.5 \text{ m} - 0.3 \text{ m} = 0.2 \text{ m}$.

Acceleration in SHM is given by $a = -\omega^2 x$.

$a = -(8\pi)^2 \times 0.2 = -64\pi^2 \times 0.2 = -12.8\pi^2 \text{ m/s}^2$.

$a \approx -12.8 \times (9.8696) \approx -126.33 \text{ m/s}^2$.

The magnitude of the acceleration is approximately $126.3 \text{ m/s}^2$ (directed towards the equilibrium position).

The question tests understanding of wave characteristics, damped motion, wave equation parameters, and kinematics of SHM.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) State Newton's second law of motion. (2 marks)

(b) A piece of metal weighing 30 g is thrown from a sling at a velocity of $20 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$. It is brought to rest in 0.05 seconds after it hits and penetrates a sand bag. Determine the:

(i) depth of the penetration in metres; (4 marks)

(ii) average retarding force of the sand in Newtons. (4 marks)

(c) With the aid of a labelled diagram, explain the measurement of fluid pressure using a manometer. (10 marks)

Answer

This question covers Newton's second law, calculations involving impulse and kinematics, and pressure measurement using manometers.

(a) Newton's Second Law of Motion:

The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on the body and takes place in the direction of the force.
Mathematically: $F_{net} = \frac{d(mv)}{dt}$.
If mass (m) is constant, this simplifies to $F_{net} = m \frac{dv}{dt} = ma$, where $a$ is the acceleration.

(b) Metal Penetrating Sand Bag:

Given: Mass ($m$) = 30 g = 0.03 kg, Initial velocity ($u$) = 20 m/s, Final velocity ($v$) = 0 m/s, Time ($\Delta t$) = 0.05 s.

(i) Depth of Penetration (Distance, d):

Assuming constant deceleration ($a$), we can use kinematic equations.

First find acceleration: $v = u + a\Delta t$

$0 = 20 + a(0.05)$

$a = -20 / 0.05 = -400 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Now find distance using $d = u\Delta t + \frac{1}{2} a (\Delta t)^2$ or $v^2 = u^2 + 2ad$.

Using $d = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)\Delta t$ (average velocity x time):

$d = \frac{1}{2}(20 + 0) \times 0.05 = \frac{1}{2}(20) \times 0.05 = 10 \times 0.05 = 0.5 \text{ m}$.

Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2ad$:

$0^2 = 20^2 + 2(-400)d$

$0 = 400 - 800d$

$800d = 400 \implies d = 400 / 800 = 0.5 \text{ m}$.

Depth of penetration = 0.5 m.

(ii) Average Retarding Force (F):

Using Newton's Second Law: $F = ma$

$F = 0.03 \text{ kg} \times (-400 \text{ m/s}^2) = -12 \text{ N}$.

Alternatively, using Impulse-Momentum Theorem: $F \Delta t = \Delta p = mv - mu$.

$F \times 0.05 = (0.03 \times 0) - (0.03 \times 20) = 0 - 0.6 = -0.6 \text{ kg m/s}$.

$F = -0.6 / 0.05 = -12 \text{ N}$.

The magnitude of the average retarding force is 12 N.

(c) Measurement of Fluid Pressure using a Manometer:

A manometer is a device used to measure pressure, typically by balancing a column of liquid against the pressure being measured.

Types and Operation:

  1. Piezometer Tube: (Simplest form for liquid pressure) A vertical tube open at the top, connected to the point where liquid pressure is measured. The liquid rises in the tube to a height 'h' such that the pressure $P = \rho g h$ balances the gauge pressure at the connection point. Limited to low pressures and cannot measure gas pressures.
  2. U-tube Manometer: (Measures gauge pressure of liquids or gases) Description: A U-shaped tube contains a manometer liquid (density $\rho_m$, e.g., mercury, water) immiscible with the fluid whose pressure ($P_A$) is measured. One end connects to the fluid A, the other is open to atmosphere ($P_{atm}$) or another reference pressure. The pressure difference causes the levels in the two arms to differ by height 'h'. To find $P_A$, balance pressures at a common level (Level X, same height as Level 1): Pressure at X (left arm) = $P_A + \rho_A g z$ (if Level 1 is z above A, $\rho_A$ is density of fluid A). Assuming gas A, $\rho_A g z$ is often negligible or Level 1 is the point A. Let's assume Level 1 is point A, $P_{left} = P_A$. Pressure at X (right arm) = $P_{atm} + \rho_m g h$. Equating pressures: $P_A = P_{atm} + \rho_m g h$. Gauge pressure = $P_A - P_{atm} = \rho_m g h$. The height difference 'h' is measured, and knowing $\rho_m$, the gauge pressure is calculated.
  3. Differential U-tube Manometer: Connects between two points (A and B) to measure the pressure difference $P_A - P_B$. Both arms connect to the fluid system. The height difference 'h' of the manometer fluid relates to the pressure difference, considering the densities of the fluid at A/B and the manometer fluid.
  4. Inclined Manometer: One arm is inclined to increase sensitivity for measuring very small pressure differences, as a small vertical difference 'h' corresponds to a larger length 'L' along the tube ($h = L \sin\theta$).

(Include one clear labelled diagram, e.g., U-tube, and explain its operation for measuring gauge pressure as detailed above).

The question tests application of Newton's laws/impulse and the principles of manometry.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) State the principle of moments. (2 marks)

(b) A beam AB measures 150 cm and weighs 1.6 N. It is placed on two supports C and D such that they are 20 cm from each end of the beam. A 0.3 N weight hangs on the beam 40 cm from C and a 0.7 N weight hangs similarly 50 cm from D. Sketch and determine the reactions at the supports. (8 marks)

(c) A hollow steel shaft transmits 200 kW of power at 150 rev/min. The total angle of twist in a length of 5 m of the shaft is $3^{\circ}$. Determine the inner and outer diameters of the shaft if the permissible shear stress is 60 MPa. (Take G = 80 GPa) (10 marks)

Answer

This question covers static equilibrium (principle of moments, beam reactions) and mechanics of materials (torsion of a hollow shaft).

(a) Principle of Moments:

For a body to be in rotational equilibrium, the algebraic sum of the moments of all forces acting on the body about any arbitrary point (pivot) must be zero. Equivalently, the sum of the clockwise moments about any point must be equal to the sum of the anticlockwise moments about the same point.

(b) Beam Reactions:

Beam AB length = 150 cm. Weight $W_B = 1.6$ N acting at the center (75 cm from A or B).

Support C is 20 cm from A. Support D is 20 cm from B (i.e., 150-20 = 130 cm from A).

Distance between supports C and D = $130 - 20 = 110$ cm.

Load $W_1 = 0.3$ N hangs 40 cm from C (i.e., $20 + 40 = 60$ cm from A).

Load $W_2 = 0.7$ N hangs 50 cm from D (i.e., $130 - 50 = 80$ cm from A).

Sketch:

Let $R_C$ and $R_D$ be the reactions at supports C and D.

For vertical equilibrium: Sum of upward forces = Sum of downward forces.

$R_C + R_D = W_1 + W_B + W_2 = 0.3 + 1.6 + 0.7 = 2.6$ N. (Eq 1)

For rotational equilibrium, take moments about point C:

Sum of clockwise moments = Sum of anticlockwise moments.

Clockwise moments about C:

  • Moment due to $W_1$: $0.3 \times 40$ cm = 12 N cm
  • Moment due to $W_B$: $1.6 \times (75 - 20)$ cm = $1.6 \times 55$ = 88 N cm
  • Moment due to $W_2$: $0.7 \times (80 - 20)$ cm = $0.7 \times 60$ = 42 N cm
Total Clockwise Moment = $12 + 88 + 42 = 142$ N cm.

Anticlockwise moments about C:

  • Moment due to $R_D$: $R_D \times (\text{distance CD}) = R_D \times 110$ cm.

Equating moments: $110 R_D = 142$.

$R_D = 142 / 110 \approx 1.291$ N.

Substitute $R_D$ into Eq 1:

$R_C + 1.291 = 2.6$

$R_C = 2.6 - 1.291 = 1.309$ N.

Reactions are: $R_C \approx 1.31$ N and $R_D \approx 1.29$ N.

(c) Torsion of Hollow Steel Shaft:

Given: Power (P) = 200 kW = $200 \times 10^3$ W, Speed (N) = 150 rev/min, Length (L) = 5 m,

Angle of twist ($\theta$) = $3^{\circ} = 3 \times (\pi/180)$ radians $\approx 0.05236$ rad.

Max shear stress ($\tau_{max}$) = 60 MPa = $60 \times 10^6$ N/m².

Modulus of Rigidity (G) = 80 GPa = $80 \times 10^9$ N/m².

Let outer diameter be $D_o$ and inner diameter be $D_i$. Let outer radius $R_o = D_o/2$ and inner radius $R_i = D_i/2$.

Step 1: Find Torque (T).

Angular speed ($\omega$) = $2\pi N / 60 = 2\pi \times 150 / 60 = 5\pi$ rad/s $\approx 15.708$ rad/s.

Power $P = T \omega$.

$T = P / \omega = (200 \times 10^3) / (5\pi) \approx 12732.4$ N m.

Step 2: Use Torsion Equations.

The torsion equation relates torque, shear stress, angle of twist, and geometry:

$\frac{T}{J} = \frac{\tau}{R} = \frac{G\theta}{L}$

Where $J$ is the polar second moment of area. For a hollow shaft: $J = \frac{\pi}{2}(R_o^4 - R_i^4)$.

Maximum shear stress occurs at the outer radius $R_o$: $\tau_{max}$ relates to $R = R_o$.

Step 3: Relate dimensions using angle of twist.

From $\frac{T}{J} = \frac{G\theta}{L}$: $J = \frac{TL}{G\theta}$.

$J = \frac{(12732.4 \text{ N m}) \times (5 \text{ m})}{(80 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2) \times (0.05236 \text{ rad})} \approx \frac{63662}{4.1888 \times 10^9} \approx 1.520 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^4$.

So, $\frac{\pi}{2}(R_o^4 - R_i^4) = 1.520 \times 10^{-5}$. (Eq A)

Step 4: Relate dimensions using maximum shear stress.

From $\frac{T}{J} = \frac{\tau_{max}}{R_o}$: $R_o = \frac{\tau_{max} J}{T}$.

$R_o = \frac{(60 \times 10^6 \text{ N/m}^2) \times (1.520 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^4)}{12732.4 \text{ N m}} \approx \frac{912}{12732.4} \approx 0.07163 \text{ m}$.

Outer radius $R_o \approx 71.63$ mm.

Outer diameter $D_o = 2 R_o \approx 143.26$ mm.

Step 5: Find inner radius ($R_i$) using Eq A.

$\frac{\pi}{2}(R_o^4 - R_i^4) = 1.520 \times 10^{-5}$

$(0.07163)^4 - R_i^4 = \frac{2 \times 1.520 \times 10^{-5}}{\pi} \approx 9.677 \times 10^{-6}$.

$R_o^4 \approx (0.07163)^4 \approx 2.628 \times 10^{-5}$.

$2.628 \times 10^{-5} - R_i^4 = 9.677 \times 10^{-6}$.

$R_i^4 = (2.628 \times 10^{-5}) - (9.677 \times 10^{-6}) = (26.28 - 9.677) \times 10^{-6} = 16.603 \times 10^{-6}$.

$R_i = (16.603 \times 10^{-6})^{1/4} \approx (16.603)^{1/4} \times 10^{-1.5}$ Incorrect calculation.

$R_i = (1.6603 \times 10^{-5})^{1/4}$. Use calculator: $R_i \approx 0.06378$ m.

Inner radius $R_i \approx 63.78$ mm.

Inner diameter $D_i = 2 R_i \approx 127.56$ mm.

Final Diameters: Outer Diameter $D_o \approx 143.3$ mm. Inner Diameter $D_i \approx 127.6$ mm.

The problem involves applying static equilibrium conditions and the torsion formula for hollow shafts.

SECTION C: ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING PRINCIPLES

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) State Kirchhoff's:

(i) voltage law; (2 marks)

(ii) current law. (2 marks)

(b) With the aid of a circuit diagram, derive an expression for the total resistance for three resistors connected in parallel. (5 marks)

(c) Figure 1 shows a bridge network. Use Kirchhoff's laws to determine the:

(i) branch currents; (8 marks)

(ii) power dissipated by $5 \Omega$ resistor. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers Kirchhoff's circuit laws, derivation of the parallel resistance formula, and analysis of a DC bridge circuit.

(a) Kirchhoff's Laws:

(i) Voltage Law (KVL): The algebraic sum of the potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop or mesh in an electric circuit is equal to zero. ( $\sum V_{loop} = 0$ ).

(ii) Current Law (KCL): The algebraic sum of the currents entering any node (or junction) in an electric circuit is equal to the algebraic sum of the currents leaving that node. Equivalently, the algebraic sum of all currents entering or leaving a node is zero. ( $\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$ or $\sum I_{node} = 0$ ).

(b) Parallel Resistors Derivation:

Consider three resistors $R_1, R_2, R_3$ connected in parallel across a voltage source V.

  1. The voltage across each resistor is the same and equal to the supply voltage V (characteristic of parallel connection).
  2. Using Ohm's Law, the current through each resistor is: $I_1 = V/R_1$, $I_2 = V/R_2$, $I_3 = V/R_3$.
  3. According to Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), the total current ($I_{total}$) drawn from the source is the sum of the individual branch currents: $I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$.
  4. Substitute the expressions for $I_1, I_2, I_3$: $I_{total} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} + \frac{V}{R_3}$.
  5. Factor out V: $I_{total} = V \left( \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} \right)$.
  6. Let $R_{eq}$ be the equivalent resistance of the parallel combination. By definition, $I_{total} = V / R_{eq}$.
  7. Equating the two expressions for $I_{total}$: $\frac{V}{R_{eq}} = V \left( \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} \right)$.
  8. Dividing both sides by V (assuming $V \neq 0$):

$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}$

This is the expression for the total equivalent resistance of three resistors in parallel.

(c) Bridge Network Analysis (Fig 1):

Nodes: Top Supply = P, Bottom Supply = Q, Junction R1/R2/R5 = A, Junction R3/R4/R5 = B.

Resistors: $R_1=2\Omega$, $R_2=3\Omega$, $R_3=4\Omega$, $R_4=6\Omega$, $R_5=5\Omega$. Supply $V=10V$.

Using Mesh Analysis:

  • Mesh 1 (P-A-B-P): Let current be $I_1$ (clockwise).
  • Mesh 2 (A-R2-Q-B-A): Let current be $I_2$ (clockwise).
  • Mesh 3 (B-R4-Q-B): Let current be $I_3$ (clockwise).

KVL Equations:

Mesh 1: $10 - R_1(I_1) - R_3(I_1 - I_3) = 0 \implies 10 - 2I_1 - 4(I_1 - I_3) = 0 \implies 10 - 6I_1 + 4I_3 = 0 \implies 6I_1 - 4I_3 = 10$ (Eq A)

Mesh 2: $-R_2(I_2) - R_5(I_2 - I_3) - R_1(I_2 - I_1) = 0$ Oh wait, Mesh 1 should involve the source.

Redefine Meshes:

  • Mesh 1 (P-A-B-P): $10 - 2I_1 - 4(I_1-I_2) = 0 \implies 10 - 6I_1 + 4I_2 = 0 \implies 6I_1 - 4I_2 = 10$ (Eq i)
  • Mesh 2 (A-R2-Q-B-A): $-3(I_2-I_1) - 6I_2 - 5(I_2-I_3) = 0 \implies$ Incorrect loops.

Standard Meshes:

  • Mesh 1 (P-A-Q-P): $10 - R_1(I_1) - R_2(I_1-I_2) = 0 \implies 10 - 2I_1 - 3(I_1-I_2) = 0 \implies 10 - 5I_1 + 3I_2 = 0 \implies 5I_1 - 3I_2 = 10$ (Eq X)
  • Mesh 2 (A-B-Q-A): $-R_5(I_2 - I_3) - R_4(I_2) - R_2(I_2 - I_1) = 0 \implies -5(I_2-I_3) - 6I_2 - 3(I_2-I_1) = 0 \implies 3I_1 - 14I_2 + 5I_3 = 0$ (Eq Y)
  • Mesh 3 (P-B-Q-P): $10 - R_3(I_3) - R_4(I_3-I_2) = 0 \implies$ Incorrect - this is not Mesh 3.
  • Mesh 3 (P-A-B-Q-P): $10 - R_1(I_1) - R_5(I_2) - R_4(I_3) = 0$ ? No.
  • Mesh 3 (P-A-B-Q through source): Need KCL.

Let's use KCL at nodes A and B, and KVL.

Assume node Q is ground (0V). Node P is 10V. Let node A voltage be $V_A$ and node B voltage be $V_B$.

KCL at Node A: $\frac{V_A - 10}{R_1} + \frac{V_A - 0}{R_2} + \frac{V_A - V_B}{R_5} = 0$

$\frac{V_A - 10}{2} + \frac{V_A}{3} + \frac{V_A - V_B}{5} = 0$

Multiply by 30: $15(V_A - 10) + 10V_A + 6(V_A - V_B) = 0$

$15V_A - 150 + 10V_A + 6V_A - 6V_B = 0 \implies 31V_A - 6V_B = 150$ (Eq I)

KCL at Node B: $\frac{V_B - 10}{R_3} + \frac{V_B - 0}{R_4} + \frac{V_B - V_A}{R_5} = 0$

$\frac{V_B - 10}{4} + \frac{V_B}{6} + \frac{V_B - V_A}{5} = 0$

Multiply by 60: $15(V_B - 10) + 10V_B + 12(V_B - V_A) = 0$

$15V_B - 150 + 10V_B + 12V_B - 12V_A = 0 \implies -12V_A + 37V_B = 150$ (Eq II)

Solving Eq I and II simultaneously:

From (I): $6V_B = 31V_A - 150 \implies V_B = \frac{31V_A - 150}{6}$

Substitute into (II): $-12V_A + 37\left(\frac{31V_A - 150}{6}\right) = 150$

Multiply by 6: $-72V_A + 37(31V_A - 150) = 900$

$-72V_A + 1147V_A - 5550 = 900$

$1075V_A = 6450$

$V_A = 6450 / 1075 = 6$ V.

Now find $V_B$: $V_B = \frac{31(6) - 150}{6} = \frac{186 - 150}{6} = \frac{36}{6} = 6$ V.

Since $V_A = V_B = 6V$, the bridge is balanced!

(i) Branch Currents:

  • Current through $R_1$ ($I_{R1}$): $\frac{V_P - V_A}{R_1} = \frac{10 - 6}{2} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 A$.
  • Current through $R_2$ ($I_{R2}$): $\frac{V_A - V_Q}{R_2} = \frac{6 - 0}{3} = \frac{6}{3} = 2 A$.
  • Current through $R_3$ ($I_{R3}$): $\frac{V_P - V_B}{R_3} = \frac{10 - 6}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1 A$.
  • Current through $R_4$ ($I_{R4}$): $\frac{V_B - V_Q}{R_4} = \frac{6 - 0}{6} = \frac{6}{6} = 1 A$.
  • Current through $R_5$ ($I_{R5}$): $\frac{V_A - V_B}{R_5} = \frac{6 - 6}{5} = 0 A$.

(ii) Power dissipated by $5 \Omega$ resistor ($P_{R5}$):

Since the current through the $5 \Omega$ resistor ($I_{R5}$) is 0 A (because the bridge is balanced), the power dissipated is:

$P_{R5} = I_{R5}^2 \times R_5 = 0^2 \times 5 = 0$ W.

The analysis uses Kirchhoff's laws (via nodal analysis). The bridge is found to be balanced, simplifying the results.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Define the following terms as used in electrostatics:

(i) electric field intensity; (1 mark)

(ii) relative permittivity. (1 mark)

(b) State the factors that determine the capacitance of a capacitor. (3 marks)

(c) (i) Two capacitor plates measuring 6 cm by 4 cm are 7 mm apart. This space is filled by 2 mm glass dielectric and 5 mm paper dielectric materials. The relative permittivities of glass and paper are 6 and 2.5 respectively. If the applied voltage is 500 V across the capacitor plates, determine the:

Assume the dielectrics are placed in series, filling the gap.

(I) capacitance of the capacitor; (5 marks)

(II) potential difference across each dielectric. (4 marks)

(ii) Draw a circuit diagram that will enable a d.c. ammeter to measure a.c. voltage. (6 marks)

Answer

This question covers electrostatics definitions, factors affecting capacitance, analysis of a multi-dielectric capacitor, and adapting a DC meter for AC voltage measurement.

(a) Electrostatics Definitions:

(i) Electric Field Intensity (E): The electric field intensity (or electric field strength) at a point in space is defined as the electric force experienced per unit positive charge placed at that point. It is a vector quantity. Unit: Newtons per Coulomb (N/C) or Volts per meter (V/m).

(ii) Relative Permittivity ($\epsilon_r$): Also known as the dielectric constant, it is the ratio of the permittivity of a dielectric material ($\epsilon$) to the permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$). $\epsilon_r = \epsilon / \epsilon_0$. It is a dimensionless quantity that indicates how effectively a material can store electrical energy compared to a vacuum.

(b) Factors Determining Capacitance:

For a parallel plate capacitor, the capacitance ($C = \frac{\epsilon_0 \epsilon_r A}{d}$) depends on:

  • Area of Overlap (A): Capacitance is directly proportional to the overlapping area of the plates.
  • Distance Between Plates (d): Capacitance is inversely proportional to the distance separating the plates.
  • Permittivity of Dielectric ($\epsilon = \epsilon_0 \epsilon_r$): Capacitance is directly proportional to the absolute permittivity of the dielectric material between the plates (or its relative permittivity $\epsilon_r$).

(c) Multi-Dielectric Capacitor:

Given: Plate Area (A) = 6 cm $\times$ 4 cm = 24 cm² = $24 \times 10^{-4}$ m².

Dielectric 1 (Glass): Thickness ($d_1$) = 2 mm = $2 \times 10^{-3}$ m, Relative permittivity ($\epsilon_{r1}$) = 6.

Dielectric 2 (Paper): Thickness ($d_2$) = 5 mm = $5 \times 10^{-3}$ m, Relative permittivity ($\epsilon_{r2}$) = 2.5.

Total distance ($d = d_1+d_2$) = 7 mm = $7 \times 10^{-3}$ m.

Applied voltage ($V_{total}$) = 500 V. $\epsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m.

(i) Capacitance of the Capacitor:

This configuration represents two capacitors ($C_1$ for glass, $C_2$ for paper) connected in series.

$C_1 = \frac{\epsilon_0 \epsilon_{r1} A}{d_1} = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12}) \times 6 \times (24 \times 10^{-4})}{2 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1.2744 \times 10^{-13}}{2 \times 10^{-3}} = 6.372 \times 10^{-11}$ F = 63.72 pF.

$C_2 = \frac{\epsilon_0 \epsilon_{r2} A}{d_2} = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12}) \times 2.5 \times (24 \times 10^{-4})}{5 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{5.31 \times 10^{-14}}{5 \times 10^{-3}} = 1.062 \times 10^{-11}$ F = 10.62 pF.

For capacitors in series, the total capacitance ($C_{total}$) is:

$\frac{1}{C_{total}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} = \frac{1}{6.372 \times 10^{-11}} + \frac{1}{1.062 \times 10^{-11}}$

$\frac{1}{C_{total}} = (0.1569 \times 10^{11}) + (0.9416 \times 10^{11}) = 1.0985 \times 10^{11}$

$C_{total} = 1 / (1.0985 \times 10^{11}) \approx 9.103 \times 10^{-12}$ F = 9.10 pF.

(ii) Potential Difference Across Each Dielectric:

In a series capacitor circuit, the charge (Q) stored on each capacitor is the same.

$Q = C_{total} \times V_{total} = (9.103 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}) \times 500 \text{ V} = 4.5515 \times 10^{-9}$ C.

Voltage across Glass ($V_1$) = $Q / C_1 = (4.5515 \times 10^{-9}) / (6.372 \times 10^{-11}) \approx 71.43$ V.

Voltage across Paper ($V_2$) = $Q / C_2 = (4.5515 \times 10^{-9}) / (1.062 \times 10^{-11}) \approx 428.58$ V.

(Check: $V_1 + V_2 \approx 71.43 + 428.58 = 500.01$ V, which matches $V_{total}$).

(ii) Circuit Diagram for DC Ammeter to Measure AC Voltage:

A DC ammeter (like a PMMC instrument) measures average current and cannot directly measure AC voltage. To adapt it, we need to:

  1. Rectify the AC voltage to produce a DC voltage/current.
  2. Use a multiplier resistor to limit the current flowing through the meter movement to its FSD value for a given AC voltage range.

Diagram shows:

  • AC Input Terminals.
  • Rectifier Block (e.g., a full-wave bridge rectifier using four diodes, or a half-wave rectifier using one diode). This converts AC to pulsating DC.
  • Multiplier Resistor ($R_{mult}$): Connected in series to limit the current. Its value depends on the desired voltage range, the meter's internal resistance ($R_m$), and its FSD current ($I_{fsd}$), considering the relationship between AC RMS/peak voltage and the average DC current after rectification. $R_{mult} \approx (V_{range\_avg} / I_{fsd}) - R_m$.
  • DC Ammeter Movement: The basic meter (e.g., PMMC) measures the average value of the rectified current.
  • The scale of the DC ammeter is calibrated to read the RMS value of the AC voltage (usually assuming a sinusoidal waveform, accounting for the form factor).

The question tests electrostatics concepts, series capacitor calculations, and the principles behind adapting DC instruments for AC measurements.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Define the term reluctance as used in magnetism. (2 marks)

(b) A circular magnetic ring has a diameter of 4.2 cm. An air gap of 2 mm has been cut off. The ring has a cross-sectional area of $6 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$ and a relative permeability of 500. If a coil of 6000 turns is wound on the ring and a current of 750 mA flows through it, determine the:

(i) total reluctance; (5 marks)

(ii) magnetomotive force drop in the air gap; (2 marks)

(iii) flux density in the magnetic material. (3 marks)

(c) Two alternating quantities are represented by $V_{1}=2 \operatorname{Sin} \omega t$ and $V_{2}=3 \operatorname{Sin}(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{12})$.

(i) Draw graphs for $V_{1}, V_{2}$ and resultant $V_{r}$ on the same graph. (5 marks)

(ii) Write an expression of $V_{r}$ in the form $V_{r}=A \operatorname{Sin}(\omega t+\alpha)$. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers magnetic circuit calculations involving reluctance and flux, and the graphical and analytical addition of sinusoidal AC quantities.

(a) Reluctance (S or $\mathcal{R}$):

Reluctance in a magnetic circuit is the opposition offered by the circuit to the establishment of magnetic flux. It is analogous to resistance in an electric circuit. It is defined as the ratio of the magnetomotive force (MMF or F) applied to the circuit to the resulting magnetic flux ($\Phi$). $S = F / \Phi$. Unit: Ampere-turns per Weber (At/Wb) or reciprocal Henry (H⁻¹).

(b) Magnetic Ring Calculation:

Given:

  • Diameter = 4.2 cm = 0.042 m $\implies$ Radius r = 0.021 m.
  • Air gap length ($l_g$) = 2 mm = $2 \times 10^{-3}$ m.
  • Cross-sectional area (A) = 6 cm² = $6 \times 10^{-4}$ m².
  • Relative permeability ($\mu_r$) = 500.
  • Number of turns (N) = 6000.
  • Current (I) = 750 mA = 0.75 A.
  • $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ H/m.

Mean circumference of ring = $\pi \times \text{diameter} = \pi \times 0.042 \approx 0.13195$ m.

Length of magnetic material path ($l_m$) = Mean circumference - Air gap length

$l_m = 0.13195 - 0.002 = 0.12995$ m.

(i) Total Reluctance ($S_{total}$):

Reluctance of material ($S_m$) = $\frac{l_m}{\mu_0 \mu_r A} = \frac{0.12995}{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 500 \times (6 \times 10^{-4})}$

$S_m = \frac{0.12995}{3.7699 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 344704$ At/Wb.

Reluctance of air gap ($S_g$) = $\frac{l_g}{\mu_0 A} = \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times (6 \times 10^{-4})}$

$S_g = \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{7.5398 \times 10^{-10}} \approx 2652582$ At/Wb.

$S_{total} = S_m + S_g = 344704 + 2652582 \approx 2997286$ At/Wb.

$S_{total} \approx 3.00 \times 10^6$ At/Wb.

(ii) MMF Drop in Air Gap ($F_g$):

First find total MMF ($F_{total}$) = $N \times I = 6000 \times 0.75 = 4500$ At.

Total flux ($\Phi$) = $F_{total} / S_{total} = 4500 / 2997286 \approx 0.001501$ Wb = 1.50 mWb.

MMF drop across air gap ($F_g$) = $\Phi \times S_g$.

$F_g = (0.001501) \times (2652582) \approx 3981.5$ At.

(Check: MMF drop across material $F_m = \Phi \times S_m = 0.001501 \times 344704 \approx 517.4$ At. $F_m + F_g \approx 517.4 + 3981.5 = 4498.9$ At, which matches $F_{total}$).

MMF drop in air gap $\approx 3982$ At.

(iii) Flux Density in Magnetic Material ($B_m$):

Assuming no flux leakage, flux is constant throughout the circuit: $\Phi_m = \Phi_g = \Phi$.

$B_m = \Phi / A = 0.001501 \text{ Wb} / (6 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2) \approx 2.502$ T.

Flux density $\approx 2.50$ T.

(c) Addition of Sinusoids:

$V_1 = 2 \sin(\omega t)$

$V_2 = 3 \sin(\omega t + \pi/12)$ [Note: $\pi/12$ radians = $180/12 = 15^{\circ}$]

Represent as phasors:

  • $V_1 = 2 \angle 0^{\circ}$
  • $V_2 = 3 \angle 15^{\circ}$

Convert to rectangular form:

$V_1 = 2 + j0$.

$V_2 = 3 \cos(15^{\circ}) + j 3 \sin(15^{\circ}) \approx 3(0.9659) + j 3(0.2588) \approx 2.898 + j 0.776$.

Resultant Phasor $V_r = V_1 + V_2 = (2 + 2.898) + j(0 + 0.776) = 4.898 + j 0.776$.

Convert back to polar form $V_r = A \angle \alpha$:

Amplitude $A = |V_r| = \sqrt{(4.898)^2 + (0.776)^2} = \sqrt{23.990 + 0.602} = \sqrt{24.592} \approx 4.959$.

Phase angle $\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{0.776}{4.898}\right) \approx \arctan(0.1584) \approx 8.99^{\circ}$.

Convert angle to radians: $\alpha \approx 8.99 \times (\pi/180) \approx 0.157$ radians (approx $\pi/20$).

(i) Graphs:

Plot three sine waves: $V_1$ with amplitude 2 starting at 0 phase. $V_2$ with amplitude 3 starting at phase $+\pi/12$ (shifted left). $V_r$ with amplitude ~4.96 starting at phase ~+0.157 rad (shifted slightly more left than $V_1$).

(ii) Expression for $V_r$:

$V_r = A \sin(\omega t + \alpha)$.

$V_r \approx 4.96 \sin(\omega t + 8.99^{\circ})$ or $V_r \approx 4.96 \sin(\omega t + 0.157)$.

The question tests magnetic circuit calculations involving air gaps and the addition of sinusoidal waveforms using phasor analysis.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) A coil has a resistance of 12 ohms and inductance of 70 mH. It is connected in parallel with a capacitor of $80 \mu \mathrm{~F}$. If the supply voltage is $240 \mathrm{~V}, 50 \mathrm{~Hz}$, determine the:

(i) supply current; (6 marks)

(ii) power factor of the circuit; (2 marks)

(iii) true power of the circuit. (2 marks)

(b) (i) Explain the three losses that occur in a transformer and state how they are minimised. (6 marks)

(ii) A 20 ohms resistor is connected across the secondary winding of a single phase transformer. If the secondary voltage is 150 V and the primary current is 5 A, determine the primary voltage and turns ratio. (Neglect the losses) (4 marks)

Answer

This question involves analysis of a parallel RLC circuit and basic transformer calculations and loss mechanisms.

(a) Parallel RLC Circuit Analysis:

Given: Supply $V = 240 V$, $f = 50 Hz$.

Branch 1 (Coil): $R = 12 \Omega$, $L = 70 mH = 0.07 H$.

Branch 2 (Capacitor): $C = 80 \mu F = 80 \times 10^{-6} F$.

Step 1: Analyze Branch 1 (RL Branch).

Inductive Reactance ($X_L$) = $2\pi f L = 2\pi \times 50 \times 0.07 = 7\pi \approx 21.99 \Omega$.

Impedance of coil ($Z_L$) = $\sqrt{R^2 + X_L^2} = \sqrt{12^2 + (21.99)^2} = \sqrt{144 + 483.56} = \sqrt{627.56} \approx 25.05 \Omega$.

Current in coil ($I_L$) = $V / Z_L = 240 / 25.05 \approx 9.58 A$.

Phase angle of coil current ($\phi_L$) = $\arctan(X_L / R) = \arctan(21.99 / 12) \approx \arctan(1.8325) \approx 61.39^{\circ}$. $I_L$ lags V by $61.39^{\circ}$.

Represent $I_L$ in rectangular form: $I_L = 9.58 \angle -61.39^{\circ} = 9.58(\cos(-61.39) + j\sin(-61.39))$ $I_L \approx 9.58(0.4788 - j0.8780) \approx 4.587 - j 8.411$ A.

Step 2: Analyze Branch 2 (C Branch).

Capacitive Reactance ($X_C$) = $\frac{1}{2\pi f C} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 50 \times (80 \times 10^{-6})} = \frac{1}{0.02513} \approx 39.79 \Omega$.

Current in capacitor ($I_C$) = $V / X_C = 240 / 39.79 \approx 6.03 A$.

$I_C$ leads V by $90^{\circ}$.

Represent $I_C$ in rectangular form: $I_C = 6.03 \angle 90^{\circ} = 0 + j 6.03$ A.

Step 3: Find Supply Current ($I_S$).

$I_S = I_L + I_C = (4.587 - j 8.411) + (0 + j 6.03) = 4.587 - j 2.381$ A.

Magnitude of supply current $|I_S| = \sqrt{(4.587)^2 + (-2.381)^2} = \sqrt{21.04 + 5.669} = \sqrt{26.709} \approx 5.168 A$.

(i) Supply current $\approx 5.17 A$.

Step 4: Find Power Factor (PF).

Phase angle of supply current ($\phi_S$) = $\arctan\left(\frac{-2.381}{4.587}\right) \approx \arctan(-0.519) \approx -27.43^{\circ}$.

The supply current lags the supply voltage (since the angle is negative, or imaginary part is negative).

Power Factor (PF) = $\cos(\phi_S) = \cos(-27.43^{\circ}) \approx 0.8875$.

(ii) Power factor $\approx 0.888$ lagging.

Step 5: Find True Power (P).

True power is only dissipated in the resistor.

$P = V \times I_S \times \cos(\phi_S) = 240 \text{ V} \times 5.168 \text{ A} \times 0.8875 \approx 1100.3$ W.

Alternatively, power is only consumed in the $12\Omega$ resistor: $P = |I_L|^2 \times R$ ? No, use the real part of $I_S$.

Active component of $I_S$ = $4.587$ A.

$P = V \times (\text{Real part of } I_S) = 240 \times 4.587 \approx 1100.9$ W.

(iii) True power $\approx 1101$ W.

(b) Transformer Losses and Calculation:

(i) Transformer Losses and Minimization:

  1. Copper Losses ($I^2R$ Losses): Heat dissipated due to the resistance of the primary ($R_p$) and secondary ($R_s$) windings when current flows ($P_{cu} = I_p^2 R_p + I_s^2 R_s$). These losses vary with the square of the load current.
    Minimization: Use low-resistance conductors (e.g., thick copper wire) for the windings. Design for optimal current density.
  2. Iron Losses (Core Losses): Occur in the core due to alternating flux, independent of load.
    • Hysteresis Loss: Energy lost due to repeated magnetization/demagnetization of the core material.
      Minimization: Use core material with low hysteresis loss (narrow hysteresis loop), such as silicon steel.
    • Eddy Current Loss: Energy lost due to induced circulating currents (eddy currents) in the core.
      Minimization: Use a laminated core (thin sheets insulated by varnish or oxide layer) to increase resistance to eddy currents. Using high-resistivity core material also helps.
  3. Stray Load Losses: Losses due to leakage flux inducing eddy currents in conductors, tank walls, and other metallic parts. More significant in large transformers. Minimized by careful design and arrangement of windings and core.
  4. Dielectric Losses: Minor losses occurring in the insulating materials (oil, paper) due to the alternating electric field. Usually negligible except at very high voltages/frequencies.
(Explain Copper and the two Iron losses).

(ii) Transformer Calculation (Ideal):

Given: Load resistance ($R_L$) = 20 $\Omega$, Secondary voltage ($V_s$) = 150 V, Primary current ($I_p$) = 5 A. Neglect losses (ideal transformer).

Secondary current ($I_s$) = $V_s / R_L = 150 V / 20 \Omega = 7.5 A$.

For an ideal transformer, $\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{I_s}{I_p} = \frac{N_p}{N_s}$.

Turns Ratio (n) is usually defined as $N_s/N_p$. $\frac{1}{n} = \frac{N_p}{N_s}$.

Find Primary Voltage ($V_p$):

$\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{I_s}{I_p} \implies V_p = V_s \times \frac{I_s}{I_p} = 150 V \times \frac{7.5 A}{5 A} = 150 \times 1.5 = 225 V$.

Find Turns Ratio (n = $N_s/N_p$):

$n = \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{150 V}{225 V} = \frac{150}{225} = \frac{2}{3}$.

Alternatively, $n = \frac{I_p}{I_s} = \frac{5 A}{7.5 A} = \frac{50}{75} = \frac{2}{3}$.

Primary Voltage = 225 V, Turns Ratio ($N_s/N_p$) = 2/3.
