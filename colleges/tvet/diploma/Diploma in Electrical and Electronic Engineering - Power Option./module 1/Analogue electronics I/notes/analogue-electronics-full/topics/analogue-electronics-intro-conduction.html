üî© CONDUCTION IN SOLIDS

All substances are made up of atoms, which have charged particles called electrons and protons.

Electricity in all forms is due to the charges on these particles.

When charged particles move in an orderly fashion, we get an electric current.

Conduction in solids

Among solids, metals are good conductors of electricity.

In metals, some electrons are not very tightly bound to the atoms.

They move about randomly in different directions within the metal.

When a voltage is applied across a piece of metal, these electrons move in an orderly fashion in one direction.

This flow of electrons is the current in the metal.

Electric Current(Amperes)

Electric Current characterizes the motion of electrically charged particles within conductors.

An electric current may cause heating of matter, electrochemical processes, and magnetization.

Definition of the direction of the current corresponds to the direction of motion of positive charges.

Direct current

When the current direction and intensity are constant in time, the current is known as direct current (dc).

Alternating Current

When the current direction and intensity of the current change periodically with time, the current is known as the alternating current (ac).

Electric resistance and conductance

The resistance $R$ is the ratio of voltage $V$ to current I :

$$R=\frac{V}{I}$$

Conductance, $\sigma$, reciprocal value of the electric resistance, the quotient of current I, and voltage V .

$$\text { conductance }=\frac{1}{\text { resistance }}=\frac{\text { Current }}{\text { Voltage }}$$

Resistivity and conductivity

Resistivity, $\rho$ (specific resistance), is a material-dependent quantity independent of the conductor's geometry. Ohm‚Ä¢meter, is the SI unit of the specific resistance $\rho$.

Conductivity, $\kappa$ (specific conductance), is the reciprocal value of the specific resistance. Siemens/meter, $\mathbf{S} / \mathbf{m}$, is the SI unit of the conductivity $\kappa$.

Resistance of a wire

$$ \text{Resistance} = \text{specific resistance} \cdot \frac{\text { length }}{\text { Area }} = \rho \cdot \frac{l}{A} $$

Voltage divider

A voltage divider is a simple series resistor circuit

Its output voltage is a fixed fraction of its input voltage.

Two resistors determine the divide-down ratio.

Input voltage $\mathrm{V}_{\text {in }}$ is applied to the top and bottom of the series resistors.

$$ V_{out} = V_{in} \left( \frac{R2}{R1 + R2} \right) $$

Current divider circuit

Current Divider circuits have two or more parallel branches for currents to flow through, but the voltage is the same for all components in the parallel circuit.

$$ I_{R2} = I_T \left( \frac{R1}{R1 + R2} \right) $$
$$ I_{R1} = I_T \left( \frac{R2}{R1 + R2} \right) $$

Capacitors

Capacitors and batteries both store electrical energy.

A capacitor is much simpler than a battery, as it can't produce new electrons -it only stores them.

Inside the capacitor, the terminals connect to two metal plates separated by a nonconducting substance, or dielectric.

Capacitor Circuit

Uses of capacitors:

  • ‚Ä¢Store charge for high-speed use. Big lasers use this technique as well to get very bright, instantaneous flashes.
  • ‚Ä¢Capacitors can also eliminate ripples
  • ‚Ä¢A capacitor can block DC voltage. If you connect a capacitor to a battery, then no current will flow between the battery poles once the capacitor charges.
  • ‚Ä¢However, an alternating current (AC) signal flows through a capacitor unimpeded. That's because the capacitor will charge and discharge as the alternating current fluctuates, making it appear that the alternating current is flowing.

üí® CONDUCTION IN GASES

Current conduction in gases

A rarefied neutral gas does not conduct current as an ideal vacuum does.

The rarefied gas can conduct only by the input of charge carriers.

Gas discharge is current conduction in gases mainly at low pressure.

Non-self-sustained discharge

a gaseous discharge where some external influence is needed to ionise the gas in order to get current to flow. eg from electron gun, X-ray, UV, Cosmic Radiation, etc

Current-voltage characteristic of a gas (non-sustained discharge)

I: recombination range

II: saturation range

III: proportional range

Saturation range: range of voltage in which nearly all ions reach the electrode. The current I is nearly independent of the voltage V.

Proportional range: the range of voltage in which the ions' and electrons' energy is sufficiently high to ionize neutral atoms and molecules. The ionization current I increases linearly with the voltage $V$.

Self-sustained gaseous discharge

In a self sustained discharge the potential gradient is sufficiently high that free electrons are accelerated hard enough to ionise atoms they collide with.

This means that as the current flows it keeps a population of ions and free electrons present.

Types of Self-sustained gaseous discharge

Glow discharge: A luminous discharge at mean current densities of $10^{-9} \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{m}^{2}<\mathrm{J}<10^{-4} \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{m}^{2}$.

The ions striking the cathode liberate electrons, which flow towards the anode.

Owing to the different mobility of the positive and negative charge carriers, zones of different space charges build up in the region between the electrodes.

Therefore, the gas between the cathode and anode does not glow uniformly.

Application: Fluorescent lamps

Arc Discharge: bright luminous discharge at current densities $\mathrm{J}>10^{-4} \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{m}^{2}$.

The cathode is heated by the incident current and emits more electrons by thermo-ionic emission or field emission.

Applications: Mercury-vapor lamp

Dark Discharge

This is a self-terminating arc discharge.

It is a gaseous discharge at very low current densities and low discharge voltages insufficient to ignite a self-sustained discharge.

Mainly it may be sustained only by external ionization due to an external source of radiation.

The dark discharge's ignition voltage depends on the electrodes' shape and distance and the gas pressure between the electrodes.

Current-voltage characteristic of a gaseous discharge (self-sustained)

Vz : ignition voltage. I: dark discharge, II: glow discharge, III: arc discharge.

Ignition voltage is the voltage at which a dark discharge turns into a glow discharge.

Self-sustained gaseous discharges have decreasing resistance characteristics or even a negative differential resistance dV/dI

A dropping resistor (current limiter) is used to prevent self-sustained gaseous discharges- an induction coil may be used for alternating current.

Vacuum Tubes

These are evacuated glass bulbs with inserted electrodes that control the flow of electrons by their electric potentials.

Cathode and anode in vacuum tubes

The cathode is the negative electrode in the tube that releases electrons by thermo-ionic emission. It is heated either directly or indirectly.

The anode is the positive electrode opposed to the cathode.

Vacuum tubes are evacuated to reduce as far as possible the collisions of electrons with gas molecules and to prevent oxidation of the hot cathode.

Anode potential, Va, is the voltage between anode and cathode.

Anode current, Ia, is the current between anode and cathode.

Plate resistance and characteristics

Vacuum Tube Devices

Vacuum-tube diode

Residual current is the current flowing in a vacuum-tube diode without an applied external voltage.

The electrons released by heating of the cathode provide a current between the cathode and anode even without an external voltage.

The current stops only when a sufficiently high counter-voltage is applied at the anode.

Vacuum-tube triode

Used for voltage amplification.

The vacuum tube triode contains a grid between anode and cathode

The anode current's magnitude may be controlled by applying a potential difference between grid and cathode

The grid remains almost free of current; hence the current control works without power consumption.

The triode amplifies the voltage signal applied to the grid.
