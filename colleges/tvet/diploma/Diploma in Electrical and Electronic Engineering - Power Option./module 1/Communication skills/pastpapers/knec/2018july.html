Question 1 (10 marks)

(a) Describe each of the following types of computers:

(i) multi-user computer system; (1 mark)

(ii) workstation; (1 mark)

(iii) laptop. (1 mark)

(b) List four secondary storage media of a computer system. (4 marks)

(c) State two reasons that make some employees to lose their jobs when their employer installs a computerized system. (3 marks)

Answer

This question defines computer types, lists secondary storage media, and explores reasons for job loss due to computerization.

(a) Types of Computers

  • (i) Multi-user Computer System: A computer system designed to allow multiple users to access its resources (CPU, memory, storage, applications) simultaneously, typically through terminals or networked connections. Examples include mainframes, minicomputers, and servers running multi-user operating systems like Unix/Linux or Windows Server.
  • (ii) Workstation: A high-performance computer designed for a single user, typically used for demanding technical or scientific applications like CAD/CAM, graphic design, complex simulations, or software development. Workstations generally have faster processors, more RAM, and higher-end graphics capabilities than standard personal computers.
  • (iii) Laptop (Notebook Computer): A portable personal computer that integrates the display, keyboard, pointing device (touchpad), processor, memory, and battery into a single, hinged unit suitable for mobile use.

(b) Secondary Storage Media

  1. Hard Disk Drive (HDD)
  2. Solid State Drive (SSD)
  3. USB Flash Drive (Thumb Drive, Pen Drive)
  4. Optical Discs (CD-ROM, CD-R, CD-RW, DVD, Blu-ray)
  5. Memory Cards (SD Card, microSD Card)
  6. External Hard Drives (HDD or SSD based)
  7. Magnetic Tape (Primarily for backup/archival)
*(Any four distinct media)*

(c) Reasons for Job Loss due to Computerized System Installation

  1. Automation of Tasks: Computerized systems often automate routine, manual tasks previously performed by employees (e.g., data entry, bookkeeping, calculations, assembly line work). This increased efficiency reduces the need for human labor to perform those specific tasks.
  2. Skill Obsolescence: The new system may require different skills than those possessed by the existing workforce. Employees unable or unwilling to acquire the necessary new skills (e.g., operating the new software, interpreting digital data) may become redundant.
  3. Increased Productivity with Fewer Staff: The new system may enable fewer employees to accomplish the same amount of work or even more, leading to downsizing as the company seeks to optimize labor costs.
  4. Restructuring and Changed Roles: The implementation of a new system often leads to restructuring of departments and job roles. Some traditional roles may be eliminated entirely, even if new roles are created elsewhere.
*(Any two reasons)*

Question 2 (10 marks)

(a) Explain each of the following types of computer software:

(i) firmware; (2 marks)

(ii) integrated software; (2 marks)

(iii) utility program. (2 marks)

(b) State four factors that should be considered when selecting an operating system for a personal computer. (4 marks)

Answer

This question defines software types (firmware, integrated, utility) and lists factors for selecting an operating system.

(a) Types of Computer Software

  • (i) Firmware: A specific class of software permanently programmed into a hardware device's read-only memory (ROM) or flash memory. It provides low-level control for the device's specific hardware and instructions on how it communicates with other hardware. Examples include the BIOS/UEFI on a computer motherboard, software embedded in printers, routers, or digital cameras.
  • (ii) Integrated Software: A software suite that bundles multiple application programs (often with related functionalities) together into a single package. These applications typically share a common user interface and allow easy data sharing between modules. Examples include Microsoft Office (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, etc.) or Google Workspace (Docs, Sheets, Slides, etc.).
  • (iii) Utility Program: A type of system software designed to help analyze, configure, optimize, or maintain a computer system's performance and security. Utility programs perform specific tasks related to managing computer hardware, operating systems, or application software. Examples include antivirus software, disk cleanup tools, file compression utilities, backup software, and disk defragmenters.

(b) Factors for Selecting an Operating System (OS) for a PC

  1. Hardware Compatibility: Ensure the OS is compatible with the computer's hardware components (processor architecture - x86/x64/ARM, RAM capacity, graphics card, peripherals). Check the OS vendor's minimum and recommended hardware requirements.
  2. Software Compatibility/Availability: Consider the specific application software the user needs to run. Verify that the required applications are available for and compatible with the chosen OS (e.g., some specialized engineering software might only run on Windows).
  3. User Interface and Ease of Use: Evaluate the user interface (GUI) based on the user's familiarity and preference. Some users might prefer the Windows interface, while others prefer macOS or a specific Linux distribution's desktop environment.
  4. Cost and Licensing: Consider the cost of the OS license. Some OSs like Windows or macOS have purchase costs, while many Linux distributions are free and open-source. Understand the licensing terms (e.g., single-user, volume licensing).
  5. Security Features and Stability: Assess the built-in security features, the OS's general reputation for stability and resistance to malware, and the frequency and reliability of security updates provided by the vendor.
  6. Support and Community: Consider the availability of technical support from the vendor or a wider user community (important for open-source OSs like Linux). Availability of documentation, forums, and third-party support resources.

Question 3 (10 marks)

(a) State four threats to information stored in a computer that may arise due to connectivity to the internet. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three benefits of using electronic mail instead of ordinary paper mail to send a job application letter. (6 marks)

Answer

This question asks about internet-related information threats and the benefits of using email for job applications.

(a) Threats to Stored Information Due to Internet Connectivity

  1. Malware Infection (Viruses, Worms, Ransomware, Spyware): Malicious software downloaded intentionally or unintentionally from websites, email attachments, or infected links can corrupt files, steal data (spyware, keyloggers), encrypt files and demand payment (ransomware), or provide remote access to attackers.
  2. Hacking / Unauthorized Access: Attackers can exploit vulnerabilities in the operating system, applications, or network configuration over the internet to gain unauthorized access to the computer and steal, alter, or delete stored information.
  3. Phishing Attacks: Deceptive emails or websites encountered online can trick users into revealing sensitive login credentials or personal data, which attackers can then use to access stored information or accounts.
  4. Data Interception (Lack of Encryption): If sensitive information is transmitted over the internet without proper encryption (e.g., via unsecure websites - HTTP vs HTTPS), it can potentially be intercepted and read by third parties ("man-in-the-middle" attacks).
  5. Denial of Service (DoS) Attacks: While primarily aimed at disrupting service availability, DoS attacks targeting a connected computer could potentially lead to system crashes or instability, indirectly risking data loss if unsaved work is affected.

(b) Benefits of Using Email for Job Application Letter (vs. Paper Mail)

  1. Speed and Immediacy: Email delivery is almost instantaneous, ensuring the application reaches the employer much faster than traditional postal mail, which is crucial when dealing with application deadlines.
  2. Cost-Effectiveness: Sending an email is virtually free, eliminating costs associated with paper, printing, envelopes, and postage stamps required for paper mail.
  3. Ease of Distribution and Record Keeping: Applicants can easily send the same application documents (letter, CV) to multiple potential employers. Both the sender and receiver automatically have a digital record of the communication, including date and time sent/received, which is easy to archive and search.
  4. Convenience and Accessibility: Applications can be sent anytime from anywhere with internet access. Employers can receive, store, and forward digital applications much more easily than managing physical mail. Attachments (like CVs, portfolios) are easily included.

Question 4 (10 marks)

(a) State the word processing formula that should be used in a table to compute each of the values:

(i) total in a column; (1.5 marks)

(ii) total in a row; (1.5 marks)

(iii) mean score in a row of scores. (1 mark)

(b) State three formatting features that may be applied to a numeric entry in a cell of a spreadsheet. (3 marks)

(c) Distinguish between relative cell references and absolute cell references as used in spreadsheets. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers formulas in word processing tables, spreadsheet numeric formatting, and cell referencing types.

Note: Word processing table formulas have limited functionality and syntax compared to spreadsheets.

(a) Word Processing Table Formulas

(Syntax typically found in programs like Microsoft Word's Table Formula feature)

  • (i) Total in a column: `=SUM(ABOVE)`
    *(This formula, placed in a cell at the bottom of a column of numbers, sums all numeric values in the cells directly above it in that column.)*
  • (ii) Total in a row: `=SUM(LEFT)` or `=SUM(RIGHT)`
    *(Placed in a cell at the end of a row, `SUM(LEFT)` sums numeric values in cells to its left in that row. `SUM(RIGHT)` sums values to its right.)*
  • (iii) Mean score in a row of scores: `=AVERAGE(LEFT)` or `=AVERAGE(RIGHT)`
    *(Similar to SUM, this calculates the average of numeric values in cells to the left or right within the same row.)*

*(Specific cell ranges like `=SUM(A1:A5)` can sometimes be used, but `ABOVE`, `LEFT`, `RIGHT` are more common positional arguments in Word.)*

(b) Formatting Features for Numeric Entries in Spreadsheets

  1. Number Format (Decimal Places): Specifying the number of decimal places to display (e.g., showing 1,234.50 instead of 1234.5).
  2. Currency Format: Displaying the number with a currency symbol (e.g., $, Ksh, £), thousands separators, and a specified number of decimal places (e.g., Ksh 1,234.50).
  3. Percentage Format: Displaying the number as a percentage by multiplying the cell value by 100 and adding a percent sign (%) (e.g., displaying 0.75 as 75%).
  4. Comma Style (Thousands Separator): Adding commas to separate thousands, millions, etc., to improve readability of large numbers (e.g., 1,234,567).
  5. Date/Time Format: Interpreting and displaying a numeric value (serial number) as a specific date or time format (e.g., dd/mm/yyyy, Month dd, yyyy, hh:mm AM/PM).
  6. Alignment: Setting the horizontal alignment within the cell (e.g., left, center, right - numbers are often right-aligned by default).
  7. Font Style/Color: Applying font styles (bold, italic), changing font color, or cell background color to highlight specific numeric values.
*(Any three features)*

(c) Relative vs. Absolute Cell References

  • Relative Cell Reference: A standard cell reference (e.g., `A1`, `B2`) in a formula that **adjusts automatically** when the formula is copied or filled to other cells. The reference changes relative to its new location. For example, if the formula `=A1+B1` in cell C1 is copied to cell C2, it automatically becomes `=A2+B2`. This is the default referencing type and is useful for applying the same calculation across multiple rows or columns.
  • Absolute Cell Reference: A cell reference in a formula that **remains fixed** on a specific cell and does **not** change when the formula is copied or filled to other locations. Absolute references are created by adding dollar signs ($) before the column letter and/or row number (e.g., `$A$1` locks both column A and row 1; `$A1` locks only column A; `A$1` locks only row 1). This is used when a formula needs to consistently refer to a specific cell, such as a constant value (e.g., a tax rate) or a fixed parameter, regardless of where the formula is copied.

Question 5 (10 marks)

(a) Outline the steps that should be followed when creating a database. (4 marks)

(b) List four views in a presentation program. (4 marks)

(c) State three formatting features that may be applied to a graphic created using a desktop publishing program. (2 marks?)

Answer

This question outlines database creation steps, lists presentation views, and identifies DTP graphic formatting features. Mark allocation for (c) seems low.

(a) Steps in Creating a Database (Conceptual & Logical Design)

(Focusing on the design process before using specific software)

  1. Define the Purpose and Scope: Clearly understand the purpose of the database – what information needs to be stored, who will use it, and what tasks it should support. Define the scope and boundaries.
  2. Identify Entities (Tables): Determine the main subjects or entities about which information needs to be stored (e.g., Customers, Products, Orders, Employees). Each entity typically becomes a table.
  3. Determine Fields (Attributes) for Each Entity: For each entity (table), identify the specific pieces of information (attributes or fields) that need to be recorded (e.g., for Customers: CustomerID, Name, Address, Phone; for Products: ProductID, Name, Price, QuantityOnHand).
  4. Choose Data Types and Define Properties for Fields: Select the appropriate data type for each field (e.g., Text, Number, Date/Time, Currency, Yes/No). Define field properties like size, format, validation rules, and whether the field is required.
  5. Identify Primary Keys: For each table, designate one or more fields as the primary key – a unique identifier for each record in the table (e.g., CustomerID, ProductID).
  6. Determine Relationships Between Tables: Identify how the tables (entities) relate to each other (one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many). Establish these relationships by using foreign keys (fields in one table that refer to the primary key in another table).
  7. Normalize the Design (Refine): Review the table structures and relationships to minimize data redundancy and improve data integrity by applying normalization rules (e.g., ensuring fields depend only on the primary key).
  8. Create the Database Structure (Implementation): Use database software (like Access, SQL Server) to create the tables, define the fields and their properties, set primary keys, and establish the relationships based on the design.
*(Focus on steps 1-7 for design, step 8 is implementation)*

(b) Views in a Presentation Program (e.g., PowerPoint)

  1. Normal View: The main editing view, typically showing the current slide large, an outline or thumbnail pane on the left, and a notes pane below the slide. Used for creating and editing slide content.
  2. Slide Sorter View: Displays thumbnails of all slides in sequence. Used for rearranging slide order, applying transitions, and setting timings.
  3. Slide Show View: Displays the presentation full-screen, one slide at a time, as it would be shown to an audience. Animations and transitions play in this view.
  4. Notes Page View: Shows the current slide at the top of the page and the corresponding speaker notes below it. Used for creating and printing speaker notes.
  5. Reading View: Similar to Slide Show view but displays the presentation within the application window (not full screen) with simple navigation controls. Useful for reviewing the presentation on one's own computer.
  6. Outline View: Displays the text content of the presentation (titles and body text) in an outline format in the left pane, allowing for easy editing and restructuring of the text content.
  7. Master Views (Slide Master, Handout Master, Notes Master): Used to edit the underlying templates that control the look and feel of slides, handouts, and notes pages.
*(Any four distinct views)*

(c) Formatting Features Applied to a Graphic in DTP

  1. Resizing/Scaling: Changing the overall dimensions (height and width) of the graphic, often with options to maintain the original aspect ratio.
  2. Cropping: Trimming away unwanted outer portions of the graphic to focus on a specific area or fit it into a designated space.
  3. Applying Borders and Outlines: Adding a visible line (border) around the graphic, with options to control its color, thickness (weight), and style (solid, dashed).
  4. Adjusting Brightness/Contrast/Color: Making basic adjustments to the graphic's appearance, such as increasing/decreasing brightness, enhancing contrast, or altering color saturation or tone (though extensive editing is better done in dedicated image software).
  5. Applying Effects: Adding visual effects like shadows, reflections, glows, or transparency to the graphic.
  6. Wrapping Text: Controlling how surrounding body text flows around the graphic (e.g., square, tight, top and bottom).
  7. Rotation/Flipping: Rotating the graphic by a specific angle or flipping it horizontally or vertically.
*(Any three features)*

Question 6 (Compulsory - 20 marks)

(a) Business organisations are increasingly using electronic communication. Explain three reasons that may account for this increase. (6 marks)

(b) It has been observed that majority of the employees of Peace Limited spend a lot of their time speaking on their cell phones. As the foreman, write a memorandum to the employees requesting them to stop this practice. (8 marks)

(c) (i) Explain the phrase "Annual General Meeting (AGM)." (2 marks)

(ii) Outline four items of agenda that may be discussed in an annual general meeting of a publicly listed company. (4 marks)

Answer

This question covers reasons for increased electronic communication, writing a memo on cellphone use, defining AGM, and typical AGM agenda items.

(a) Reasons for Increased Use of Electronic Communication in Business

  1. Speed and Efficiency: Electronic communication methods like email, instant messaging, and video conferencing allow for near-instantaneous transmission of messages and documents, significantly speeding up communication processes, decision-making, and workflows compared to traditional methods like postal mail or physical meetings.
  2. Cost Reduction: Electronic communication eliminates costs associated with paper, printing, postage, physical storage, and often reduces travel costs (through video conferencing instead of physical meetings), making it a more cost-effective way to communicate, especially over long distances or with large groups.
  3. Globalization and Remote Work Facilitation: Electronic tools enable seamless communication and collaboration across different geographical locations and time zones, supporting global business operations, remote work arrangements, and virtual teams, expanding reach and flexibility.
  4. Enhanced Record Keeping and Accessibility: Digital communications often create automatic records (e.g., email archives, chat logs) that are easily stored, searched, retrieved, and shared, improving documentation and knowledge management within the organization.

(b) Memorandum on Employee Cell Phone Use

PEACE LIMITED - INTERNAL MEMORANDUM

TO: All Employees, Workshop Department

FROM: [Your Name], Foreman

DATE: 26th July 2018

SUBJECT: Appropriate Use of Personal Cell Phones During Working Hours

It has been observed recently that there is a significant increase in the amount of time employees are spending on personal cell phone calls and activities during working hours within the workshop.

While we understand the need to stay connected, excessive use of personal cell phones during work time leads to several concerns, including:

  • Reduced productivity and focus on assigned tasks.
  • Potential safety hazards due to distraction in a workshop environment.
  • Disruptions to colleagues and workflow.

Therefore, I am requesting all employees to strictly limit the use of personal cell phones during working hours. Personal calls should be reserved for designated break times (lunch, tea breaks) or emergencies only. If an emergency call must be taken during work time, please keep it brief and conduct it in a non-disruptive location away from active work areas.

Using cell phones for personal browsing, social media, or entertainment during work hours is prohibited.

Adherence to this guideline is essential for maintaining productivity, ensuring safety, and respecting our work environment. Your cooperation in this matter is expected and appreciated.

Please see me if you have any urgent concerns regarding this policy.


Regards,

[Your Name]

Foreman

(c) Annual General Meeting (AGM)

  • (i) Explanation: An Annual General Meeting (AGM) is a mandatory yearly gathering of the shareholders (owners) and directors of a company (especially a publicly listed one or as required by bylaws). Its primary purpose is to allow shareholders to receive information about the company's performance, elect directors, approve key decisions, and hold the management accountable.
  • (ii) Typical AGM Agenda Items (Publicly Listed Company):
    1. Consideration and Adoption of Financial Statements: Presentation and approval of the company's audited annual financial statements (Balance Sheet, Profit & Loss Account) and the reports of the Directors and Auditors thereon.
    2. Declaration of Dividend: Approval of the dividend amount recommended by the Board of Directors to be paid to shareholders.
    3. Election/Re-election of Directors: Electing new directors or re-electing directors who are retiring by rotation according to the company's articles.
    4. Appointment and Remuneration of Auditors: Appointing the company's auditors for the upcoming financial year and authorizing the Board to fix their remuneration.
    5. Special Business (if any): Discussion and voting on any specific resolutions proposed by the company or shareholders that require shareholder approval (e.g., changes to company articles, major acquisitions).

Question 7 (10 marks)

(a) State four guidelines that should be followed when using tables to provide information in a report. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three measures that the management of an organisation may take to encourage upward communication. (6 marks)

Answer

This question provides guidelines for using tables in reports and measures to encourage upward communication.

(a) Guidelines for Using Tables in Reports

  1. Clear Title and Numbering: Each table should have a clear, descriptive title that explains its content, and be numbered sequentially (e.g., Table 1, Table 2) for easy reference within the report text.
  2. Appropriate Data Presentation: Use tables primarily for presenting precise numerical data or detailed textual information in an organized row-and-column format that facilitates comparison and easy lookup of specific values. Avoid cluttering tables with excessive information.
  3. Clear Labels and Units: Label all columns and rows clearly and concisely. Include units of measurement (e.g., Ksh, %, kg) where applicable, either in the column/row headings or in a footnote to the table.
  4. Reference in Text: Refer to the table by its number within the main body of the report text (e.g., "As shown in Table 3,..."). Briefly explain the table's significance or highlight key findings derived from it in the text; don't just insert the table without context.
  5. Consistent Formatting: Maintain consistent formatting (fonts, alignment, borders, spacing) for all tables within the report to ensure a professional appearance and ease of reading.
  6. Source Citation (If Applicable): If the data presented in the table is derived from an external source, cite the source clearly, usually in a footnote below the table.

(b) Measures to Encourage Upward Communication

  1. Establish Clear and Accessible Channels: Create formal mechanisms for employees to communicate upwards, such as suggestion boxes/schemes, regular employee surveys, open forums or town hall meetings with management, grievance procedures, and performance appraisal sessions that explicitly invite feedback.
  2. Promote an "Open Door" Policy and Receptive Culture: Management should actively encourage employees to voice concerns, ideas, or feedback directly to their supervisors or higher management without fear of reprisal. Managers need to be trained to be approachable, listen actively, and be genuinely receptive to employee input.
  3. Act on Feedback and Communicate Outcomes: Demonstrate that upward communication is valued by acknowledging employee feedback promptly and, where feasible, acting upon suggestions or addressing concerns. Crucially, communicate back to employees about the actions taken (or reasons why action wasn't taken) as a result of their feedback. This closes the loop and shows that input matters.
  4. Management by Walking Around (MBWA): Encourage managers to spend time informally interacting with employees in their workspaces. This can create opportunities for spontaneous, informal upward communication and help managers understand employee perspectives better.
*(See also Q6b from July 2015 paper)*

Question 8 (10 marks)

(a) Distinguish between a conference and a panel discussion. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Explain the term etiquette as used in communication (2 marks)

(ii) Outline four ways in which an interviewee may demonstrate etiquette during an interview. (4 marks)

Answer

This question distinguishes conferences from panel discussions and covers communication etiquette, particularly in interviews.

(a) Conference vs. Panel Discussion

  • Conference: A large, often multi-day formal meeting bringing together people with shared interests (e.g., academic, professional, industry-specific) to exchange information, discuss issues, network, and learn. Conferences typically feature multiple sessions running concurrently, including keynote speeches, presentations of papers, workshops, exhibitions, and potentially panel discussions as one component. The scope is generally broad within the overarching theme.
  • Panel Discussion: A specific type of meeting or session (often part of a larger conference, but can be standalone) where a small group of experts (panelists) discuss a specific topic in front of an audience. A moderator typically guides the discussion, poses questions to the panelists, and often facilitates questions from the audience. The focus is on interactive discussion among the experts and between the panel and the audience on a particular subject.

Key Distinction: A conference is a large event with varied formats covering a broad theme, while a panel discussion is a specific format focused on expert discussion of a particular topic, often as just one part of a conference.

(b) Etiquette in Communication and Interviews

  • (i) Etiquette in Communication: Refers to the customary code of polite behavior and courtesy expected during communication interactions. It involves showing respect, consideration, and appropriateness in how one speaks, listens, writes, and uses non-verbal cues according to social or professional norms. Examples include active listening, using polite language, appropriate forms of address, respecting turns in conversation, and maintaining professionalism. *(See also Q6d from July 2014 paper)*
  • (ii) Demonstrating Etiquette During an Interview:
    1. Punctuality: Arriving on time (or logging in on time for virtual interviews) shows respect for the interviewer's schedule.
    2. Professional Attire and Grooming: Dressing appropriately for the company culture demonstrates professionalism and respect for the occasion.
    3. Polite Greetings and Body Language: Offering a firm handshake (if in person), making appropriate eye contact, smiling, using polite language ("Please," "Thank you"), addressing interviewers formally (e.g., Mr./Ms. Last Name unless invited otherwise), and maintaining good posture.
    4. Active Listening and Respectful Interaction: Paying close attention when interviewers are speaking, avoiding interruptions, answering questions thoughtfully and concisely, and asking relevant questions when invited demonstrates engagement and respect. Thanking the interviewers for their time at the end.
    5. Phone Etiquette (if applicable): Silencing personal mobile phones and putting them away during the interview.

Question 9 (10 marks)

(a) Despite persuasion from friends, Danton is determined to quit salaried employment for self employment. Explain three reasons that may account for this determination. (6 marks)

(b) Explain the way in which each of the following cultural factors may enhance entrepreneurial development:

(i) time consciousness; (2 marks)

(ii) work ethics. (2 marks)

Answer

This question explores motivations for choosing self-employment over salaried employment and how specific cultural factors can enhance entrepreneurship.

(a) Reasons for Danton's Determination for Self-Employment

  1. Desire for Autonomy and Control: Danton may strongly desire independence – the freedom to make his own decisions, set his own working hours, choose the projects he works on, and control the strategic direction of his work without reporting to a boss or adhering to corporate structures.
  2. Pursuit of Passion and Fulfillment: Self-employment might offer Danton the opportunity to pursue a personal passion, work in a field he deeply cares about, or build something meaningful from scratch, providing a level of personal satisfaction and fulfillment he feels is lacking in his current salaried job.
  3. Potential for Greater Financial Rewards: He might perceive a higher earning potential in self-employment, believing that the direct link between his effort/success and financial return could ultimately yield more income than a fixed salary, despite the associated risks.
  4. Identification of a Specific Business Opportunity: Danton might have identified a specific market gap or a compelling business opportunity that he is eager to exploit, believing strongly in its potential for success.
  5. Dissatisfaction with Salaried Employment: He might be unhappy with aspects of his current job, such as lack of recognition, limited growth opportunities, bureaucracy, job insecurity, or conflict with management, motivating him to seek an alternative where he has more control.
*(Any three plausible reasons)*

(b) Cultural Factors Enhancing Entrepreneurial Development

  • (i) Time Consciousness: A cultural emphasis on punctuality, efficiency, meeting deadlines, and valuing time as a resource can enhance entrepreneurship. Entrepreneurs operating in such a culture are more likely to be organized, reliable in delivering products/services on schedule, and efficient in managing their operations, which builds trust with customers and partners and contributes to business success.
  • (ii) Strong Work Ethic: A cultural value system that emphasizes hard work, diligence, discipline, responsibility, and commitment to quality can significantly boost entrepreneurship. Entrepreneurs with a strong work ethic are more likely to persevere through challenges, maintain high standards, dedicate the necessary long hours, and build successful, sustainable businesses based on effort and reliability.

Question 10 (10 marks)

(a) Outline four guidelines that the management of an enterprise should follow to implement effective social responsibility. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three factors that may complicate the management of a family business. (6 marks)

Answer

This question outlines guidelines for effective social responsibility and factors complicating family business management.

(a) Guidelines for Effective Social Responsibility Implementation

  1. Leadership Commitment: Secure genuine commitment and support from top management, who should champion CSR initiatives and integrate them into the company's vision and strategy.
  2. Stakeholder Engagement: Identify key stakeholders (employees, customers, community, etc.) and engage them to understand relevant social/environmental issues and expectations, ensuring initiatives are meaningful.
  3. Clear Objectives and Measurement: Define specific, measurable goals for CSR activities and establish metrics to track progress and impact, allowing for evaluation and reporting.
  4. Integration into Business Practices: Embed CSR considerations into core business operations, supply chains, product development, and employee policies, rather than treating it as a separate, peripheral activity.
  5. Transparency and Communication: Communicate openly about CSR goals, activities, progress, and challenges to build trust and accountability with stakeholders.
*(See also Q9b from July 2015 paper)*

(b) Factors Complicating Family Business Management

  1. Mixing Family Relationships with Business Decisions: Emotional ties, family history, and personal relationships can interfere with objective business decision-making regarding strategy, resource allocation, or performance evaluation. Favoritism or conflicts between family members can spill over into the workplace.
  2. Succession Planning Challenges: Determining who will lead the business in the next generation can be fraught with difficulty. Issues arise regarding the competence vs. birthright of potential successors, managing expectations of different family members, and ensuring a smooth leadership transition without damaging family harmony or business continuity.
  3. Conflict Between Family and Business Goals: Family needs or priorities (e.g., providing employment for relatives regardless of qualification, drawing excessive funds for personal use) may conflict with the financial health, strategic direction, or professional management needs of the business.
  4. Difficulties in Attracting/Retaining Non-Family Talent: Non-family employees may perceive limited opportunities for advancement to top leadership positions (reserved for family) or feel caught in the middle of family disputes, making it harder to attract and retain skilled external talent.
  5. Informal Structures and Lack of Professionalization: Family businesses sometimes rely on informal communication and decision-making structures, lacking clear policies, defined roles, or formal governance mechanisms (like an independent board), which can hinder growth and efficiency as the business scales.

Question 11 (10 marks)

(a) Distinguish between social entrepreneur and a political entrepreneur. (4 marks)

(b) Outline six activities that may be included in the induction programme for new employees in a manufacturing firm. (6 marks)

Answer

This question distinguishes social from political entrepreneurs and outlines induction program activities.

(a) Social Entrepreneur vs. Political Entrepreneur

  • Social Entrepreneur: An individual who identifies a social or environmental problem and uses entrepreneurial principles (innovation, risk-taking, resourcefulness) to create, organize, and manage a venture (which can be non-profit, for-profit, or hybrid) with the primary goal of achieving **social impact and positive change**, rather than solely maximizing personal profit. Their success is measured largely by their social or environmental outcomes.
  • Political Entrepreneur: An individual (often a politician, policy advocate, or interest group leader) who identifies opportunities within the **political arena** and uses entrepreneurial strategies (building coalitions, mobilizing resources, framing issues, navigating political processes) to achieve specific political goals, influence policy outcomes, gain political power, or implement political reforms. Their focus is on achieving political objectives through strategic action within the political system.

Key Distinction: Social entrepreneurs focus on creating ventures for social/environmental impact; political entrepreneurs focus on achieving goals within the political system.

(b) Activities in an Induction Programme (Manufacturing Firm)

An induction (or onboarding) programme helps new employees integrate smoothly into the organization.

  1. Welcome and Introduction to the Company: Overview of the company's history, mission, vision, values, culture, organizational structure, key products, and introduction to key personnel.
  2. Job Role Clarification: Detailed explanation of the new employee's specific job duties, responsibilities, performance expectations, reporting lines, and introduction to their immediate team and supervisor.
  3. Health and Safety Training: Specific safety procedures relevant to the manufacturing environment, use of personal protective equipment (PPE), emergency evacuation procedures, hazard identification, and reporting mechanisms.
  4. Tour of Facilities: A guided tour of the manufacturing plant, including workstations, tool areas, canteen, restrooms, first aid stations, and emergency exits.
  5. Introduction to Company Policies and Procedures: Explanation of key HR policies (code of conduct, working hours, leave policy, grievance procedures), operational procedures, quality standards, and IT usage policies. Completion of necessary paperwork (contracts, tax forms, benefits enrollment).
  6. Basic Skills/Equipment Training: Initial training on specific machinery, tools, software systems, or processes required for the employee's immediate role.
  7. Introduction to Team/Buddy System: Facilitating introductions to colleagues within the department and potentially assigning a 'buddy' or mentor to help the new employee navigate the first few weeks.

Question 12 (10 marks)

(a) Outline four limitations of using a focus group discussion to generate a business idea. (4 marks)

(b) Jacob is in the process of writing a business plan. Outline six types of information that he should include in the business description section of the plan. (6 marks)

Answer

This question outlines limitations of focus groups for idea generation and contents of a business description section in a business plan.

(a) Limitations of Focus Group Discussions for Business Idea Generation

  1. Groupthink / Conformity Pressure: Participants may conform to the perceived group consensus or be hesitant to express unconventional or dissenting ideas for fear of judgment, potentially stifling truly innovative concepts.
  2. Dominant Participants: One or two vocal individuals might dominate the discussion, overshadowing quieter participants and limiting the diversity of ideas generated.
  3. Moderator Bias/Influence: The way the moderator frames questions, guides the discussion, or reacts to ideas can unintentionally influence the direction and nature of the ideas generated.
  4. Artificial Setting: The focus group environment is not a natural setting, and participants' responses might not accurately reflect their real-world behavior or genuine needs. They might say what they think the moderator wants to hear.
  5. Limited Representativeness: Findings from a small group may not be representative of the broader target market, potentially leading to ideas based on the specific biases or experiences of the group members.
  6. Focus on Existing Problems (Incremental Ideas): Discussions often revolve around existing products or known problems, which may lead to incremental improvement ideas rather than radical or breakthrough innovations.

(b) Information in the Business Description Section of a Business Plan

This section provides a high-level overview of the business.

  1. Business Name and Legal Structure: The official name of the business and its chosen legal form (e.g., sole proprietorship, partnership, limited liability company, corporation).
  2. Mission Statement: A concise statement defining the fundamental purpose of the business – why it exists and what it aims to achieve.
  3. Vision Statement: A description of the desired future state or long-term aspiration of the business.
  4. Products/Services Overview: A brief description of the core products or services the business will offer, highlighting the key customer needs they address or problems they solve.
  5. Target Market Summary: A brief identification of the primary group(s) of customers the business intends to serve.
  6. Business Goals and Objectives: Outline the key short-term and long-term goals the business aims to achieve (e.g., market share target, revenue goal, launch timeline).
  7. Brief History (If Applicable): If the business already exists or is building on past work, a short summary of its history or the founder's relevant background.
  8. Location and Facilities Overview (Brief): Mention the planned or existing location and type of facilities, if relevant to the core description.
