Question 1 (10 marks)

(a) Explain the way in which each of the following categories of input devices enter data in a computer:

(i) pointing; (2 marks)

(ii) scanning. (2 marks)

(b) Explain the function of each of the following features in spreadsheet applications:

(i) wrap text; (2 marks)

(ii) merge cells; (2 marks)

(iii) sort. (2 marks)

Answer

This question explains how pointing and scanning input devices work, and the function of spreadsheet features (wrap text, merge cells, sort).

(a) Data Entry by Input Device Categories

  • (i) Pointing Devices: Enter data primarily by controlling the position of a cursor or pointer on the display screen and signaling actions (like clicks or drags) at specific locations. They translate the user's physical movements (of the device or finger) into cursor movement. Actions like clicking or tapping select items, activate commands, or draw graphical elements.
    Examples: Mouse, touchpad, trackball, touchscreen, stylus.
  • (ii) Scanning Devices: Enter data by capturing an image or pattern from a physical document or object and converting it into a digital format that the computer can process. This involves using light sensors or cameras to read the visual information.
    Examples: Flatbed scanner (captures document images), barcode reader (reads barcode patterns), QR code scanner, Optical Character Recognition (OCR) scanner (converts scanned text images into editable text).

(b) Spreadsheet Features Functions

  • (i) Wrap Text: Automatically displays text content within a cell on multiple lines if it is too long to fit within the current cell width. It adjusts the row height as needed to make all the text visible within the cell boundaries, preventing text from overflowing into adjacent cells or being cut off.
  • (ii) Merge Cells: Combines two or more selected adjacent cells (horizontally or vertically) into a single, larger cell. This is often used to create titles or headings that span across multiple columns or rows for better table structure and presentation.
  • (iii) Sort: Rearranges the rows of data in a selected range or entire table based on the values in one or more specified columns. Data can be sorted alphabetically (A-Z, Z-A), numerically (smallest to largest, largest to smallest), or chronologically (by date/time), allowing users to organize and analyze data more easily.

Question 2 (10 marks)

(a) Outline four features of Wide Area Networks (WANs). (4 marks)

(b) Explain the circumstance under which each of the following database objects may be used:

(i) report; (2 marks)

(ii) query; (2 marks)

(iii) form. (2 marks)

Answer

This question outlines WAN features and explains circumstances for using database reports, queries, and forms.

(a) Features of Wide Area Networks (WANs)

  1. Large Geographical Coverage: WANs span extensive geographical areas, such as across cities, countries, or even continents, connecting networks that are far apart.
  2. Use of Public/Leased Communication Links: Typically rely on communication lines and infrastructure provided by telecommunication companies (e.g., fiber optic cables, satellite links, microwave links, leased lines) rather than privately owned cables like most LANs. The internet itself is the largest WAN.
  3. Slower Data Transmission Speeds (Historically/Compared to LAN): Generally, data transfer speeds across WAN links are slower than those within a LAN due to longer distances, reliance on third-party infrastructure, and potentially higher latency. However, modern WAN technologies like fiber optics offer very high speeds.
  4. Interconnection of Multiple LANs/MANs: A primary function of a WAN is to connect multiple Local Area Networks (LANs) or Metropolitan Area Networks (MANs) together, allowing communication and resource sharing between geographically dispersed sites of an organization or between different organizations.
  5. Higher Cost: Setting up and maintaining WAN connections, especially using dedicated leased lines, is typically more expensive than setting up a LAN due to the cost of long-distance communication links and equipment (routers).

(b) Circumstances for Using Database Objects

  • (i) Report: Used when there is a need to **present or summarize data** from tables or queries in a formatted, organized, and often printable layout. Appropriate for generating invoices, financial summaries, mailing labels, inventory lists, or any situation requiring a structured presentation of data, often including calculations (totals, averages) and grouping.
  • (ii) Query: Used when there is a need to **retrieve specific data** that meets certain criteria, combine data from multiple related tables, perform calculations on data, or automate data manipulation tasks (like updating or deleting records based on criteria - action queries). Appropriate for answering specific questions about the data (e.g., "Which customers ordered product X last month?", "What is the average salary by department?").
  • (iii) Form: Used when there is a need for a **user-friendly interface** to **enter, view, or edit data** in tables, typically one record at a time. Appropriate for data entry tasks, viewing detailed information for a specific record (like a customer's full details), or controlling how users interact with the data (e.g., using dropdown lists, validation rules).

Question 3 (10 marks)

(a) Distinguish between normal layout view and slide sorter view with respect to presentation software. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three functions of a computer operating system. (6 marks)

Answer

This question distinguishes between presentation software views and explains key operating system functions.

(a) Normal Layout View vs. Slide Sorter View

  • Normal View: This is the primary **editing view** in presentation software. It typically displays three panes: a large pane showing the **current slide** for detailed editing of content (text, images, objects), a smaller pane on the left showing **thumbnails or an outline** of all slides for navigation, and often a **notes pane** below the current slide for adding speaker notes. Its purpose is content creation and modification on individual slides.
  • Slide Sorter View: This view displays **thumbnail images of all slides** in the presentation simultaneously in a horizontal grid. It does not show the content of any single slide in detail. Its primary purpose is to allow the user to easily **view the overall flow** of the presentation and **rearrange the order** of slides by dragging and dropping the thumbnails. It's also useful for applying transitions or timings to multiple slides efficiently.

Key Distinction: Normal view focuses on editing content within a single slide (with navigation aids), while Slide Sorter view focuses on viewing and rearranging the sequence of all slides.

*(See also Q5b(i) from Nov 2021 paper)*

(b) Functions of a Computer Operating System (OS)

  1. Resource Management: The OS manages the computer's hardware resources, including allocating CPU time to different processes (process scheduling), managing main memory (RAM allocation and deallocation), controlling access to storage devices (file management), and managing input/output (I/O) devices through device drivers.
  2. User Interface Provision: It provides the means by which users interact with the computer. This can be a Graphical User Interface (GUI) with icons, windows, and menus, or a Command-Line Interface (CLI) where users type commands.
  3. Process and Task Management: The OS controls the execution of application programs and system tasks. It handles starting, stopping, suspending, and resuming processes, managing their concurrent execution (multitasking), and facilitating communication between them.
  4. File System Management: Organizes data on storage devices (hard drives, SSDs) into files and directories (folders). It manages file creation, deletion, reading, writing, copying, moving, and access permissions, providing a structured way to store and retrieve information.
  5. Security and Access Control: Provides mechanisms to protect the system and user data from unauthorized access, such as user authentication (login/password), file permissions, and often basic firewall capabilities.

Question 6 (Compulsory - 20 marks)

(a) Outline three indicators of a poorly written essay. (3 marks)

(b) State two ways in which a receiver may exhibit etiquette during a telephone conversation. (2 marks)

(c) Some managers prefer to use written communication when conveying information to employees. State three reasons that may account for this preference. (3 marks)

(d) Distinguish between lateral communication and diagonal communication. (4 marks)

(e) Highlight three benefits that an engineering firm may derive from effective interpersonal communication. (3 marks)

(f) Outline three ways in which information communication technology (ICT) may promote effective business communication. (3 marks)

(g) State two strategies that may be adopted in an organization to enhance the loyalty of a new customer. (2 marks)

Answer

This compulsory question covers indicators of poor essays, telephone etiquette, preference for written communication, types of communication flow, benefits of interpersonal communication, ICT's role in communication, and customer loyalty strategies.

(a) Indicators of a Poorly Written Essay

  1. Lack of Clear Thesis/Focus: The main argument or purpose of the essay is unclear, unfocused, or absent, making it difficult for the reader to understand the central point.
  2. Poor Organization and Structure: Ideas are presented haphazardly without logical flow; paragraphs lack topic sentences or coherence; transitions between ideas or paragraphs are weak or missing.
  3. Weak Supporting Evidence/Development: Claims or arguments are made without sufficient supporting evidence, examples, explanations, or details, leaving points unsubstantiated or underdeveloped.
  4. Grammar, Spelling, and Punctuation Errors: Frequent errors in grammar, spelling, punctuation, and sentence structure distract the reader and undermine the credibility of the writing.
  5. Inappropriate Tone or Style: The language, tone, or style used is not suitable for the intended audience or purpose of the essay (e.g., too informal, overly simplistic, unnecessarily complex).
*(Any three indicators)*

(b) Receiver's Etiquette During a Telephone Conversation

  1. Active Listening: Paying close attention to the caller, avoiding distractions, providing verbal cues (e.g., "I see," "Okay") to show engagement, and waiting for the caller to finish before responding.
  2. Speaking Clearly and Politely: Responding clearly and audibly, using polite language ("please," "thank you"), and maintaining a courteous and helpful tone throughout the conversation.
  3. Taking Accurate Messages (If Necessary): If the intended person is unavailable, accurately recording the caller's name, number, reason for calling, date/time, and ensuring the message is relayed promptly and correctly.
  4. Ending the Call Professionally: Summarizing any actions agreed upon, thanking the caller, and waiting for the caller to hang up first (often considered polite) or using a polite closing phrase (e.g., "Thank you for calling, goodbye.").
*(Any two ways)*

(c) Reasons Managers Prefer Written Communication

  1. Provides a Permanent Record: Creates a documented record of instructions, policies, or decisions that can be stored and referenced later, ensuring accountability and reducing disputes about what was communicated.
  2. Ensures Consistency and Accuracy for Complex Information: Allows managers to carefully formulate the message, ensuring complex details, procedures, or policies are communicated accurately and consistently to all recipients, reducing the risk of misinterpretation inherent in oral communication.
  3. Reaches Multiple Employees Simultaneously: Written formats like emails or memos allow managers to efficiently disseminate the same information to numerous employees at once, regardless of their location or immediate availability.
  4. Allows Recipient Time for Review: Gives employees time to read, digest, and refer back to the information at their own pace, which is particularly useful for detailed instructions or policy changes.

(d) Lateral vs. Diagonal Communication

  • Lateral (Horizontal) Communication: Communication flow between individuals or departments at the **same hierarchical level** within the organization (peers communicating with peers). Purpose: Coordination, information sharing, problem-solving among equals.
  • Diagonal Communication: Communication flow between individuals or departments at **different hierarchical levels and in different functional areas** (crossing both vertical and horizontal lines). Purpose: Often used in project teams or matrix structures for efficient cross-functional coordination and information sharing, bypassing strict chain of command.
*(See also Q8a from July 2016 paper)*

(e) Benefits of Effective Interpersonal Communication (Engineering Firm)

  1. Improved Teamwork and Collaboration: Clear communication, active listening, and constructive feedback among engineers and team members foster trust and understanding, leading to more effective collaboration on complex design projects and problem-solving.
  2. Reduced Errors and Misunderstandings: Precise communication regarding technical specifications, project requirements, and design changes minimizes the risk of errors, costly rework, and delays caused by misinterpretation.
  3. Enhanced Client Relationships: Strong interpersonal skills enable engineers to better understand client needs, explain technical concepts clearly, manage expectations, and build trust, leading to higher client satisfaction and repeat business.
  4. Better Knowledge Sharing: Open communication encourages engineers to share technical knowledge, best practices, and lessons learned, contributing to the overall expertise and innovation capacity of the firm.

(f) How ICT Promotes Effective Business Communication

  1. Speed and Efficiency: ICT tools like email, instant messaging, and VoIP enable rapid transmission of messages and documents across distances, speeding up decision-making and workflows compared to traditional methods.
  2. Wider Reach and Accessibility: Allows businesses to easily communicate with employees, customers, and partners globally through websites, email, video conferencing, and social media, overcoming geographical barriers.
  3. Enhanced Collaboration: Tools like shared drives (cloud storage), collaborative document editing (Google Workspace, MS 365), and project management software facilitate teamwork among dispersed individuals, allowing simultaneous access and contribution to shared information.
  4. Improved Record Keeping: Many ICT tools automatically create digital records of communication (emails, chat logs), which are easily stored, searched, and retrieved for reference or accountability.
*(See also Q12b from July 2016 paper)*

(g) Strategies to Enhance New Customer Loyalty

  1. Excellent Onboarding/First Experience: Ensure the customer's initial purchase or service experience is smooth, positive, and exceeds expectations. Provide clear instructions, follow up after the sale, and make them feel valued from the start.
  2. Personalized Follow-up Communication: Send a personalized welcome message or thank-you note. Offer relevant tips, resources, or follow-up information related to their purchase. Avoid generic mass communication immediately after the first interaction.
  3. Incentivize Repeat Business: Offer a discount on their next purchase, enrollment in a loyalty program with immediate benefits, or exclusive access to content/promotions as a thank you for their first purchase.

(a) The management of Sifa Engineering Works has noted reduced upward communication. Explain two measures that may be taken to address this situation. (4 marks)

Answer

This question is identical to Question 7 in Section B from the June/July 2021 paper. Please refer to the answer provided for Question 7 (Comm Skills) in the `2021j.html` file.

(a) Measures to Address Reduced Upward Communication

  1. Create Formal and Anonymous Feedback Channels: Implement structured ways for employees to provide feedback upwards, such as regular employee surveys focusing on specific issues, a formal suggestion scheme (physical box or online portal, allowing anonymity), or scheduled 'skip-level' meetings where employees can talk to managers above their direct supervisor. Guaranteeing anonymity can encourage more honest feedback from those hesitant to speak directly.
  2. Foster an Open and Trusting Culture with Management Training: Train managers and supervisors on the importance of upward communication, active listening skills, and how to be receptive to feedback (both positive and negative) without being defensive or punitive. Management must demonstrate through actions that feedback is valued and acted upon where appropriate.
  3. Regular Team Meetings with Feedback Sessions: Structure regular team meetings to include dedicated time for open discussion and feedback where employees are explicitly invited to share concerns, challenges, or suggestions related to work processes or the work environment.
*(Any two measures explained)*

(b) Steps in the Process of Communication (with Illustration)

The communication process describes how a message travels from a sender to a receiver.

1. Sender (Source): Has an idea/thought/feeling to convey.
2. Encoding: Translates the idea into symbols (words, gestures) forming the message.
3. Message: The encoded information being transmitted.
4. Channel: The medium through which the message travels (e.g., air, email, phone line).
5. Receiver (Destination): Receives the message via the channel.
6. Decoding: Interprets the symbols in the message to understand the meaning.
7. Feedback: Receiver's response sent back to the sender, indicating reception and understanding.

*(Noise: Interference that can distort the message at any stage)*

Question 9 (10 marks)

(a) Outline four functions served by the business description section of a business plan. (4 marks)

(b) Distinguish between limited liability and unlimited liability as used in business ownership. (4 marks)

(c) State two ways in which an entrepreneur may benefit from agency banking services. (2 marks)

Answer

This question is identical to Question 9 in Section C from the June/July 2021 paper. Please refer to the answer provided for Question 9 (Entrep) in the `2021j.html` file.

(a) Functions of the Business Description Section in a Business Plan

  1. Provides an Overview: Introduces the reader (e.g., investor, lender) to the fundamental nature of the business - what it is, what it does, and what industry it operates in.
  2. Defines the Business Concept: Clearly articulates the core business idea, the products or services offered, and the target market it aims to serve.
  3. States Mission and Vision: Outlines the business's purpose (mission) and its long-term aspirations (vision), providing context for its goals and strategies.
  4. Highlights Legal Structure and Status: Identifies the chosen legal form of the business (sole proprietorship, partnership, company) and its current status (start-up, existing).
  5. Summarizes Goals and Objectives: Briefly presents the key goals and objectives the business seeks to achieve.

(b) Limited Liability vs. Unlimited Liability

  • Unlimited Liability: The business owner(s) are **personally responsible** for all the debts and obligations incurred by the business. There is **no legal distinction** between the owner's personal assets and the business's assets. If the business cannot pay its debts, creditors can pursue the owner's personal assets (house, car, savings) to recover the amount owed. This applies to **Sole Proprietorships** and **General Partnerships**.
  • Limited Liability: The business is considered a **separate legal entity** from its owners. The owners' (shareholders' or members') financial liability for the business's debts is **limited** to the amount they have invested in the company (e.g., the value of their shares). Creditors generally cannot pursue the owners' personal assets to satisfy business debts. This protection is a key feature of **Limited Liability Companies (LLCs)** and **Corporations (Limited Companies)**.

(c) Benefits of Agency Banking Services for an Entrepreneur

Agency banking involves authorized agents (like local shopkeepers) providing basic banking services on behalf of a financial institution.

  1. Increased Accessibility and Convenience: Provides access to basic banking services (cash deposits, withdrawals, balance inquiries, bill payments) closer to the entrepreneur's business location, especially in rural or underserved areas far from traditional bank branches, saving time and travel costs.
  2. Extended Service Hours: Banking agents often operate for longer hours or during weekends compared to bank branches, offering greater flexibility for entrepreneurs to conduct transactions outside standard banking hours.
  3. Reduced Transaction Costs (Potentially): Transaction fees at agents might sometimes be lower than those at a bank branch or ATM for certain services, or reduced travel costs make it cheaper overall.

Question 11 (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Advantages of Sales Promotion

Sales promotion involves short-term incentives to encourage purchase (e.g., discounts, coupons, contests).

  1. Stimulates Quick Sales Boost: Provides immediate incentives that encourage quick purchase decisions, leading to a short-term increase in sales volume.
  2. Encourages Product Trial: Techniques like free samples or introductory offers can persuade potential customers to try a newly launched product.
  3. Attracts New Customers: Special offers can lure customers away from competitors or attract price-sensitive buyers.
  4. Creates Excitement and Urgency: Limited-time offers, contests, or special events can generate buzz and a sense of urgency among consumers.
  5. Supports Other Promotional Activities: Can be used effectively alongside advertising or personal selling to reinforce the marketing message and provide a direct call to action.

(b) Reasons for Business Failure During Start-up Phase

  1. Lack of Market Need / Poor Product-Market Fit: Offering a product/service that customers don't actually want or need.
  2. Running Out of Cash / Poor Financial Management: Insufficient capital, poor cash flow management, or inability to secure funding.
  3. Inadequate Management Team / Lack of Experience: Founders lacking essential business, financial, or industry-specific skills.
  4. Flawed Business Model: An unsustainable model where costs exceed potential revenues, or pricing is incorrect.
  5. Strong Competition: Failure to differentiate or compete effectively against established players.
  6. Poor Marketing and Sales Strategy: Inability to effectively reach and attract the target market or convert leads into sales.

(c) Innovation in Entrepreneurship

Innovation refers to the implementation of a new or significantly improved product (good or service), process, a new marketing method, or a new organizational method in business practices, workplace organization, or external relations. It's about creating value by introducing novelty or improving existing approaches, not just invention but successful application.

Question 12 (10 marks)

(a) Explain two purposes served by carrying out a pre-feasibility study of a business opportunity. (4 marks)

(b) State three ways in which a business organization may be socially responsible towards each of the following stakeholders:

(i) suppliers; (3 marks)

(ii) employees. (3 marks)

Answer

This question is identical to Question 12 in Section C from the June/July 2021 paper. Please refer to the answer provided for Question 12 (Entrep) in the `2021j.html` file.

(a) Purposes of a Pre-Feasibility Study

A pre-feasibility study is a preliminary analysis conducted before a full feasibility study.

  1. Initial Screening of Viability ("Go/No-Go" Decision): To quickly determine if a business opportunity has enough potential merit to warrant the significant resources required for a full feasibility study and business plan. It helps eliminate unpromising ideas early.
  2. Identifying Key Issues and Information Gaps: To highlight the critical factors, major risks, uncertainties, and areas where more detailed information and analysis are needed if the project proceeds to a full feasibility study. It helps define the scope of further investigation.
  3. Evaluating Broad Alternatives: To compare several different business concepts or project approaches at a high level to select the most promising option(s) for more detailed study.

(b) Social Responsibility Towards Stakeholders

  • (i) Towards Suppliers:
    1. Fair and Prompt Payment: Paying suppliers according to agreed terms and without unnecessary delays.
    2. Ethical Sourcing: Engaging in fair negotiations, respecting contracts, avoiding exploitation, and potentially considering suppliers' own social/environmental practices.
    3. Building Stable Relationships: Fostering long-term, mutually beneficial relationships based on trust and open communication.
  • (ii) Towards Employees:
    1. Fair Compensation and Benefits: Providing fair wages, timely payments, and adequate benefits (health, retirement, leave).
    2. Safe and Healthy Work Environment: Ensuring workplace safety, providing necessary training and equipment, and promoting well-being.
    3. Opportunities for Growth and Development: Offering training, skill development, and career advancement opportunities.
    4. Respectful and Fair Treatment: Ensuring non-discrimination, providing equal opportunities, and addressing grievances fairly.
