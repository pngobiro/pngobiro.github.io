Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Determine the fifth term of the binomial expansion of $(3 x+4 y)^{16}$ and evaluate its value at $x=\frac{1}{3}$ and $y=\frac{1}{4}$. (7 marks)

(b) Obtain the first three terms in the binomial expansion of $(8-x)^{\frac{1}{3}}$. State the range of $x$ for which the expansion is valid. (7 marks)

(c) The resonant frequency of a series circuit is given by $f=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathrm{LC}}}$, where L is inductance, C is capacitance. Use binomial theorem to determine the approximate change in $f$ if L increases by $1 \%$ and C decreases by $2 \%$. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves applying the binomial theorem for positive integer powers, fractional powers, and approximating percentage changes.

(a) Fifth term of $(3x+4y)^{16}$.

General term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$. Here $a=3x, b=4y, n=16$.

For the fifth term, $r+1=5 \implies r=4$.

$T_5 = \binom{16}{4} (3x)^{16-4} (4y)^4 = \binom{16}{4} (3x)^{12} (4y)^4$.

$\binom{16}{4} = \frac{16!}{4!(16-4)!} = \frac{16!}{4!12!} = \frac{16 \times 15 \times 14 \times 13}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = 4 \times 5 \times 7 \times 13 = 1820$.

$T_5 = 1820 (3^{12} x^{12}) (4^4 y^4) = 1820 (531441 x^{12}) (256 y^4)$.

$T_5 = 1820 \times 531441 \times 256 x^{12} y^4$.

$T_5 = 247697979520 x^{12} y^4$. (Calculation: $1820 \times 531441 \times 256$)

Evaluate when $x=1/3$ and $y=1/4$.

$T_5 = 247697979520 (1/3)^{12} (1/4)^4$.

$T_5 = 247697979520 \left(\frac{1}{531441}\right) \left(\frac{1}{256}\right)$.

$T_5 = \frac{247697979520}{531441 \times 256} = \frac{1820 \times 531441 \times 256}{531441 \times 256} = 1820$.

Alternatively, using the numbers from the calculation before coefficients:

$T_5 = 1820 (3x)^{12} (4y)^4 = 1820 (3 \cdot \frac{1}{3})^{12} (4 \cdot \frac{1}{4})^4$.

$T_5 = 1820 (1)^{12} (1)^4 = 1820 \times 1 \times 1 = 1820$.

(b) First three terms of $(8-x)^{1/3}$.

Factor out 8: $(8(1 - x/8))^{1/3} = 8^{1/3} (1 - x/8)^{1/3} = 2 (1 - x/8)^{1/3}$.

Use $(1+u)^k \approx 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2!}u^2$.

Here $u=-x/8, k=1/3$.

Expansion $\approx 2 \left[ 1 + (\frac{1}{3})(-\frac{x}{8}) + \frac{\frac{1}{3}(\frac{1}{3}-1)}{2}(-\frac{x}{8})^2 \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} + \frac{\frac{1}{3}(-\frac{2}{3})}{2}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} + \frac{-2/9}{2}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} - \frac{1}{9}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} - \frac{x^2}{576} \right]$.

$\approx 2 - \frac{2x}{24} - \frac{2x^2}{576} = 2 - \frac{x}{12} - \frac{x^2}{288}$.

Range of validity: $|u| < 1 \implies |-x/8| < 1 \implies |x|/8 < 1 \implies |x| < 8$. Valid for $-8 < x < 8$.

(c) Percentage change in $f = \frac{1}{2\pi} (LC)^{-1/2}$.

$f \propto L^{-1/2} C^{-1/2}$.

New L: $L' = L(1+0.01)$.

New C: $C' = C(1-0.02)$.

New frequency $f' \propto (L')^{-1/2} (C')^{-1/2}$.

$f' \propto [L(1.01)]^{-1/2} [C(0.98)]^{-1/2}$.

$f' \propto L^{-1/2} C^{-1/2} (1.01)^{-1/2} (0.98)^{-1/2}$.

$f' \approx f (1+0.01)^{-1/2} (1-0.02)^{-1/2}$.

Use binomial approximation $(1+x)^n \approx 1+nx$.

$(1+0.01)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(0.01) = 1 - 0.005 = 0.995$.

$(1-0.02)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(-0.02) = 1 + 0.01 = 1.01$.

$f' \approx f (0.995)(1.01)$.

$0.995 \times 1.01 = 0.995(1+0.01) = 0.995 + 0.00995 = 1.00495$.

$f' \approx f (1.00495)$.

Change = $f' - f \approx f(1.00495 - 1) = 0.00495 f$.

Percentage change = $\frac{f' - f}{f} \times 100\% \approx 0.00495 \times 100\% = 0.495\%$.

Approximate change is an increase of $0.5\%$.

Alternative using calculus: $\ln f = \ln(\frac{1}{2\pi}) - \frac{1}{2}\ln L - \frac{1}{2}\ln C$. $\frac{\delta f}{f} \approx -\frac{1}{2}\frac{\delta L}{L} - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\delta C}{C}$. $\% \text{ change in } f \approx -\frac{1}{2}(\% \text{ change in } L) - \frac{1}{2}(\% \text{ change in } C)$. $\approx -\frac{1}{2}(+1\%) - \frac{1}{2}(-2\%) = -0.5\% + 1.0\% = +0.5\%$.

Binomial theorem applied correctly for finding terms, expanding series, and estimating percentage changes.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are the roots of the equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$ where $a, b$ and $c$ are constants. Express in terms of $a, b$ and $c$.

(i) $\alpha^{2}+\beta^{2}$; (3 marks)

(ii) $\frac{1}{\alpha^{2}}+\frac{1}{\beta^{2}}$. (3 marks)

(b) Solve the equation by using formula method:

$\frac{1}{(x-3)}+\frac{4}{(x-1)}=2$. (6 marks)

(c) By applying Kirchoff's law to a d.c network, the following simultaneous equations are obtained:

$2 \mathrm{I}_{1}-3 \mathrm{I}_{2}+\mathrm{I}_{3}=4$

$3 \mathrm{I}_{1}+2 \mathrm{I}_{2}-2 \mathrm{I}_{3}=2$

$4 \mathrm{I}_{1}-\mathrm{I}_{2}+3 \mathrm{I}_{3}=16$

Use substitution method to determine the values of the currents, correct to 2 decimal places. (8 marks)

Answer

This question involves Vieta's formulas relating roots and coefficients, solving a rational equation leading to a quadratic, and solving a system of linear equations using substitution.

(a) Roots $\alpha, \beta$ of $ax^2+bx+c=0$.

From Vieta's formulas: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$ and $\alpha \beta = c/a$.

(i) Express $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$.

Use the identity $(\alpha + \beta)^2 = \alpha^2 + 2\alpha\beta + \beta^2$.

$\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$.

Substitute the formulas: $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (-b/a)^2 - 2(c/a) = \frac{b^2}{a^2} - \frac{2c}{a}$.

Combine over common denominator $a^2$: $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = \frac{b^2 - 2ac}{a^2}$.

(ii) Express $\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2}$.

Combine fractions: $\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} = \frac{\beta^2 + \alpha^2}{\alpha^2 \beta^2} = \frac{\alpha^2 + \beta^2}{(\alpha \beta)^2}$.

Substitute results from above:

$\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} = \frac{(b^2 - 2ac)/a^2}{(c/a)^2} = \frac{(b^2 - 2ac)/a^2}{c^2/a^2}$.

Simplify by canceling $a^2$: $\frac{b^2 - 2ac}{c^2}$.

(b) Solve $\frac{1}{(x-3)}+\frac{4}{(x-1)}=2$ using formula method.

Combine fractions on LHS. LCD is $(x-3)(x-1)$. Assume $x \neq 3, x \neq 1$.

$\frac{1(x-1) + 4(x-3)}{(x-3)(x-1)} = 2$.

$\frac{x - 1 + 4x - 12}{x^2 - x - 3x + 3} = 2$.

$\frac{5x - 13}{x^2 - 4x + 3} = 2$.

Multiply by denominator: $5x - 13 = 2(x^2 - 4x + 3)$.

$5x - 13 = 2x^2 - 8x + 6$.

Rearrange into standard quadratic form $ax^2+bx+c=0$:

$2x^2 - 8x - 5x + 6 + 13 = 0$.

$2x^2 - 13x + 19 = 0$.

Use quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ with $a=2, b=-13, c=19$.

$x = \frac{-(-13) \pm \sqrt{(-13)^2 - 4(2)(19)}}{2(2)}$.

$x = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{169 - 152}}{4} = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{17}}{4}$.

$\sqrt{17} \approx 4.123$.

$x_1 = \frac{13 + 4.123}{4} = \frac{17.123}{4} \approx 4.281$.

$x_2 = \frac{13 - 4.123}{4} = \frac{8.877}{4} \approx 2.219$.

Solutions: $x = \frac{13 + \sqrt{17}}{4} \approx 4.28$ and $x = \frac{13 - \sqrt{17}}{4} \approx 2.22$.

(c) Solve system using substitution.

(1) $2 I_{1}-3 I_{2}+I_{3}=4$

(2) $3 I_{1}+2 I_{2}-2 I_{3}=2$

(3) $4 I_{1}-I_{2}+3 I_{3}=16$

From (1), express $I_3$ in terms of $I_1, I_2$: $I_3 = 4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2$.

Substitute $I_3$ into (2):

$3I_1 + 2I_2 - 2(4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2) = 2$.

$3I_1 + 2I_2 - 8 + 4I_1 - 6I_2 = 2$.

$7I_1 - 4I_2 = 10 \quad (4)$.

Substitute $I_3$ into (3):

$4I_1 - I_2 + 3(4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2) = 16$.

$4I_1 - I_2 + 12 - 6I_1 + 9I_2 = 16$.

$-2I_1 + 8I_2 = 16 - 12 = 4$.

Divide by -2: $I_1 - 4I_2 = -2 \quad (5)$.

Now solve the system in $I_1, I_2$ using substitution:

(4) $7I_1 - 4I_2 = 10$

(5) $I_1 - 4I_2 = -2$

From (5), $I_1 = 4I_2 - 2$. Substitute into (4).

$7(4I_2 - 2) - 4I_2 = 10$.

$28I_2 - 14 - 4I_2 = 10$.

$24I_2 = 24$.

$I_2 = 1$.

Substitute $I_2=1$ back into $I_1 = 4I_2 - 2$.

$I_1 = 4(1) - 2 = 2$.

Substitute $I_1=2, I_2=1$ back into $I_3 = 4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2$.

$I_3 = 4 - 2(2) + 3(1) = 4 - 4 + 3 = 3$.

Solution: $I_1 = 2.00$ A, $I_2 = 1.00$ A, $I_3 = 3.00$ A.

Vieta's formulas applied correctly. Rational equation converted to quadratic and solved. Linear system solved systematically using substitution.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Simplify:

(i) $\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{y}\right) \div \frac{\left(y^{2}-x^{2}\right)}{x^{2} y^{2}}$ (4 marks)

(ii) $\quad \log_{\frac{1}{2}} 8+\log _{2}\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)+\log _{3}\left(\frac{1}{9}\right)$. (4 marks)

(b) Solve the equations:

(i) $5^{\left(5^{\log 10 x}\right)}+5^{(2-\log 10 x)}=30$; (Typo likely, maybe $5^{\log_{10} x} + ...$?) (6 marks)

(ii) $\quad \log _{4} x-2 \log _{x} 4=1$. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves algebraic fraction simplification, simplifying logarithmic expressions with different bases, and solving logarithmic equations (one complex, one involving change of base).

(a) Simplify.

(i) Simplify $\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{y}\right) \div \frac{\left(y^{2}-x^{2}\right)}{x^{2} y^{2}}$.

Simplify the first part: $\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{y} = \frac{y-x}{xy}$.

The expression is $\frac{y-x}{xy} \div \frac{y^2-x^2}{x^2 y^2}$.

Division is multiplication by the reciprocal:

$\frac{y-x}{xy} \times \frac{x^2 y^2}{y^2-x^2}$.

Factor $y^2-x^2 = (y-x)(y+x)$.

$= \frac{y-x}{xy} \times \frac{x^2 y^2}{(y-x)(y+x)}$.

Cancel common factors $xy$ and $(y-x)$ (assuming $x, y \neq 0$ and $y \neq x$):

$= \frac{1}{1} \times \frac{xy}{y+x} = \frac{xy}{x+y}$.

(ii) Simplify $\log_{\frac{1}{2}} 8+\log _{2}\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)+\log _{3}\left(\frac{1}{9}\right)$.

Evaluate each term:

Term 1: $\log_{1/2} 8$. Let $\log_{1/2} 8 = a$. Then $(1/2)^a = 8$. $(2^{-1})^a = 2^3 \implies 2^{-a} = 2^3 \implies -a = 3 \implies a = -3$.

Term 2: $\log_2(1/8) = \log_2(8^{-1}) = \log_2((2^3)^{-1}) = \log_2(2^{-3}) = -3$.

Term 3: $\log_3(1/9) = \log_3(9^{-1}) = \log_3((3^2)^{-1}) = \log_3(3^{-2}) = -2$.

Sum = $(-3) + (-3) + (-2) = -8$.

(b) Solve equations.

(i) Solve $5^{\left(5^{\log_{10} x}\right)}+5^{(2-\log_{10} x)}=30$.

The first term $5^{(5^{\log_{10} x})}$ seems unusually complex and likely a typo. A more standard form might be $5^{\log_{10} x}$ or perhaps $5 \cdot 5^{\log_{10} x}$. Let's assume the equation is $5^{\log_{10} x}+5^{(2-\log_{10} x)}=30$.

Rewrite the second term: $5^{(2-\log_{10} x)} = 5^2 \cdot 5^{-\log_{10} x} = 25 \cdot \frac{1}{5^{\log_{10} x}}$.

Let $y = 5^{\log_{10} x}$. The equation becomes $y + \frac{25}{y} = 30$.

Multiply by $y$ (note $y$ must be positive): $y^2 + 25 = 30y$.

$y^2 - 30y + 25 = 0$.

Factor? Need numbers multiplying to 25, adding to -30. No easy integers. Use quadratic formula.

$y = \frac{-(-30) \pm \sqrt{(-30)^2 - 4(1)(25)}}{2(1)} = \frac{30 \pm \sqrt{900 - 100}}{2} = \frac{30 \pm \sqrt{800}}{2}$.

$\sqrt{800} = \sqrt{400 \times 2} = 20\sqrt{2}$.

$y = \frac{30 \pm 20\sqrt{2}}{2} = 15 \pm 10\sqrt{2}$. Both are positive.

Substitute back $y = 5^{\log_{10} x}$.

Case 1: $5^{\log_{10} x} = 15 + 10\sqrt{2}$.

Take $\log_5$ of both sides: $\log_{10} x = \log_5(15 + 10\sqrt{2})$.

$x = 10^{\log_5(15 + 10\sqrt{2})}$. Use change of base: $\log_5 A = \frac{\log_{10} A}{\log_{10} 5}$.

$x = 10^{\frac{\log_{10}(15 + 10\sqrt{2})}{\log_{10} 5}}$.

$15+10\sqrt{2} \approx 15+14.14 = 29.14$. $\log_{10}(29.14) \approx 1.464$. $\log_{10} 5 \approx 0.699$.

Exponent $\approx 1.464 / 0.699 \approx 2.095$. $x \approx 10^{2.095} \approx 124.5$.

Case 2: $5^{\log_{10} x} = 15 - 10\sqrt{2}$.

$\log_{10} x = \log_5(15 - 10\sqrt{2})$.

$x = 10^{\log_5(15 - 10\sqrt{2})}$.

$15-10\sqrt{2} \approx 15-14.14 = 0.86$. $\log_{10}(0.86) \approx -0.0655$.

Exponent $\approx -0.0655 / 0.699 \approx -0.0937$. $x \approx 10^{-0.0937} \approx 0.806$.

Solutions (based on assumed equation): $x \approx 124.5$ and $x \approx 0.806$.

(ii) Solve $\log _{4} x-2 \log _{x} 4=1$.

Use change of base: $\log_x 4 = \frac{\log_4 4}{\log_4 x} = \frac{1}{\log_4 x}$.

Let $y = \log_4 x$. Equation is $y - 2(\frac{1}{y}) = 1$.

$y - \frac{2}{y} = 1$.

Multiply by $y$ (assuming $y \neq 0 \implies x \neq 1$): $y^2 - 2 = y$.

$y^2 - y - 2 = 0$.

Factor: $(y-2)(y+1) = 0$.

Solutions for y are $y=2$ or $y=-1$.

Substitute back $y = \log_4 x$.

Case 1: $\log_4 x = 2 \implies x = 4^2 = 16$.

Case 2: $\log_4 x = -1 \implies x = 4^{-1} = 1/4$.

Check domain: $x>0$ and $x \neq 1$. Both $x=16$ and $x=1/4$ are valid.

Solutions: $x=16$ and $x=1/4$.

Algebraic simplification and logarithm properties applied. Interpretation of likely typo needed for one equation. Logarithmic equation solved using change of base and substitution.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $\operatorname{Sin} \mathrm{A}=\frac{24}{25}$ and $\operatorname{Cos} \mathrm{B}=-\frac{5}{13}$ where A is acute and B is obtuse. Determine:

(i) $\quad \operatorname{Sin}(A-B)$; (4 marks)

(ii) $\operatorname{Cos}(\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B})$. (4 marks)

(b) Prove the identities:

(i) $\frac{1}{(1-\operatorname{Sin} \theta)}-\frac{1}{(1+\operatorname{Sin} \theta)}=2 \operatorname{Tan} \theta \operatorname{Sec} \theta$; (4 marks)

(ii) $\frac{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A+\operatorname{Sin} 6 A+\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A-\operatorname{Sin} 6 A-\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}=\operatorname{Cot}^{2} A$. (4 marks)

(c) Express the function:

(i) $7 \operatorname{Sin} \phi+4 \operatorname{Cos} \phi$ in the form $\mathrm{R} \operatorname{Cos}(\phi-\alpha)$ where $\mathrm{R}>0$ and $0 \leq \alpha \leq 90^{\circ}$. (2 marks)

(ii) Hence solve $7 \operatorname{Sin} \phi+4 \operatorname{Cos} \phi=\sqrt{65}$ for $0 \leq \phi \leq 360^{\circ} $. (2 marks)

Answer

This question involves compound angle formulas, proving trigonometric identities, and expressing trigonometric sums in R-formula form to solve equations.

(a) Given $\sin A = 24/25$ (A acute) and $\cos B = -5/13$ (B obtuse).

Find $\cos A$ and $\sin B$.

A acute (Q1): $\cos A > 0$. $\cos^2 A = 1 - \sin^2 A = 1 - (24/25)^2 = 1 - 576/625 = (625-576)/625 = 49/625$. $\cos A = \sqrt{49/625} = 7/25$.

B obtuse (Q2): $\sin B > 0$. $\sin^2 B = 1 - \cos^2 B = 1 - (-5/13)^2 = 1 - 25/169 = (169-25)/169 = 144/169$. $\sin B = \sqrt{144/169} = 12/13$.

(i) Find $\sin(A-B)$.

$\sin(A-B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$.

$= (24/25)(-5/13) - (7/25)(12/13)$.

$= -120/325 - 84/325 = -204/325$.

(ii) Find $\cos(A+B)$.

$\cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B$.

$= (7/25)(-5/13) - (24/25)(12/13)$.

$= -35/325 - 288/325 = -323/325$.

(b) Prove identities.

(i) Prove $\frac{1}{1-\sin \theta}-\frac{1}{1+\sin \theta}=2 \tan \theta \sec \theta$.

Combine LHS fractions: $\frac{(1+\sin\theta) - (1-\sin\theta)}{(1-\sin\theta)(1+\sin\theta)}$.

Numerator = $1+\sin\theta - 1+\sin\theta = 2\sin\theta$.

Denominator = $1 - \sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta$.

LHS = $\frac{2\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$.

Split the fraction: $\frac{2\sin\theta}{\cos\theta \cdot \cos\theta} = 2 \left(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right) \left(\frac{1}{\cos\theta}\right) = 2 \tan\theta \sec\theta$ = RHS. (Proven)

(ii) Prove $\frac{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A+\operatorname{Sin} 6 A+\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A-\operatorname{Sin} 6 A-\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}=\operatorname{Cot}^{2} A$.

Use sum-to-product: $\sin C + \sin D = 2\sin(\frac{C+D}{2})\cos(\frac{C-D}{2})$.

Numerator: $2\sin 4A + (\sin 6A + \sin 2A) = 2\sin 4A + 2\sin(\frac{6A+2A}{2})\cos(\frac{6A-2A}{2})$.

$= 2\sin 4A + 2\sin 4A \cos 2A = 2\sin 4A (1 + \cos 2A)$.

Use $1+\cos 2A = 2\cos^2 A$.

Numerator = $2\sin 4A (2\cos^2 A) = 4\sin 4A \cos^2 A$.

Denominator: $2\sin 4A - (\sin 6A + \sin 2A)$.

$= 2\sin 4A - (2\sin 4A \cos 2A) = 2\sin 4A (1 - \cos 2A)$.

Use $1-\cos 2A = 2\sin^2 A$.

Denominator = $2\sin 4A (2\sin^2 A) = 4\sin 4A \sin^2 A$.

Fraction = $\frac{4\sin 4A \cos^2 A}{4\sin 4A \sin^2 A}$. Cancel $4\sin 4A$ (assuming $\sin 4A \neq 0$).

$= \frac{\cos^2 A}{\sin^2 A} = \left(\frac{\cos A}{\sin A}\right)^2 = \cot^2 A$ = RHS. (Proven)

(c)

(i) Express $7 \sin \phi+4 \cos \phi$ as $R \cos(\phi-\alpha)$.

$R \cos(\phi-\alpha) = R(\cos\phi \cos\alpha + \sin\phi \sin\alpha) = (R\sin\alpha)\sin\phi + (R\cos\alpha)\cos\phi$.

Compare with $7\sin\phi + 4\cos\phi$.

$R \sin \alpha = 7$.

$R \cos \alpha = 4$.

$R^2 = 4^2 + 7^2 = 16 + 49 = 65 \implies R = \sqrt{65}$.

$\tan \alpha = \frac{R \sin \alpha}{R \cos \alpha} = \frac{7}{4} = 1.75$.

$\alpha = \arctan(1.75)$. Since $\sin \alpha, \cos \alpha$ positive, $\alpha$ is in Q1.

$\alpha \approx 60.26^{\circ}$.

So, $7 \sin \phi+4 \cos \phi = \sqrt{65} \cos(\phi - 60.26^{\circ})$.

(ii) Solve $7 \sin \phi+4 \cos \phi=\sqrt{65}$ for $0 \leq \phi \leq 360^{\circ} $.

Using part (i): $\sqrt{65} \cos(\phi - 60.26^{\circ}) = \sqrt{65}$.

$\cos(\phi - 60.26^{\circ}) = 1$.

Let $\beta = \phi - 60.26^{\circ}$. $\cos \beta = 1$.

The principal value is $\beta = 0^{\circ}$. The general solution is $\beta = n \cdot 360^{\circ}$.

So, $\phi - 60.26^{\circ} = n \cdot 360^{\circ}$.

$\phi = 60.26^{\circ} + n \cdot 360^{\circ}$.

For $n=0$: $\phi = 60.26^{\circ}$.

For $n=1$: $\phi = 60.26^{\circ} + 360^{\circ} = 420.26^{\circ}$ (outside range).

The only solution in the range $0 \le \phi \le 360^\circ$ is $\phi \approx 60.3^{\circ}$.

Compound angle formulas, trig identities (including sum-to-product), and R-formula method applied.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Given the complex numbers $Z_{1}=2+3 J, Z_{2}=4-3 j$ and $Z_{3}=1+4 j$, express $Z=\frac{Z_{1} Z_{2} Z_{3}}{Z_{1}+Z_{2}+Z_{3}}$ in the form $a+j b$. (8 marks)

(b) Find all the roots of the equation $Z^{3}-1-j \sqrt{3}=0$ in polar form. (6 marks)

(c) Given that $Z=-2-j$ is a root of the equation $Z^{4}+10 Z^{3}+39 Z^{2}+70 Z+50=0$ determine the other roots. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves complex number arithmetic (multiplication, addition, division) and finding roots of complex numbers and polynomial equations.

(a) Calculate $Z=\frac{Z_{1} Z_{2} Z_{3}}{Z_{1}+Z_{2}+Z_{3}}$.

Numerator: $N = Z_1 Z_2 Z_3$.

$Z_1 Z_2 = (2+3j)(4-3j) = 2(4)+2(-3j)+3j(4)+3j(-3j) = 8-6j+12j-9j^2 = 8+6j+9 = 17+6j$.

$N = (Z_1 Z_2) Z_3 = (17+6j)(1+4j) = 17(1)+17(4j)+6j(1)+6j(4j) = 17+68j+6j+24j^2 = 17+74j-24 = -7+74j$.

Denominator: $D = Z_1+Z_2+Z_3 = (2+3j) + (4-3j) + (1+4j)$.

$D = (2+4+1) + (3-3+4)j = 7 + 4j$.

Now calculate $Z = N/D = \frac{-7+74j}{7+4j}$.

Rationalize: $Z = \frac{(-7+74j)(7-4j)}{(7+4j)(7-4j)} = \frac{-7(7)+(-7)(-4j)+74j(7)+74j(-4j)}{7^2 - (4j)^2}$.

$Z = \frac{-49 + 28j + 518j - 296j^2}{49 - (-16)} = \frac{-49 + 546j - 296(-1)}{49 + 16} = \frac{-49 + 546j + 296}{65}$.

$Z = \frac{247 + 546j}{65}$.

$Z = \frac{247}{65} + j \frac{546}{65}$.

(b) Find roots of $Z^{3}-1-j \sqrt{3}=0$, i.e., $Z^3 = 1+j\sqrt{3}$.

Find cube roots of $w = 1+j\sqrt{3}$.

Convert $w$ to polar form $r(\cos\phi + j\sin\phi)$.

$r = |w| = \sqrt{1^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1+3} = \sqrt{4} = 2$.

$\phi = \arg(w)$. Real positive, Imaginary positive $\implies$ Q1.

$\tan \phi = \sqrt{3}/1 = \sqrt{3}$. $\phi = \pi/3$. Use $\phi = \pi/3 + 2k\pi$.

$w = 2(\cos(\pi/3) + j\sin(\pi/3))$.

Let $Z = R(\cos\Theta + j\sin\Theta)$. $Z^3 = R^3(\cos 3\Theta + j\sin 3\Theta)$.

$R^3 = 2 \implies R = \sqrt[3]{2}$.

$3\Theta = \pi/3 + 2k\pi \implies \Theta = \pi/9 + 2k\pi/3$.

Roots for $k=0, 1, 2$:

$k=0: \Theta_0 = \pi/9$. $z_0 = \sqrt[3]{2}(\cos(\pi/9) + j \sin(\pi/9))$.

$k=1: \Theta_1 = \pi/9 + 2\pi/3 = \pi/9 + 6\pi/9 = 7\pi/9$. $z_1 = \sqrt[3]{2}(\cos(7\pi/9) + j \sin(7\pi/9))$.

$k=2: \Theta_2 = \pi/9 + 4\pi/3 = \pi/9 + 12\pi/9 = 13\pi/9$. $z_2 = \sqrt[3]{2}(\cos(13\pi/9) + j \sin(13\pi/9))$.

The roots in polar form are $z_k = \sqrt[3]{2} \text{ cis}(\frac{\pi}{9} + \frac{2k\pi}{3})$ for $k=0, 1, 2$.

(c) Given $Z=-2-j$ is a root of $Z^{4}+10 Z^{3}+39 Z^{2}+70 Z+50=0$. Find other roots.

Coefficients are real, so the conjugate $Z_2 = -2+j$ is also a root.

Quadratic factor for these roots: $(Z - (-2-j))(Z - (-2+j))$.

$= ((Z+2)+j)((Z+2)-j) = (Z+2)^2 - j^2 = (Z^2+4Z+4) - (-1) = Z^2+4Z+5$.

Divide the polynomial by this factor using long division:

        Z^2   +6Z   +10
      ____________________
Z^2+4Z+5 | Z^4 + 10Z^3 + 39Z^2 + 70Z + 50
        -(Z^4 +  4Z^3 +  5Z^2)
        ____________________
               6Z^3 + 34Z^2 + 70Z
             -(6Z^3 + 24Z^2 + 30Z)
             ____________________
                     10Z^2 + 40Z + 50
                   -(10Z^2 + 40Z + 50)
                   _________________
                            0

The quotient is $Z^2+6Z+10$. Find roots of $Z^2+6Z+10=0$.

Use quadratic formula: $Z = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{6^2 - 4(1)(10)}}{2(1)}$.

$Z = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{36 - 40}}{2} = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} = \frac{-6 \pm 2j}{2}$.

$Z = -3 \pm j$.

The other roots are $Z_3 = -3+j$ and $Z_4 = -3-j$.

All roots: $-2-j, -2+j, -3+j, -3-j$.

Complex arithmetic performed correctly. Roots found using De Moivre's theorem and polynomial division based on conjugate root property.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $M \operatorname{Cosh} 3 x+N \operatorname{Sinh} 3 x \equiv 7 e^{3 x}+6 e^{-3 x}$ determine the values of M and N . (6 marks)

(b) (i) Prove that $\operatorname{Sinh}^{-1}(x)=\ln \left(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\right)$ (5 marks)

(ii) Hence evaluate $\operatorname{Sinh}^{-1}(3)$ to three decimal places. (2 marks)

(c) Show that:

(i) $\frac{\operatorname{Sinh} x}{(\operatorname{Cosh} x-1)}=\operatorname{Coth} x+\operatorname{Cosech} x$ (Typo? Maybe $\coth(x/2)$?) (4 marks)

(ii) $\tanh 2 x=\frac{2 \tanh x}{1+\tanh ^{2} x}$ (3 marks)

Answer

This question deals with hyperbolic functions, relating them to exponentials, finding logarithmic forms of inverse functions, and proving identities.

(a) Determine M and N.

Use definitions: $\cosh 3x = \frac{e^{3x}+e^{-3x}}{2}$, $\sinh 3x = \frac{e^{3x}-e^{-3x}}{2}$.

$M\left(\frac{e^{3x}+e^{-3x}}{2}\right) + N\left(\frac{e^{3x}-e^{-3x}}{2}\right) = 7e^{3x} + 6e^{-3x}$.

Multiply by 2: $M(e^{3x}+e^{-3x}) + N(e^{3x}-e^{-3x}) = 14e^{3x} + 12e^{-3x}$.

Group terms: $(M+N)e^{3x} + (M-N)e^{-3x} = 14e^{3x} + 12e^{-3x}$.

Equate coefficients:

$M+N = 14 \quad (1)$

$M-N = 12 \quad (2)$

Add (1) and (2): $2M = 26 \implies M=13$.

Substitute $M=13$ into (1): $13+N=14 \implies N=1$.

Values are $M=13, N=1$.

(b)

(i) Prove $\operatorname{Sinh}^{-1}(x)=\ln \left(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\right)$.

Let $y = \sinh^{-1} x$. Then $x = \sinh y = (e^y - e^{-y})/2$.

$2x = e^y - e^{-y}$. Multiply by $e^y$: $2xe^y = (e^y)^2 - 1$.

$(e^y)^2 - 2x e^y - 1 = 0$.

Let $u=e^y$. $u^2 - 2xu - 1 = 0$.

$u = \frac{-(-2x) \pm \sqrt{(-2x)^2 - 4(1)(-1)}}{2(1)} = \frac{2x \pm \sqrt{4x^2+4}}{2} = x \pm \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

Since $u=e^y > 0$, and $\sqrt{x^2+1}>|x|$, we must take the positive root: $e^y = x + \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

$y = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$. So $\sinh^{-1} x = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$. (Proven)

(ii) Evaluate $\sinh^{-1}(3)$.

$\sinh^{-1}(3) = \ln(3 + \sqrt{3^2+1}) = \ln(3 + \sqrt{10})$.

$\sqrt{10} \approx 3.162278$.

$\sinh^{-1}(3) \approx \ln(3 + 3.162278) = \ln(6.162278)$.

$\sinh^{-1}(3) \approx 1.818448$.

To three decimal places: $1.818$.

(c) Show identities.

(i) Show $\frac{\operatorname{Sinh} x}{(\operatorname{Cosh} x-1)}=\operatorname{Coth} x+\operatorname{Cosech} x$.

Let's check this identity. RHS = $\frac{\cosh x}{\sinh x} + \frac{1}{\sinh x} = \frac{\cosh x + 1}{\sinh x}$.

Use half-angle identities: $\cosh x + 1 = 2\cosh^2(x/2)$. $\sinh x = 2\sinh(x/2)\cosh(x/2)$.

RHS = $\frac{2\cosh^2(x/2)}{2\sinh(x/2)\cosh(x/2)} = \frac{\cosh(x/2)}{\sinh(x/2)} = \coth(x/2)$.

Now check LHS: $\frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x - 1}$. Use $\cosh x - 1 = 2\sinh^2(x/2)$.

LHS = $\frac{2\sinh(x/2)\cosh(x/2)}{2\sinh^2(x/2)} = \frac{\cosh(x/2)}{\sinh(x/2)} = \coth(x/2)$.

Since LHS = $\coth(x/2)$ and RHS = $\coth(x/2)$, the identity should be $\frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x - 1} = \coth(x/2)$. The identity as written in the question ($\frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x - 1} = \coth x + \text{csch} x$) appears incorrect.

Let's prove $\frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x - 1} = \coth(x/2)$.

LHS = $\frac{2\sinh(x/2)\cosh(x/2)}{2\sinh^2(x/2)} = \frac{\cosh(x/2)}{\sinh(x/2)} = \coth(x/2)$. (Proven)

(ii) Show $\tanh 2 x=\frac{2 \tanh x}{1+\tanh ^{2} x}$.

This is a standard identity, proven in Q1(a)(i) and Q8(a)(ii) of July 2015 paper. It follows from $\tanh(x+x)$.

M and N found using exponential definitions. Inverse sinh derived and evaluated. Hyperbolic identities proven (with correction noted for one).

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Find $\frac{d y}{d x}$ of $y=e^{x}$ from first principles. (5 marks)

(b) Given the following functions, find $\frac{d y}{d x}$

(i) $y=x^{2} \operatorname{Cos}^{3}(4 x)$ (5 marks)

(ii) $y=\ln \left(\frac{2 x}{3 x^{2}+4}\right)$ (5 marks)

(iii) $y=\frac{(x-1)^{2}}{x+4}$ (Not listed in OCR, assume typo or part missing?)

(c) A function $z=f(x, y)=e^{x y}$, show that: $\frac{1}{y} \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}+\frac{1}{x} \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=2 z$. (5 marks)

Answer

This question covers differentiation from first principles, standard differentiation rules (product, chain, quotient, logarithmic), and partial differentiation.

Note: Part (b)(iii) seems missing or cut off in the provided OCR text.

(a) Derivative of $y=e^x$ from first principles.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{x+h} - e^x}{h}$.

$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^x e^h - e^x}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^x (e^h - 1)}{h}$.

Since $e^x$ does not depend on $h$, we can take it out of the limit:

$\frac{dy}{dx} = e^x \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h}$.

Using the standard limit definition $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = e^x (1) = e^x$.

(b) Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$.

(i) $y=x^{2} \cos^{3}(4 x)$. Use product rule and chain rule.

Let $u=x^2$, $v=\cos^3(4x)$. $u'=2x$.

To find $v'$, let $w=\cos(4x)$, so $v=w^3$. $v' = \frac{dv}{dw}\frac{dw}{dx}$.

$\frac{dv}{dw} = 3w^2 = 3\cos^2(4x)$.

$\frac{dw}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(\cos(4x)) = -\sin(4x) \cdot 4 = -4\sin(4x)$.

$v' = (3\cos^2(4x))(-4\sin(4x)) = -12\cos^2(4x)\sin(4x)$.

Product rule: $y' = u'v + uv'$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = (2x)(\cos^3(4x)) + (x^2)(-12\cos^2(4x)\sin(4x))$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x\cos^3(4x) - 12x^2\cos^2(4x)\sin(4x)$.

Factor out common terms: $2x \cos^2(4x) [\cos(4x) - 6x \sin(4x)]$.

(ii) $y=\ln \left(\frac{2 x}{3 x^{2}+4}\right)$. Use log properties first.

$y = \ln(2x) - \ln(3x^2+4)$.

Differentiate: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(2x)) - \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(3x^2+4))$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{2x} \cdot (2) - \frac{1}{3x^2+4} \cdot (6x)$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x} - \frac{6x}{3x^2+4}$.

Combine fractions: $\frac{1(3x^2+4) - 6x(x)}{x(3x^2+4)} = \frac{3x^2+4 - 6x^2}{x(3x^2+4)} = \frac{4-3x^2}{x(3x^2+4)}$.

(iii) (Part missing from OCR)

If the function was $y=\frac{(x-1)^{2}}{x+4}$, use quotient rule.

$u=(x-1)^2 \implies u'=2(x-1)(1)=2(x-1)$.

$v=x+4 \implies v'=1$.

$y' = \frac{v u' - u v'}{v^2} = \frac{(x+4)(2(x-1)) - (x-1)^2(1)}{(x+4)^2}$.

$y' = \frac{2(x+4)(x-1) - (x-1)^2}{(x+4)^2}$. Factor out $(x-1)$.

$y' = \frac{(x-1)[2(x+4) - (x-1)]}{(x+4)^2} = \frac{(x-1)[2x+8 - x+1]}{(x+4)^2} = \frac{(x-1)(x+9)}{(x+4)^2}$.

(c) Show $\frac{1}{y} \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}+\frac{1}{x} \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=2 z$ for $z=e^{xy}$.

Partial derivatives:

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = e^{xy} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy) = e^{xy} \cdot y = y e^{xy} = yz$.

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = e^{xy} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy) = e^{xy} \cdot x = x e^{xy} = xz$.

Substitute into the LHS of the equation to show:

LHS = $\frac{1}{y} \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{x} \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$.

LHS = $\frac{1}{y} (yz) + \frac{1}{x} (xz)$.

LHS = $z + z = 2z$.

Since LHS = $2z$ and RHS = $2z$, the identity is shown.

Differentiation from first principles, product rule, chain rule, quotient rule, and partial differentiation applied correctly.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Evaluate the integrals:

(i) $\int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{12}} \tan ^{2}(3 x) d x$ (4 marks)

(ii) $\int \frac{x+15}{(x-2)(x+3)} d x$ (5 marks)

(iii) $\int_{1}^{4} \sqrt{5 x-4} d x$ (4 marks)

(b) (i) Sketch the region bounded by $y=x^{2}$ and $x=y^{2}$ and hence find the area. (4 marks)

(ii) Determine the mean value of the current function $I=50 \operatorname{Sin}(\omega t)$ over the interval $t=0$ and $t=\pi$ in terms of $\omega$ and $\pi$. (3 marks)

Answer

This question involves definite and indefinite integration using trigonometric identities, partial fractions, power rule, finding the area between curves, and calculating the mean value of a function.

(a) Evaluate integrals.

(i) $\int_{0}^{\pi/12} \tan ^{2}(3 x) d x$.

Use identity $\tan^2 A = \sec^2 A - 1$. Let $A=3x$.

Integral = $\int_{0}^{\pi/12} (\sec^2(3x) - 1) dx$.

Integrate: $\int \sec^2(ax) dx = \frac{1}{a}\tan(ax)$. $\int 1 dx = x$.

$= \left[ \frac{\tan(3x)}{3} - x \right]_0^{\pi/12}$.

$= \left( \frac{\tan(3\pi/12)}{3} - \frac{\pi}{12} \right) - \left( \frac{\tan(0)}{3} - 0 \right)$.

$= \left( \frac{\tan(\pi/4)}{3} - \frac{\pi}{12} \right) - (0 - 0)$.

$\tan(\pi/4) = 1$.

$= \frac{1}{3} - \frac{\pi}{12}$.

(ii) $\int \frac{x+15}{(x-2)(x+3)} d x$.

Partial fractions: $\frac{x+15}{(x-2)(x+3)} = \frac{A}{x-2} + \frac{B}{x+3}$.

$x+15 = A(x+3) + B(x-2)$.

Set $x=2$: $2+15 = A(2+3) + B(0) \implies 17 = 5A \implies A=17/5$.

Set $x=-3$: $-3+15 = A(0) + B(-3-2) \implies 12 = -5B \implies B=-12/5$.

Integral = $\int \left( \frac{17/5}{x-2} - \frac{12/5}{x+3} \right) dx$.

$= \frac{17}{5} \int \frac{1}{x-2} dx - \frac{12}{5} \int \frac{1}{x+3} dx$.

$= \frac{17}{5} \ln|x-2| - \frac{12}{5} \ln|x+3| + C$.

(iii) $\int_{1}^{4} \sqrt{5 x-4} d x$.

Let $u = 5x-4$. Then $du = 5 dx \implies dx = du/5$.

Change limits: When $x=1, u=5(1)-4=1$. When $x=4, u=5(4)-4=16$.

Integral = $\int_{1}^{16} \sqrt{u} (\frac{du}{5}) = \frac{1}{5} \int_{1}^{16} u^{1/2} du$.

$= \frac{1}{5} \left[ \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} \right]_1^{16} = \frac{1}{5} \left[ \frac{2}{3} u^{3/2} \right]_1^{16}$.

$= \frac{2}{15} [u^{3/2}]_1^{16} = \frac{2}{15} [16^{3/2} - 1^{3/2}]$.

$16^{3/2} = (16^{1/2})^3 = 4^3 = 64$. $1^{3/2} = 1$.

$= \frac{2}{15} (64 - 1) = \frac{2}{15} (63) = \frac{2 \times 21}{5} = \frac{42}{5}$ or $8.4$.

(b)

(i) Area bounded by $y=x^{2}$ and $x=y^{2}$ (or $y=\sqrt{x}$ for upper part).

Intersection points: Substitute $y=x^2$ into $x=y^2 \implies x=(x^2)^2 = x^4$.

$x^4 - x = 0 \implies x(x^3-1) = 0$. So $x=0$ or $x^3=1 \implies x=1$.

Points are $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$.

Sketch: $y=x^2$ is standard parabola. $x=y^2$ (or $y=\sqrt{x}$ for $y \ge 0$) is parabola opening right.

In the interval $[0, 1]$, the upper curve is $y=\sqrt{x}$ and the lower curve is $y=x^2$.

Area $A = \int_0^1 (\sqrt{x} - x^2) dx = \int_0^1 (x^{1/2} - x^2) dx$.

$A = \left[ \frac{x^{3/2}}{3/2} - \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^1 = \left[ \frac{2}{3}x^{3/2} - \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^1$.

$A = \left( \frac{2}{3}(1)^{3/2} - \frac{1^3}{3} \right) - (0 - 0) = \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$.

Area = $1/3$ square units.

(ii) Mean value of $I=50 \sin(\omega t)$ over $t=0$ to $t=\pi$.

Mean value $I_{mean} = \frac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b I(t) dt$.

$I_{mean} = \frac{1}{\pi - 0} \int_0^\pi 50 \sin(\omega t) dt = \frac{50}{\pi} \int_0^\pi \sin(\omega t) dt$.

Integrate: $\int \sin(\omega t) dt = -\frac{\cos(\omega t)}{\omega}$.

$I_{mean} = \frac{50}{\pi} \left[ -\frac{\cos(\omega t)}{\omega} \right]_0^\pi$.

$I_{mean} = \frac{50}{\pi \omega} [-\cos(\omega t)]_0^\pi$.

$I_{mean} = \frac{50}{\pi \omega} [ (-\cos(\omega \pi)) - (-\cos(0)) ]$.

$I_{mean} = \frac{50}{\pi \omega} [ 1 - \cos(\omega \pi) ]$.

Integrals evaluated using standard techniques. Area between curves calculated correctly. Mean value formula applied.
