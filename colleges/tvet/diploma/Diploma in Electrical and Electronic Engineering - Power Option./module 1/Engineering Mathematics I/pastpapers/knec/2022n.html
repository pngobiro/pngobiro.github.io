Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Determine the fifth term of the binomial expansion of $(3 x+4 y)^{16}$ and evaluate its value at $x=\frac{1}{3}$ and $y=\frac{1}{4}$. (7 marks)

(b) Obtain the first three terms in the binomial expansion of $(8-x)^{\frac{1}{3}}$. State the range of $x$ for which the expansion is valid. (7 marks)

(c) The resonant frequency of a series circuit is given by $f=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathrm{LC}}}$, where L is inductance, C is capacitance. Use binomial theorem to determine the approximate change in $f$ if L increases by $1 \%$ and C decreases by $2 \%$. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves applying the binomial theorem for positive integer powers, fractional powers, and approximating percentage changes.

(a) Fifth term of $(3x+4y)^{16}$.

The general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$. Here $a=3x, b=4y, n=16$.

For the fifth term, $r+1=5 \implies r=4$.

$T_5 = \binom{16}{4} (3x)^{16-4} (4y)^4 = \binom{16}{4} (3x)^{12} (4y)^4$.

$\binom{16}{4} = \frac{16!}{4!(16-4)!} = \frac{16!}{4!12!} = \frac{16 \times 15 \times 14 \times 13}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = 4 \times 5 \times 7 \times 13 = 1820$.

$T_5 = 1820 (3^{12} x^{12}) (4^4 y^4) = 1820 \times 3^{12} \times 4^4 x^{12} y^4$.

$T_5 = 1820 \times 531441 \times 256 x^{12} y^4 = 247697979520 x^{12} y^4$.

Evaluate when $x=1/3$ and $y=1/4$.

Value = $1820 (3 \cdot \frac{1}{3})^{12} (4 \cdot \frac{1}{4})^4 = 1820 (1)^{12} (1)^4 = 1820$.

(b) First three terms of $(8-x)^{1/3}$.

Factor out 8: $(8(1 - x/8))^{1/3} = 8^{1/3} (1 - x/8)^{1/3} = 2 (1 - x/8)^{1/3}$.

Use $(1+u)^k \approx 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2!}u^2$.

Here $u=-x/8, k=1/3$.

Expansion $\approx 2 \left[ 1 + (\frac{1}{3})(-\frac{x}{8}) + \frac{\frac{1}{3}(\frac{1}{3}-1)}{2}(-\frac{x}{8})^2 \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} + \frac{\frac{1}{3}(-\frac{2}{3})}{2}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} + \frac{-2/9}{2}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right]$.

$\approx 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} - \frac{1}{9}(\frac{x^2}{64}) \right] = 2 \left[ 1 - \frac{x}{24} - \frac{x^2}{576} \right]$.

$\approx 2 - \frac{2x}{24} - \frac{2x^2}{576} = 2 - \frac{x}{12} - \frac{x^2}{288}$.

Range of validity: $|u| < 1 \implies |-x/8| < 1 \implies |x|/8 < 1 \implies |x| < 8$. Valid for $-8 < x < 8$.

(c) Percentage change in $f = \frac{1}{2 \pi} (LC)^{-1/2}$.

$f \propto L^{-1/2} C^{-1/2}$.

New L: $L' = L(1+0.01)$. New C: $C' = C(1-0.02)$.

New frequency $f' \propto (L')^{-1/2} (C')^{-1/2} = [L(1.01)]^{-1/2} [C(0.98)]^{-1/2}$.

$f' \propto L^{-1/2} C^{-1/2} (1.01)^{-1/2} (0.98)^{-1/2}$.

$f' \approx f (1+0.01)^{-1/2} (1-0.02)^{-1/2}$.

Use $(1+x)^n \approx 1+nx$.

$(1+0.01)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(0.01) = 1 - 0.005 = 0.995$.

$(1-0.02)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(-0.02) = 1 + 0.01 = 1.01$.

$f' \approx f (0.995)(1.01) = f (1.00495)$.

Change = $f' - f \approx f(1.00495 - 1) = 0.00495 f$.

Percentage change $\approx 0.00495 \times 100\% = 0.495\% \approx 0.5\%$ increase.

Binomial theorem applied for finding specific term, series expansion, and approximation of percentage change.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are the roots of the equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$ where $a, b$ and $c$ are constants. Express in terms of $a, b$ and $c$.

(i) $\alpha^{2}+\beta^{2}$; (3 marks)

(ii) $\frac{1}{\alpha^{2}}+\frac{1}{\beta^{2}}$. (3 marks)

(b) Solve the equation by using formula method:

$\frac{1}{(x-3)}+\frac{4}{(x-1)}=2$. (6 marks)

(c) By applying Kirchoff's law to a d.c network, the following simultaneous equations are obtained:

$2 \mathrm{I}_{1}-3 \mathrm{I}_{2}+\mathrm{I}_{3}=4$

$3 \mathrm{I}_{1}+2 \mathrm{I}_{2}-2 \mathrm{I}_{3}=2$

$4 \mathrm{I}_{1}-\mathrm{I}_{2}+3 \mathrm{I}_{3}=16$

Use substitution method to determine the values of the currents, correct to 2 decimal places. (8 marks)

Answer

This question involves using Vieta's formulas for quadratic roots, solving a rational equation leading to a quadratic, and solving a system of linear equations using substitution.

(a) Roots $\alpha, \beta$ of $ax^2+bx+c=0$.

Known: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$, $\alpha \beta = c/a$.

(i) $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta = (-b/a)^2 - 2(c/a) = \frac{b^2}{a^2} - \frac{2c}{a} = \frac{b^2 - 2ac}{a^2}$.

(ii) $\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} = \frac{\beta^2 + \alpha^2}{\alpha^2 \beta^2} = \frac{\alpha^2 + \beta^2}{(\alpha \beta)^2}$.

Substitute results: $= \frac{(b^2 - 2ac)/a^2}{(c/a)^2} = \frac{(b^2 - 2ac)/a^2}{c^2/a^2} = \frac{b^2 - 2ac}{c^2}$.

(b) Solve $\frac{1}{x-3}+\frac{4}{x-1}=2$ using formula.

Combine fractions: $\frac{1(x-1) + 4(x-3)}{(x-3)(x-1)} = 2$.

$\frac{x-1+4x-12}{x^2-4x+3} = 2 \implies \frac{5x-13}{x^2-4x+3} = 2$.

$5x-13 = 2(x^2-4x+3) = 2x^2-8x+6$.

$2x^2 - 13x + 19 = 0$.

Use quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$.

$x = \frac{-(-13) \pm \sqrt{(-13)^2 - 4(2)(19)}}{2(2)} = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{169 - 152}}{4} = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{17}}{4}$.

Solutions: $x = \frac{13 + \sqrt{17}}{4} \approx 4.28$ and $x = \frac{13 - \sqrt{17}}{4} \approx 2.22$.

(c) Solve system using substitution.

(1) $2 I_{1}-3 I_{2}+I_{3}=4$

(2) $3 I_{1}+2 I_{2}-2 I_{3}=2$

(3) $4 I_{1}-I_{2}+3 I_{3}=16$

From (1), $I_3 = 4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2$. Substitute into (2) and (3).

Into (2): $3I_1 + 2I_2 - 2(4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2) = 2$.

$3I_1 + 2I_2 - 8 + 4I_1 - 6I_2 = 2 \implies 7I_1 - 4I_2 = 10 \quad (4)$.

Into (3): $4I_1 - I_2 + 3(4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2) = 16$.

$4I_1 - I_2 + 12 - 6I_1 + 9I_2 = 16 \implies -2I_1 + 8I_2 = 4$.

Divide by -2: $I_1 - 4I_2 = -2 \quad (5)$.

Solve (4) and (5). From (5), $I_1 = 4I_2 - 2$. Substitute into (4).

$7(4I_2 - 2) - 4I_2 = 10$.

$28I_2 - 14 - 4I_2 = 10 \implies 24I_2 = 24 \implies I_2 = 1$.

Substitute $I_2=1$ into $I_1 = 4I_2 - 2 \implies I_1 = 4(1) - 2 = 2$.

Substitute $I_1=2, I_2=1$ into $I_3 = 4 - 2I_1 + 3I_2 \implies I_3 = 4 - 2(2) + 3(1) = 4 - 4 + 3 = 3$.

Solution: $I_1 = 2.00$ A, $I_2 = 1.00$ A, $I_3 = 3.00$ A.

Vieta's formulas, quadratic formula, and substitution method for linear systems applied correctly.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Simplify:

(i) $\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{y}\right) \div \frac{\left(y^{2}-x^{2}\right)}{x^{2} y^{2}}$ (4 marks)

(ii) $\quad \log_{\frac{1}{2}} 8+\log _{2}\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)+\log _{3}\left(\frac{1}{9}\right)$. (4 marks)

(b) Solve the equations:

(i) $5^{(5^{\log_{10} x})}+5^{(2-\log_{10} x)}=30$; (Typo likely, assume $5^{\log_{10} x}$) (6 marks)

(ii) $\quad \log _{4} x-2 \log _{x} 4=1$. (6 marks)

Answer

This question tests simplification of algebraic fractions and logarithmic expressions, and solving logarithmic equations.

(a) Simplify.

(i) $\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{y}\right) \div \frac{y^{2}-x^{2}}{x^{2} y^{2}} = \left(\frac{y-x}{xy}\right) \times \frac{x^2 y^2}{y^2-x^2}$.

$= \frac{y-x}{xy} \times \frac{x^2 y^2}{(y-x)(y+x)} = \frac{xy}{x+y}$.

(ii) $\log_{1/2} 8 + \log_2(1/8) + \log_3(1/9)$.

$\log_{1/2} 8 = \log_{2^{-1}} 2^3 = \frac{3}{-1}\log_2 2 = -3$.

$\log_2(1/8) = \log_2(2^{-3}) = -3$.

$\log_3(1/9) = \log_3(3^{-2}) = -2$.

Sum = $-3 + (-3) + (-2) = -8$.

(b) Solve equations.

(i) Assuming $5^{\log_{10} x} + 5^{(2-\log_{10} x)} = 30$.

Let $y = 5^{\log_{10} x}$. The equation is $y + 5^2 \cdot 5^{-\log_{10} x} = 30 \implies y + \frac{25}{y} = 30$.

$y^2 + 25 = 30y \implies y^2 - 30y + 25 = 0$.

$y = \frac{30 \pm \sqrt{900 - 4(1)(25)}}{2} = \frac{30 \pm \sqrt{800}}{2} = 15 \pm 10\sqrt{2}$.

Case 1: $5^{\log_{10} x} = 15 + 10\sqrt{2}$. $\log_{10} x = \log_5(15+10\sqrt{2})$. $x = 10^{\log_5(15+10\sqrt{2})} \approx 124.5$.

Case 2: $5^{\log_{10} x} = 15 - 10\sqrt{2}$. $\log_{10} x = \log_5(15-10\sqrt{2})$. $x = 10^{\log_5(15-10\sqrt{2})} \approx 0.806$.

(ii) Solve $\log _{4} x-2 \log _{x} 4=1$.

Use $\log_x 4 = 1/\log_4 x$. Let $y = \log_4 x$.

$y - 2(1/y) = 1 \implies y^2 - 2 = y \implies y^2 - y - 2 = 0$.

$(y-2)(y+1) = 0$. $y=2$ or $y=-1$.

If $\log_4 x = 2$, $x = 4^2 = 16$.

If $\log_4 x = -1$, $x = 4^{-1} = 1/4$.

Solutions: $x=16, x=1/4$.

Simplification and equation solving performed, interpreting a likely typo in (b)(i).

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $\operatorname{Sin} \mathrm{A}=\frac{24}{25}$ and $\operatorname{Cos} \mathrm{B}=-\frac{5}{13}$ where A is acute and B is obtuse. Determine:

(i) $\quad \operatorname{Sin}(A-B)$; (4 marks)

(ii) $\operatorname{Cos}(\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B})$. (4 marks)

(b) Prove the identities:

(i) $\frac{1}{(1-\operatorname{Sin} \theta)}-\frac{1}{(1+\operatorname{Sin} \theta)}=2 \operatorname{Tan} \theta \operatorname{Sec} \theta$; (4 marks)

(ii) $\frac{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A+\operatorname{Sin} 6 A+\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}{2 \operatorname{Sin} 4 A-\operatorname{Sin} 6 A-\operatorname{Sin} 2 A}=\operatorname{Cot}^{2} A$. (4 marks)

(c) Express the function:

(i) $7 \operatorname{Sin} \phi+4 \operatorname{Cos} \phi$ in the form $\mathrm{R} \operatorname{Cos}(\phi-\alpha)$ where $\mathrm{R}>0$ and $0 \leq \alpha \leq 90^{\circ}$. (2 marks)

(ii) Hence solve $7 \operatorname{Sin} \phi+4 \operatorname{Cos} \phi=\sqrt{65}$ for $0 \leq \phi \leq 360^{\circ} $. (2 marks)

Answer

This question involves evaluating compound angle functions, proving trigonometric identities, and using the R-formula to solve equations.

(a) Given $\sin A = 24/25$ (A acute) and $\cos B = -5/13$ (B obtuse).

A acute (Q1): $\cos A = \sqrt{1-(24/25)^2} = \sqrt{49/625} = 7/25$.

B obtuse (Q2): $\sin B = \sqrt{1-(-5/13)^2} = \sqrt{144/169} = 12/13$.

(i) $\sin(A-B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B = (24/25)(-5/13) - (7/25)(12/13) = -120/325 - 84/325 = -204/325$.

(ii) $\cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B = (7/25)(-5/13) - (24/25)(12/13) = -35/325 - 288/325 = -323/325$.

(b) Prove identities.

(i) LHS = $\frac{(1+\sin\theta)-(1-\sin\theta)}{(1-\sin\theta)(1+\sin\theta)} = \frac{2\sin\theta}{1-\sin^2\theta} = \frac{2\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = 2\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\frac{1}{\cos\theta} = 2\tan\theta\sec\theta$. (Proven)

(ii) Numerator = $2\sin 4A + (\sin 6A + \sin 2A) = 2\sin 4A + 2\sin 4A \cos 2A = 2\sin 4A (1+\cos 2A)$.

Denominator = $2\sin 4A - (\sin 6A + \sin 2A) = 2\sin 4A - 2\sin 4A \cos 2A = 2\sin 4A (1-\cos 2A)$.

Fraction = $\frac{2\sin 4A (1+\cos 2A)}{2\sin 4A (1-\cos 2A)} = \frac{1+\cos 2A}{1-\cos 2A} = \frac{2\cos^2 A}{2\sin^2 A} = \cot^2 A$. (Proven)

(c)

(i) Express $7\sin\phi + 4\cos\phi$ as $R\cos(\phi-\alpha)$.

$R\cos(\phi-\alpha) = R\cos\phi\cos\alpha + R\sin\phi\sin\alpha$. Compare with $4\cos\phi + 7\sin\phi$.

$R\cos\alpha = 4$, $R\sin\alpha = 7$.

$R = \sqrt{4^2+7^2} = \sqrt{16+49} = \sqrt{65}$.

$\tan\alpha = 7/4 = 1.75$. $\alpha = \arctan(1.75) \approx 60.26^\circ$.

Result: $\sqrt{65} \cos(\phi - 60.26^\circ)$.

(ii) Solve $\sqrt{65} \cos(\phi - 60.26^\circ) = \sqrt{65}$.

$\cos(\phi - 60.26^\circ) = 1$.

Let $\beta = \phi - 60.26^\circ$. $\cos\beta = 1 \implies \beta = n \cdot 360^\circ$.

$\phi - 60.26^\circ = n \cdot 360^\circ \implies \phi = 60.26^\circ + n \cdot 360^\circ$.

For $0 \le \phi \le 360^\circ$, the only solution is $\phi \approx 60.3^\circ$ (for $n=0$).

Compound angles, trig identities, and R-formula applied successfully.

Question 5 (From OCR - Originally Q5 for Nov 2016) (20 marks)

(a) Find the inverse of the function $f(x)=\frac{3 x+2}{x-2}$. (4 marks)

(b) Given that $\mathrm{Ae}^{x}-\mathrm{Be}^{-x}=8 \cosh x-2 \sinh x$, find the values of A and B . (6 marks)

(c) Prove the hyperbolic identities:

(i) $\tanh (\theta-\phi)=\frac{\tanh \theta-\tanh \phi}{1-\tanh \theta \tanh \phi}$ (4 marks)

(ii) $\sinh ^{2} \theta+\cosh ^{2} \theta-1=\frac{1}{2} \tanh ^{4} \theta$ (Typo? Seems incorrect) (2 marks)

(d) Solve the equation $4 \cosh 2 x=4+2 \sinh 2 x$ giving the answer correct to three decimal places. (4 marks)

Answer

This question involves finding inverse functions, relating hyperbolic and exponential functions, proving identities, and solving hyperbolic equations.

Note: Assuming this question is from Nov 2016 paper as Q5 for Nov 2021 is missing in the provided OCR.

(a) Inverse of $f(x)=\frac{3x+2}{x-2}$.

Let $y = \frac{3x+2}{x-2}$. Swap: $x = \frac{3y+2}{y-2}$.

$x(y-2)=3y+2 \implies xy-2x=3y+2 \implies xy-3y=2x+2$.

$y(x-3)=2x+2 \implies y = \frac{2x+2}{x-3}$. $f^{-1}(x) = \frac{2x+2}{x-3}$.

(b) Find A and B.

RHS = $8(\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}) - 2(\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}) = 4(e^x+e^{-x}) - (e^x-e^{-x}) = 3e^x+5e^{-x}$.

Compare $Ae^x - Be^{-x} = 3e^x + 5e^{-x}$.

$A=3$. $-B=5 \implies B=-5$.

(c) Prove identities.

(i) $\tanh(\theta-\phi) = \tanh(\theta+(-\phi)) = \frac{\tanh\theta+\tanh(-\phi)}{1+\tanh\theta\tanh(-\phi)}$. Use $\tanh(-u)=-\tanh u$.

$= \frac{\tanh\theta-\tanh\phi}{1+\tanh\theta(-\tanh\phi)} = \frac{\tanh\theta-\tanh\phi}{1-\tanh\theta\tanh\phi}$. (Proven)

(ii) $\sinh^2\theta+\cosh^2\theta-1$. Use $\cosh^2\theta=1+\sinh^2\theta$.

LHS = $\sinh^2\theta + (1+\sinh^2\theta) - 1 = 2\sinh^2\theta$.

The identity $\sinh ^{2} \theta+\cosh ^{2} \theta-1=\frac{1}{2} \tanh ^{4} \theta$ is incorrect as $2\sinh^2\theta \neq \frac{1}{2}\tanh^4\theta$.

(d) Solve $4 \cosh 2 x=4+2 \sinh 2 x$.

$4(\frac{e^{2x}+e^{-2x}}{2}) = 4 + 2(\frac{e^{2x}-e^{-2x}}{2})$.

$2(e^{2x}+e^{-2x}) = 4 + (e^{2x}-e^{-2x})$.

$2e^{2x}+2e^{-2x} = 4 + e^{2x}-e^{-2x}$.

$e^{2x} + 3e^{-2x} - 4 = 0$. Multiply by $e^{2x}$.

$(e^{2x})^2 + 3 - 4e^{2x} = 0$. Let $y=e^{2x}$.

$y^2 - 4y + 3 = 0 \implies (y-1)(y-3)=0$.

$y=1$ or $y=3$.

$e^{2x}=1 \implies 2x=0 \implies x=0$.

$e^{2x}=3 \implies 2x=\ln 3 \implies x = \frac{1}{2}\ln 3 \approx 0.549$.

Solutions: $x=0.000, x \approx 0.549$.

Inverse function, hyperbolic relations, and equation solving performed correctly, noting typo in c(ii).

Question 6 (From OCR - Originally Q6 for Nov 2016) (20 marks)

(a) Given $y=\sin (2 x+3)$, find $\frac{d y}{d x}$ from first principles. (6 marks)

(b) Given that $y=\ln \left(\frac{1-x^{2}}{1+x^{2}}\right)$, show that $\frac{d y}{d x}=\frac{-4 x}{1-x^{4}}$. (6 marks)

(c) The power developed in a resistor R by a battery of emf E and internal resistance r is given by $P=\frac{E^{2} R}{(R+r)^{2}}$.

(i) find $\frac{d P}{d R}$; (4 marks)

(ii) show that the power is maximum when $\mathrm{R}=\mathrm{r}$. (4 marks)

Answer

This question covers differentiation from first principles, chain/log rules, quotient rule, and optimization.

Note: Assuming this question is from Nov 2016 paper as Q6 for Nov 2021 is missing in the provided OCR.

(a) Derivative of $y=\sin(2x+3)$ from first principles.

$y' = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\sin(2(x+h)+3) - \sin(2x+3)}{h}$.

Use $\sin A - \sin B = 2\cos(\frac{A+B}{2})\sin(\frac{A-B}{2})$.

$A=2x+2h+3, B=2x+3$. $\frac{A+B}{2}=2x+h+3$. $\frac{A-B}{2}=h$.

$y' = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{2\cos(2x+h+3)\sin(h)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} 2\cos(2x+h+3) \cdot \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h}$.

$y' = 2\cos(2x+3) \cdot 1 = 2\cos(2x+3)$.

(b) Show $y' = \frac{-4x}{1-x^4}$ for $y=\ln(\frac{1-x^2}{1+x^2})$.

$y = \ln(1-x^2) - \ln(1+x^2)$.

$y' = \frac{-2x}{1-x^2} - \frac{2x}{1+x^2} = -2x (\frac{1}{1-x^2} + \frac{1}{1+x^2})$.

$y' = -2x (\frac{1+x^2+1-x^2}{(1-x^2)(1+x^2)}) = -2x (\frac{2}{1-x^4}) = \frac{-4x}{1-x^4}$. (Shown)

(c) Power $P = \frac{E^2 R}{(R+r)^2}$.

(i) Find $dP/dR$. Use quotient rule.

$u=E^2 R \implies u'=E^2$. $v=(R+r)^2 \implies v'=2(R+r)$.

$\frac{dP}{dR} = \frac{v u' - u v'}{v^2} = \frac{(R+r)^2 E^2 - E^2 R (2(R+r))}{((R+r)^2)^2}$.

$= \frac{E^2(R+r)[(R+r) - 2R]}{(R+r)^4} = \frac{E^2(R+r)(r-R)}{(R+r)^4} = \frac{E^2(r-R)}{(R+r)^3}$.

(ii) Show max power when R=r.

Set $dP/dR = 0 \implies \frac{E^2(r-R)}{(R+r)^3} = 0$. Since $E \neq 0, R+r \neq 0$, this requires $r-R=0 \implies R=r$.

Check sign of $dP/dR$. If $R0 \implies dP/dR > 0$. If $R>r$, $r-R<0 \implies dP/dR < 0$. Derivative changes from +ve to -ve, confirming maximum at $R=r$.

Differentiation rules and optimization applied correctly.

Question 7 (From OCR - Originally Q7 for Nov 2016) (20 marks)

(a) If $Z=\frac{x}{y} \ln y$, show that $\left(\frac{1}{y \ln y}-\frac{1}{y}\right) \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial x \partial y}$. (6 marks)

(b) Given that the volume of a cone is $V=\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h$, use partial differentiation to determine the approximate change in volume if the radius increases from 5 cm to 6 cm , and the height decreases from 4 cm to 3.5 cm . (8 marks)

(c) Locate the stationary points of the function $\mathrm{Z}=2 x^{2}-3 y^{2}+8 x y-4 x+6 y+6$ and determine their nature. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves verifying relations with partial derivatives, approximating changes, and finding/classifying stationary points.

Note: Assuming this question is from Nov 2016 paper as Q7 for Nov 2021 is missing in the provided OCR.

(a) Verify relation for $Z=\frac{x}{y}\ln y$.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{y}\ln y$.

$\frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(\frac{\ln y}{y}) = \frac{y(1/y)-(\ln y)(1)}{y^2} = \frac{1-\ln y}{y^2}$.

LHS = $(\frac{1}{y\ln y} - \frac{1}{y})\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = (\frac{1-\ln y}{y\ln y})(\frac{\ln y}{y}) = \frac{1-\ln y}{y^2}$.

Since LHS = RHS, the relation is shown.

(b) Approximate change in $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$.

$r_0=5, h_0=4$. $\delta r = 6-5=1$. $\delta h = 3.5-4 = -0.5$.

$\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = \frac{2}{3}\pi r h$. $\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2$.

At (5,4): $\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = \frac{2}{3}\pi(5)(4) = \frac{40\pi}{3}$. $\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \frac{1}{3}\pi(5^2) = \frac{25\pi}{3}$.

$\delta V \approx \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\delta r + \frac{\partial V}{\partial h}\delta h = (\frac{40\pi}{3})(1) + (\frac{25\pi}{3})(-0.5)$.

$\delta V \approx \frac{40\pi}{3} - \frac{12.5\pi}{3} = \frac{27.5\pi}{3} \approx 28.8$ cm$^3$.

(c) Stationary points of $Z=2x^2-3y^2+8xy-4x+6y+6$.

$Z_x = 4x+8y-4$. $Z_y = -6y+8x+6$.

Set to 0: $4x+8y=4 \implies x+2y=1$. $8x-6y=-6 \implies 4x-3y=-3$.

From first eqn: $x=1-2y$. Substitute into second: $4(1-2y)-3y=-3$.

$4-8y-3y=-3 \implies 4-11y=-3 \implies 11y=7 \implies y=7/11$.

$x=1-2(7/11) = 1-14/11 = -3/11$.

Stationary point: $(-3/11, 7/11)$.

$Z_{xx}=4$. $Z_{yy}=-6$. $Z_{xy}=8$.

$D = Z_{xx}Z_{yy} - (Z_{xy})^2 = (4)(-6) - (8)^2 = -24 - 64 = -88$.

Since $D<0$, the point $(-3/11, 7/11)$ is a saddle point.

Partial derivatives, approximation, and stationary point classification performed correctly.

Question 8 (From OCR - Originally Q8 for Nov 2016) (20 marks)

(a) Evaluate the integrals:

(i) $\int(3 x-5)^{4} d x$ (3 marks)

(ii) $\int \sin ^{2} 3 x d x$ (4 marks)

(iii) $\int_{6}^{7} \frac{18+21 x-x^{2}}{(x-5)(x+2)^{2}} d x$ (8 marks)

(b) Figure 1 shows a sketch of the graph of the function $y=e^{x}$.

Determine the:

(i) area enclosed by the curve, the x -axis, the y -axis and the ordinates $x=3$. (2 marks)

(ii) centroid of the area in (i) above. (3 marks)

Answer

This question covers integration techniques and applications (area, centroid).

Note: Assuming this question is from Nov 2016 paper as Q8 for Nov 2021 is missing in the provided OCR.

(a) Evaluate integrals.

(i) $\int(3x-5)^4 dx$. Let $u=3x-5, du=3dx$.

$\int u^4 (du/3) = \frac{1}{3}\frac{u^5}{5}+C = \frac{(3x-5)^5}{15}+C$.

(ii) $\int \sin^2(3x) dx$. Use $\sin^2 A = (1-\cos 2A)/2$. $A=3x, 2A=6x$.

$\int \frac{1-\cos(6x)}{2} dx = \frac{1}{2}(x - \frac{\sin(6x)}{6}) + C = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{\sin(6x)}{12} + C$.

(iii) $\int_{6}^{7} \frac{18+21 x-x^{2}}{(x-5)(x+2)^{2}} d x$. Partial fractions: $\frac{A}{x-5}+\frac{B}{x+2}+\frac{C}{(x+2)^2}$.

$18+21x-x^2 = A(x+2)^2 + B(x-5)(x+2) + C(x-5)$.

$x=5 \implies 18+105-25 = A(49) \implies 98=49A \implies A=2$.

$x=-2 \implies 18-42-4 = C(-7) \implies -28=-7C \implies C=4$.

Coeff of $x^2$: $-1 = A+B \implies -1=2+B \implies B=-3$.

Integral = $\int_6^7 (\frac{2}{x-5}-\frac{3}{x+2}+\frac{4}{(x+2)^2}) dx$.

$= [2\ln|x-5|-3\ln|x+2|-4(x+2)^{-1}]_6^7$.

$= (2\ln 2 - 3\ln 9 - 4/9) - (2\ln 1 - 3\ln 8 - 4/8)$.

$= (2\ln 2 - 6\ln 3 - 4/9) - (0 - 9\ln 2 - 1/2)$.

$= 11\ln 2 - 6\ln 3 + 1/18 \approx 1.088$.

(b) Area and centroid under $y=e^x$ from $x=0$ to $x=3$.

(i) Area $A = \int_0^3 e^x dx = [e^x]_0^3 = e^3 - e^0 = e^3 - 1$.

(ii) Centroid $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{A}\int_0^3 x y dx = \frac{1}{e^3-1}\int_0^3 x e^x dx$.

Integrate $xe^x$ by parts: $[xe^x - e^x]_0^3 = (3e^3-e^3)-(0-e^0) = 2e^3+1$.

$\bar{x} = \frac{2e^3+1}{e^3-1}$.

$\bar{y} = \frac{1}{A}\int_0^3 \frac{1}{2}y^2 dx = \frac{1}{2A}\int_0^3 (e^x)^2 dx = \frac{1}{2(e^3-1)}\int_0^3 e^{2x} dx$.

$\int_0^3 e^{2x} dx = [\frac{e^{2x}}{2}]_0^3 = \frac{e^6-e^0}{2} = \frac{e^6-1}{2}$.

$\bar{y} = \frac{1}{2(e^3-1)} \frac{e^6-1}{2} = \frac{e^6-1}{4(e^3-1)} = \frac{(e^3-1)(e^3+1)}{4(e^3-1)} = \frac{e^3+1}{4}$.

Centroid: $(\frac{2e^3+1}{e^3-1}, \frac{e^3+1}{4})$.

Integrals evaluated, area and centroid calculated correctly.
