Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Solve the following equation for all values of $\theta$ between $0^{\circ}$ and $360^{\circ}$.

$2 \operatorname{Sin} \theta-3 \operatorname{Cos} \theta=2$. (6 marks)

(b) Solve for x in the following equations:

(i) $\quad \log _{3}(2 x-3)=-1$; (3 marks)

(ii) $3^{2 x}=4\left(3^{x}\right)+3$. (5 marks)

(c) Find the minimum and maximum ordinates of the curve $y=x^{2}(x+1)$ and classify them. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves solving trigonometric equations using the R-formula method, solving logarithmic and exponential equations (one reducible to quadratic), and finding and classifying turning points using calculus.

(a) Solve $2 \sin \theta - 3 \cos \theta = 2$ for $0^{\circ} \leq \theta \leq 360^{\circ}$.

Express the LHS in the form $R \sin(\theta - \alpha)$, where $R > 0$.

$R \sin(\theta - \alpha) = R(\sin \theta \cos \alpha - \cos \theta \sin \alpha) = (R \cos \alpha) \sin \theta - (R \sin \alpha) \cos \theta$.

Comparing with $2 \sin \theta - 3 \cos \theta$, we have:

$R \cos \alpha = 2$

$R \sin \alpha = 3$

Square and add: $R^2 \cos^2 \alpha + R^2 \sin^2 \alpha = 2^2 + 3^2$.

$R^2 (\cos^2 \alpha + \sin^2 \alpha) = 4 + 9 = 13$.

$R^2 = 13 \implies R = \sqrt{13}$ (since $R > 0$).

Divide the equations: $\frac{R \sin \alpha}{R \cos \alpha} = \frac{3}{2} \implies \tan \alpha = 1.5$.

$\alpha = \arctan(1.5)$. Since $\sin \alpha$ and $\cos \alpha$ are positive, $\alpha$ is in the first quadrant.

$\alpha \approx 56.31^{\circ}$.

The equation becomes $\sqrt{13} \sin(\theta - 56.31^{\circ}) = 2$.

$\sin(\theta - 56.31^{\circ}) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{13}} \approx 0.5547$.

Let $\phi = \theta - 56.31^{\circ}$. Then $\sin \phi = 0.5547$.

The principal value (acute angle) for $\phi$ is $\arcsin(0.5547) \approx 33.69^{\circ}$.

Since $\sin \phi$ is positive, solutions for $\phi$ are in the 1st and 2nd quadrants.

1st Quadrant: $\phi_1 = 33.69^{\circ}$.

2nd Quadrant: $\phi_2 = 180^{\circ} - 33.69^{\circ} = 146.31^{\circ}$.

Now find $\theta = \phi + 56.31^{\circ}$.

$\theta_1 = 33.69^{\circ} + 56.31^{\circ} = 90^{\circ}$.

$\theta_2 = 146.31^{\circ} + 56.31^{\circ} = 202.62^{\circ}$.

Check the range $0^{\circ} \leq \theta \leq 360^{\circ}$. Both solutions are within the range.

Solutions: $\theta = 90^{\circ}, 202.6^{\circ}$ (approx).

(b)

(i) Solve $\log _{3}(2 x-3)=-1$.

Convert to exponential form: $2x - 3 = 3^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}$.

$2x = 3 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{9+1}{3} = \frac{10}{3}$.

$x = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{10}{3} = \frac{5}{3}$.

Check domain: $2x-3 > 0$. $2(5/3) - 3 = 10/3 - 9/3 = 1/3 > 0$. Solution is valid.

$x = 5/3$.

(ii) Solve $3^{2 x}=4\left(3^{x}\right)+3$.

Rewrite as $(3^x)^2 = 4(3^x) + 3$.

Let $y = 3^x$. The equation is $y^2 = 4y + 3$.

Rearrange: $y^2 - 4y - 3 = 0$.

This doesn't factor easily. Use the quadratic formula: $y = \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(1)(-3)}}{2(1)}$.

$y = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 12}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{28}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{4 \times 7}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2\sqrt{7}}{2} = 2 \pm \sqrt{7}$.

Substitute back $y = 3^x$.

Case 1: $3^x = 2 + \sqrt{7}$. Since $2+\sqrt{7}$ is positive, a real solution exists.

$x \ln 3 = \ln(2+\sqrt{7})$.

$x = \frac{\ln(2+\sqrt{7})}{\ln 3} \approx \frac{\ln(2+2.6458)}{\ln 3} = \frac{\ln(4.6458)}{1.0986} \approx \frac{1.5359}{1.0986} \approx 1.398$.

Case 2: $3^x = 2 - \sqrt{7}$. Since $\sqrt{7} \approx 2.6458$, $2-\sqrt{7}$ is negative. $3^x$ cannot be negative, so there is no real solution in this case.

Solution: $x = \frac{\ln(2+\sqrt{7})}{\ln 3} \approx 1.398$.

(c) Min/Max ordinates of $y=x^{2}(x+1) = x^3 + x^2$.

Find the first derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 2x$.

Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ for stationary points: $3x^2 + 2x = 0 \implies x(3x+2) = 0$.

Stationary points occur at $x=0$ and $x=-2/3$.

Find the corresponding y-values (ordinates):

$y(0) = 0^3 + 0^2 = 0$. Point $(0, 0)$.

$y(-2/3) = (-2/3)^3 + (-2/3)^2 = -8/27 + 4/9 = -8/27 + 12/27 = 4/27$. Point $(-2/3, 4/27)$.

Find the second derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x + 2$.

Classify the points:

At $x=0$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(0) + 2 = 2$. Since $y'' > 0$, this is a local minimum.

At $x=-2/3$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(-2/3) + 2 = -4 + 2 = -2$. Since $y'' < 0$, this is a local maximum.

The minimum ordinate is $y=0$ (at $x=0$).

The maximum ordinate is $y=4/27$ (at $x=-2/3$).

Solutions involve standard techniques for trig equations, logs, exponentials, and finding/classifying turning points.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Expand $\left(1+\frac{2}{x}\right)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ upto the term in $x^{3}$. (Mistake in question, likely meant $1/x^3$). (6 marks)

(ii) Use the series above to determine $(1.02 )^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ correct to 4 decimal places. (Mistake in question, doesn't relate to (i)). (4 marks)

(b) Given that $x^{2}+3 x y-y^{2}=3$, find $\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}$ at the point $(1,1)$. (6 marks)

(c) The height $S$ metres of a mass thrown vertically upwards at time $t$ seconds is given by $\mathrm{S}=90 \mathrm{t}-17 \mathrm{t}^{2}$. Determine how long the mass will take after being thrown to reach a height of 60 metres on the ascent and descent. (4 marks)

Answer

This question deals with binomial expansion (with apparent errors in the question statement), implicit differentiation (second derivative), and basic kinematics.

(a) Binomial Expansion

There are inconsistencies in part (a). (i) Expanding $(1 + 2/x)^{-1/2}$ is valid for $|2/x| < 1$, i.e., $|x| > 2$. The expansion will be in terms of $1/x$, $1/x^2$, $1/x^3$, etc. The question asks for the term in $x^3$, which won't appear in this expansion. Let's assume it meant expand up to the term in $(1/x)^3$. (ii) Evaluating $(1.02)^{-1/2}$ requires an expansion of $(1+u)^k$ where $u$ is small. This doesn't match the form in (i). Let's solve (ii) independently assuming it meant expand $(1+0.02)^{-1/2}$.

(a)(i) (Assuming expansion up to $(1/x)^3$)

Expand $(1 + \frac{2}{x})^{-1/2}$ using $(1+u)^k = 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2!}u^2 + \frac{k(k-1)(k-2)}{3!}u^3 + \dots$

Here $u = 2/x$ and $k = -1/2$.

$(1 + \frac{2}{x})^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(\frac{2}{x}) + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2}-1)}{2}(\frac{2}{x})^2 + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2}-1)(-\frac{1}{2}-2)}{6}(\frac{2}{x})^3$

$\approx 1 - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})}{2}(\frac{4}{x^2}) + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})(-\frac{5}{2})}{6}(\frac{8}{x^3})$

$\approx 1 - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{3/4}{2}(\frac{4}{x^2}) + \frac{-15/8}{6}(\frac{8}{x^3})$

$\approx 1 - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{3}{8}(\frac{4}{x^2}) - \frac{15}{48}(\frac{8}{x^3})$

$\approx 1 - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{3}{2x^2} - \frac{15}{6x^3} = 1 - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{3}{2x^2} - \frac{5}{2x^3}$.

(a)(ii) (Assuming evaluation of $(1.02)^{-1/2}$)

$(1.02)^{-1/2} = (1+0.02)^{-1/2}$. Use the expansion $(1+u)^k$ with $u=0.02, k=-1/2$.

$(1+0.02)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2}u^2 + \frac{k(k-1)(k-2)}{6}u^3$

Term 1: $1$.

Term 2: $ku = (-\frac{1}{2})(0.02) = -0.01$.

Term 3: $\frac{k(k-1)}{2}u^2 = \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})}{2}(0.02)^2 = \frac{3/4}{2}(0.0004) = \frac{3}{8}(0.0004) = 3(0.00005) = 0.00015$.

Term 4: $\frac{k(k-1)(k-2)}{6}u^3 = \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})(-\frac{5}{2})}{6}(0.02)^3 = \frac{-15/8}{6}(0.000008) = -\frac{15}{48}(8 \times 10^{-6}) = -\frac{5}{16}(8 \times 10^{-6}) = -2.5 \times 10^{-6} = -0.0000025$.

$(1.02)^{-1/2} \approx 1 - 0.01 + 0.00015 - 0.0000025 = 0.9901475$.

Correct to 4 decimal places: $0.9901$.

(b) Find $\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}$ for $x^{2}+3 x y-y^{2}=3$ at $(1,1)$.

First find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ by implicit differentiation:

$2x + (3y + 3x \frac{dy}{dx}) - 2y \frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.

$(3x - 2y)\frac{dy}{dx} = -2x - 3y$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-2x - 3y}{3x - 2y} = \frac{2x + 3y}{2y - 3x}$. Let $y' = \frac{dy}{dx}$.

Evaluate $y'$ at $(1,1)$: $y'(1,1) = \frac{2(1)+3(1)}{2(1)-3(1)} = \frac{5}{-1} = -5$.

Now find $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = y''$ using the quotient rule on $y' = \frac{2x + 3y}{2y - 3x}$:

$y'' = \frac{(2y-3x)\frac{d}{dx}(2x+3y) - (2x+3y)\frac{d}{dx}(2y-3x)}{(2y-3x)^2}$.

$\frac{d}{dx}(2x+3y) = 2 + 3\frac{dy}{dx} = 2 + 3y'$.

$\frac{d}{dx}(2y-3x) = 2\frac{dy}{dx} - 3 = 2y' - 3$.

$y'' = \frac{(2y-3x)(2+3y') - (2x+3y)(2y'-3)}{(2y-3x)^2}$.

Evaluate $y''$ at $(1,1)$, using $x=1, y=1, y'=-5$:

$y''(1,1) = \frac{(2(1)-3(1))(2+3(-5)) - (2(1)+3(1))(2(-5)-3)}{(2(1)-3(1))^2}$.

$y''(1,1) = \frac{(-1)(2-15) - (5)(-10-3)}{(-1)^2}$.

$y''(1,1) = \frac{(-1)(-13) - (5)(-13)}{1} = \frac{13 - (-65)}{1} = 13 + 65 = 78$.

$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ at $(1,1)$ is 78.

(c) Time to reach $S=60$m for $S=90t - 17t^2$.

Set $S=60$: $60 = 90t - 17t^2$.

Rearrange into standard quadratic form: $17t^2 - 90t + 60 = 0$.

Use the quadratic formula: $t = \frac{-(-90) \pm \sqrt{(-90)^2 - 4(17)(60)}}{2(17)}$.

$t = \frac{90 \pm \sqrt{8100 - 4080}}{34} = \frac{90 \pm \sqrt{4020}}{34}$.

$\sqrt{4020} \approx 63.40$.

$t = \frac{90 \pm 63.40}{34}$.

Two solutions:

$t_1 = \frac{90 - 63.40}{34} = \frac{26.60}{34} \approx 0.782$ seconds (Ascent).

$t_2 = \frac{90 + 63.40}{34} = \frac{153.40}{34} \approx 4.512$ seconds (Descent).

The mass reaches 60m at approximately 0.78s (on the way up) and 4.51s (on the way down).

Careful application of binomial theorem, implicit differentiation rules, and kinematic equations is required. Note the likely errors in the phrasing of part (a).

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Determine how many ways in which committees can be formed from a set of 5 governors and 7 senators if each committee contains 3 governors and 4 senators. (4 marks)

(b) Evaluate $\int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \operatorname{Sin}^{2} x \operatorname{Cos}^{3} x dx$. (8 marks)

(c) Determine $\int \frac{11-3 x}{x^{2}+2 x-3} dx$. (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers combinations and definite/indefinite integration techniques, including trigonometric integrals and partial fractions.

(a) Committee Formation.

The number of ways to choose 3 governors from 5 is $\binom{5}{3}$.

$\binom{5}{3} = \frac{5!}{3!(5-3)!} = \frac{5!}{3!2!} = \frac{5 \times 4}{2 \times 1} = 10$.

The number of ways to choose 4 senators from 7 is $\binom{7}{4}$.

$\binom{7}{4} = \frac{7!}{4!(7-4)!} = \frac{7!}{4!3!} = \frac{7 \times 6 \times 5}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = 7 \times 5 = 35$.

The total number of ways to form the committee is the product of the number of ways to choose governors and senators:

Total ways = $\binom{5}{3} \times \binom{7}{4} = 10 \times 35 = 350$.

(b) Evaluate $\int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \sin^{2} x \cos^{3} x dx$.

The power of cosine is odd. Save one factor of $\cos x$ and convert the rest to sines using $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$.

$\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sin^2 x \cos^2 x \cos x dx = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sin^2 x (1 - \sin^2 x) \cos x dx$.

Use substitution: Let $u = \sin x$. Then $du = \cos x dx$.

Change limits of integration:

When $x = 0$, $u = \sin 0 = 0$.

When $x = \pi/2$, $u = \sin(\pi/2) = 1$.

The integral becomes: $\int_{0}^{1} u^2 (1 - u^2) du = \int_{0}^{1} (u^2 - u^4) du$.

Integrate with respect to $u$: $\left[ \frac{u^3}{3} - \frac{u^5}{5} \right]_0^1$.

Evaluate: $\left( \frac{1^3}{3} - \frac{1^5}{5} \right) - \left( \frac{0^3}{3} - \frac{0^5}{5} \right) = \left( \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5} \right) - 0$.

$= \frac{5 - 3}{15} = \frac{2}{15}$.

(c) Determine $\int \frac{11-3 x}{x^{2}+2 x-3} dx$.

Factor the denominator: $x^2 + 2x - 3 = (x+3)(x-1)$.

Use partial fraction decomposition:

$\frac{11-3x}{(x+3)(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x+3} + \frac{B}{x-1}$.

$11 - 3x = A(x-1) + B(x+3)$.

Set $x=1$: $11 - 3(1) = A(0) + B(1+3) \implies 8 = 4B \implies B = 2$.

Set $x=-3$: $11 - 3(-3) = A(-3-1) + B(0) \implies 11 + 9 = -4A \implies 20 = -4A \implies A = -5$.

The integral becomes: $\int \left( \frac{-5}{x+3} + \frac{2}{x-1} \right) dx$.

$= -5 \int \frac{1}{x+3} dx + 2 \int \frac{1}{x-1} dx$.

$= -5 \ln|x+3| + 2 \ln|x-1| + C$.

$= \ln\left(\frac{(x-1)^2}{|x+3|^5}\right) + C$.

Combinations are calculated using binomial coefficients. Integration requires appropriate substitution for trig integrals and partial fractions for rational functions.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Given that $Z=\frac{1}{2+j 3}+\frac{1}{1-\mathrm{j} 2}$, express $Z$ in the form $a+j b$. (5 marks)

(ii) Express $\mathrm{Z}=(2-\mathrm{j} 7)$ in polar form. (3 marks)

(b) If $\mathrm{Z}=5 x^{4}+2 x^{3} y^{2}-3 y$, determine:

(i) $\frac{\partial \mathrm{z}}{\partial \chi}$ (2 marks)

(ii) $\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$. (2 marks)

(c) Show that if $Z=\frac{x}{y} \ln y$, then $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}=\frac{x \partial^{2} Z}{\partial y \partial x}$. (Mistake in question? Should likely be $y \frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = ...$ or similar relation) (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers complex number arithmetic (addition of reciprocals, conversion to polar form) and partial differentiation.

(a) Complex Numbers.

(i) Express $Z=\frac{1}{2+j 3}+\frac{1}{1-\mathrm{j} 2}$ in the form $a+j b$.

Rationalize each term:

$\frac{1}{2+3j} = \frac{1(2-3j)}{(2+3j)(2-3j)} = \frac{2-3j}{2^2 - (3j)^2} = \frac{2-3j}{4 - (-9)} = \frac{2-3j}{13}$.

$\frac{1}{1-2j} = \frac{1(1+2j)}{(1-2j)(1+2j)} = \frac{1+2j}{1^2 - (2j)^2} = \frac{1+2j}{1 - (-4)} = \frac{1+2j}{5}$.

Add the terms:

$Z = \frac{2-3j}{13} + \frac{1+2j}{5} = \frac{5(2-3j) + 13(1+2j)}{13 \times 5}$.

$Z = \frac{10 - 15j + 13 + 26j}{65} = \frac{(10+13) + (-15+26)j}{65} = \frac{23 + 11j}{65}$.

$Z = \frac{23}{65} + j \frac{11}{65}$.

(ii) Express $Z = 2 - 7j$ in polar form $r(\cos \theta + j \sin \theta)$ or $re^{j\theta}$.

$r = |Z| = \sqrt{2^2 + (-7)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 49} = \sqrt{53}$.

$\theta = \arg(Z)$. Since the real part is positive and the imaginary part is negative, Z is in the 4th quadrant.

The reference angle $\alpha = \arctan\left|\frac{-7}{2}\right| = \arctan(3.5)$.

$\alpha \approx 74.05^{\circ}$.

The angle $\theta$ in the 4th quadrant is $360^{\circ} - \alpha$ or $-\alpha$.

$\theta \approx -74.05^{\circ}$ or $285.95^{\circ}$.

In radians: $\theta \approx -1.2925$ radians.

Polar form: $Z = \sqrt{53} (\cos(-74.05^{\circ}) + j \sin(-74.05^{\circ}))$ or $Z = \sqrt{53} e^{-j 1.2925}$.

$Z \approx 7.28 (\cos(-74.1^{\circ}) + j \sin(-74.1^{\circ}))$.

(b) Partial Derivatives of $Z=5 x^{4}+2 x^{3} y^{2}-3 y$.

(i) $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x}$: Treat y as a constant.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(5x^4) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x^3 y^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3y)$.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = 20x^3 + (6x^2)y^2 - 0 = 20x^3 + 6x^2y^2$.

(ii) $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}$: Treat x as a constant.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(5x^4) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2x^3 y^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3y)$.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = 0 + 2x^3 (2y) - 3 = 4x^3y - 3$.

(c) Show that if $Z=\frac{x}{y} \ln y$, then $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}=\frac{x \partial^{2} Z}{\partial y \partial x}$.

The identity as stated seems unlikely to be correct. Let's calculate the derivatives involved and see if a relationship emerges, possibly involving $y \frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}$.

$Z = x y^{-1} \ln y$.

Calculate $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}$ using the product rule $(uv)' = u'v + uv'$ with $u=y^{-1}$ and $v=\ln y$.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = x \left[ (-1 y^{-2})(\ln y) + (y^{-1})(\frac{1}{y}) \right]$.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = x \left[ -\frac{\ln y}{y^2} + \frac{1}{y^2} \right] = \frac{x(1 - \ln y)}{y^2}$.

Calculate $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x}$: Treat y as constant.

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{y} \ln y$.

Calculate $\frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\ln y}{y}\right)$.

Use quotient rule: $\frac{v u' - u v'}{v^2}$ with $u=\ln y, v=y$.

$\frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{y(1/y) - (\ln y)(1)}{y^2} = \frac{1 - \ln y}{y^2}$.

Now compare $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y}$ and $\frac{x \partial^{2} Z}{\partial y \partial x}$:

$\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = \frac{x(1 - \ln y)}{y^2}$.

$x \frac{\partial^{2} Z}{\partial y \partial x} = x \left(\frac{1 - \ln y}{y^2}\right)$.

Yes, the identity $\frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = x \frac{\partial^{2} Z}{\partial y \partial x}$ holds true for this function.

(Proven).

Complex number arithmetic, polar conversion, and partial differentiation rules are applied correctly. The identity in part (c) was verified.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Determine by integration the area bounded by the three straight lines $\mathrm{y}=4-x, \mathrm{y}=3 x$ and $3 \mathrm{y}=x$. (8 marks)

(b) The curve $y=x^{2}+4$ is rotated one revolution about the x -axis between the limits $x=1$ and $x=4$. Determine the volume of solid of revolution. (6 marks)

(c) Find the derivative of the function $\mathrm{y}=2 x^{2}$ by first principles. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves finding the area of a region bounded by lines using integration, calculating the volume of revolution using the disk method, and differentiation from first principles.

(a) Area bounded by $y=4-x$, $y=3x$, and $y=x/3$.

First, find the vertices of the triangle formed by these lines.

Vertex 1: Intersection of $y=3x$ and $y=x/3$.

$3x = x/3 \implies 9x = x \implies 8x = 0 \implies x=0$. If $x=0$, $y=0$. Vertex is $(0,0)$.

Vertex 2: Intersection of $y=3x$ and $y=4-x$.

$3x = 4-x \implies 4x = 4 \implies x=1$. If $x=1$, $y=3(1)=3$. Vertex is $(1,3)$.

Vertex 3: Intersection of $y=x/3$ and $y=4-x$.

$x/3 = 4-x \implies x = 12 - 3x \implies 4x = 12 \implies x=3$. If $x=3$, $y=3/3=1$. Vertex is $(3,1)$.

The vertices are $(0,0)$, $(1,3)$, and $(3,1)$.

Sketch the region. It's a triangle. We can integrate with respect to x. We need to split the integral at $x=1$.

Area $A = \int_{0}^{1} (\text{Upper Line} - \text{Lower Line}) dx + \int_{1}^{3} (\text{Upper Line} - \text{Lower Line}) dx$.

Region 1 ($0 \le x \le 1$): Upper line is $y=3x$. Lower line is $y=x/3$.

Region 2 ($1 \le x \le 3$): Upper line is $y=4-x$. Lower line is $y=x/3$.

$A = \int_{0}^{1} (3x - \frac{x}{3}) dx + \int_{1}^{3} (4-x - \frac{x}{3}) dx$.

$A = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{8x}{3} dx + \int_{1}^{3} (4 - \frac{4x}{3}) dx$.

Integrate:

Part 1: $\left[ \frac{8}{3} \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_0^1 = \left[ \frac{4x^2}{3} \right]_0^1 = \frac{4(1)^2}{3} - 0 = \frac{4}{3}$.

Part 2: $\left[ 4x - \frac{4}{3} \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_1^3 = \left[ 4x - \frac{2x^2}{3} \right]_1^3$.

$= \left( 4(3) - \frac{2(3)^2}{3} \right) - \left( 4(1) - \frac{2(1)^2}{3} \right)$.

$= \left( 12 - \frac{18}{3} \right) - \left( 4 - \frac{2}{3} \right) = (12 - 6) - (\frac{12-2}{3}) = 6 - \frac{10}{3} = \frac{18-10}{3} = \frac{8}{3}$.

Total Area $A = \frac{4}{3} + \frac{8}{3} = \frac{12}{3} = 4$ square units.

(b) Volume of revolution for $y=x^{2}+4$ about x-axis, $x=1$ to $x=4$.

Using the disk method, Volume $V = \int_{a}^{b} \pi [y(x)]^2 dx$.

$V = \int_{1}^{4} \pi (x^2+4)^2 dx = \pi \int_{1}^{4} (x^4 + 8x^2 + 16) dx$.

Integrate: $V = \pi \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} + \frac{8x^3}{3} + 16x \right]_1^4$.

$V = \pi \left[ \left(\frac{4^5}{5} + \frac{8(4^3)}{3} + 16(4)\right) - \left(\frac{1^5}{5} + \frac{8(1^3)}{3} + 16(1)\right) \right]$.

$V = \pi \left[ \left(\frac{1024}{5} + \frac{8(64)}{3} + 64\right) - \left(\frac{1}{5} + \frac{8}{3} + 16\right) \right]$.

$V = \pi \left[ \left(\frac{1024}{5} + \frac{512}{3} + 64\right) - \left(\frac{1}{5} + \frac{8}{3} + 16\right) \right]$.

Group terms with common denominators:

$V = \pi \left[ \left(\frac{1024-1}{5}\right) + \left(\frac{512-8}{3}\right) + (64-16) \right]$.

$V = \pi \left[ \frac{1023}{5} + \frac{504}{3} + 48 \right]$.

$V = \pi \left[ \frac{1023}{5} + 168 + 48 \right] = \pi \left[ \frac{1023}{5} + 216 \right]$.

$V = \pi \left[ \frac{1023 + 5(216)}{5} \right] = \pi \left[ \frac{1023 + 1080}{5} \right] = \frac{2103 \pi}{5}$.

$V \approx \frac{2103 \times 3.14159}{5} \approx 1321.3$ cubic units.

(c) Derivative of $y=2x^2$ from first principles.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.

$f(x) = 2x^2$.

$f(x+h) = 2(x+h)^2 = 2(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) = 2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2) - (2x^2)}{h}$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h}$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h(4x + 2h)}{h}$.

Assuming $h \neq 0$, cancel $h$: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} (4x + 2h)$.

Evaluate the limit: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x + 2(0) = 4x$.

Area calculation requires finding intersection points and setting up correct integral bounds. Volume calculation uses the disk method. First principles differentiation involves the limit definition.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $(a+b)+j(a-b)=9+j 10$, find the values of $a$ and $b$. (4 marks)

(b) Find the equation of the tangent to the curve $\mathrm{y}=\chi^{2}-\chi-2$ at the point $(1,-2)$. (6 marks)

(c) A rectangular sheet of metal having dimensions 20 cm by 12 cm has squares removed from each of the four corners and the sides bent upwards to form an open box. Determine the maximum possible volume of the box. (10 marks)

Answer

This question involves equating real and imaginary parts of complex numbers, finding the equation of a tangent using differentiation, and an optimization problem involving finding the maximum volume.

(a) Equating complex numbers.

Given $(a+b)+j(a-b)=9+j 10$.

Equate the real parts: $a+b = 9 \quad (1)$.

Equate the imaginary parts: $a-b = 10 \quad (2)$.

Solve the simultaneous equations.

Add (1) and (2): $(a+b) + (a-b) = 9 + 10 \implies 2a = 19 \implies a = 19/2 = 9.5$.

Substitute $a=9.5$ into (1): $9.5 + b = 9 \implies b = 9 - 9.5 = -0.5$.

Values are $a = 9.5$ and $b = -0.5$.

(b) Equation of the tangent to $y=x^{2}-x-2$ at $(1,-2)$.

First, find the slope of the tangent by differentiating $y$ with respect to $x$.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x - 1$.

Evaluate the slope at the point $x=1$.

Slope $m = \frac{dy}{dx} \bigg|_{x=1} = 2(1) - 1 = 1$.

Use the point-slope form of a line: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.

With point $(1, -2)$ and slope $m=1$:

$y - (-2) = 1(x - 1)$.

$y + 2 = x - 1$.

Rearrange to standard form: $y = x - 3$ or $x - y - 3 = 0$.

(c) Maximum volume of an open box.

Let the side length of the square removed from each corner be $x$ cm.

The dimensions of the sheet are 20 cm by 12 cm.

After removing squares and folding, the dimensions of the base of the box will be:

Length $L = 20 - 2x$.

Width $W = 12 - 2x$.

The height of the box will be $H = x$.

Constraints: $x$ must be positive. Also, the dimensions must be positive: $20-2x > 0 \implies x < 10$ and $12-2x > 0 \implies x < 6$. So, the valid range for $x$ is $0 < x < 6$.

The volume $V$ of the box is $V = L \times W \times H$.

$V(x) = (20 - 2x)(12 - 2x)(x)$.

$V(x) = (240 - 40x - 24x + 4x^2)(x) = (240 - 64x + 4x^2)(x)$.

$V(x) = 4x^3 - 64x^2 + 240x$.

To find the maximum volume, find the derivative $V'(x)$ and set it to zero.

$V'(x) = \frac{dV}{dx} = 12x^2 - 128x + 240$.

Set $V'(x) = 0$: $12x^2 - 128x + 240 = 0$.

Divide by 4: $3x^2 - 32x + 60 = 0$.

Use the quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-(-32) \pm \sqrt{(-32)^2 - 4(3)(60)}}{2(3)}$.

$x = \frac{32 \pm \sqrt{1024 - 720}}{6} = \frac{32 \pm \sqrt{304}}{6}$.

$\sqrt{304} = \sqrt{16 \times 19} = 4\sqrt{19}$.

$x = \frac{32 \pm 4\sqrt{19}}{6} = \frac{16 \pm 2\sqrt{19}}{3}$.

Approximate values: $\sqrt{19} \approx 4.359$.

$x_1 = \frac{16 + 2(4.359)}{3} = \frac{16 + 8.718}{3} = \frac{24.718}{3} \approx 8.24$.

$x_2 = \frac{16 - 2(4.359)}{3} = \frac{16 - 8.718}{3} = \frac{7.282}{3} \approx 2.43$.

Check the valid range $0 < x < 6$. Only $x \approx 2.43$ is valid.

Use the second derivative test to confirm it's a maximum.

$V''(x) = 24x - 128$.

$V''(2.43) = 24(2.43) - 128 = 58.32 - 128 = -69.68$.

Since $V''(x) < 0$, this value of $x$ corresponds to a local maximum volume.

The value of $x$ that maximizes volume is $x = \frac{16 - 2\sqrt{19}}{3}$ cm.

Calculate the maximum volume using this $x \approx 2.43$ cm.

$L = 20 - 2(2.43) = 20 - 4.86 = 15.14$ cm.

$W = 12 - 2(2.43) = 12 - 4.86 = 7.14$ cm.

$H = 2.43$ cm.

$V_{max} \approx 15.14 \times 7.14 \times 2.43 \approx 262.7$ cm$^3$.

Alternatively, use the exact value of x in $V(x) = 4x^3 - 64x^2 + 240x$. This is more complex.

Let's use $x=2.427$ for better precision.

$V(2.427) = 4(2.427)^3 - 64(2.427)^2 + 240(2.427)$

$V \approx 4(14.30) - 64(5.89) + 240(2.427)$

$V \approx 57.2 - 377.0 + 582.5 \approx 262.7$ cm$^3$.

Problems involve algebraic manipulation of complex numbers, standard calculus for tangents, and setting up and solving an optimization problem using derivatives.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) The area A of a triangle is given by $\mathrm{A}=\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{ac} \operatorname{Sin} \mathrm{B}$, where B is the angle between sides a and c . If a is increasing at 0.4 units/s, c is decreasing at 0.8 units/s and B is increasing at 0.2 units/s (assume radians/s), find the rate of change of the area of the triangle correct to 3 significant figures, when a is 3 units, c is 4 units and B is $\frac{\pi}{6}$ radians. (10 marks)

(b) Find the stationary points of the surface $Z=x^{3}-x y+y^{3}$ and distinguish between them. (10 marks)

Answer

This question involves related rates using the chain rule for multivariable functions (or total differential) and finding and classifying stationary points of a function of two variables.

(a) Related Rates for Area $A = \frac{1}{2} ac \sin B$.

We are given:

  • $\frac{da}{dt} = +0.4$ units/s
  • $\frac{dc}{dt} = -0.8$ units/s
  • $\frac{dB}{dt} = +0.2$ rad/s

We want to find $\frac{dA}{dt}$ when $a=3$, $c=4$, $B=\pi/6$.

Use the chain rule for multivariable functions (total derivative with respect to time t):

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{\partial A}{\partial a}\frac{da}{dt} + \frac{\partial A}{\partial c}\frac{dc}{dt} + \frac{\partial A}{\partial B}\frac{dB}{dt}$.

Calculate the partial derivatives of $A = \frac{1}{2} ac \sin B$:

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial a} = \frac{1}{2} (1) c \sin B = \frac{1}{2} c \sin B$.

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial c} = \frac{1}{2} a (1) \sin B = \frac{1}{2} a \sin B$.

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial B} = \frac{1}{2} ac \cos B$.

Evaluate the partial derivatives at the given instant ($a=3, c=4, B=\pi/6$):

$\sin B = \sin(\pi/6) = 1/2$.

$\cos B = \cos(\pi/6) = \sqrt{3}/2$.

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial a} = \frac{1}{2} (4) (\frac{1}{2}) = 1$.

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial c} = \frac{1}{2} (3) (\frac{1}{2}) = \frac{3}{4}$.

$\frac{\partial A}{\partial B} = \frac{1}{2} (3)(4) (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) = 6 (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) = 3\sqrt{3}$.

Now substitute the partial derivatives and the given rates into the chain rule formula:

$\frac{dA}{dt} = (1)(0.4) + (\frac{3}{4})(-0.8) + (3\sqrt{3})(0.2)$.

$\frac{dA}{dt} = 0.4 - (0.75)(0.8) + 0.6\sqrt{3}$.

$\frac{dA}{dt} = 0.4 - 0.6 + 0.6\sqrt{3}$.

$\frac{dA}{dt} = -0.2 + 0.6\sqrt{3}$.

$\sqrt{3} \approx 1.732$.

$\frac{dA}{dt} \approx -0.2 + 0.6(1.732) = -0.2 + 1.0392 = 0.8392$.

The rate of change of the area is approximately $0.839$ square units/s (correct to 3 significant figures).

(b) Stationary points of $Z=x^{3}-x y+y^{3}$.

Find first partial derivatives:

$Z_x = \frac{\partial Z}{\partial x} = 3x^2 - y$.

$Z_y = \frac{\partial Z}{\partial y} = -x + 3y^2$.

Set derivatives to zero to find stationary points:

$3x^2 - y = 0 \quad (1)$

$-x + 3y^2 = 0 \quad (2)$

From (1), $y = 3x^2$. Substitute this into (2):

$-x + 3(3x^2)^2 = 0$.

$-x + 3(9x^4) = 0$.

$-x + 27x^4 = 0$.

$x(27x^3 - 1) = 0$.

This gives two possibilities for $x$:

Case 1: $x=0$. Substitute into $y=3x^2$: $y = 3(0)^2 = 0$. Point is $(0,0)$.

Case 2: $27x^3 - 1 = 0 \implies 27x^3 = 1 \implies x^3 = 1/27 \implies x = 1/3$. Substitute into $y=3x^2$: $y = 3(1/3)^2 = 3(1/9) = 1/3$. Point is $(1/3, 1/3)$.

The stationary points are $(0,0)$ and $(1/3, 1/3)$.

Find second partial derivatives to classify them:

$Z_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial x^2} = 6x$.

$Z_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial y^2} = 6y$.

$Z_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial x \partial y} = -1$.

Calculate the discriminant $D = Z_{xx} Z_{yy} - (Z_{xy})^2 = (6x)(6y) - (-1)^2 = 36xy - 1$.

Classify Point 1: $(0,0)$.

$Z_{xx}(0,0) = 6(0) = 0$.

$D(0,0) = 36(0)(0) - 1 = -1$.

Since $D < 0$, the point $(0, 0)$ is a saddle point.

Classify Point 2: $(1/3, 1/3)$.

$Z_{xx}(1/3, 1/3) = 6(1/3) = 2$.

$D(1/3, 1/3) = 36(1/3)(1/3) - 1 = 36(1/9) - 1 = 4 - 1 = 3$.

Since $D > 0$ and $Z_{xx} > 0$, the point $(1/3, 1/3)$ is a local minimum.

Value at minimum: $Z(1/3, 1/3) = (1/3)^3 - (1/3)(1/3) + (1/3)^3 = 1/27 - 1/9 + 1/27 = 2/27 - 3/27 = -1/27$.

The rate of change of area is calculated using partial derivatives and the chain rule. Stationary points are found by setting first derivatives to zero and classified using the second derivative test.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Simplify $\frac{\operatorname{Cos} 3 x+j \operatorname{Sin} 3 x}{\operatorname{Cos} x+j \operatorname{Sin} x}$. (4 marks)

(b) Express $\mathrm{Z}=(-1+\mathrm{j})$ in the form $\mathrm{re}^{\mathrm{j} \theta}$, where r is positive and $-\pi<\theta<\pi$. (4 marks)

(c) Show that $\frac{1+\tan ^{2} B}{1+\cot ^{2} B}=\tan ^{2} B$. (4 marks)

(d) The power $P$ consumed in a resistor is given by $P=\frac{V^{2}}{R}$ watts. Determine the approximate change in power when V increases by $5 \%$ and R decreases by $0.5 \%$ if the original values of V and R are 50 volts and 12.5 ohms respectively. (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers simplification of complex numbers using De Moivre's theorem/exponential form, conversion to exponential form, proving trigonometric identities, and calculating approximate percentage change using partial derivatives or binomial approximation.

(a) Simplify $\frac{\operatorname{Cos} 3 x+j \operatorname{Sin} 3 x}{\operatorname{Cos} x+j \operatorname{Sin} x}$.

Using Euler's formula $e^{j\phi} = \cos \phi + j \sin \phi$.

Numerator = $\cos 3x + j \sin 3x = e^{j(3x)}$.

Denominator = $\cos x + j \sin x = e^{jx}$.

The fraction is $\frac{e^{j3x}}{e^{jx}} = e^{j3x - jx} = e^{j(2x)}$.

Convert back to rectangular form: $e^{j2x} = \cos(2x) + j \sin(2x)$.

Alternatively, using De Moivre's theorem: $\cos 3x + j \sin 3x = (\cos x + j \sin x)^3$.

So the fraction is $\frac{(\cos x + j \sin x)^3}{\cos x + j \sin x} = (\cos x + j \sin x)^2$.

$= \cos^2 x + 2(\cos x)(j \sin x) + (j \sin x)^2$

$= \cos^2 x + 2j \sin x \cos x - \sin^2 x$

$= (\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x) + j (2 \sin x \cos x) = \cos(2x) + j \sin(2x)$.

(b) Express $Z=-1+j$ in the form $re^{j\theta}$ with $-\pi < \theta < \pi$.

$r = |Z| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (1)^2} = \sqrt{1+1} = \sqrt{2}$.

$\theta = \arg(Z)$. Real part is negative, imaginary part is positive. Z is in the 2nd quadrant.

Reference angle $\alpha = \arctan\left|\frac{1}{-1}\right| = \arctan(1) = \pi/4$ radians (or $45^\circ$).

The angle $\theta$ in the 2nd quadrant is $\pi - \alpha$.

$\theta = \pi - \pi/4 = 3\pi/4$.

This angle is within the range $-\pi < \theta < \pi$.

Exponential form: $Z = \sqrt{2} e^{j 3\pi/4}$.

(c) Show that $\frac{1+\tan ^{2} B}{1+\cot ^{2} B}=\tan ^{2} B$.

Use the identities $1 + \tan^2 B = \sec^2 B$ and $1 + \cot^2 B = \csc^2 B$.

LHS = $\frac{\sec^2 B}{\csc^2 B}$.

Express in terms of $\sin B$ and $\cos B$: $\sec B = 1/\cos B$, $\csc B = 1/\sin B$.

LHS = $\frac{(1/\cos B)^2}{(1/\sin B)^2} = \frac{1/\cos^2 B}{1/\sin^2 B}$.

$= \frac{1}{\cos^2 B} \times \frac{\sin^2 B}{1} = \frac{\sin^2 B}{\cos^2 B}$.

$= \left(\frac{\sin B}{\cos B}\right)^2 = \tan^2 B$. (Shown)

(d) Approximate change in $P=\frac{V^{2}}{R}$.

Given $V_0 = 50$ V, $R_0 = 12.5$ ohms.

Change in V: $\delta V = 5\% \times V_0 = 0.05 \times 50 = 2.5$ V.

Change in R: $\delta R = -0.5\% \times R_0 = -0.005 \times 12.5 = -0.0625$ ohms.

Use the total differential (or small changes approximation):

$\delta P \approx \frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\delta V + \frac{\partial P}{\partial R}\delta R$.

Calculate partial derivatives:

$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = \frac{2V}{R}$.

$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = V^2 (-1 R^{-2}) = -\frac{V^2}{R^2}$.

Evaluate partial derivatives at the original values ($V_0=50, R_0=12.5$):

$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} \bigg|_{(50, 12.5)} = \frac{2(50)}{12.5} = \frac{100}{12.5} = 8$.

$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} \bigg|_{(50, 12.5)} = -\frac{50^2}{(12.5)^2} = -\frac{2500}{156.25} = -16$.

Calculate the approximate change $\delta P$:

$\delta P \approx (8)(\delta V) + (-16)(\delta R)$.

$\delta P \approx 8(2.5) + (-16)(-0.0625)$.

$\delta P \approx 20 + 1 = 21$.

The approximate change in power is an increase of 21 watts.

(Optional: Find original power $P_0 = V_0^2 / R_0 = 50^2 / 12.5 = 2500 / 12.5 = 200$ watts. Percentage change $\approx (21/200) * 100\% = 10.5\%$).

Complex number simplification, trigonometric identities, and approximation of changes using partial derivatives are demonstrated.
