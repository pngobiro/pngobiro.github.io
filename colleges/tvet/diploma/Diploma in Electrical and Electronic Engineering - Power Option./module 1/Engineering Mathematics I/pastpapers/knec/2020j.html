Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Express the equation of the parabola $y^{2}=8-4x$ in polar form. (4 marks)

(b) Solve the equation $4^{4 x}-4^{x+1}+3=0$, correct to three decimal places. (Typo likely, assume $4^{2x} - 4^{x+1} + 3 = 0$) (8 marks)

(c) Three currents $I_{1}, I_{2}$ and $I_{3}$ in amperes flowing in an electric circuit satisfy the simultaneous equations:

$2 I_{1}+3 I_{2}-4 I_{3}=-4$

$3 I_{1}+4 I_{3}-I_{3}=8$ (Typo? Assume $3I_1 + 4I_2 - I_3 = 8$)

$I_{1}-5 I_{2}+I_{3}=-6$ (Typo? Assume $I_1$ or $I_3$ has coeff 1)

Use the method of elimination to determine the values of the currents. (8 marks)

Answer

This question involves converting Cartesian to polar coordinates, solving an exponential equation reducible to quadratic (with interpretation of a likely typo), and solving a system of linear equations.

(a) Convert $y^{2}=8-4x$ to polar form.

Use $x = r \cos \theta$ and $y = r \sin \theta$.

$(r \sin \theta)^2 = 8 - 4(r \cos \theta)$.

$r^2 \sin^2 \theta = 8 - 4r \cos \theta$.

This is the polar form. It can be rearranged, but this form is acceptable.

Alternative rearrangement: $r^2 \sin^2 \theta + 4r \cos \theta - 8 = 0$. This is a quadratic in $r$ if $\sin^2\theta \neq 0$. $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$. $r^2(1-\cos^2\theta) + 4r\cos\theta - 8 = 0$.

(b) Solve $4^{2 x}-4^{x+1}+3=0$.

The original OCR shows $4^{4x} - 4^{\pi+1} + 3 = 0$, which seems highly improbable due to the $\pi$. Assuming the intended equation was $4^{2x} - 4^{x+1} + 3 = 0$.

Rewrite the equation: $(4^x)^2 - 4^1 \cdot 4^x + 3 = 0$.

Let $y = 4^x$. The equation becomes $y^2 - 4y + 3 = 0$.

Factor the quadratic: $(y-1)(y-3) = 0$.

Solutions for y are $y=1$ or $y=3$.

Case 1: $4^x = 1$. Since $4^0 = 1$, $x=0$.

Case 2: $4^x = 3$. Take logarithms (e.g., natural log):

$x \ln 4 = \ln 3$.

$x = \frac{\ln 3}{\ln 4} = \frac{\ln 3}{\ln(2^2)} = \frac{\ln 3}{2 \ln 2}$.

$x \approx \frac{1.098612}{2 \times 0.693147} = \frac{1.098612}{1.386294} \approx 0.79248$.

Correct to three decimal places: $x = 0.792$.

Solutions (based on assumed equation): $x = 0$ and $x \approx 0.792$.

(c) Solve the system of linear equations.

Interpreting typos in the second and third equations from OCR. Let's assume the system is:

(1) $2 I_{1}+3 I_{2}-4 I_{3}=-4$

(2) $3 I_{1}+4 I_{2}-I_{3}=8$ (Assumed $I_2$ instead of $I_3$ for coeff 4)

(3) $I_{1}-5 I_{2}+I_{3}=-6$ (Assumed $I_1$ has coeff 1)

Eliminate $I_3$.

Add (2) and (3): $(3I_1+4I_2-I_3) + (I_1-5I_2+I_3) = 8 + (-6)$.

$4I_1 - I_2 = 2 \quad (4)$

Eliminate $I_3$ using (1) and (3).

4*(3) + (1): $4(I_1-5I_2+I_3) + (2I_1+3I_2-4I_3) = 4(-6) + (-4)$.

$(4I_1-20I_2+4I_3) + (2I_1+3I_2-4I_3) = -24 - 4$.

$6I_1 - 17I_2 = -28 \quad (5)$

Now solve the system in $I_1, I_2$:

(4) $4I_1 - I_2 = 2$

(5) $6I_1 - 17I_2 = -28$

From (4), $I_2 = 4I_1 - 2$. Substitute into (5).

$6I_1 - 17(4I_1 - 2) = -28$.

$6I_1 - 68I_1 + 34 = -28$.

$-62I_1 = -28 - 34 = -62$.

$I_1 = 1$.

Substitute $I_1=1$ back into $I_2 = 4I_1 - 2$.

$I_2 = 4(1) - 2 = 2$.

Substitute $I_1=1, I_2=2$ back into (3).

$1 - 5(2) + I_3 = -6$.

$1 - 10 + I_3 = -6$.

$-9 + I_3 = -6$.

$I_3 = 3$.

Solution (based on assumed equations): $I_1=1$ A, $I_2=2$ A, $I_3=3$ A.

Polar conversion uses standard formulas. Exponential equation requires interpretation due to likely typo. Linear system solved via elimination, assuming corrections for apparent typos.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Simplify the expressions:

(i) $\frac{\left.(1-x)^{\frac{1}{2}}-(1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\right]}{(1-x)^{3}}$ (Typo? Extra bracket/square root placement?) (5 marks)

(ii) $\frac{\log 125-\frac{1}{2} \log 25+\log 625}{\log 3125+\frac{1}{2} \log 25}$ (Assuming base 10 or consistent base) (5 marks)

(b) Solve the equations:

(i) $13.2\left(12^{2+5}\right)=16$ (Typo? $13.2(12^{x+5})=16$ or similar?) correct to four decimal places. (5 marks)

(ii) $\log _{x} 2-\log _{4} x+\frac{7}{6}=0$. (5 marks)

Answer

This question involves simplifying expressions with indices and logarithms, and solving logarithmic and exponential equations (with interpretation of likely typos).

(a) Simplify expressions.

(i) Simplify $\frac{\left.(1-x)^{\frac{1}{2}}-(1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\right]}{(1-x)^{3}}$.

The expression has bracket/syntax issues. Assuming it's intended to be $\frac{(1-x)^{1/2} - (1-x)^{-1/2}}{(1-x)^3}$.

Simplify numerator: $(1-x)^{1/2} - \frac{1}{(1-x)^{1/2}} = \frac{(1-x) - 1}{(1-x)^{1/2}} = \frac{-x}{(1-x)^{1/2}}$.

Full expression: $\frac{-x / (1-x)^{1/2}}{(1-x)^3} = \frac{-x}{(1-x)^{1/2} (1-x)^3} = \frac{-x}{(1-x)^{7/2}}$.

(ii) Simplify $\frac{\log 125-\frac{1}{2} \log 25+\log 625}{\log 3125+\frac{1}{2} \log 25}$.

Express numbers as powers of 5:

Numerator: $\log 5^3 - \frac{1}{2}\log 5^2 + \log 5^4 = 3\log 5 - \frac{1}{2}(2\log 5) + 4\log 5 = (3-1+4)\log 5 = 6 \log 5$.

Denominator: $\log 5^5 + \frac{1}{2}\log 5^2 = 5\log 5 + \frac{1}{2}(2\log 5) = 5\log 5 + 1\log 5 = 6 \log 5$.

Fraction: $\frac{6 \log 5}{6 \log 5} = 1$ (assuming $\log 5 \neq 0$).

(b) Solve equations.

(i) Solve $13.2\left(12^{x+5}\right)=16$.

OCR shows $13.2(12^{2+5})=16$, which is $13.2 \times 12^7 = 16$, clearly false. Assuming the variable $x$ was intended: $13.2(12^{x+5})=16$.

$12^{x+5} = \frac{16}{13.2}$.

Take logarithms (e.g., natural log):

$(x+5) \ln 12 = \ln(16/13.2)$.

$x+5 = \frac{\ln(16/13.2)}{\ln 12}$.

$x = \frac{\ln(16/13.2)}{\ln 12} - 5$.

$\ln(16/13.2) \approx \ln(1.2121) \approx 0.19239$.

$\ln 12 \approx 2.4849$.

$x \approx \frac{0.19239}{2.4849} - 5 \approx 0.07742 - 5 = -4.92258$.

Correct to four decimal places: $x \approx -4.9226$.

(ii) Solve $\log _{x} 2-\log _{4} x+\frac{7}{6}=0$.

Use change of base to a common base, e.g., base 2.

$\log_x 2 = \frac{\log_2 2}{\log_2 x} = \frac{1}{\log_2 x}$.

$\log_4 x = \frac{\log_2 x}{\log_2 4} = \frac{\log_2 x}{2}$.

Substitute into the equation: $\frac{1}{\log_2 x} - \frac{\log_2 x}{2} + \frac{7}{6} = 0$.

Let $y = \log_2 x$. The equation is $\frac{1}{y} - \frac{y}{2} + \frac{7}{6} = 0$.

Multiply by the LCD, $6y$, assuming $y \neq 0$ (i.e., $x \neq 1$):

$6y(\frac{1}{y}) - 6y(\frac{y}{2}) + 6y(\frac{7}{6}) = 0$.

$6 - 3y^2 + 7y = 0$.

Rearrange: $3y^2 - 7y - 6 = 0$.

Factor the quadratic. Need numbers multiplying to $3(-6)=-18$ and adding to $-7$. These are $-9$ and $2$.

$3y^2 - 9y + 2y - 6 = 0$.

$3y(y-3) + 2(y-3) = 0$.

$(3y+2)(y-3) = 0$.

Solutions for y are $y = -2/3$ or $y = 3$.

Substitute back $y = \log_2 x$.

Case 1: $\log_2 x = -2/3 \implies x = 2^{-2/3} = \frac{1}{2^{2/3}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt[3]{4}}$.

Case 2: $\log_2 x = 3 \implies x = 2^3 = 8$.

Check domain: $x>0$ and $x \neq 1$. Both $x=8$ and $x=1/\sqrt[3]{4}$ are valid.

Solutions: $x=8$ and $x=2^{-2/3}$.

Simplification uses index and log rules. Solving equations requires algebraic manipulation, interpretation of typos, change of base, and substitution leading to quadratic equations.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Given the functions $f(x)=9 x$ and $g(x)=x+2$, determine:

(i) $\quad f g(x)$ (2 marks)

(ii) $(f g)^{-1}(9)$. (Typo? $(fg)^{-1}(x)$ or $f(g^{-1}(9))$ or $(fg)(9)$?) (4 marks)

(b) By expressing $\sinh ^{-1} x$ in logarithmic form, determine the value of $\sinh ^{-1}(0.2)$. (7 marks)

(C) Solve the equation 4 cosh $2 x-\sinh 2 x=4$ correct to three decimals places. (7 marks)

Answer

This question covers composite functions and their inverses (with interpretation of a possible typo), expressing inverse hyperbolic functions in logarithmic form, and solving hyperbolic equations.

(a) Composite functions.

(i) Find $fg(x) = f(g(x))$.

$g(x) = x+2$.

$f(g(x)) = f(x+2) = 9(x+2) = 9x + 18$.

$fg(x) = 9x + 18$.

(ii) Determine $(fg)^{-1}(9)$.

The notation is ambiguous. Let's assume it means finding the value of $x$ such that $fg(x)=9$. Another interpretation is finding the inverse function $(fg)^{-1}(x)$ and evaluating it at $x=9$. Let's do the latter first.

Let $y = fg(x) = 9x + 18$.

To find the inverse, swap $x$ and $y$ and solve for $y$.

$x = 9y + 18$.

$x - 18 = 9y$.

$y = \frac{x-18}{9}$.

So, $(fg)^{-1}(x) = \frac{x-18}{9}$.

Now evaluate at $x=9$: $(fg)^{-1}(9) = \frac{9-18}{9} = \frac{-9}{9} = -1$.

Alternative interpretation: Find $x$ such that $fg(x)=9$.

$9x + 18 = 9 \implies 9x = -9 \implies x = -1$. This gives the value of $x$ whose image under $fg$ is 9, which is consistent with the inverse function evaluation.

(b) Express $\sinh ^{-1} x$ in logarithmic form and find $\sinh^{-1}(0.2)$.

Let $y = \sinh^{-1} x$. Then $x = \sinh y = \frac{e^y - e^{-y}}{2}$.

$2x = e^y - e^{-y}$. Multiply by $e^y$: $2x e^y = (e^y)^2 - 1$.

Rearrange: $(e^y)^2 - 2x e^y - 1 = 0$.

Let $u = e^y$. $u^2 - 2xu - 1 = 0$.

Using quadratic formula for $u$: $u = \frac{-(-2x) \pm \sqrt{(-2x)^2 - 4(1)(-1)}}{2(1)} = \frac{2x \pm \sqrt{4x^2+4}}{2} = x \pm \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

Since $u=e^y$ must be positive, and $\sqrt{x^2+1} > |x|$, the only positive solution is $u = x + \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

$e^y = x + \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

$y = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$.

So, $\sinh^{-1} x = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$.

Now find $\sinh^{-1}(0.2)$.

$\sinh^{-1}(0.2) = \ln(0.2 + \sqrt{(0.2)^2 + 1}) = \ln(0.2 + \sqrt{0.04 + 1}) = \ln(0.2 + \sqrt{1.04})$.

$\sqrt{1.04} \approx 1.0198$.

$\sinh^{-1}(0.2) \approx \ln(0.2 + 1.0198) = \ln(1.2198)$.

$\sinh^{-1}(0.2) \approx 0.1986$.

(c) Solve $4 \cosh 2x - \sinh 2x = 4$.

Use exponential definitions: $\cosh 2x = \frac{e^{2x}+e^{-2x}}{2}$, $\sinh 2x = \frac{e^{2x}-e^{-2x}}{2}$.

$4\left(\frac{e^{2x}+e^{-2x}}{2}\right) - \left(\frac{e^{2x}-e^{-2x}}{2}\right) = 4$.

Multiply by 2: $4(e^{2x}+e^{-2x}) - (e^{2x}-e^{-2x}) = 8$.

$4e^{2x} + 4e^{-2x} - e^{2x} + e^{-2x} = 8$.

Combine terms: $3e^{2x} + 5e^{-2x} = 8$.

Multiply by $e^{2x}$: $3(e^{2x})^2 + 5 = 8e^{2x}$.

Let $y = e^{2x}$. $3y^2 + 5 = 8y$.

Rearrange: $3y^2 - 8y + 5 = 0$.

Factor: $(3y-5)(y-1) = 0$.

Solutions for y are $y=5/3$ or $y=1$.

Case 1: $e^{2x} = 5/3$. Take logs: $2x = \ln(5/3)$.

$x = \frac{1}{2} \ln(5/3) \approx \frac{1}{2}(0.510825) \approx 0.25541$.

Case 2: $e^{2x} = 1$. $2x = \ln 1 = 0$. So $x=0$.

Solutions correct to three decimal places: $x=0.000$ and $x \approx 0.255$.

Composite functions found correctly. Logarithmic form of inverse sinh derived and used. Hyperbolic equation solved using exponential definitions and solving the resulting quadratic.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Five components are to be chosen from 7 resistors and 6 diodes. Determine the number of ways in which the components can be selected so that there are at least 3 resistors in the choice. (5 marks)

(b) Find the term in $x^{4}$ in the binomial expansion of $(3 x-2)^{14}$, and determine its value when $x=\frac{1}{10}$, correct to three decimal places. (Note: OCR has exponent 4, assuming 14) (7 marks)

(c) (i) Use the binomial theorem to expand $\left(\frac{1-x}{1+2 x}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ up to the term in $x^{2}$. (5 marks)

(ii) Hence, evaluate $\left(\frac{0.8}{1.1}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$, correct to three decimal places. (3 marks)

Answer

This question involves combinations with constraints, finding specific terms in a binomial expansion, and using binomial series for approximation.

(a) Combinations: Choose 5 components from 7 Resistors (R) and 6 Diodes (D), with at least 3 R.

Possible compositions:

Case 1: 3 Resistors and 2 Diodes.

Ways = $\binom{7}{3} \times \binom{6}{2} = 35 \times 15 = 525$. (Calculations from July 2016 Q3a)

Case 2: 4 Resistors and 1 Diode.

Ways = $\binom{7}{4} \times \binom{6}{1} = 35 \times 6 = 210$.

Case 3: 5 Resistors and 0 Diodes.

Ways = $\binom{7}{5} \times \binom{6}{0} = 21 \times 1 = 21$.

Total number of ways = $525 + 210 + 21 = 756$.

(b) Term in $x^4$ in $(3x-2)^{14}$.

OCR shows $(3x-2)^4$. If n=4, the term in $x^4$ is the last term. If n=14 is intended (as suggested by scribbles in OCR), let's use n=14.

General term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$. Here $a=3x, b=-2, n=14$.

$T_{r+1} = \binom{14}{r} (3x)^{14-r} (-2)^r = \binom{14}{r} 3^{14-r} x^{14-r} (-2)^r$.

We want the term with $x^4$. So, $14-r = 4 \implies r=10$.

The term is $T_{10+1} = T_{11} = \binom{14}{10} 3^{14-10} x^4 (-2)^{10}$.

$T_{11} = \binom{14}{10} 3^4 x^4 (-2)^{10}$.

$\binom{14}{10} = \binom{14}{4} = \frac{14 \times 13 \times 12 \times 11}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = 14 \times 13 \times 11 / 2 = 7 \times 13 \times 11 = 1001$.

$3^4 = 81$. $(-2)^{10} = 1024$.

$T_{11} = 1001 \times 81 \times 1024 x^4 = 81081 \times 1024 x^4 = 83026944 x^4$.

The term in $x^4$ is $83026944 x^4$.

Value when $x=1/10 = 0.1$:

$83026944 (0.1)^4 = 83026944 (0.0001) = 8302.6944$.

Correct to three decimal places: $8302.694$.

(c)

(i) Expand $\left(\frac{1-x}{1+2 x}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = (1-x)^{1/2}(1+2x)^{-1/2}$ up to $x^2$.

Use $(1+u)^k \approx 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2}u^2$.

Part 1: $(1-x)^{1/2}$. $u=-x, k=1/2$.

$\approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}(-x) + \frac{\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2})}{2}(-x)^2 = 1 - \frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{8}x^2$.

Part 2: $(1+2x)^{-1/2}$. $u=2x, k=-1/2$.

$\approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(2x) + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})}{2}(2x)^2 = 1 - x + \frac{3/4}{2}(4x^2) = 1 - x + \frac{3}{8}(4x^2) = 1 - x + \frac{3}{2}x^2$.

Multiply expansions (up to $x^2$):

$(1 - \frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{8}x^2)(1 - x + \frac{3}{2}x^2)$.

$\approx 1(1 - x + \frac{3}{2}x^2) - \frac{1}{2}x(1 - x) - \frac{1}{8}x^2(1)$.

$\approx 1 - x + \frac{3}{2}x^2 - \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{1}{2}x^2 - \frac{1}{8}x^2$.

$\approx 1 + (-1-\frac{1}{2})x + (\frac{3}{2}+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{8})x^2$.

$\approx 1 - \frac{3}{2}x + (\frac{12}{8}+\frac{4}{8}-\frac{1}{8})x^2 = 1 - \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{15}{8}x^2$.

(ii) Evaluate $\left(\frac{0.8}{1.1}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$.

Match the form $\frac{1-x}{1+2x}$.

Set $1-x = 0.8 \implies x = 0.2$.

Check denominator: $1+2x = 1+2(0.2) = 1+0.4 = 1.4$. This does not match $1.1$.

Let's try matching the denominator: $1+2x = 1.1 \implies 2x = 0.1 \implies x = 0.05$.

Check numerator: $1-x = 1-0.05 = 0.95$. This does not match $0.8$.

There might be an error in the question or the value to evaluate. Let's assume we should use $x=0.1$ as suggested by the OCR scribbles for part (b).

If $x=0.1$, the expression is $\sqrt{\frac{1-0.1}{1+2(0.1)}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.9}{1.2}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{12}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{4}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 0.866$.

Use the expansion $1 - \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{15}{8}x^2$ with $x=0.1$.

$\approx 1 - \frac{3}{2}(0.1) + \frac{15}{8}(0.1)^2 = 1 - 0.15 + \frac{15}{8}(0.01) = 1 - 0.15 + \frac{0.15}{8}$.

$\approx 1 - 0.15 + 0.01875 = 0.85 + 0.01875 = 0.86875$.

Correct to three decimal places: $0.869$. (This approximates $\sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.866$).

If we were to evaluate the original $\left(\frac{0.8}{1.1}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{8/11} \approx \sqrt{0.7272} \approx 0.8528$. The expansion method requires matching the form.

Combinations calculated using casework. Binomial theorem applied, noting likely typo in exponent in (b) and potentially inconsistent values in (c)(ii).

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Differentiate $f(x)=\frac{1}{4 x}$, from first principles. (5 marks)

(b) Given that $z=\frac{x+y}{x-y}$, show that $\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial y^{2}}=\frac{4(x+y)}{(x-y)^{3}}$. (Typo? Likely relationship involves first derivatives or is simpler). (8 marks)

(c) Locate the stationary points of the function $z=x^{3}-9 x^{2}-4 y^{2}$ (Typo? Maybe $z=x^3-9x-4y^2$?) and determine their nature. (7 marks)

Answer

This question covers differentiation from first principles, second-order partial derivatives (with a check on a possibly complex identity), and finding/classifying stationary points of a function of two variables.

(a) Differentiate $f(x)=\frac{1}{4x}$ from first principles.

$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.

$f(x+h) = \frac{1}{4(x+h)}$.

$f(x+h) - f(x) = \frac{1}{4(x+h)} - \frac{1}{4x}$.

Combine fractions: $\frac{x - (x+h)}{4x(x+h)} = \frac{x - x - h}{4x(x+h)} = \frac{-h}{4x(x+h)}$.

$\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \frac{-h / [4x(x+h)]}{h} = \frac{-1}{4x(x+h)}$.

Take the limit as $h \to 0$:

$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-1}{4x(x+h)} = \frac{-1}{4x(x+0)} = \frac{-1}{4x^2}$.

(b) Show $\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial y^{2}}=\frac{4(x+y)}{(x-y)^{3}}$ for $z=\frac{x+y}{x-y}$.

Let's calculate the second derivatives. This identity seems complex and might have a typo.

Use quotient rule. $u=x+y, v=x-y$.

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{(x-y)(1) - (x+y)(1)}{(x-y)^2} = \frac{x-y-x-y}{(x-y)^2} = \frac{-2y}{(x-y)^2}$.

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = \frac{(x-y)(1) - (x+y)(-1)}{(x-y)^2} = \frac{x-y - (-x-y)}{(x-y)^2} = \frac{x-y+x+y}{(x-y)^2} = \frac{2x}{(x-y)^2}$.

Second derivatives:

$\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-2y(x-y)^{-2}) = -2y \cdot (-2)(x-y)^{-3} \cdot (1) = \frac{4y}{(x-y)^3}$.

$\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2x(x-y)^{-2}) = 2x \cdot (-2)(x-y)^{-3} \cdot (-1) = \frac{4x}{(x-y)^3}$.

Add them: $\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} z}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{4y}{(x-y)^3} + \frac{4x}{(x-y)^3} = \frac{4y+4x}{(x-y)^3} = \frac{4(x+y)}{(x-y)^3}$.

The identity holds true as stated. (Shown)

(c) Stationary points of $z=x^{3}-9 x^{2}-4 y^{2}$.

The term $-9x^2$ seems plausible. If it were $-9x$, the result would differ.

Find first partial derivatives:

$z_x = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 3x^2 - 18x$.

$z_y = \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -8y$.

Set derivatives to zero:

$z_x = 0 \implies 3x^2 - 18x = 0 \implies 3x(x-6) = 0$. So $x=0$ or $x=6$.

$z_y = 0 \implies -8y = 0 \implies y=0$.

Stationary points occur when both are zero:

Point 1: $(0, 0)$.

Point 2: $(6, 0)$.

Find second partial derivatives:

$z_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2} = 6x - 18$.

$z_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial y^2} = -8$.

$z_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x \partial y} = 0$.

Calculate the discriminant $D = z_{xx} z_{yy} - (z_{xy})^2 = (6x-18)(-8) - 0^2 = -48(x-3)$.

Classify Point 1: $(0, 0)$.

$z_{xx}(0,0) = 6(0) - 18 = -18$.

$D(0,0) = -48(0-3) = 144$.

Since $D > 0$ and $z_{xx} < 0$, the point $(0, 0)$ is a local maximum.

Value: $z(0,0) = 0^3 - 9(0)^2 - 4(0)^2 = 0$. (Assuming function is $z=x^3-9x^2-4y^2$. If $z=x^3-9x^2-4y^2+2$ as in Nov 2013 Q8c, the value is 2).

Classify Point 2: $(6, 0)$.

$z_{xx}(6,0) = 6(6) - 18 = 36 - 18 = 18$.

$D(6,0) = -48(6-3) = -48(3) = -144$.

Since $D < 0$, the point $(6, 0)$ is a saddle point.

Value: $z(6,0) = 6^3 - 9(6)^2 - 4(0)^2 = 216 - 9(36) = 216 - 324 = -108$.

First principles differentiation, calculation of second partial derivatives, and classification of stationary points using the second derivative test are demonstrated.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $\sin A=\frac{4}{5}$ and $\cos B=\frac{8}{10}$, where $A$ and $B$ are acute angles, determine the values of:

(i) $\cos(A-B)$

(ii) $\tan(A+B)$ (6 marks)

(b) Solve the equation $3 \cos ^{2} 2 \theta-\sin \theta+2=0$. for values of $\theta$ between $0^{\circ}$ to $360^{\circ}$ inclusive. (Typo? $\cos^2 \theta$ or $\cos 2\theta$?) (8 marks)

(c) The angle of depression of a ship viewed from the top of a 65 metre vertical cliff is $22^{\circ}$. If the ship sails away from the cliff a distance $x$ metres, the angle of depression from the top of the cliff is $18^{\circ}$. Determine the distance $x$. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves evaluating compound trigonometric functions, solving trigonometric equations involving double angles, and solving a problem using trigonometry (angles of depression).

(a) Given $\sin A = 4/5$ and $\cos B = 8/10 = 4/5$ (A, B acute).

Find $\cos A$ and $\sin B$.

Since A is acute, $\cos A > 0$. $\cos^2 A = 1 - \sin^2 A = 1 - (4/5)^2 = 1 - 16/25 = 9/25$. So $\cos A = 3/5$.

Since B is acute, $\sin B > 0$. $\sin^2 B = 1 - \cos^2 B = 1 - (4/5)^2 = 9/25$. So $\sin B = 3/5$.

Note: Since $\sin A = \cos B$ and A, B are acute, $A+B=90^\circ$.

Also find tangents: $\tan A = \sin A / \cos A = (4/5)/(3/5) = 4/3$. $\tan B = \sin B / \cos B = (3/5)/(4/5) = 3/4$.

(i) Find $\cos(A-B)$.

$\cos(A-B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B$.

$= (3/5)(4/5) + (4/5)(3/5) = 12/25 + 12/25 = 24/25$.

(ii) Find $\tan(A+B)$.

$\tan(A+B) = \frac{\tan A + \tan B}{1 - \tan A \tan B}$.

$= \frac{4/3 + 3/4}{1 - (4/3)(3/4)} = \frac{(16+9)/12}{1 - 12/12} = \frac{25/12}{0}$.

Since the denominator is zero, $\tan(A+B)$ is undefined. This occurs when $A+B = 90^\circ + n \cdot 180^\circ$. Since A, B are acute, $A+B=90^\circ$.

(b) Solve $3 \cos ^{2} 2 \theta-\sin \theta+2=0$.

The equation looks unusual. If it's $3\cos 2\theta - \sin \theta + 2 = 0$: Use $\cos 2\theta = 1-2\sin^2 \theta$.

$3(1-2\sin^2\theta) - \sin\theta + 2 = 0$.

$3 - 6\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta + 2 = 0$.

$-6\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta + 5 = 0$.

$6\sin^2\theta + \sin\theta - 5 = 0$.

Let $y=\sin\theta$. $6y^2+y-5=0$. Use quadratic formula.

$y = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1^2 - 4(6)(-5)}}{2(6)} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 120}}{12} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{121}}{12} = \frac{-1 \pm 11}{12}$.

$y_1 = (-1+11)/12 = 10/12 = 5/6$. $y_2 = (-1-11)/12 = -12/12 = -1$.

Case 1: $\sin\theta = 5/6$. $\theta_1 = \arcsin(5/6) \approx 56.44^\circ$. $\theta_2 = 180 - 56.44 = 123.56^\circ$.

Case 2: $\sin\theta = -1$. $\theta_3 = 270^\circ$.

Solutions (assuming typo $\cos 2\theta$): $\approx 56.4^\circ, 123.6^\circ, 270^\circ$.

If the equation is exactly $3 \cos^2(2\theta) - \sin\theta + 2 = 0$: Use $\cos^2(2\theta) = 1-\sin^2(2\theta)$?

$3(1-\sin^2(2\theta)) - \sin\theta + 2 = 0$. This mixes $\sin\theta$ and $\sin 2\theta$, hard to solve.

Or use $\cos^2 A = (1+\cos 2A)/2$. Let $A=2\theta$. $\cos^2(2\theta) = (1+\cos 4\theta)/2$.

$3(\frac{1+\cos 4\theta}{2}) - \sin\theta + 2 = 0$. Mixes $\cos 4\theta$ and $\sin\theta$. Also unlikely.

Proceeding with the assumption $3\cos 2\theta - \sin \theta + 2 = 0$.

(c) Angle of depression problem.

Let H = height of cliff = 65 m.

Let $d_1$ = initial horizontal distance of ship from cliff base.

Let $d_2$ = final horizontal distance of ship from cliff base.

Initial angle of depression = $22^\circ$. Final angle of depression = $18^\circ$.

Distance ship sailed away $x = d_2 - d_1$.

In triangle TPS1: $\tan(22^\circ) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} = \frac{H}{d_1}$.

$d_1 = \frac{H}{\tan(22^\circ)} = \frac{65}{\tan(22^\circ)}$.

In triangle TPS2: $\tan(18^\circ) = \frac{H}{d_2}$.

$d_2 = \frac{H}{\tan(18^\circ)} = \frac{65}{\tan(18^\circ)}$.

Distance $x = d_2 - d_1 = \frac{65}{\tan(18^\circ)} - \frac{65}{\tan(22^\circ)}$.

$x = 65 \left( \frac{1}{\tan(18^\circ)} - \frac{1}{\tan(22^\circ)} \right)$.

$\tan(18^\circ) \approx 0.3249$. $\tan(22^\circ) \approx 0.4040$.

$x \approx 65 \left( \frac{1}{0.3249} - \frac{1}{0.4040} \right)$.

$x \approx 65 (3.0777 - 2.4751) = 65 (0.6026)$.

$x \approx 39.169$.

The distance the ship sailed is approximately 39.2 metres.

Compound angle formulas used correctly. Trig equation solved assuming likely typo. Angle of depression problem solved using tangent ratios.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Evaluate the intergrals:

(i) $\int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{3}} \frac{3}{1+\cos x} d x$ (Use t-substitution?) (6 marks)

(ii) $\int_{0}^{\pi} x^{2} \sin x d x$ (Typo? $\pi$ instead of a?) (6 marks)

(iii) $\int_{1}^{2} \frac{2}{x^{2} \sqrt{1+x^{2}}} d x$ (Trig substitution?) (8 marks)

(b) Sketch the region bounded by the curve $y=x^{3}-2$ and the line $y=-4-3 x$ (Typo? Maybe $y=-4+3x$ or $y=4-3x$?) and use integration to determine its value (Area?).

Answer

This question involves evaluating various definite integrals, requiring techniques like half-angle identities (or t-substitution), integration by parts, trigonometric substitution, and finding the area between curves.

Note potential typos in parts (a)(ii) and (b).

(a) Evaluate integrals.

(i) $\int_{0}^{\pi/3} \frac{3}{1+\cos x} dx$.

Use half-angle identity $1+\cos x = 2\cos^2(x/2)$.

Integral = $\int_0^{\pi/3} \frac{3}{2\cos^2(x/2)} dx = \frac{3}{2} \int_0^{\pi/3} \sec^2(x/2) dx$.

Integrate: $\int \sec^2(au) du = \frac{1}{a}\tan(au)$. Here $a=1/2$.

Integral = $\frac{3}{2} \left[ \frac{\tan(x/2)}{1/2} \right]_0^{\pi/3} = \frac{3}{2} [ 2\tan(x/2) ]_0^{\pi/3} = 3 [\tan(x/2)]_0^{\pi/3}$.

$= 3 [\tan(\frac{\pi/3}{2}) - \tan(\frac{0}{2})] = 3 [\tan(\pi/6) - \tan(0)]$.

$\tan(\pi/6) = 1/\sqrt{3}$. $\tan(0) = 0$.

$= 3 [1/\sqrt{3} - 0] = \frac{3}{\sqrt{3}} = \sqrt{3}$.

(ii) $\int_{0}^{\pi} x^{2} \sin x d x$. (Assuming $\pi$ was intended limit).

Use integration by parts ($\int u dv = uv - \int v du$) twice.

First pass: $u=x^2, dv=\sin x dx \implies du=2x dx, v=-\cos x$.

$\int x^2 \sin x dx = x^2(-\cos x) - \int (-\cos x)(2x dx) = -x^2 \cos x + 2 \int x \cos x dx$.

Second pass (for $\int x \cos x dx$): $u=x, dv=\cos x dx \implies du=dx, v=\sin x$.

$\int x \cos x dx = x\sin x - \int \sin x dx = x\sin x - (-\cos x) = x\sin x + \cos x$.

Substitute back: $\int x^2 \sin x dx = -x^2 \cos x + 2(x\sin x + \cos x)$.

Now evaluate the definite integral:

$\int_0^\pi x^2 \sin x dx = [-x^2 \cos x + 2x\sin x + 2\cos x]_0^\pi$.

$= (-\pi^2 \cos \pi + 2\pi\sin \pi + 2\cos \pi) - (-0^2 \cos 0 + 2(0)\sin 0 + 2\cos 0)$.

$\cos \pi = -1, \sin \pi = 0, \cos 0 = 1, \sin 0 = 0$.

$= (-\pi^2(-1) + 2\pi(0) + 2(-1)) - (0 + 0 + 2(1))$.

$= (\pi^2 - 2) - (2) = \pi^2 - 4$.

(iii) $\int_{1}^{2} \frac{2}{x^{2} \sqrt{1+x^{2}}} d x$.

Use trigonometric substitution $x = \tan \theta$. Then $dx = \sec^2 \theta d\theta$.

$\sqrt{1+x^2} = \sqrt{1+\tan^2 \theta} = \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta} = |\sec \theta|$.

Change limits: When $x=1, \tan\theta=1 \implies \theta = \pi/4$. When $x=2, \tan\theta=2 \implies \theta = \arctan 2$. Since x is positive, $\theta$ is in $(0, \pi/2)$, so $\sec\theta > 0$.

Integral = $\int_{\pi/4}^{\arctan 2} \frac{2}{(\tan^2 \theta) (\sec \theta)} (\sec^2 \theta d\theta)$.

$= \int_{\pi/4}^{\arctan 2} \frac{2 \sec \theta}{\tan^2 \theta} d\theta = 2 \int_{\pi/4}^{\arctan 2} \frac{1/\cos \theta}{(\sin^2 \theta / \cos^2 \theta)} d\theta$.

$= 2 \int_{\pi/4}^{\arctan 2} \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \frac{\cos^2 \theta}{\sin^2 \theta} d\theta = 2 \int_{\pi/4}^{\arctan 2} \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin^2 \theta} d\theta$.

Use substitution $u = \sin \theta$, $du = \cos \theta d\theta$.

Change limits: When $\theta=\pi/4, u=\sin(\pi/4)=1/\sqrt{2}$. When $\theta=\arctan 2$, we have a triangle with opp=2, adj=1, hyp=$\sqrt{5}$, so $u=\sin(\arctan 2)=2/\sqrt{5}$.

Integral = $2 \int_{1/\sqrt{2}}^{2/\sqrt{5}} \frac{1}{u^2} du = 2 \int_{1/\sqrt{2}}^{2/\sqrt{5}} u^{-2} du$.

$= 2 \left[ \frac{u^{-1}}{-1} \right]_{1/\sqrt{2}}^{2/\sqrt{5}} = -2 \left[ \frac{1}{u} \right]_{1/\sqrt{2}}^{2/\sqrt{5}}$.

$= -2 \left( \frac{1}{2/\sqrt{5}} - \frac{1}{1/\sqrt{2}} \right) = -2 \left( \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} - \sqrt{2} \right)$.

$= -(\sqrt{5} - 2\sqrt{2}) = 2\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{5}$.

Approx value: $2(1.414) - 2.236 = 2.828 - 2.236 = 0.592$.

(b) Area between $y=x^{3}-2$ and $y=-4-3 x$.

The line equation seems unusual. Let's assume it's correct and find intersections.

$x^3 - 2 = -4 - 3x$.

$x^3 + 3x + 2 = 0$.

Check for integer roots (factors of 2): $\pm 1, \pm 2$.

If $x=-1$: $(-1)^3 + 3(-1) + 2 = -1 - 3 + 2 = -2 \neq 0$.

If $x=-2$: $(-2)^3 + 3(-2) + 2 = -8 - 6 + 2 = -12 \neq 0$.

If $x=1$: $(1)^3 + 3(1) + 2 = 1+3+2 = 6 \neq 0$.

If $x=2$: $(2)^3 + 3(2) + 2 = 8+6+2 = 16 \neq 0$.

Let $P(x) = x^3+3x+2$. $P(-0.5) = (-0.5)^3 + 3(-0.5) + 2 = -0.125 - 1.5 + 2 = 0.375$. $P(-1)=-2$. There must be a root between -1 and -0.5. Let's check $x=-0.596$ (approx root). This suggests the intersection is difficult to find, possibly indicating a typo in the line equation. If the line was $y=x-2$, intersection: $x^3-2=x-2 \implies x^3-x=0 \implies x(x^2-1)=0 \implies x=0, \pm 1$. This is more typical.

Assuming the intended question was different or cannot be easily solved for area with the given equations.

Integrals solved using appropriate techniques (half-angle identity, parts, trig substitution). Area calculation hindered by difficulty finding intersection points for the given equations, suggesting a possible typo.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Given the complex numbers $z_{1}=2+j$ and $z_{2}=5 j$, determine $\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}$, expressing the answer in exponential form. (6 marks)

(b) Use DeMoive's theorem to show that $\sin ^{5} \theta=\frac{1}{16}(\sin 5 \theta-5 \sin 3 \theta+10 \sin \theta)$. (6 marks)

(c) If $z=x+j y$, determine the equation of the locus defined by $\arg \left(\frac{z}{z-2}\right)=\frac{\pi}{4}$. (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers complex number division and conversion to exponential form, using De Moivre's theorem for trigonometric power reduction (specifically for sine), and finding the locus defined by the argument of a complex function.

(a) Calculate $\frac{z_1}{z_2}$ and express in exponential form.

$Z = \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{2+j}{5j}$.

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator, which is $-5j$ (or just by $j$):

$Z = \frac{(2+j)(-j)}{(5j)(-j)} = \frac{-2j - j^2}{-5j^2} = \frac{-2j - (-1)}{-5(-1)} = \frac{1 - 2j}{5}$.

$Z = \frac{1}{5} - \frac{2}{5}j = 0.2 - 0.4j$.

Convert to exponential form $re^{j\theta}$:

$r = |Z| = \sqrt{(0.2)^2 + (-0.4)^2} = \sqrt{0.04 + 0.16} = \sqrt{0.20} = \sqrt{20/100} = \sqrt{1/5} = 1/\sqrt{5} = \sqrt{5}/5$.

$\theta = \arg(Z)$. Real part positive, imaginary part negative $\implies$ 4th quadrant.

Reference angle $\alpha = \arctan\left|\frac{-0.4}{0.2}\right| = \arctan(2)$.

$\alpha \approx 1.1071$ radians.

Angle $\theta = -\alpha \approx -1.1071$ radians.

Exponential form: $Z = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} e^{-j \arctan(2)}$ or $Z \approx 0.447 e^{-j 1.107}$.

(b) Show $\sin ^{5} \theta=\frac{1}{16}(\sin 5 \theta-5 \sin 3 \theta+10 \sin \theta)$.

Let $z = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$. Then $z^{-1} = \cos\theta - j\sin\theta$.

$z - z^{-1} = 2j\sin\theta$.

$z^n - z^{-n} = 2j\sin n\theta$.

We need $(2j\sin\theta)^5 = (z - z^{-1})^5$.

$32 j^5 \sin^5 \theta = (z - z^{-1})^5$. $j^5 = j^4 \cdot j = 1 \cdot j = j$.

$32j \sin^5 \theta = (z - z^{-1})^5$.

Expand $(z - z^{-1})^5$ using binomial coefficients (1 -5 10 -10 5 -1):

$= z^5 + 5z^4(-z^{-1}) + 10z^3(-z^{-1})^2 + 10z^2(-z^{-1})^3 + 5z(-z^{-1})^4 + (-z^{-1})^5$.

$= z^5 - 5z^3 + 10z - 10z^{-1} + 5z^{-3} - z^{-5}$.

Group terms: $(z^5 - z^{-5}) - 5(z^3 - z^{-3}) + 10(z - z^{-1})$.

Substitute $z^n - z^{-n} = 2j\sin n\theta$:

$= (2j\sin 5\theta) - 5(2j\sin 3\theta) + 10(2j\sin \theta)$.

$= 2j\sin 5\theta - 10j\sin 3\theta + 20j\sin \theta$.

$= 2j (\sin 5\theta - 5\sin 3\theta + 10\sin \theta)$.

Equate this to $32j \sin^5 \theta$:

$32j \sin^5 \theta = 2j (\sin 5\theta - 5\sin 3\theta + 10\sin \theta)$.

Divide by $32j$: $\sin^5 \theta = \frac{2j}{32j} (\sin 5\theta - 5\sin 3\theta + 10\sin \theta)$.

$\sin^5 \theta = \frac{1}{16} (\sin 5\theta - 5\sin 3\theta + 10\sin \theta)$. (Shown)

(c) Locus for $\arg \left(\frac{z}{z-2}\right)=\frac{\pi}{4}$.

Let $W = \frac{z}{z-2} = \frac{x+jy}{(x-2)+jy}$.

Rationalize: $W = \frac{(x+jy)((x-2)-jy)}{((x-2)+jy)((x-2)-jy)}$.

$W = \frac{x(x-2) - jxy + jy(x-2) - j^2y^2}{(x-2)^2 - (jy)^2}$.

$W = \frac{x^2-2x - jxy + jxy - 2jy + y^2}{(x-2)^2 + y^2}$.

$W = \frac{(x^2-2x+y^2) + j(-2y)}{(x-2)^2 + y^2}$.

Real part: $\text{Re}(W) = \frac{x^2-2x+y^2}{(x-2)^2+y^2}$.

Imaginary part: $\text{Im}(W) = \frac{-2y}{(x-2)^2+y^2}$.

We are given $\arg(W) = \pi/4$. $\tan(\arg(W)) = \tan(\pi/4) = 1$.

$\tan(\arg(W)) = \frac{\text{Im}(W)}{\text{Re}(W)} = \frac{-2y / (\dots)}{ (x^2-2x+y^2) / (\dots)} = \frac{-2y}{x^2-2x+y^2}$.

So, $1 = \frac{-2y}{x^2-2x+y^2}$.

$x^2-2x+y^2 = -2y$.

$x^2-2x+y^2+2y = 0$.

Complete the square:

$(x^2-2x+1) + (y^2+2y+1) = 0 + 1 + 1$.

$(x-1)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 2$.

This is the equation of a circle with centre $(1, -1)$ and radius $\sqrt{2}$.

Complex division and exponential form calculated. De Moivre's theorem used for power reduction. Locus derived by finding real and imaginary parts and using the definition of argument.
