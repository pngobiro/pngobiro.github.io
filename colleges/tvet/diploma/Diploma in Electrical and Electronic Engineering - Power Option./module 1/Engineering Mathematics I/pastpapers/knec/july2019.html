Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Given $U=\tan ^{-1}(y / x)$, show that

$\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}=0$. (8 marks)

(b) The radius of a cylinder increases at the rate of $6 \mathrm{~cm} / \mathrm{s}$ and its height decreases at the rate of $3 \mathrm{~cm} / \mathrm{s}$. Use the partial differentiation to determine the rate at which the volume is changing at the instant when its radius and height are 12 cm and 18 cm respectively. (6 marks)

(c) Locate the stationary points of the function $f(x, y)=x^{2}+2 y^{2}-6 x+12 y$, and determine their nature. (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves second-order partial derivatives (Laplace's equation), related rates using partial differentiation, and finding/classifying stationary points for a function of two variables.

(a) Show $U=\tan ^{-1}(y / x)$ satisfies Laplace's equation.

First partial derivatives:

$\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{1+(y/x)^2} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(yx^{-1}) = \frac{1}{1+y^2/x^2} \cdot (y(-1)x^{-2}) = \frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2} \cdot \frac{-y}{x^2} = \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}$.

$\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{1+(y/x)^2} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y/x) = \frac{1}{1+y^2/x^2} \cdot (\frac{1}{x}) = \frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2} \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}$.

Second partial derivatives:

$\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\right) = -y \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2+y^2)^{-1} = -y \cdot (-1)(x^2+y^2)^{-2} \cdot (2x) = \frac{2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2}$.

$\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right) = x \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2+y^2)^{-1} = x \cdot (-1)(x^2+y^2)^{-2} \cdot (2y) = \frac{-2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2}$.

Add the second derivatives:

$\frac{\partial^{2} U}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} U}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2} + \frac{-2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = 0$. (Shown)

(b) Related rates for cylinder volume $V = \pi r^2 h$.

Given: $\frac{dr}{dt} = +6$ cm/s, $\frac{dh}{dt} = -3$ cm/s.

Find $\frac{dV}{dt}$ when $r=12$ cm, $h=18$ cm.

Use the chain rule (total derivative w.r.t. time t):

$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\frac{dr}{dt} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial h}\frac{dh}{dt}$.

Partial derivatives of $V = \pi r^2 h$:

$\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = \pi (2r) h = 2\pi r h$.

$\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \pi r^2 (1) = \pi r^2$.

Evaluate partial derivatives at $r=12, h=18$:

$\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = 2\pi (12)(18) = 432\pi$.

$\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \pi (12)^2 = 144\pi$.

Substitute into the chain rule formula:

$\frac{dV}{dt} = (432\pi)(\frac{dr}{dt}) + (144\pi)(\frac{dh}{dt})$.

$\frac{dV}{dt} = (432\pi)(6) + (144\pi)(-3)$.

$\frac{dV}{dt} = 2592\pi - 432\pi = 2160\pi$.

The rate of change of volume is $2160\pi$ cm$^3$/s.

Approximate value: $2160 \times 3.14159 \approx 6785.8$ cm$^3$/s.

(c) Stationary points of $f(x, y)=x^{2}+2 y^{2}-6 x+12 y$.

Find first partial derivatives:

$f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x - 6$.

$f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 4y + 12$.

Set derivatives to zero:

$f_x = 0 \implies 2x - 6 = 0 \implies 2x = 6 \implies x = 3$.

$f_y = 0 \implies 4y + 12 = 0 \implies 4y = -12 \implies y = -3$.

The only stationary point is $(3, -3)$.

Find second partial derivatives to classify:

$f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = 2$.

$f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = 4$.

$f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = 0$.

Calculate the discriminant $D = f_{xx} f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2$.

$D = (2)(4) - (0)^2 = 8$.

Since $D > 0$ and $f_{xx} = 2 > 0$, the stationary point $(3, -3)$ is a local minimum.

Value at minimum: $f(3,-3) = (3)^2 + 2(-3)^2 - 6(3) + 12(-3) = 9 + 2(9) - 18 - 36 = 9 + 18 - 18 - 36 = -27$.

Partial differentiation is used to verify Laplace's equation and solve a related rates problem. The second derivative test is used to find and classify the stationary point of a function of two variables.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Solve the equations:

(i) $\quad 2^{2 x+1}-7\left(2^{x}\right)+6=0$ (5 marks)

(ii) $\quad 2 \log _{9}(x+1)+\log _{3} x=1$ (7 marks)

(b) Three forces $\mathrm{F}_{1}, \mathrm{~F}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{F}_{3}$ in newtons, necessary for the equilibrium of a certain mechanical system satisfy the simultaneous equations:

$\mathrm{F}_{1}-2 \mathrm{~F}_{2}+\mathrm{F}_{3} =1$

$\mathrm{F}_{1}+3 \mathrm{~F}_{2}-2 \mathrm{~F}_{3} =2$

$\mathrm{F}_{1}+\mathrm{F}_{2}+\mathrm{F}_{3} =7$

Use elimination method to solve the equations. (8 marks)

Answer

This question involves solving exponential and logarithmic equations, including changing logarithm bases, and solving a system of linear equations using elimination.

(a) Solve equations.

(i) Solve $2^{2 x+1}-7\left(2^{x}\right)+6=0$.

Rewrite using index laws: $2^1 \cdot 2^{2x} - 7(2^x) + 6 = 0 \implies 2(2^x)^2 - 7(2^x) + 6 = 0$.

Let $y = 2^x$. The equation becomes $2y^2 - 7y + 6 = 0$.

Factor the quadratic. Need numbers multiplying to $2 \times 6 = 12$ and adding to $-7$. These are $-4$ and $-3$.

$2y^2 - 4y - 3y + 6 = 0$.

$2y(y-2) - 3(y-2) = 0$.

$(2y-3)(y-2) = 0$.

Solutions for y are $y=3/2$ or $y=2$.

Case 1: $2^x = 3/2$. Take logarithms: $x \ln 2 = \ln(3/2) = \ln 3 - \ln 2$.

$x = \frac{\ln 3 - \ln 2}{\ln 2} = \frac{\ln 3}{\ln 2} - 1 = \log_2 3 - 1$.

$x \approx \frac{1.0986 - 0.6931}{0.6931} = \frac{0.4055}{0.6931} \approx 0.585$.

Case 2: $2^x = 2 = 2^1$. So $x=1$.

Solutions: $x=1$ and $x = \log_2(3/2) \approx 0.585$.

(ii) Solve $2 \log _{9}(x+1)+\log _{3} x=1$.

Change base 9 logarithm to base 3: $\log_9(x+1) = \frac{\log_3(x+1)}{\log_3 9} = \frac{\log_3(x+1)}{2}$.

Substitute into the equation:

$2 \left(\frac{\log_3(x+1)}{2}\right) + \log_3 x = 1$.

$\log_3(x+1) + \log_3 x = 1$.

Combine logs on LHS: $\log_3[x(x+1)] = 1$.

Convert to exponential form: $x(x+1) = 3^1 = 3$.

$x^2 + x = 3$.

$x^2 + x - 3 = 0$.

Use quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1^2 - 4(1)(-3)}}{2(1)}$.

$x = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 12}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{13}}{2}$.

$\sqrt{13} \approx 3.6056$.

$x_1 = \frac{-1 + 3.6056}{2} = \frac{2.6056}{2} \approx 1.3028$.

$x_2 = \frac{-1 - 3.6056}{2} = \frac{-4.6056}{2} \approx -2.3028$.

Check domain of original logarithms: $x+1 > 0 \implies x > -1$ and $x > 0$. We need $x>0$.

Only $x_1 \approx 1.3028$ satisfies the domain condition.

Solution: $x = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{13}}{2} \approx 1.303$.

(b) Solve the system of linear equations for forces.

(1) $F_{1}-2 F_{2}+F_{3} =1$

(2) $F_{1}+3 F_{2}-2 F_{3} =2$

(3) $F_{1}+F_{2}+F_{3} =7$

Eliminate $F_1$.

(2) - (1): $(F_1+3F_2-2F_3) - (F_1-2F_2+F_3) = 2 - 1$.

$5F_2 - 3F_3 = 1 \quad (4)$

(3) - (1): $(F_1+F_2+F_3) - (F_1-2F_2+F_3) = 7 - 1$.

$3F_2 = 6 \implies F_2 = 2$.

Substitute $F_2=2$ into (4):

$5(2) - 3F_3 = 1$.

$10 - 3F_3 = 1$.

$-3F_3 = 1 - 10 = -9$.

$F_3 = 3$.

Substitute $F_2=2$ and $F_3=3$ into (3):

$F_1 + 2 + 3 = 7$.

$F_1 + 5 = 7$.

$F_1 = 2$.

Solution: $F_1 = 2$ N, $F_2 = 2$ N, $F_3 = 3$ N.

Exponential equations solved using substitution and quadratic formula. Logarithmic equation solved by changing base and converting to quadratic. Linear system solved efficiently using elimination.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Given the complex numbers $Z_{1}=2+5 j, Z_{2}=1-3 j$ and $Z_{3}=2+3 j$, determine:

(i) $4 Z_{1}^{2}+3 Z_{2} Z_{3}$ (6 marks)

(ii) $Z$, if $\frac{1}{Z}=\frac{1}{Z_{1}}+\frac{1}{Z_{2}}+\frac{1}{Z_{3}}$, giving the answers in the form $a+j b$. (8 marks)

(b) Determine all the roots of the equation:

$Z^{3}+\frac{1}{2}(1+j \sqrt{3})=0$ (6 marks)

Answer

This question involves complex number arithmetic (squaring, multiplication, addition, reciprocals) and finding the roots of a complex number.

(a) Complex number operations.

(i) Calculate $4 Z_{1}^{2}+3 Z_{2} Z_{3}$.

$Z_1^2 = (2+5j)^2 = 2^2 + 2(2)(5j) + (5j)^2 = 4 + 20j + 25j^2 = 4 + 20j - 25 = -21 + 20j$.

$4Z_1^2 = 4(-21 + 20j) = -84 + 80j$.

$Z_2 Z_3 = (1-3j)(2+3j) = 1(2) + 1(3j) - 3j(2) - 3j(3j) = 2 + 3j - 6j - 9j^2 = 2 - 3j - 9(-1) = 2 - 3j + 9 = 11 - 3j$.

$3Z_2 Z_3 = 3(11 - 3j) = 33 - 9j$.

$4Z_1^2 + 3Z_2 Z_3 = (-84 + 80j) + (33 - 9j) = (-84+33) + (80-9)j = -51 + 71j$.

(ii) Find $Z$ if $\frac{1}{Z}=\frac{1}{Z_{1}}+\frac{1}{Z_{2}}+\frac{1}{Z_{3}}$.

Find reciprocals:

$\frac{1}{Z_1} = \frac{1}{2+5j} = \frac{2-5j}{(2+5j)(2-5j)} = \frac{2-5j}{4 - (-25)} = \frac{2-5j}{29}$.

$\frac{1}{Z_2} = \frac{1}{1-3j} = \frac{1+3j}{(1-3j)(1+3j)} = \frac{1+3j}{1 - (-9)} = \frac{1+3j}{10}$.

$\frac{1}{Z_3} = \frac{1}{2+3j} = \frac{2-3j}{(2+3j)(2-3j)} = \frac{2-3j}{4 - (-9)} = \frac{2-3j}{13}$.

Add reciprocals:

$\frac{1}{Z} = \frac{2-5j}{29} + \frac{1+3j}{10} + \frac{2-3j}{13}$.

Combine real and imaginary parts:

Real: $\frac{2}{29} + \frac{1}{10} + \frac{2}{13}$. LCD(290, 13) = 3770.

$= \frac{2(130) + 1(377) + 2(290)}{3770} = \frac{260 + 377 + 580}{3770} = \frac{1217}{3770}$.

Imaginary: $\frac{-5}{29} + \frac{3}{10} + \frac{-3}{13}$.

$= \frac{-5(130) + 3(377) - 3(290)}{3770} = \frac{-650 + 1131 - 870}{3770} = \frac{-389}{3770}$.

So, $\frac{1}{Z} = \frac{1217}{3770} - j \frac{389}{3770}$.

$Z = \frac{1}{\frac{1217 - 389j}{3770}} = \frac{3770}{1217 - 389j}$.

Rationalize: $Z = \frac{3770 (1217 + 389j)}{(1217 - 389j)(1217 + 389j)} = \frac{3770 (1217 + 389j)}{1217^2 + 389^2}$.

$1217^2 = 1481089$. $389^2 = 151321$. Sum = 1632410.

$Z = \frac{3770 (1217 + 389j)}{1632410}$.

$Z = \frac{3770 \times 1217}{1632410} + j \frac{3770 \times 389}{1632410}$.

$Z = \frac{4590090}{1632410} + j \frac{1466530}{1632410}$.

$Z \approx 2.8118 + 0.8984j$.

(b) Solve $Z^{3}+\frac{1}{2}(1+j \sqrt{3})=0$.

$Z^3 = -\frac{1}{2}(1+j\sqrt{3}) = -\frac{1}{2} - j\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

Find the cube roots of $w = -1/2 - j\sqrt{3}/2$.

Convert $w$ to polar form $r(\cos \phi + j \sin \phi)$.

$r = |w| = \sqrt{(-1/2)^2 + (-\sqrt{3}/2)^2} = \sqrt{1/4 + 3/4} = \sqrt{1} = 1$.

$\phi = \arg(w)$. Real negative, Imaginary negative $\implies$ 3rd quadrant.

Reference angle $\alpha = \arctan\left|\frac{-\sqrt{3}/2}{-1/2}\right| = \arctan(\sqrt{3}) = \pi/3$.

Angle $\phi = \pi + \alpha = \pi + \pi/3 = 4\pi/3$. Or equivalently $\phi = -2\pi/3$. Let's use $\phi = 4\pi/3 + 2k\pi$.

$w = 1(\cos(4\pi/3) + j \sin(4\pi/3))$.

Let $Z = R(\cos \Theta + j \sin \Theta)$. Then $Z^3 = R^3 (\cos 3\Theta + j \sin 3\Theta)$.

$R^3 = 1 \implies R = 1$.

$3\Theta = 4\pi/3 + 2k\pi \implies \Theta = \frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2k\pi}{3}$.

Find roots for $k=0, 1, 2$.

$k=0: \Theta_0 = 4\pi/9$. $z_0 = \cos(4\pi/9) + j \sin(4\pi/9)$. ($80^\circ$)

$k=1: \Theta_1 = 4\pi/9 + 2\pi/3 = 4\pi/9 + 6\pi/9 = 10\pi/9$. $z_1 = \cos(10\pi/9) + j \sin(10\pi/9)$. ($200^\circ$)

$k=2: \Theta_2 = 4\pi/9 + 4\pi/3 = 4\pi/9 + 12\pi/9 = 16\pi/9$. $z_2 = \cos(16\pi/9) + j \sin(16\pi/9)$. ($320^\circ$ or $-40^\circ$)

Approximate values in $a+jb$ form:

$z_0 = \cos(80^\circ) + j \sin(80^\circ) \approx 0.1736 + 0.9848j$.

$z_1 = \cos(200^\circ) + j \sin(200^\circ) \approx -0.9397 - 0.3420j$.

$z_2 = \cos(320^\circ) + j \sin(320^\circ) \approx 0.7660 - 0.6428j$.

Complex number arithmetic involves careful application of rules. Finding roots requires conversion to polar form and applying De Moivre's theorem for roots.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Evaluate the integrals:

(i) $\int_{0}^{1} \frac{x+1}{x^{2}+x+1} d x$ (6 marks)

(ii) $\int_{0}^{1} \frac{x+2}{(x+1)^{2}(x+3)} d x$ (8 marks)

(b) Sketch the region enclosed between the curve $y=1-x^{2}$ and the line $y=2 x-2$, and use integration to determine the area of the region. (6 marks)

Answer

This question covers definite integration, involving techniques like substitution/manipulation for specific forms and partial fractions, as well as finding the area between two curves.

(a) Evaluate integrals.

(i) $\int_{0}^{1} \frac{x+1}{x^{2}+x+1} d x$.

The derivative of the denominator is $2x+1$. We can split the numerator to get this derivative.

$\frac{x+1}{x^2+x+1} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}(2x+1) + \frac{1}{2}}{x^2+x+1} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{2x+1}{x^2+x+1} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{x^2+x+1}$.

Integral 1: $\int_0^1 \frac{1}{2} \frac{2x+1}{x^2+x+1} dx$. Let $u=x^2+x+1, du=(2x+1)dx$.

$= \frac{1}{2} \int_{x=0}^{x=1} \frac{du}{u} = \frac{1}{2} [\ln|u|] = \frac{1}{2} [\ln(x^2+x+1)]_0^1$.

$= \frac{1}{2} [\ln(1+1+1) - \ln(0+0+1)] = \frac{1}{2} (\ln 3 - \ln 1) = \frac{1}{2} \ln 3$.

Integral 2: $\int_0^1 \frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{x^2+x+1} dx$. Complete the square in the denominator.

$x^2+x+1 = (x^2+x+\frac{1}{4}) + 1 - \frac{1}{4} = (x+\frac{1}{2})^2 + \frac{3}{4}$.

Integral = $\frac{1}{2} \int_0^1 \frac{1}{(x+1/2)^2 + (\sqrt{3}/2)^2} dx$.

This is of the form $\int \frac{1}{u^2+a^2} du = \frac{1}{a} \arctan(\frac{u}{a})$. Here $u=x+1/2, a=\sqrt{3}/2$.

$= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}/2} \arctan\left(\frac{x+1/2}{\sqrt{3}/2}\right) \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \arctan\left(\frac{2x+1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \right]_0^1$.

$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \left[ \arctan\left(\frac{2x+1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \right]_0^1$.

$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \left[ \arctan\left(\frac{2(1)+1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) - \arctan\left(\frac{2(0)+1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \right]$.

$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \left[ \arctan\left(\frac{3}{\sqrt{3}}\right) - \arctan\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} [ \arctan(\sqrt{3}) - \arctan(1/\sqrt{3}) ]$.

$\arctan(\sqrt{3}) = \pi/3$. $\arctan(1/\sqrt{3}) = \pi/6$.

$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} (\pi/3 - \pi/6) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} (\pi/6) = \frac{\pi}{6\sqrt{3}}$.

Total integral = Integral 1 + Integral 2 = $\frac{1}{2} \ln 3 + \frac{\pi}{6\sqrt{3}}$.

(ii) $\int_{0}^{1} \frac{x+2}{(x+1)^{2}(x+3)} d x$.

Partial fractions: $\frac{x+2}{(x+1)^2(x+3)} = \frac{A}{x+1} + \frac{B}{(x+1)^2} + \frac{C}{x+3}$.

$x+2 = A(x+1)(x+3) + B(x+3) + C(x+1)^2$.

$x+2 = A(x^2+4x+3) + B(x+3) + C(x^2+2x+1)$.

Set $x=-1$: $-1+2 = A(0) + B(-1+3) + C(0) \implies 1 = 2B \implies B=1/2$.

Set $x=-3$: $-3+2 = A(0) + B(0) + C(-3+1)^2 \implies -1 = C(-2)^2 = 4C \implies C=-1/4$.

Equate coefficients of $x^2$: $0 = A + C$.

$0 = A + (-1/4) \implies A = 1/4$.

Integrand is $\frac{1/4}{x+1} + \frac{1/2}{(x+1)^2} - \frac{1/4}{x+3}$.

Integral = $\int_0^1 \left( \frac{1}{4(x+1)} + \frac{1}{2(x+1)^2} - \frac{1}{4(x+3)} \right) dx$.

$= \left[ \frac{1}{4}\ln|x+1| + \frac{1}{2}\frac{(x+1)^{-1}}{-1} - \frac{1}{4}\ln|x+3| \right]_0^1$.

$= \left[ \frac{1}{4}\ln|x+1| - \frac{1}{2(x+1)} - \frac{1}{4}\ln|x+3| \right]_0^1$.

$= \left( \frac{1}{4}\ln 2 - \frac{1}{2(2)} - \frac{1}{4}\ln 4 \right) - \left( \frac{1}{4}\ln 1 - \frac{1}{2(1)} - \frac{1}{4}\ln 3 \right)$.

$= \left( \frac{1}{4}\ln 2 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4}\ln(2^2) \right) - \left( 0 - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{4}\ln 3 \right)$.

$= \frac{1}{4}\ln 2 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{2}{4}\ln 2 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4}\ln 3$.

$= (\frac{1}{4}-\frac{2}{4})\ln 2 + (-\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2}) + \frac{1}{4}\ln 3$.

$= -\frac{1}{4}\ln 2 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}\ln 3 = \frac{1}{4} (1 + \ln 3 - \ln 2) = \frac{1}{4} (1 + \ln(3/2))$.

Approx value: $\frac{1}{4}(1 + \ln 1.5) \approx \frac{1}{4}(1 + 0.4055) = \frac{1.4055}{4} \approx 0.3514$.

(b) Area between $y=1-x^{2}$ and $y=2 x-2$.

$y=1-x^2$ is a parabola opening downwards, vertex (0,1).

$y=2x-2$ is a line with slope 2, y-intercept -2.

Find intersection points: $1-x^2 = 2x-2$.

$0 = x^2 + 2x - 3$.

$0 = (x+3)(x-1)$. Intersections at $x=-3$ and $x=1$.

If $x=-3$, $y = 1-(-3)^2 = 1-9=-8$. Line: $y=2(-3)-2=-6-2=-8$. Point $(-3,-8)$.

If $x=1$, $y = 1-(1)^2 = 0$. Line: $y=2(1)-2=0$. Point $(1,0)$.

Sketch: Parabola opening down, line sloping up, intersecting at $(-3,-8)$ and $(1,0)$. The parabola is the upper curve between these points.

Area $A = \int_{-3}^{1} (\text{Upper curve} - \text{Lower curve}) dx$.

$A = \int_{-3}^{1} [(1-x^2) - (2x-2)] dx$.

$A = \int_{-3}^{1} (1 - x^2 - 2x + 2) dx = \int_{-3}^{1} (-x^2 - 2x + 3) dx$.

Integrate: $A = \left[ -\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{2x^2}{2} + 3x \right]_{-3}^{1} = \left[ -\frac{x^3}{3} - x^2 + 3x \right]_{-3}^{1}$.

$A = \left( -\frac{1^3}{3} - 1^2 + 3(1) \right) - \left( -\frac{(-3)^3}{3} - (-3)^2 + 3(-3) \right)$.

$A = \left( -\frac{1}{3} - 1 + 3 \right) - \left( -(\frac{-27}{3}) - 9 - 9 \right)$.

$A = \left( -\frac{1}{3} + 2 \right) - \left( 9 - 9 - 9 \right)$.

$A = \left( \frac{-1+6}{3} \right) - (-9) = \frac{5}{3} + 9 = \frac{5 + 27}{3} = \frac{32}{3}$.

Area = $32/3$ square units.

Integration techniques including completing the square, partial fractions, and finding the area between curves are demonstrated.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Determine the term in $x^{5}$ in the binomial expansion of $(2 x+3 y)^{9}$, and find its value when $x=\frac{1}{3}$ and $y=\frac{1}{2}$. (6 marks)

(b) Find the first four terms in the binomial expansion of $(9-3 x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$, and state the values of $x$ for which the expansion is valid. (8 marks)

(c) (i) use the binomial theorem to show that, for $x$ very small, $\sqrt{\left(\frac{1+\frac{1}{2} x}{1-\frac{1}{2} x}\right)}=1+\frac{1}{2} x+\frac{1}{8} x^{2}$, approximately. (4 marks)

(ii) By setting $x=\frac{1}{25}$ in (i), determine the approximate value of $\sqrt{51}$, correct to four decimal places. (2 marks)

Answer

This question tests the binomial theorem for positive integer and fractional/negative powers, including finding specific terms, generating expansions, and using them for approximation.

(a) Term in $x^5$ in $(2x+3y)^9$.

General term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$. Here $a=2x, b=3y, n=9$.

$T_{r+1} = \binom{9}{r} (2x)^{9-r} (3y)^r = \binom{9}{r} 2^{9-r} x^{9-r} 3^r y^r$.

We want the term with $x^5$. So, $9-r = 5 \implies r=4$.

The term is $T_{4+1} = T_5 = \binom{9}{4} 2^{9-4} x^5 3^4 y^4$.

$T_5 = \binom{9}{4} 2^5 x^5 3^4 y^4$.

$\binom{9}{4} = \frac{9!}{4!5!} = \frac{9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = 9 \times 2 \times 7 = 126$.

$T_5 = 126 (32) x^5 (81) y^4 = 126 \times 32 \times 81 x^5 y^4 = 4032 \times 81 x^5 y^4 = 326592 x^5 y^4$.

The term in $x^5$ is $326592 x^5 y^4$.

Value when $x=1/3, y=1/2$:

$326592 (1/3)^5 (1/2)^4 = 326592 \left(\frac{1}{243}\right) \left(\frac{1}{16}\right)$.

$= \frac{326592}{243 \times 16} = \frac{326592}{3888} = 84$.

(b) First four terms of $(9-3x)^{-1/2}$.

Factor out 9: $(9(1 - \frac{3x}{9}))^{-1/2} = (9(1 - x/3))^{-1/2} = 9^{-1/2} (1 - x/3)^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{3} (1 - x/3)^{-1/2}$.

Use $(1+u)^k \approx 1 + ku + \frac{k(k-1)}{2!}u^2 + \frac{k(k-1)(k-2)}{3!}u^3$.

Here $u=-x/3, k=-1/2$.

Expansion $\approx \frac{1}{3} \left[ 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{x}{3}) + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})}{2}(-\frac{x}{3})^2 + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})(-\frac{5}{2})}{6}(-\frac{x}{3})^3 \right]$.

$\approx \frac{1}{3} \left[ 1 + \frac{x}{6} + \frac{3/4}{2}(\frac{x^2}{9}) + \frac{-15/8}{6}(-\frac{x^3}{27}) \right]$.

$\approx \frac{1}{3} \left[ 1 + \frac{x}{6} + \frac{3}{8}(\frac{x^2}{9}) + \frac{15}{48}(\frac{x^3}{27}) \right]$.

$\approx \frac{1}{3} \left[ 1 + \frac{x}{6} + \frac{x^2}{24} + \frac{5}{16}(\frac{x^3}{27}) \right]$.

$\approx \frac{1}{3} \left[ 1 + \frac{x}{6} + \frac{x^2}{24} + \frac{5x^3}{432} \right]$.

$\approx \frac{1}{3} + \frac{x}{18} + \frac{x^2}{72} + \frac{5x^3}{1296}$.

Range of validity: $|u| < 1 \implies |-x/3| < 1 \implies |x|/3 < 1 \implies |x| < 3$. Valid for $-3 < x < 3$.

(c)

(i) Show $\sqrt{\frac{1+x/2}{1-x/2}} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{1}{8}x^2$.

Let the expression be $(1+x/2)^{1/2} (1-x/2)^{-1/2}$. Use $(1+u)^k \approx 1+ku+\frac{k(k-1)}{2}u^2$.

$(1+x/2)^{1/2} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}(\frac{x}{2}) + \frac{\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2})}{2}(\frac{x}{2})^2 = 1 + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{1}{8}(\frac{x^2}{4}) = 1 + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{x^2}{32}$.

$(1-x/2)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + (-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{x}{2}) + \frac{-\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{3}{2})}{2}(-\frac{x}{2})^2 = 1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3/4}{2}(\frac{x^2}{4}) = 1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3}{8}(\frac{x^2}{4}) = 1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3x^2}{32}$.

Multiply expansions (up to $x^2$):

$(1 + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{x^2}{32})(1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3x^2}{32})$.

$\approx 1(1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3x^2}{32}) + \frac{x}{4}(1 + \frac{x}{4}) - \frac{x^2}{32}(1)$.

$\approx 1 + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{3x^2}{32} + \frac{x}{4} + \frac{x^2}{16} - \frac{x^2}{32}$.

$\approx 1 + (\frac{x}{4}+\frac{x}{4}) + (\frac{3}{32}+\frac{2}{32}-\frac{1}{32})x^2$.

$\approx 1 + \frac{2x}{4} + \frac{4}{32}x^2 = 1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{1}{8}x^2$. (Shown)

(ii) Use $x=1/25$ to approximate $\sqrt{51}$.

Evaluate $\sqrt{\frac{1+x/2}{1-x/2}}$ with $x=1/25$.

$\sqrt{\frac{1+(1/25)/2}{1-(1/25)/2}} = \sqrt{\frac{1+1/50}{1-1/50}} = \sqrt{\frac{51/50}{49/50}} = \sqrt{\frac{51}{49}} = \frac{\sqrt{51}}{\sqrt{49}} = \frac{\sqrt{51}}{7}$.

Use the approximation $1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{1}{8}x^2$ with $x=1/25$.

$\frac{\sqrt{51}}{7} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{25}) + \frac{1}{8}(\frac{1}{25})^2 = 1 + \frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{8}(\frac{1}{625})$.

$\frac{\sqrt{51}}{7} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{5000} = 1 + 0.02 + 0.0002 = 1.0202$.

$\sqrt{51} \approx 7 \times 1.0202 = 7.1414$.

Correct to four decimal places: $7.1414$.

Binomial theorem applied correctly for integer and fractional powers, calculation of specific terms, and approximations.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Prove the identities:

(i) $\frac{1+\cos \theta}{1-\cos \theta}=(\operatorname{Cosec} \theta+\operatorname{Cot} \theta)^{2}$ (5 marks)

(ii) $\operatorname{Cos} \theta+\operatorname{Cos}\left(\theta+120^{\circ}\right)+\operatorname{Cos}\left(\theta+240^{\circ}\right)=0$ (5 marks)

(b) (i) Express $7 \sin \theta-5 \cos \theta$ in the form $R \sin (\theta-\alpha)$, where $R>0$ and $0^{\circ} \leq \alpha \leq 90^{\circ}$ (5 marks)

(ii) Hence solve the equation: $7 \operatorname{Sin} \theta-5 \operatorname{Cos} \theta=4.3$, for values of $\theta$ between $0^{\circ}$ and $360^{\circ}$ inclusive. (5 marks)

Answer

This question involves proving trigonometric identities using fundamental definitions and sum/compound angle formulas, and solving trigonometric equations using the R-formula method.

(a) Prove identities.

(i) Prove $\frac{1+\cos \theta}{1-\cos \theta}=(\operatorname{Cosec} \theta+\operatorname{Cot} \theta)^{2}$.

Work with RHS: $(\csc \theta + \cot \theta)^2 = \left(\frac{1}{\sin \theta} + \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}\right)^2$.

$= \left(\frac{1+\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}\right)^2 = \frac{(1+\cos \theta)^2}{\sin^2 \theta}$.

Use $\sin^2 \theta = 1 - \cos^2 \theta = (1-\cos\theta)(1+\cos\theta)$.

RHS = $\frac{(1+\cos \theta)^2}{(1-\cos\theta)(1+\cos\theta)}$.

Assuming $1+\cos\theta \neq 0$, cancel one factor:

RHS = $\frac{1+\cos \theta}{1-\cos \theta}$ = LHS. (Proven)

(ii) Prove $\operatorname{Cos} \theta+\operatorname{Cos}\left(\theta+120^{\circ}\right)+\operatorname{Cos}\left(\theta+240^{\circ}\right)=0$.

Use the sum formula $\cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B$.

$\cos(\theta+120^\circ) = \cos\theta \cos 120^\circ - \sin\theta \sin 120^\circ$.

$\cos 120^\circ = -1/2$, $\sin 120^\circ = \sqrt{3}/2$.

$\cos(\theta+120^\circ) = \cos\theta (-\frac{1}{2}) - \sin\theta (\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) = -\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta$.

$\cos(\theta+240^\circ) = \cos\theta \cos 240^\circ - \sin\theta \sin 240^\circ$.

$\cos 240^\circ = -1/2$, $\sin 240^\circ = -\sqrt{3}/2$.

$\cos(\theta+240^\circ) = \cos\theta (-\frac{1}{2}) - \sin\theta (-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) = -\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta$.

Now add the three terms:

LHS = $\cos\theta + (-\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta) + (-\frac{1}{2}\cos\theta + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta)$.

LHS = $(\cos\theta - \frac{1}{2}\cos\theta - \frac{1}{2}\cos\theta) + (-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\sin\theta)$.

LHS = $(1 - 1/2 - 1/2)\cos\theta + 0 = 0 \cdot \cos\theta = 0$. (Proven)

Alternatively, use sum-to-product on $\cos(\theta+120) + \cos(\theta+240)$.

$= 2 \cos\left(\frac{(\theta+120)+(\theta+240)}{2}\right) \cos\left(\frac{(\theta+240)-(\theta+120)}{2}\right)$.

$= 2 \cos\left(\frac{2\theta+360}{2}\right) \cos\left(\frac{120}{2}\right) = 2 \cos(\theta+180^\circ) \cos(60^\circ)$.

$\cos(\theta+180^\circ) = -\cos\theta$. $\cos(60^\circ)=1/2$.

$= 2 (-\cos\theta) (1/2) = -\cos\theta$.

LHS = $\cos\theta + (-\cos\theta) = 0$.

(b)

(i) Express $7 \sin \theta-5 \cos \theta$ as $R \sin (\theta-\alpha)$.

$R \sin(\theta-\alpha) = R(\sin\theta \cos\alpha - \cos\theta \sin\alpha) = (R\cos\alpha)\sin\theta - (R\sin\alpha)\cos\theta$.

Compare coefficients:

$R \cos \alpha = 7$.

$R \sin \alpha = 5$.

$R^2 = 7^2 + 5^2 = 49 + 25 = 74 \implies R = \sqrt{74}$.

$\tan \alpha = \frac{R \sin \alpha}{R \cos \alpha} = \frac{5}{7}$.

$\alpha = \arctan(5/7)$. Since $\sin \alpha, \cos \alpha$ are positive, $\alpha$ is in the 1st quadrant.

$\alpha \approx 35.54^{\circ}$.

So, $7 \sin \theta - 5 \cos \theta = \sqrt{74} \sin(\theta - 35.54^{\circ})$.

(ii) Solve $7 \operatorname{Sin} \theta-5 \operatorname{Cos} \theta=4.3$.

Using part (i): $\sqrt{74} \sin(\theta - 35.54^{\circ}) = 4.3$.

$\sin(\theta - 35.54^{\circ}) = \frac{4.3}{\sqrt{74}}$. $\sqrt{74} \approx 8.6023$.

$\sin(\theta - 35.54^{\circ}) \approx \frac{4.3}{8.6023} \approx 0.499866$.

Let $\phi = \theta - 35.54^{\circ}$. Then $\sin \phi \approx 0.49987$.

Principal value $\phi_1 = \arcsin(0.49987) \approx 29.99^{\circ} \approx 30.0^{\circ}$.

Since $\sin \phi$ is positive, solutions for $\phi$ are in 1st and 2nd quadrants.

1st Quad: $\phi_1 \approx 30.0^{\circ}$.

2nd Quad: $\phi_2 = 180^{\circ} - 30.0^{\circ} = 150.0^{\circ}$.

Find $\theta = \phi + 35.54^{\circ}$.

$\theta_1 = 30.0^{\circ} + 35.54^{\circ} = 65.54^{\circ}$.

$\theta_2 = 150.0^{\circ} + 35.54^{\circ} = 185.54^{\circ}$.

Both solutions are in the range $0^\circ$ to $360^\circ$.

Solutions: $\theta \approx 65.5^{\circ}$ and $\theta \approx 185.5^{\circ}$.

Trigonometric identities proven using fundamental relations and sum/compound angle formulas. Equation solved using the R-formula method.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Given that $p \sinh x+q \cosh x=5 e^{x}+3 e^{-x}$, determine the values of p and $q$. (6 marks)

(b) Prove the identities:

(i) $\operatorname{Cosh}(x+y)=\cosh x \cosh y+\sinh x \sinh y$ (5 marks)

(ii) $\tanh 2 x=\frac{2 \tanh x}{1+\tanh ^{2} x}$ (4 marks)

(c) Express $\sinh ^{-1} x$ in logarithmic form. (5 marks)

Answer

This question focuses on hyperbolic functions, including relating them to exponentials, proving identities, and finding the logarithmic form of an inverse hyperbolic function.

(a) Determine p and q.

Substitute definitions: $\sinh x = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}$, $\cosh x = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}$.

LHS = $p\left(\frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}\right) + q\left(\frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}\right)$.

Combine over denominator 2: LHS = $\frac{p(e^x - e^{-x}) + q(e^x + e^{-x})}{2}$.

LHS = $\frac{pe^x - pe^{-x} + qe^x + qe^{-x}}{2} = \frac{(p+q)e^x + (q-p)e^{-x}}{2}$.

Given LHS = RHS: $\frac{(p+q)e^x + (q-p)e^{-x}}{2} = 5e^x + 3e^{-x}$.

Multiply by 2: $(p+q)e^x + (q-p)e^{-x} = 10e^x + 6e^{-x}$.

Equate coefficients of $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$:

$p+q = 10 \quad (1)$

$q-p = 6 \quad (2)$

Add (1) and (2): $(p+q) + (q-p) = 10 + 6 \implies 2q = 16 \implies q=8$.

Substitute $q=8$ into (1): $p+8 = 10 \implies p=2$.

So, $p=2$ and $q=8$.

(b) Prove identities.

(i) Prove $\operatorname{Cosh}(x+y)=\cosh x \cosh y+\sinh x \sinh y$.

Use exponential definitions:

RHS = $\left(\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}\right)\left(\frac{e^y+e^{-y}}{2}\right) + \left(\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}\right)\left(\frac{e^y-e^{-y}}{2}\right)$.

Combine over common denominator 4:

RHS = $\frac{1}{4} [ (e^x+e^{-x})(e^y+e^{-y}) + (e^x-e^{-x})(e^y-e^{-y}) ]$.

Expand products:

Term 1: $e^{x+y} + e^{x-y} + e^{-x+y} + e^{-x-y}$.

Term 2: $e^{x+y} - e^{x-y} - e^{-x+y} + e^{-x-y}$.

Add Term 1 and Term 2:

$(e^{x+y} + e^{x-y} + e^{-x+y} + e^{-x-y}) + (e^{x+y} - e^{x-y} - e^{-x+y} + e^{-x-y})$.

$= 2e^{x+y} + 2e^{-x-y} = 2(e^{x+y} + e^{-(x+y)})$.

Substitute back into RHS expression:

RHS = $\frac{1}{4} [ 2(e^{x+y} + e^{-(x+y)}) ] = \frac{e^{x+y} + e^{-(x+y)}}{2} = \cosh(x+y)$ = LHS. (Proven)

(ii) Prove $\tanh 2 x=\frac{2 \tanh x}{1+\tanh ^{2} x}$.

This was proven in Q1(a)(i) of the July 2015 paper and Q8(a)(ii) of the July 2015 paper. It is a standard identity derived from $\tanh(x+x)$.

$\tanh(x+x) = \frac{\tanh x + \tanh x}{1 + \tanh x \tanh x} = \frac{2 \tanh x}{1 + \tanh^2 x}$. (Proven)

(c) Express $\sinh ^{-1} x$ in logarithmic form.

Let $y = \sinh^{-1} x$. Then $x = \sinh y$.

$x = \frac{e^y - e^{-y}}{2}$.

$2x = e^y - e^{-y}$.

Multiply by $e^y$: $2x e^y = (e^y)^2 - 1$.

Rearrange into quadratic form in $e^y$: $(e^y)^2 - 2x e^y - 1 = 0$.

Let $u = e^y$. $u^2 - 2xu - 1 = 0$.

Use quadratic formula for $u$: $u = \frac{-(-2x) \pm \sqrt{(-2x)^2 - 4(1)(-1)}}{2(1)}$.

$u = \frac{2x \pm \sqrt{4x^2 + 4}}{2} = \frac{2x \pm \sqrt{4(x^2+1)}}{2} = \frac{2x \pm 2\sqrt{x^2+1}}{2}$.

$u = x \pm \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

So $e^y = x \pm \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

Since $e^y$ must be positive, and $\sqrt{x^2+1} > \sqrt{x^2} = |x|$, the term $x - \sqrt{x^2+1}$ is always negative.

Therefore, we must take the positive sign: $e^y = x + \sqrt{x^2+1}$.

Take natural logarithm: $y = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$.

Since $y=\sinh^{-1} x$, we have $\sinh^{-1} x = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$.

Hyperbolic functions related to exponentials, identities proven, and logarithmic form of inverse sinh derived.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Find $\frac{d y}{d x}$ from first principles, given

$y=\frac{x-1}{x+2}$ (8 marks)

(b) Use implicit differentiation to determine the equation of the normal to the curve $x^{2}+y^{2}-4 x y+6 x+4 y=8$, at the point $(1,1)$. (7 marks)

(c) Determine the stationary points of the function $f(x)=2 x^{3}+3 x^{2}-12 x+6$, and state their nature. (5 marks)

Answer

This question covers differentiation from first principles for a rational function, implicit differentiation to find the normal equation, and finding/classifying stationary points for a polynomial.

(a) Derivative of $y=\frac{x-1}{x+2}$ from first principles.

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.

$f(x) = \frac{x-1}{x+2}$.

$f(x+h) = \frac{(x+h)-1}{(x+h)+2} = \frac{x+h-1}{x+h+2}$.

$f(x+h) - f(x) = \frac{x+h-1}{x+h+2} - \frac{x-1}{x+2}$.

Combine fractions: $\frac{(x+h-1)(x+2) - (x-1)(x+h+2)}{(x+h+2)(x+2)}$.

Expand numerator:

Term 1: $(x+h-1)(x+2) = x(x+2) + h(x+2) - 1(x+2) = x^2+2x + hx+2h - x-2 = x^2+x+hx+2h-2$.

Term 2: $(x-1)(x+h+2) = x(x+h+2) - 1(x+h+2) = x^2+xh+2x - x-h-2 = x^2+xh+x-h-2$.

Numerator = $(x^2+x+hx+2h-2) - (x^2+xh+x-h-2)$.

$= x^2+x+hx+2h-2 - x^2-xh-x+h+2$.

$= (x^2-x^2) + (x-x) + (hx-xh) + (2h+h) + (-2+2)$.

$= 0 + 0 + 0 + 3h + 0 = 3h$.

So, $f(x+h) - f(x) = \frac{3h}{(x+h+2)(x+2)}$.

$\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \frac{3h / [(x+h+2)(x+2)]}{h} = \frac{3}{(x+h+2)(x+2)}$.

Take the limit as $h \to 0$:

$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3}{(x+h+2)(x+2)} = \frac{3}{(x+0+2)(x+2)} = \frac{3}{(x+2)^2}$.

(b) Normal to $x^{2}+y^{2}-4 x y+6 x+4 y=8$ at $(1,1)$.

Differentiate implicitly w.r.t. $x$:

$2x + 2y y' - (4y + 4x y') + 6 + 4y' = 0$. (Note: $y' = dy/dx$)

$2x + 2y y' - 4y - 4x y' + 6 + 4y' = 0$.

Group terms with $y'$: $(2y - 4x + 4)y' = 4y - 2x - 6$.

$y' = \frac{4y - 2x - 6}{2y - 4x + 4} = \frac{2(2y - x - 3)}{2(y - 2x + 2)} = \frac{2y - x - 3}{y - 2x + 2}$.

Evaluate slope of tangent $m_t$ at $(1,1)$:

$m_t = \frac{2(1) - 1 - 3}{1 - 2(1) + 2} = \frac{2 - 1 - 3}{1 - 2 + 2} = \frac{-2}{1} = -2$.

Slope of normal $m_n = -1/m_t = -1/(-2) = 1/2$.

Equation of normal using point-slope form $y-y_1 = m_n(x-x_1)$:

$y - 1 = \frac{1}{2}(x - 1)$.

Multiply by 2: $2(y - 1) = 1(x - 1)$.

$2y - 2 = x - 1$.

$x - 2y + 1 = 0$.

(c) Stationary points of $f(x)=2 x^{3}+3 x^{2}-12 x+6$.

Find first derivative: $f'(x) = 6x^2 + 6x - 12$.

Set $f'(x) = 0$: $6x^2 + 6x - 12 = 0$.

Divide by 6: $x^2 + x - 2 = 0$.

Factor: $(x+2)(x-1) = 0$.

Stationary points occur at $x=-2$ and $x=1$.

Find corresponding y-values:

$f(-2) = 2(-2)^3 + 3(-2)^2 - 12(-2) + 6 = 2(-8) + 3(4) + 24 + 6 = -16 + 12 + 24 + 6 = 26$. Point $(-2, 26)$.

$f(1) = 2(1)^3 + 3(1)^2 - 12(1) + 6 = 2 + 3 - 12 + 6 = -1$. Point $(1, -1)$.

Find second derivative: $f''(x) = 12x + 6$.

Classify points:

At $x=-2$: $f''(-2) = 12(-2) + 6 = -24 + 6 = -18$. Since $f'' < 0$, this is a local maximum.

At $x=1$: $f''(1) = 12(1) + 6 = 18$. Since $f'' > 0$, this is a local minimum.

Stationary points: Local maximum at $(-2, 26)$ and local minimum at $(1, -1)$.

Standard differentiation techniques applied: first principles, implicit differentiation, finding and classifying stationary points using first and second derivatives.
