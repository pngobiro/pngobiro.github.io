Irregular areas and volumes, and mean values

üìè 28.1 Areas of irregular figures

Areas of irregular plane surfaces may be approximately determined by using

  • (a) a planimeter,
  • (b) the trapezoidal rule,
  • (c) the mid-ordinate rule, or
  • (d) Simpson's rule.

Such methods may be used by, for example, engineers estimating areas of indicator diagrams of steam engines, surveyors estimating areas of plots of land or naval architects estimating areas of water planes or transverse sections of ships.

  • (a) A planimeter is an instrument for directly measuring small areas bounded by an irregular curve. There are many different kinds of planimeters but all operate in a similar way. A pointer on the planimeter is used to trace around the boundary of the shape. This induces a movement in another part of the instrument and a reading of this is used to establish the area of the shape.
  • (b) Trapezoidal rule
    To determine the area PQRS in Figure 28.1,
    Figure 28.1
    1. Divide base PS into any number of equal intervals, each of width d (the greater the number of intervals, the greater the accuracy).
    2. Accurately measure ordinates $y_{1}, y_{2}, y_{3}$, etc.
    3. Area PQRS $=d\left[\frac{y_{1}+y_{7}}{2}+y_{2}+y_{3}+y_{4}+y_{5}+y_{6}\right]$.

    In general, the trapezoidal rule states
    $$\begin{aligned} \text{Area }= &\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{width of} \\ \text{interval} \end{array}\right)\left[\frac{1}{2}\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{first + last} \\ \text{ordinate} \end{array}\right)\right. \\ & \left.+\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{sum of} \\ \text{remaining} \\ \text{ordinates} \end{array}\right)\right]\end{aligned}$$
  • (c) Mid-ordinate rule
    Figure 28.2
    To determine the area ABCD of Figure 28.2,
    1. Divide base AD into any number of equal intervals, each of width d (the greater the number of intervals, the greater the accuracy).
    2. Erect ordinates in the middle of each interval (shown by broken lines in Figure 28.2).
    3. Accurately measure ordinates $y_{1}, y_{2}, y_{3}$, etc.
    4. Area ABCD $=d\left(y_{1}+y_{2}+y_{3}+y_{4}+y_{5}+y_{6}\right)$.

    In general, the mid-ordinate rule states
    Area = (width of interval)(sum of mid-ordinates)
  • (d) Simpson's rule
    To determine the area PQRS of Figure 28.1,
    1. Divide base PS into an even number of intervals, each of width d (the greater the number of intervals, the greater the accuracy).
    2. Accurately measure ordinates $y_{1}, y_{2}, y_{3}$, etc.
    3. Area PQRS $=\frac{d}{3}\left[\left(y_{1}+y_{7}\right)+4\left(y_{2}+y_{4}+y_{6}\right) +2\left(y_{3}+y_{5}\right)\right]$

    In general, Simpson's rule states
    $$\begin{aligned} \text{Area } = &\frac{1}{3}\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{width of} \\ \text{interval} \end{array}\right)\left[\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{first + last} \\ \text{ordinate} \end{array}\right)\right. \\ & \left.+4\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{sum of even} \\ \text{ordinates} \end{array}\right)+2\left(\begin{array}{c} \text{sum of remaining} \\ \text{odd ordinates} \end{array}\right)\right]\end{aligned}$$

üßä 28.2 Volumes of irregular solids

If the cross-sectional areas $A_{1}, A_{2}, A_{3}, \ldots$ of an irregular solid bounded by two parallel planes are known at equal intervals of width d (as shown in Figure 28.5), by Simpson's rule

$$\text{Volume, } V = \frac{d}{3}\left[\left(A_{1}+A_{7}\right)+4\left(A_{2}+A_{4}+A_{6}\right) +2\left(A_{3}+A_{5}\right)\right]$$

üí° Problem 3. A tree trunk is 12 m in length and has a varying cross-section. The cross-sectional areas at intervals of 2 m measured from one end are $0.52,0.55,0.59,0.63,0.72,0.84 \text{ and } 0.97 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$. Estimate the volume of the tree trunk

A sketch of the tree trunk is similar to that shown in Figure 28.5 above, where $d=2 \mathrm{~m}, A_{1}=0.52 \mathrm{~m}^{2}, A_{2}=0.55 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$, and so on.

Using Simpson's rule for volumes gives

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Volume} &= \frac{2}{3}[(0.52+0.97)+4(0.55+0.63+0.84) \\ &+2(0.59+0.72)] \\ &= \frac{2}{3}[1.49+8.08+2.62]=\mathbf{8.13} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}\end{aligned}$$

üí° Problem 4. The areas of seven horizontal cross-sections of a water reservoir at intervals of 10 m are $210,250,320,350,290,230 \text{ and } 170 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$. Calculate the capacity of the reservoir in litres

Using Simpson's rule for volumes gives

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Volume} &= \frac{10}{3}[(210+170)+4(250+350+230) \\ &+2(320+290)] \\ &= \frac{10}{3}[380+3320+1220]=\mathbf{16400} \mathbf{~ m}^{3}\end{aligned}$$

$16400 \mathrm{~m}^{3}=16400 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$.

Since 1 litre $=1000 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$,

$$\begin{aligned} \text{capacity of reservoir} &= \frac{16400 \times 10^{6}}{1000} \text{ litres} \\ &= 16400000 = \mathbf{16.4} \times \mathbf{10}^{\mathbf{6}} \text{ litres}\end{aligned}$$

üìä 28.3 Mean or average values of waveforms

The mean or average value, $y$, of the waveform shown in Figure 28.6 is given by

$$y=\frac{\text{area under curve}}{\text{length of base, } b}$$

If the mid-ordinate rule is used to find the area under the curve, then

$$y=\frac{\text{sum of mid-ordinates}}{\text{number of mid-ordinates}}$$ $$\left(=\frac{y_{1}+y_{2}+y_{3}+y_{4}+y_{5}+y_{6}+y_{7}}{7} \text{ for Figure 28.6}\right)$$

Figure 28.6

For a sine wave, the mean or average value

  • (a) over one complete cycle is zero (see Figure 28.7(a)),
  • (b) over half a cycle is $\mathbf{0.637} \times \text{maximum value}$ or $\frac{2}{\pi} \times \text{maximum value}$,
  • (c) of a full-wave rectified waveform (see Figure 28.7(b)) is $\mathbf{0.637} \times \text{maximum value}$,
  • (d) of a half-wave rectified waveform (see Figure 28.7(c)) is $\mathbf{0.318} \times \text{maximum value}$ or $\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\pi} \times \text{maximum value}$.

üí° Problem 5. Determine the average values over half a cycle of the periodic waveforms shown in Figure 28.8
Figure 28.8

  • (a) Area under triangular waveform (a) for a half cycle is given by
    $$\begin{aligned} \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{perpendicular height}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2}(2 \times 10^{-3})(20)=20 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~Vs}\end{aligned}$$
    Average value of waveform $=\frac{\text{area under curve}}{\text{length of base}} = \frac{20 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~Vs}}{2 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~s}}=\mathbf{10} \mathrm{V}$
  • (b) Area under waveform (b) for a half cycle $=(1 \times 1)+(3 \times 2)=7 \mathrm{As}$
    Average value of waveform $=\frac{\text{area under curve}}{\text{length of base}} = \frac{7 \mathrm{As}}{3 \mathrm{~s}}=\mathbf{2.33A}$
  • (c) A half cycle of the voltage waveform (c) is completed in 4 ms .
    Area under curve $=\frac{1}{2}\left\{(3-1) 10^{-3}\right\}(10) = 10 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~Vs}$
    Average value of waveform $=\frac{\text{area under curve}}{\text{length of base}} = \frac{10 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~Vs}}{4 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~s}}=\mathbf{2.5} \mathbf{V}$

Figure 28.9

  • (a) One cycle of the trapezoidal waveform (a) is completed in 10 ms (i.e. the periodic time is 10 ms ).

üí° Problem 8. Figure 28.11 shows a sinusoidal output voltage of a full-wave rectifier. Determine, using the mid-ordinate rule with 6 intervals, the mean output voltage
Figure 28.11

One cycle of the output voltage is completed in $\pi$ radians or $180^{\circ}$. The base is divided into 6 intervals, each of width $30^{\circ}$. The mid-ordinate of each interval will lie at $15^{\circ}, 45^{\circ}, 75^{\circ}$, etc.

At $15^{\circ}$ the height of the mid-ordinate is $10 \sin 15^{\circ}=2.588 \mathrm{~V}$,

At $45^{\circ}$ the height of the mid-ordinate is $10 \sin 45^{\circ}=7.071 \mathrm{~V}$, and so on.

The results are tabulated below.

Mid-ordinateHeight of mid-ordinate
$15^{\circ}$$10 \sin 15^{\circ}=2.588 \mathrm{~V}$
$45^{\circ}$$10 \sin 45^{\circ}=7.071 \mathrm{~V}$
$75^{\circ}$$10 \sin 75^{\circ}=9.659 \mathrm{~V}$
$105^{\circ}$$10 \sin 105^{\circ}=9.659 \mathrm{~V}$
$135^{\circ}$$10 \sin 135^{\circ}=7.071 \mathrm{~V}$
$165^{\circ}$$10 \sin 165^{\circ}=2.588 \mathrm{~V}$
Sum of mid-ordinates $=38.636 \mathrm{~V}$
$$\text{Mean or average value of output voltage} = \frac{\text{sum of mid-ordinates}}{\text{number of mid-ordinates}} = \frac{38.636}{6}=\mathbf{6.439V}$$

(With a larger number of intervals a more accurate answer may be obtained.)

For a sine wave the actual mean value is $0.637 \times \text{maximum value}$, which in this problem gives 6.37 V .

üí° Problem 9. An indicator diagram for a steam engine is shown in Figure 28.12. The base line has been divided into 6 equally spaced intervals and the lengths of the 7 ordinates measured with the results shown in centimetres. Determine
(a) the area of the indicator diagram using Simpson's rule
(b) the mean pressure in the cylinder given that 1 cm represents 100 kPa .
Figure 28.12

  • (a) The width of each interval is $\frac{12.0}{6} \mathrm{~cm}$. Using Simpson's rule,
    $$\begin{aligned} \text{area} &= \frac{1}{3}(2.0)[(3.6+1.6)+4(4.0+2.9+1.7) +2(3.5+2.2)] \\ &= \frac{2}{3}[5.2+34.4+11.4]=\mathbf{34} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}\end{aligned}$$
  • (b) Mean height of ordinates $=\frac{\text{area of diagram}}{\text{length of base}} = \frac{34}{12}=2.83 \mathrm{~cm}$
    Since 1 cm represents 100 kPa , mean pressure in the cylinder $=2.83 \mathrm{~cm} \times 100 \mathrm{~kPa}/\mathrm{cm}=\mathbf{283} \mathbf{~ k P a}$
