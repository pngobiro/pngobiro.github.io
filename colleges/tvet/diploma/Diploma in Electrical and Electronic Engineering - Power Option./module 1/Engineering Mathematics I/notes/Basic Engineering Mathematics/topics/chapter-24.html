Cartesian and polar co-ordinates

üéØ 24.1 Introduction

There are two ways in which the position of a point in a plane can be represented. These are

  • (a) Cartesian co-ordinates, i.e. $(x, y)$.
  • (b) Polar co-ordinates, i.e. $(r, \theta)$, where $r$ is a radius from a fixed point and $\theta$ is an angle from a fixed point.

üîÑ 24.2 Changing from Cartesian to polar co-ordinates

In Figure 24.1, if lengths $x$ and $y$ are known then the length of $r$ can be obtained from Pythagoras' theorem (see Chapter 21) since $OPQ$ is a right-angled triangle.

Figure 24.1

Hence, $r^{2}=(x^{2}+y^{2})$, from which $\quad \boldsymbol{r}=\sqrt{\boldsymbol{x}^{2}+\boldsymbol{y}^{\mathbf{2}}}$

From trigonometric ratios (see Chapter 21), $\tan \theta=\frac{y}{x}$ from which $\theta=\tan^{-1} \frac{y}{x}$

$r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}$ and $\theta=\tan^{-1} \frac{y}{x}$ are the two formulae we need to change from Cartesian to polar co-ordinates. The angle $\theta$, which may be expressed in degrees or radians, must always be measured from the positive $x$-axis; i.e., measured from the line $OQ$ in Figure 24.1. It is suggested that when changing from Cartesian to polar co-ordinates a diagram should always be sketched.

üí° Problem 1. Change the Cartesian co-ordinates $(3,4)$ into polar co-ordinates

A diagram representing the point $(3,4)$ is shown in Figure 24.2.

Figure 24.2

From Pythagoras' theorem, $r=\sqrt{3^{2}+4^{2}}=5$ (note that -5 has no meaning in this context).

By trigonometric ratios, $\theta=\tan^{-1} \frac{4}{3}=53.13^{\circ}$ or 0.927 rad .

Note that $53.13^{\circ}=53.13 \times \frac{\pi}{180} \mathrm{~rad}=0.927 \mathrm{~rad}$.

Hence, (3, 4) in Cartesian co-ordinates corresponds to (5, 53.13$^{\circ}$) or (5, 0.927 rad) in polar co-ordinates.

üí° Problem 2. Express in polar co-ordinates the position (-4, 3)

A diagram representing the point using the Cartesian co-ordinates (-4,3) is shown in Figure 24.3.

Figure 24.3

From Pythagoras' theorem, $r=\sqrt{4^{2}+3^{2}}=5$

By trigonometric ratios, $\quad \alpha=\tan^{-1} \frac{3}{4}=36.87^{\circ}$ or 0.644 rad

Hence, $\theta=180^{\circ}-36.87^{\circ}=143.13^{\circ}$

or $\quad \theta=\pi-0.644=2.498 \mathrm{~rad}$

Hence, the position of point P in polar co-ordinate form is (5, 143.13$^{\circ}$) or (5, 2.498 rad).

A sketch showing the position (-5,-12) is shown in Figure 24.4.

$r=\sqrt{5^{2}+12^{2}}=13$ and $\alpha=\tan^{-1} \frac{12}{5}=67.38^{\circ}$ or 1.176 rad

From the right-angled triangle $OPQ$ in Figure 24.6, $\cos \theta=\frac{x}{r}$ and $\sin \theta=\frac{y}{r}$ from trigonometric ratios

Hence, $x=r \cos \theta$ and $y=r \sin \theta$.

If lengths $r$ and angle $\theta$ are known then $x=r \cos \theta$ and $y=r \sin \theta$ are the two formulae we need to change from polar to Cartesian co-ordinates.

üîÑ 24.4 Use of Pol/Rec functions on calculators

Another name for Cartesian co-ordinates is rectangular co-ordinates. Many scientific notation calculators have Pol and Rec functions. 'Rec' is an abbreviation of 'rectangular' (i.e. Cartesian) and 'Pol' is an abbreviation of 'polar'. Check the operation manual for your particular calculator to determine how to use these two functions. They make changing from Cartesian to polar coordinates, and vice-versa, so much quicker and easier. For example, with the Casio fx-83ES calculator, or similar, to change the Cartesian number (3,4) into polar form, the following procedure is adopted.

  • 1. Press 'shift'
  • 2. Press 'Pol'
  • 3. Enter 3
  • 4. Enter 'comma' (obtained by 'shift' then ) )
  • 5. Enter 4
  • 6. Press )
  • 7. Press =

The answer is $\boldsymbol{r}=\mathbf{5}, \boldsymbol{\theta}=\mathbf{53.13}^{\circ}$

Hence, (3, 4) in Cartesian form is the same as $(5,53.13^{\circ})$ in polar form.

If the angle is required in radians, then before repeating the above procedure press 'shift', 'mode' and then 4 to change your calculator to radian mode.

Similarly, to change the polar form number $(7,126^{\circ})$ into Cartesian or rectangular form, adopt the following procedure.

  • 1. Press 'shift'
  • 2. Press 'Rec'
  • 3. Enter 7
  • 4. Enter 'comma'
  • 5. Enter 126 (assuming your calculator is in degrees mode)
  • 6. Press )
  • 7. Press =

The answer is $\boldsymbol{X}=\mathbf{-4.11}$ and, scrolling across, $\boldsymbol{Y}=\mathbf{5.66}$, correct to 2 decimal places.

Hence, $(7,126^{\circ})$ in polar form is the same as (-4.11, 5.66) in rectangular or Cartesian form.
