Graphs reducing non-linear laws to linear form

üéØ 18.1 Introduction

In Chapter 17 we discovered that the equation of a straight line graph is of the form $y=mx+c$, where $m$ is the gradient and $c$ is the $y$-axis intercept. This chapter explains how the law of a graph can still be determined even when it is not of the linear form $y=mx+c$. The method used is called determination of law and is explained in the following sections.

üìà 18.2 Determination of law

Frequently, the relationship between two variables, say $x$ and $y$, is not a linear one; i.e., when $x$ is plotted against $y$ a curve results. In such cases the non-linear equation may be modified to the linear form, $y=mx+c$, so that the constants, and thus the law relating the variables, can be determined. This technique is called 'determination of law'.

Some examples of the reduction of equations to linear form include

  • (i) $y=ax^{2}+b$ compares with $Y=mX+c$, where $m=a, c=b$ and $X=x^{2}$.
    Hence, $y$ is plotted vertically against $x^{2}$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $a$ and $y$-axis intercept $b$.
  • (ii) $y=\frac{a}{x}+b$, i.e. $y=a\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)+b$
    $y$ is plotted vertically against $\frac{1}{x}$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $a$ and $y$-axis intercept $b$.
  • (iii) $y=ax^{2}+bx$
    Dividing both sides by $x$ gives $\frac{y}{x}=ax+b$.
    Comparing with $Y=mX+c$ shows that $\frac{y}{x}$ is plotted vertically against $x$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $a$ and $\frac{y}{x}$ axis intercept $b$.

Here are some worked problems to demonstrate determination of law.

üí° Problem 1. Experimental values of $x$ and $y$, shown below, are believed to be related by the law $y=ax^{2}+b$. By plotting a suitable graph, verify this law and determine approximate values of $a$ and $b$
$x$12345
$y$9.815.224.236.553.0

If $y$ is plotted against $x$ a curve results and it is not possible to determine the values of constants $a$ and $b$ from the curve.

Comparing $y=ax^{2}+b$ with $Y=mX+c$ shows that $y$ is to be plotted vertically against $x^{2}$ horizontally. A table of values is drawn up as shown below.

$x$12345
$x^{2}$1491625
$y$9.815.224.236.553.0

A graph of $y$ against $x^{2}$ is shown in Figure 18.1, with the best straight line drawn through the points. Since a straight line graph results, the law is verified.

üí° Problem 3. The solubility $s$ of potassium chlorate is shown by the following table.
$t^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$10203040506080100
$s$4.97.611.115.420.426.440.658.0
The relationship between $s$ and $t$ is thought to be of the form $s=3+at+bt^{2}$. Plot a graph to test the supposition and use the graph to find approximate values of $a$ and $b$. Hence, calculate the solubility of potassium chlorate at $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$

Rearranging $s=3+at+bt^{2}$ gives

$$s-3 = at+bt^{2}$$

and $\frac{s-3}{t}=a+bt$

or $\frac{s-3}{t}=bt+a$

which is of the form $\quad Y=mX+c$

This shows that $\frac{s-3}{t}$ is to be plotted vertically and $t$ horizontally, with gradient $b$ and vertical axis intercept $a$.

Another table of values is drawn up as shown below.

$t$10203040506080100
$s$4.97.611.115.420.426.440.658.0
$\frac{s-3}{t}$0.190.230.270.310.350.390.470.55

A graph of $\frac{s-3}{t}$ against $t$ is shown plotted in Figure 18.3. A straight line fits the points, which shows that $s$ and $t$ are related by $s=3+at+bt^{2}$.

Figure 18.3

$$\text{Gradient of straight line, } b=\frac{AB}{BC} = \frac{0.39-0.19}{60-10}$$ $$= \frac{0.20}{50}=\mathbf{0.004}$$

Vertical axis intercept, $\boldsymbol{a}=\mathbf{0.15}$

Hence, the law of the graph is $\boldsymbol{s}=\mathbf{3+0.15t+0.004} \boldsymbol{t}^{\mathbf{2}}$.

The solubility of potassium chlorate at $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ is given by

$$\begin{aligned} s &= 3+0.15(70)+0.004(70)^{2} \\ &= 3+10.5+19.6=\mathbf{33.1} \end{aligned}$$

üìê 18.3 Revision of laws of logarithms

The laws of logarithms were stated in Chapter 15 as follows:

$$\log (\mathrm{A} \times \mathrm{B}) = \log \mathrm{A} + \log \mathrm{B}$$ $$\log \left(\frac{\mathrm{A}}{\mathrm{B}}\right) = \log \mathrm{A} - \log \mathrm{B}$$ $$\log \mathrm{A}^{n} = n \times \log \mathrm{A}$$

Also, $\quad \ln e=1$ and if, say, $\lg x=1.5$, then $x=10^{1.5}=31.62$

Further, if $3^{x}=7$ then $\lg 3^{x}=\lg 7$ and $x \lg 3=\lg 7$, from which $x=\frac{\lg 7}{\lg 3}=1.771$

These laws and techniques are used whenever non-linear laws of the form $y=ax^{n}, y=ab^{x}$ and $y=ae^{bx}$ are reduced to linear form with the values of $a$ and $b$ needing to be calculated. This is demonstrated in the following section.

üìà 18.4 Determination of laws involving logarithms

Examples of the reduction of equations to linear form involving logarithms include

  • (a) $y=ax^{n}$
    Taking logarithms to a base of 10 of both sides gives
    $$\begin{aligned} \lg y &= \lg (ax^{n}) \\ &= \lg a + \lg x^{n} \quad \text{by law (1)} \\ \text{i.e.} \quad \lg y &= n\lg x + \lg a \quad \text{by law (3)} \end{aligned}$$
    which compares with $Y=mX+c$
    and shows that $\lg y$ is plotted vertically against $\lg x$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $\boldsymbol{n}$ and $\lg \boldsymbol{y}$-axis intercept $\lg a$.
    See worked Problems 4 and 5 to demonstrate how this law is determined.
  • (b) $y=ab^{x}$
    Taking logarithms to a base of 10 of both sides gives
    $$\begin{aligned} \lg y &= \lg (ab^{x}) \\ \text{i.e.} \quad \lg y &= \lg a + \lg b^{x} \quad \text{by law (1)} \\ \text{i.e.} \quad \lg y &= \lg a + x\lg b \quad \text{by law (3)} \\ \text{i.e.} \quad \lg y &= x\lg b + \lg a \quad \\ \text{or} \quad \lg y &= (\lg b)x + \lg a \end{aligned}$$
    which compares with $Y=mX+c$
    and shows that $\lg \boldsymbol{y}$ is plotted vertically against $\boldsymbol{x}$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $\lg b$ and $\lg y$-axis intercept $\lg a$.
    See worked Problem 6 to demonstrate how this law is determined.
  • (c) $y=ae^{bx}$
    Taking logarithms to a base of $e$ of both sides
    gives $\quad \ln y=\ln (ae^{bx})$
    i.e. $\quad \ln y=\ln a+\ln e^{bx} \quad$ by law (1)
    i.e. $\quad \ln y=\ln a+bx\ln e \quad$ by law (3)
    i.e. $\quad \ln y=bx+\ln a \quad$ since $\ln e=1$
    which compares with $Y=mX+c$
    and shows that $\ln \boldsymbol{y}$ is plotted vertically against $\boldsymbol{x}$ horizontally to produce a straight line graph of gradient $\boldsymbol{b}$ and $\ln \boldsymbol{y}$-axis intercept $\ln a$.
    See worked Problem 7 to demonstrate how this law is determined.

üí° Problem 4. The current flowing in, and the power dissipated by, a resistor are measured experimentally for various values and the results are as shown below.
Current, $I$ (amperes)2.23.64.15.66.8
Power, $P$ (watts)1163114037531110
Show that the law relating current and power is of the form $P=RI^{n}$, where $R$ and $n$ are constants, and determine the law

Taking logarithms to a base of 10 of both sides of $P=RI^{n}$ gives

$$\lg P = \lg (RI^{n}) = \lg R + \lg I^{n} = \lg R + n\lg I$$

by the laws of logarithms

i.e. $\quad \lg P = n\lg I + \lg R$

which is of the form $Y=mX+c$, showing that $\lg P$ is to be plotted vertically against $\lg I$ horizontally.

A table of values for $\lg I$ and $\lg P$ is drawn up as shown below.

$I$2.23.64.15.66.8
$\lg I$0.3420.5560.6130.7480.833
$P$1163114037531110
$\lg P$2.0642.4932.6052.8773.045

A graph of $\lg P$ against $\lg I$ is shown in Figure 18.4 and, since a straight line results, the law $P=RI^{\boldsymbol{n}}$ is verified.

Figure 18.4

$$\text{Gradient of straight line, } \boldsymbol{n} = \frac{AB}{BC} = \frac{2.98-2.18}{0.8-0.4}$$ $$= \frac{0.80}{0.4}=\mathbf{2}$$

It is not possible to determine the vertical axis intercept on sight since the horizontal axis scale does not start at zero. Selecting any point from the graph, say point D, where $\lg I=0.70$ and $\lg P=2.78$ and substituting values into

$$\lg P = n\lg I + \lg R$$

gives $2.78=(2)(0.70)+\lg R$

from which, $\lg R=2.78-1.40=1.38$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{R}=\operatorname{antilog} 1.38=10^{1.38}=\mathbf{24.0}$

Hence, the law of the graph is $\boldsymbol{P}=\mathbf{24.0} \boldsymbol{I}^{\mathbf{2}}$

üí° Problem 7. The current $i \mathrm{~mA}$ flowing in a capacitor which is being discharged varies with time $t \mathrm{~ms}$, as shown below.
$i$ (mA)20361.1422.496.132.490.615
$t$ (ms)100160210275320390
Show that these results are related by a law of the form $i=Ie^{t/T}$, where $I$ and $T$ are constants. Determine the approximate values of $I$ and $T$

Taking Napierian logarithms of both sides of $i=Ie^{t/T}$ gives $\ln i = \ln (Ie^{t/T})=\ln I + \ln e^{t/T} = \ln I + \frac{t}{T}\ln e$

i.e. $\quad \ln i = \ln I + \frac{t}{T}$ since $\ln e=1$

or $\quad \ln i = \left(\frac{1}{T}\right)t+\ln I$

which compares with $\quad y=mx+c$

showing that $\ln i$ is plotted vertically against $t$ horizontally, with gradient $\frac{1}{T}$ and vertical-axis intercept $\ln I$.

Another table of values is drawn up as shown below.

$t$100160210275320390
$i$20361.1422.496.132.490.615
$\ln i$5.314.113.111.810.91-0.49

A graph of $\ln i$ against $t$ is shown in Figure 18.7 and, since a straight line results, the law $i=I \boldsymbol{e}^{\boldsymbol{t} /T}$ is verified.

Figure 18.7

Gradient of straight line, $\quad \frac{1}{T} = \frac{AB}{BC} = \frac{5.30-1.30}{100-300} = \frac{4.0}{-200}=-0.02$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{T}=\frac{1}{-0.02}=\mathbf{-50}$

Selecting any point on the graph, say point D, where $t=200$ and $\ln i=3.31$, and substituting into $\ln i = \left(\frac{1}{T}\right)t+\ln I$ gives $3.31=-\frac{1}{50}(200)+\ln I$

from which, $\quad \ln I=3.31+4.0=7.31$

and $\quad \boldsymbol{I}=\operatorname{antilog} 7.31=e^{7.31}=1495$ or $\mathbf{1 500}$ correct to 3 significant figures.

Hence, the law of the graph is $\boldsymbol{i}=\mathbf{1500} \boldsymbol{e}^{-\boldsymbol{t} /50}$
