Vectors

üéØ 29.1 Introduction

This chapter initially explains the difference between scalar and vector quantities and shows how a vector is drawn and represented.

Any object that is acted upon by an external force will respond to that force by moving in the line of the force. However, if two or more forces act simultaneously, the result is more difficult to predict; the ability to add two or more vectors then becomes important.

This chapter thus shows how vectors are added and subtracted, both by drawing and by calculation, and how finding the resultant of two or more vectors has many uses in engineering. (Resultant means the single vector which would have the same effect as the individual vectors.) Relative velocities and vector $\boldsymbol{i}, \boldsymbol{j}, \boldsymbol{k}$ notation are also briefly explained.

‚û°Ô∏è 29.2 Scalars and vectors

The time taken to fill a water tank may be measured as, say, 50 s . Similarly, the temperature in a room may be measured as, say, $16^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ or the mass of a bearing may be measured as, say, 3 kg . Quantities such as time, temperature and mass are entirely defined by a numerical value and are called scalars or scalar quantities.

Not all quantities are like this. Some are defined by more than just size; some also have direction. For example, the velocity of a car may be $90 \mathrm{~km}/\mathrm{h}$ due west, a force of 20 N may act vertically downwards, or an acceleration of $10 \mathrm{~m}/\mathrm{s}^{2}$ may act at $50^{\circ}$ to the horizontal.

Quantities such as velocity, force and acceleration, which have both a magnitude and a direction, are called vectors.

‚úèÔ∏è 29.3 Drawing a vector

A vector quantity can be represented graphically by a line, drawn so that

  • (a) the length of the line denotes the magnitude of the quantity, and
  • (b) the direction of the line denotes the direction in which the vector quantity acts.

An arrow is used to denote the sense, or direction, of the vector.

The arrow end of a vector is called the 'nose' and the other end the 'tail'. For example, a force of 9 N acting at $45^{\circ}$ to the horizontal is shown in Figure 29.1. Note that an angle of +45$^{\circ}$ is drawn from the horizontal and moves anticlockwise.

Figure 29.1

A velocity of $20 \mathrm{~m}/\mathrm{s}$ at $-60^{\circ}$ is shown in Figure 29.2. Note that an angle of -60$^{\circ}$ is drawn from the horizontal and moves clockwise.

Figure 29.2

29.3.1 Representing a vector

There are a number of ways of representing vector quantities. These include

  • (a) Using bold print.
  • (b) $\overrightarrow{A B}$ where an arrow above two capital letters denotes the sense of direction, where A is the starting point and B the end point of the vector.
  • (c) $\overline{A B}$ or $\bar{a}$; i.e., a line over the top of letter.
  • (d) $\underline{a}$; i.e., underlined letter.

The force of 9 N at $45^{\circ}$ shown in Figure 29.1 may be represented as

$$\mathbf{0} \boldsymbol{a} \text{ or } \overline{0} \vec{a} \text{ or } \overline{0a}$$

The magnitude of the force is $0a$.

Similarly, the velocity of $20 \mathrm{~m}/\mathrm{s}$ at $-60^{\circ}$ shown in Figure 29.2 may be represented as

$$\mathbf{0} \boldsymbol{b} \text{ or } \overline{0} \vec{b} \text{ or } \overline{0b}$$

The magnitude of the velocity is $0b$.

In this chapter a vector quantity is denoted by bold print.

‚ûï 29.4 Addition of vectors by drawing

Adding two or more vectors by drawing assumes that a ruler, pencil and protractor are available. Results obtained by drawing are naturally not as accurate as those obtained by calculation.

  • (a) Nose-to-tail method
    Two force vectors, $F_{1}$ and $F_{2}$, are shown in Figure 29.3. When an object is subjected to more than one force, the resultant of the forces is found by the addition of vectors.
    Figure 29.3
    To add forces $F_{1}$ and $F_{2}$,
    1. Force $F_{1}$ is drawn to scale horizontally, shown as $\mathbf{0}\boldsymbol{a}$ in Figure 29.4.
    2. From the nose of $F_{1}$, force $F_{2}$ is drawn at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal, shown as $\boldsymbol{ab}$.
    3. The resultant force is given by length $\mathbf{0b}$, which may be measured.

    Figure 29.4
    This procedure is called the 'nose-to-tail' or 'triangle' method.
  • (b) Parallelogram method
    To add the two force vectors, $F_{1}$ and $F_{2}$ of Figure 29.3,
    1. A line $cb$ is constructed which is parallel to and equal in length to $\mathbf{0}\boldsymbol{a}$ (see Figure 29.5).
    2. A line $ab$ is constructed which is parallel to and equal in length to $\mathbf{0c}$.
    3. The resultant force is given by the diagonal of the parallelogram; i.e., length $\mathbf{0b}$.

    Figure 29.5
    This procedure is called the 'parallelogram' method.

‚ÜóÔ∏è 29.5 Resolving vectors into horizontal and vertical components

A force vector $\boldsymbol{F}$ is shown in Figure 29.13 at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal. Such a vector can be resolved into two components such that the vector addition of the components is equal to the original vector.

Figure 29.13

The two components usually taken are a horizontal component and a vertical component. If a right-angled triangle is constructed as shown in Figure 29.14, 0a is called the horizontal component of F and ab is called the vertical component of F.

Figure 29.14

From trigonometry (see Chapter 21 and remember SOH CAH TOA),

$\cos \theta=\frac{0a}{0b}$, from which $0a=0b \cos \theta=F \cos \theta$

i.e. the horizontal component of $\boldsymbol{F}=\boldsymbol{F} \cos \boldsymbol{\theta}$, and

$\sin \theta=\frac{ab}{0b}$ from which, $ab=0b \sin \theta=F \sin \theta$

i.e. the vertical component of $\boldsymbol{F}=\boldsymbol{F} \sin \boldsymbol{\theta}$.

‚ûï 29.6 Addition of vectors by calculation

Two force vectors, $F_{1}$ and $F_{2}$, are shown in Figure 29.18, $F_{1}$ being at an angle of $\theta_{1}$ and $F_{2}$ at an angle of $\theta_{2}$.

Figure 29.18

A method of adding two vectors together is to use horizontal and vertical components.

The horizontal component of force $F_{1}$ is $F_{1} \cos \theta_{1}$ and the horizontal component of force $F_{2}$ is $F_{2} \cos \theta_{2}$. The total horizontal component of the two forces, $H=F_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+F_{2} \cos \theta_{2}$

The vertical component of force $F_{1}$ is $F_{1} \sin \theta_{1}$ and the vertical component of force $F_{2}$ is $F_{2} \sin \theta_{2}$. The total vertical component of the two forces, $V=F_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+F_{2} \sin \theta_{2}$

Since we have $H$ and $V$, the resultant of $F_{1}$ and $F_{2}$ is obtained by using the theorem of Pythagoras. From Figure 29.19,

$$0b^{2}=H^{2}+V^{2}$$

i.e. $\quad \text{resultant} = \sqrt{\boldsymbol{H}^{2}+\boldsymbol{V}^{2}}$ at an angle given by $\boldsymbol{\theta}=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\boldsymbol{V}}{\boldsymbol{H}}\right)$

Figure 29.19

üí° Problem 7. A force of 5 N is inclined at an angle of $45^{\circ}$ to a second force of 8 N , both forces acting at a point. Calculate the magnitude of the resultant of these two forces and the direction of the resultant with respect to the 8 N force

The two forces are shown in Figure 29.20.

Figure 29.20

The horizontal component of the 8 N force is $8 \cos 0^{\circ}$ and the horizontal component of the 5 N force is $5 \cos 45^{\circ}$. The total horizontal component of the two forces, $\boldsymbol{H}=8 \cos 0^{\circ}+5 \cos 45^{\circ}=8+3.5355=\mathbf{11.5355}$

The vertical component of the 8 N force is $8 \sin 0^{\circ}$ and the vertical component of the 5 N force is $5 \sin 45^{\circ}$. The total vertical component of the two forces, $\boldsymbol{V}=8 \sin 0^{\circ}+5 \sin 45^{\circ}=0+3.5355=\mathbf{3.5355}$

Figure 29.21

From Figure 29.21, magnitude of resultant vector $=\sqrt{H^{2}+V^{2}} = \sqrt{11.5355^{2}+3.5355^{2}}=\mathbf{12.07} \mathbf{~ N}$

The direction of the resultant vector, $\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{V}{H}\right) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3.5355}{11.5355}\right) = \tan^{-1} 0.30648866 \ldots=\mathbf{17.04}^{\circ}$

Thus, the resultant of the two forces is a single vector of 12.07 N at $17.04^{\circ}$ to the 8 N vector.

üí° Problem 8. Forces of 15 N and 10 N are at an angle of $90^{\circ}$ to each other as shown in Figure 29.22. Calculate the magnitude of the resultant of these two forces and its direction with respect to the 15 N force
Figure 29.22

The horizontal component of the 15 N force is $15 \cos 0^{\circ}$ and the horizontal component of the 10 N force is $10 \cos 90^{\circ}$. The total horizontal component of the two velocities, $\boldsymbol{H}=15 \cos 0^{\circ}+10 \cos 90^{\circ}=15+0=\mathbf{15}$

The vertical component of the 15 N force is $15 \sin 0^{\circ}$ and the vertical component of the 10 N force is $10 \sin 90^{\circ}$. The total vertical component of the two velocities, $\boldsymbol{V}=15 \sin 0^{\circ}+10 \sin 90^{\circ}=0+10=\mathbf{10}$

Magnitude of resultant vector $=\sqrt{H^{2}+V^{2}}=\sqrt{15^{2}+10^{2}}=\mathbf{18.03} \mathbf{~ N}$

The direction of the resultant vector, $\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{V}{H}\right)=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{10}{15}\right)=\mathbf{33.69^{\circ}}$

Thus, the resultant of the two forces is a single vector of 18.03 N at $33.69^{\circ}$ to the 15 N vector.

üí® 29.8 Relative velocity

For relative velocity problems, some fixed datum point needs to be selected. This is often a fixed point on the earth's surface. In any vector equation, only the start and finish points affect the resultant vector of a system. Two different systems are shown in Figure 29.39, but, in each of the systems, the resultant vector is $\boldsymbol{ad}$.

Figure 29.39

The vector equation of the system shown in Figure 29.39(a) is $\boldsymbol{ad}=\boldsymbol{ab}+\boldsymbol{bd}$

and that for the system shown in Figure 29.39(b) is $\boldsymbol{ad}=\boldsymbol{ab}+\boldsymbol{bc}+\boldsymbol{cd}$

Thus, in vector equations of this form, only the first and last letters, a and d, respectively, fix the magnitude and direction of the resultant vector. This principle is used in relative velocity problems.

üí° Problem 13. Two cars, $P$ and $Q$, are travelling towards the junction of two roads which are at right angles to one another. Car $P$ has a velocity of $45 \mathrm{~km}/\mathrm{h}$ due east and car $Q$ a velocity of $55 \mathrm{~km}/\mathrm{h}$ due south. Calculate (a) the velocity of car $P$ relative to car $Q$ and (b) the velocity of car $Q$ relative to car $P$

  • (a) The directions of the cars are shown in Figure 29.40(a), which is called a space diagram. The velocity diagram is shown in Figure 29.40(b), in which $\boldsymbol{p e}$ is taken as the velocity of car P relative to point e on the earth's surface. The velocity of P relative to Q is vector $\boldsymbol{p q}$ and the vector equation is $\boldsymbol{p q}=\boldsymbol{p e} \boldsymbol{+} \boldsymbol{e q}$. Hence, the vector directions are as shown, $\boldsymbol{e q}$ being in the opposite direction to $\boldsymbol{qe}$.
    Figure 29.40
    From the geometry of the vector triangle, the magnitude of pq $=\sqrt{45^{2}+55^{2}}=71.06 \mathrm{~km}/\mathrm{h}$ and the direction of pq $=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{55}{45}\right)=50.71^{\circ}$
    That is, the velocity of car P relative to car Q is $\mathbf{71.06} \mathbf{~ k m}/\mathbf{h}$ at $\mathbf{50.71}^{\circ}$
  • (b) The velocity of car Q relative to car P is given by the vector equation $\boldsymbol{q} \boldsymbol{p}=\boldsymbol{q} \boldsymbol{e}+\boldsymbol{e} \boldsymbol{p}$ and the vector diagram is as shown in Figure 29.40(c), having $\boldsymbol{e p}$ opposite in direction to $\boldsymbol{p e}$.
    From the geometry of this vector triangle, the magnitude of $\boldsymbol{q} \boldsymbol{p}=\sqrt{45^{2}+55^{2}}=71.06 \mathrm{~m}/\mathrm{s}$ and the direction of $\boldsymbol{q} \boldsymbol{p}=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{55}{45}\right)=50.71^{\circ}$ but must lie in the third quadrant; i.e., the required angle is $180^{\circ}+50.71^{\circ}=230.71^{\circ}$
    That is, the velocity of car Q relative to car P is $\mathbf{71.06} \mathbf{~ m}/\mathbf{s}$ at $\mathbf{230.71}^{\circ}$

‚õõ 29.9 $i, j$ and $k$ notation

A method of completely specifying the direction of a vector in space relative to some reference point is to use three unit vectors, $\boldsymbol{i}, \boldsymbol{j}$ and $\boldsymbol{k}$, mutually at right angles to each other, as shown in Figure 29.41.

Figure 29.41

Calculations involving vectors given in $\boldsymbol{i}, \boldsymbol{j}, \boldsymbol{k}$ notation are carried out in exactly the same way as standard algebraic calculations, as shown in the worked examples below.

üí° Problem 14. Determine $(3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{j}+2\boldsymbol{k})-(4\boldsymbol{i}-3\boldsymbol{j}+2\boldsymbol{k})$

$$\begin{aligned} (3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{j}+2\boldsymbol{k})-(4\boldsymbol{i}-3\boldsymbol{j}+2\boldsymbol{k}) &= 3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{j}+2\boldsymbol{k}-4\boldsymbol{i}+3\boldsymbol{j}-2\boldsymbol{k} \\ &= \mathbf{-i+5j} \end{aligned}$$

üí° Problem 15. Given $\boldsymbol{p}=3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{k}, \boldsymbol{q}=4\boldsymbol{i}-2\boldsymbol{j}+3\boldsymbol{k}$ and $\boldsymbol{r}=-3\boldsymbol{i}+5\boldsymbol{j}-4\boldsymbol{k}$, determine
(a) $-\boldsymbol{r}$ (b) $3\boldsymbol{p}$ (c) $2\boldsymbol{p}+3\boldsymbol{q}$ (d) $-\boldsymbol{p}+2\boldsymbol{r}$ (e) $0.2\boldsymbol{p}+0.6\boldsymbol{q}-3.2\boldsymbol{r}$

  • (a) $-\boldsymbol{r}=-(-3\boldsymbol{i}+5\boldsymbol{j}-4\boldsymbol{k})=\mathbf{+3i-5j+4k}$
  • (b) $3\boldsymbol{p}=3(3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{k})=\mathbf{9i+6k}$
  • (c) $2\boldsymbol{p}+3\boldsymbol{q}=2(3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{k})+3(4\boldsymbol{i}-2\boldsymbol{j}+3\boldsymbol{k}) = 6\boldsymbol{i}+4\boldsymbol{k}+12\boldsymbol{i}-6\boldsymbol{j}+9\boldsymbol{k} = \mathbf{18i-6j+13k}$
  • (d) $- \boldsymbol{p}+2\boldsymbol{r} = -(3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{k})+2(-3\boldsymbol{i}+5\boldsymbol{j}-4\boldsymbol{k}) = -3\boldsymbol{i}-2\boldsymbol{k}+(-6\boldsymbol{i}+10\boldsymbol{j}-8\boldsymbol{k}) = -3\boldsymbol{i}-2\boldsymbol{k}-6\boldsymbol{i}+10\boldsymbol{j}-8\boldsymbol{k} = \mathbf{-9i+10j-10k}$
  • (e) $0.2\boldsymbol{p}+0.6\boldsymbol{q}-3.2\boldsymbol{r} = 0.2(3\boldsymbol{i}+2\boldsymbol{k})+0.6(4\boldsymbol{i}-2\boldsymbol{j}+3\boldsymbol{k}) -3.2(-3\boldsymbol{i}+5\boldsymbol{j}-4\boldsymbol{k}) = 0.6\boldsymbol{i}+0.4\boldsymbol{k}+2.4\boldsymbol{i}-1.2\boldsymbol{j}+1.8\boldsymbol{k} +9.6\boldsymbol{i}-16\boldsymbol{j}+12.8\boldsymbol{k} = \mathbf{12.6i-17.2j+15k}$
