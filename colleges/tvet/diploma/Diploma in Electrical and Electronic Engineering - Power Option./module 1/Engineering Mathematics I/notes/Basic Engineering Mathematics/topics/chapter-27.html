Volumes of common solids

üéØ 27.1 Introduction

The volume of any solid is a measure of the space occupied by the solid. Volume is measured in cubic units such as $\mathrm{mm}^{3}, \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$ and $\mathrm{m}^{3}$.

This chapter deals with finding volumes of common solids; in engineering it is often important to be able to calculate volume or capacity to estimate, say, the amount of liquid, such as water, oil or petrol, in different shaped containers.

A prism is a solid with a constant cross-section and with two ends parallel. The shape of the end is used to describe the prism. For example, there are rectangular prisms (called cuboids), triangular prisms and circular prisms (called cylinders).

On completing this chapter you will be able to calculate the volumes and surface areas of rectangular and other prisms, cylinders, pyramids, cones and spheres, together with frusta of pyramids and cones. Volumes of similar shapes are also considered.

üßä 27.2 Volumes and surface areas of common shapes

27.2.1 Cuboids or rectangular prisms

A cuboid is a solid figure bounded by six rectangular faces; all angles are right angles and opposite faces are equal. A typical cuboid is shown in Figure 27.1 with length $l$, breadth $b$ and height $h$.

$$\text{Volume of cuboid}=l \times b \times h$$

and

$$\text{surface area}=2bh+2hl+2lb=2(bh+hl+lb)$$

Figure 27.1

A cube is a square prism. If all the sides of a cube are $x$ then

$$\text{Volume}=x^{3} \text{ and surface area }=6x^{2}$$

üí° Problem 1. A cuboid has dimensions of 12 cm by 4 cm by 3 cm . Determine (a) its volume and (b) its total surface area

The cuboid is similar to that in Figure 27.1, with $l=12 \mathrm{~cm}, b=4 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $h=3 \mathrm{~cm}$.

  • (a) Volume of cuboid $=l \times b \times h = 12 \times 4 \times 3 = \mathbf{144} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$
  • (b) Surface area $=2(bh+hl+lb) = 2(4 \times 3+3 \times 12+12 \times 4) = 2(12+36+48) = 2 \times 96 = \mathbf{192} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 2. An oil tank is the shape of a cube, each edge being of length 1.5 m . Determine (a) the maximum capacity of the tank in $\mathrm{m}^{3}$ and litres and (b) its total surface area ignoring input and output orifices

  • (a) Volume of oil tank = volume of cube $= 1.5 \mathrm{~m} \times 1.5 \mathrm{~m} \times 1.5 \mathrm{~m} = 1.5^{3} \mathrm{~m}^{3}=\mathbf{3.375} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
    $1 \mathrm{~m}^{3}=100 \mathrm{~cm} \times 100 \mathrm{~cm} \times 100 \mathrm{~cm}=10^{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$.
    Hence, volume of tank $=3.375 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$
    1 litre $=1000 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$, hence oil tank capacity $=\frac{3.375 \times 10^{6}}{1000} \text{ litres}=\mathbf{3375} \text{ litres}$
  • (b) Surface area of one side $=1.5 \mathrm{~m} \times 1.5 \mathrm{~m} = 2.25 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$
    A cube has six identical sides, hence total surface area of oil tank $=6 \times 2.25 = 13.5 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 3. A water tank is the shape of a rectangular prism having length 2 m , breadth 75 cm and height 500 mm . Determine the capacity of the tank in (a) $\mathrm{m}^{3}$ (b) $\mathrm{cm}^{3}$ (c) litres

Capacity means volume; when dealing with liquids, the word capacity is usually used.

The water tank is similar in shape to that in Figure 27.1, with $l=2 \mathrm{~m}, b=75 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $h=500 \mathrm{~mm}$.

  • (a) Capacity of water tank $=l \times b \times h$. To use this formula, all dimensions must be in the same units. Thus, $l=2 \mathrm{~m}, b=0.75 \mathrm{~m}$ and $h=0.5 \mathrm{~m}$ (since $1 \mathrm{~m}=100 \mathrm{~cm}=1000 \mathrm{~mm}$).
    Hence, capacity of tank $=2 \times 0.75 \times 0.5=\mathbf{0.75} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
  • (b) $1 \mathrm{~m}^{3}=1 \mathrm{~m} \times 1 \mathrm{~m} \times 1 \mathrm{~m} = 100 \mathrm{~cm} \times 100 \mathrm{~cm} \times 100 \mathrm{~cm}$
    i.e., $\mathbf{1 \mathbf{~ m}^{3} = \mathbf{1000} \mathbf{000} = \mathbf{10}^{\mathbf{6}} \mathbf{c m}^{\mathbf{3}}}$. Hence, capacity $=0.75 \mathrm{~m}^{3}=0.75 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} = \mathbf{750} \mathbf{000} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
  • (c) 1 litre $=1000 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$. Hence, $750000 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}=\frac{750,000}{1000}=\mathbf{750} \text{ litres}$

27.2.2 Cylinders

A cylinder is a circular prism. A cylinder of radius $r$ and height $h$ is shown in Figure 27.2.

Figure 27.2

$$\text{Volume} = \pi r^{2}h$$
$$\text{Curved surface area} = 2\pi rh$$
$$\text{Total surface area} = 2\pi rh+2\pi r^{2}$$

Total surface area means the curved surface area plus the area of the two circular ends.

üí° Problem 4. A solid cylinder has a base diameter of 12 cm and a perpendicular height of 20 cm . Calculate (a) the volume and (b) the total surface area

  • (a) Volume $=\pi r^{2}h=\pi \times \left(\frac{12}{2}\right)^{2} \times 20 = 720\pi=\mathbf{2262} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
  • (b) Total surface area $=2\pi rh+2\pi r^{2} = (2 \times \pi \times 6 \times 20) + (2 \times \pi \times 6^{2}) = 240\pi+72\pi=312\pi=\mathbf{980} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 5. A copper pipe has the dimensions shown in Figure 27.3. Calculate the volume of copper in the pipe, in cubic metres.
Figure 27.3

Outer diameter, $D=25 \mathrm{~cm}=0.25 \mathrm{~m}$ and inner diameter, $d=12 \mathrm{~cm}=0.12 \mathrm{~m}$.

$$\text{Area of cross-section of copper} = \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4}-\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}=\frac{\pi(0.25)^{2}}{4}-\frac{\pi(0.12)^{2}}{4}$$ $$= 0.0491-0.0113=0.0378 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$$

Hence, volume of copper $= (\text{cross-sectional area}) \times \text{length of pipe} = 0.0378 \times 2.5 = \mathbf{0.0945} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}$

27.2.3 More prisms

A right-angled triangular prism is shown in Figure 27.4 with dimensions $b, h$ and $l$.

Figure 27.4

$$\text{Volume} = \frac{1}{2} bhl$$

and

$$\text{surface area} = \text{area of each end} + \text{area of three sides}$$

Notice that the volume is given by the area of the end (i.e. area of triangle $=\frac{1}{2} b h$) multiplied by the length $l$. In fact, the volume of any shaped prism is given by the area of an end multiplied by the length.

üí° Problem 6. Determine the volume (in $\mathrm{cm}^{3}$) of the shape shown in Figure 27.5
Figure 27.5

The solid shown in Figure 27.5 is a triangular prism. The volume $V$ of any prism is given by $V=Ah$, where $A$ is the cross-sectional area and $h$ is the perpendicular height. Hence,

$$\begin{aligned} \text{volume} &= \frac{1}{2} \times 16 \times 12 \times 40=3840 \mathrm{~mm}^{3} \\ &= \mathbf{3.840} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3} \quad (\text{since } 1 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}=1000 \mathrm{~mm}^{3})\end{aligned}$$

üí° Problem 7. Calculate the volume of the right-angled triangular prism shown in Figure 27.6. Also, determine its total surface area
Figure 27.6

$$\text{Volume of right-angled triangular prism} = \frac{1}{2}bhl = \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times 6 \times 40$$ $$\text{i.e. } \quad \text{volume} = \mathbf{960} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

Total surface area = area of each end + area of three sides.

In triangle ABC, $\quad AC^{2}=AB^{2}+BC^{2}$

from which, $\quad AC=\sqrt{AB^{2}+BC^{2}} = \sqrt{6^{2}+8^{2}} = 10 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, total surface area

$$\begin{aligned} &= 2\left(\frac{1}{2}bh\right)+(AC \times 40)+(BC \times 40)+(AB \times 40) \\ &= (8 \times 6)+(10 \times 40)+(8 \times 40)+(6 \times 40) \\ &= 48+400+320+240 \end{aligned}$$

i.e. total surface area = 1008 cm$^{2}$

üí° Problem 8. Calculate the volume and total surface area of the solid prism shown in Figure 27.7
Figure 27.7

The solid shown in Figure 27.7 is a trapezoidal prism.

$$\text{Volume of prism} = \text{cross-sectional area} \times \text{height}$$ $$=\frac{1}{2}(11+5)4 \times 15 = 32 \times 15 = \mathbf{480} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$
$$\text{Surface area of prism} = \text{sum of two trapeziums} + 4 \text{ rectangles}$$ $$= (2 \times 32)+(5 \times 15)+(11 \times 15)+2(5 \times 15)$$ $$= 64+75+165+150=\mathbf{454} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$$

27.2.4 Pyramids

Volume of any pyramid $= \frac{1}{3} \times \text{area of base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$

A square-based pyramid is shown in Figure 27.9 with base dimension $x$ by $x$ and the perpendicular height of the pyramid $h$. For the square-base pyramid shown, volume $=\frac{1}{3}x^{2}h$

Figure 27.9

üí° Problem 9. A square pyramid has a perpendicular height of 16 cm . If a side of the base is 6 cm , determine the volume of a pyramid

$$\text{Volume of pyramid} = \frac{1}{3} \times \text{area of base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$$ $$= \frac{1}{3} \times (6 \times 6) \times 16 = \mathbf{192} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$$

üí° Problem 10. Determine the volume and the total surface area of the square pyramid shown in Figure 27.10 if its perpendicular height is 12 cm .
Figure 27.10

$$\text{Volume of pyramid} = \frac{1}{3}(\text{area of base}) \times \text{perpendicular height}$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}(5 \times 5) \times 12 = \mathbf{100} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

The total surface area consists of a square base and 4 equal triangles.

Area of triangle ADE $= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{perpendicular height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times AC$

The length AC may be calculated using Pythagoras' theorem on triangle ABC, where AB = 12 cm and BC = $\frac{1}{2} \times 5 = 2.5 \mathrm{~cm}$.

$AC=\sqrt{AB^{2}+BC^{2}}=\sqrt{12^{2}+2.5^{2}}=12.26 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, area of triangle ADE $=\frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 12.26=30.65 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$

Total surface area of pyramid $=(5 \times 5)+4(30.65) = \mathbf{147.6} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 11. A rectangular prism of metal having dimensions of 5 cm by 6 cm by 18 cm is melted down and recast into a pyramid having a rectangular base measuring 6 cm by 10 cm . Calculate the perpendicular height of the pyramid, assuming no waste of metal

Volume of rectangular prism of metal $=5 \times 6 \times 18=540 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Volume of pyramid $= \frac{1}{3} \times \text{area of base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$

Hence, $\quad 540=\frac{1}{3} \times (6 \times 10) \times h$

from which, $\quad h=\frac{3 \times 540}{6 \times 10}=27 \mathrm{~cm}$

i.e. perpendicular height of pyramid = 27 cm

27.2.5 Cones

A cone is a circular-based pyramid. A cone of base radius $r$ and perpendicular height $h$ is shown in Figure 27.11.

Volume $=\frac{1}{3} \times \text{area of base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$

Figure 27.11

i.e. Volume $=\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h$

Curved surface area $=\pi r l$

Total surface area $=\pi r l+\pi r^{2}$

üí° Problem 12. Calculate the volume, in cubic centimetres, of a cone of radius 30 mm and perpendicular height 80 mm

$$\text{Volume of cone}=\frac{1}{3}\pi r^{2}h=\frac{1}{3} \times \pi \times 30^{2} \times 80 = 75398.2236\ldots \mathrm{~mm}^{3}$$

$1 \mathrm{~cm}=10 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $1 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}=10 \mathrm{~mm} \times 10 \mathrm{~mm} \times 10 \mathrm{~mm}=10^{3} \mathrm{~mm}^{3}$, or $1 \mathrm{~mm}^{3}=10^{-3} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Hence, $75398.2236\ldots \mathrm{~mm}^{3} = 75398.2236\ldots \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

i.e., volume = 75.40 cm$^{3}$

Alternatively, from the question, $r=30 \mathrm{~mm}=3 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $h=80 \mathrm{~mm}=8 \mathrm{~cm}$. Hence,

volume $=\frac{1}{3}\pi r^{2}h=\frac{1}{3} \times \pi \times 3^{2} \times 8 = \mathbf{75.40} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$

üí° Problem 13. Determine the volume and total surface area of a cone of radius 5 cm and perpendicular height 12 cm

The cone is shown in Figure 27.12.

Figure 27.12

$$\text{Volume of cone} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^{2}h = \frac{1}{3} \times \pi \times 5^{2} \times 12 = \mathbf{314.2} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

Total surface area = curved surface area + area of base $=\pi rl+\pi r^{2}$

From Figure 27.12, slant height $l$ may be calculated using Pythagoras' theorem: $l=\sqrt{12^{2}+5^{2}}=13 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, total surface area $=(\pi \times 5 \times 13) + (\pi \times 5^{2}) = \mathbf{282.7} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{2}}$.

27.2.6 Spheres

For the sphere shown in Figure 27.13:

Volume $=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3} \quad$ and $\quad$ surface area $=4\pi r^{2}$

Figure 27.13

üí° Problem 14. Find the volume and surface area of a sphere of diameter 10 cm

Since diameter $=10 \mathrm{~cm}$, radius, $r=5 \mathrm{~cm}$.

$$\text{Volume of sphere} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3} = \frac{4}{3} \times \pi \times 5^{3} = \mathbf{523.6} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$$
$$\text{Surface area of sphere} = 4\pi r^{2} = 4 \times \pi \times 5^{2} = \mathbf{314.2} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$$

üí° Problem 15. The surface area of a sphere is $201.1 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$. Find the diameter of the sphere and hence its volume

Surface area of sphere $=4\pi r^{2}$.

Hence, $201.1 \mathrm{~cm}^{2} = 4 \times \pi \times r^{2}$,

from which $\quad r^{2}=\frac{201.1}{4 \times \pi}=16.0$

and $\quad$ radius, $r=\sqrt{16.0}=4.0 \mathrm{~cm}$

from which, diameter $=2 \times r=2 \times 4.0=\mathbf{8.0} \mathbf{~ c m}$

$$\text{Volume of sphere} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3} = \frac{4}{3} \times \pi \times (4.0)^{3} = \mathbf{268.1} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

üßä 27.4 More complex volumes and surface areas

Here are some worked problems involving more complex and composite solids.

üí° Problem 16. A wooden section is shown in Figure 27.14. Find (a) its volume in $\mathrm{m}^{3}$ and (b) its total surface area
Figure 27.14

  • (a) The section of wood is a prism whose end comprises a rectangle and a semicircle. Since the radius of the semicircle is 8 cm , the diameter is 16 cm . Hence, the rectangle has dimensions 12 cm by 16 cm .
    Area of end $=(12 \times 16) + \frac{1}{2}\pi 8^{2} = 292.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$
    Volume of wooden section = area of end $\times$ perpendicular height $=292.5 \times 300 = \mathbf{87750} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3} = \frac{87750}{10^{6}} \mathrm{~m}^{3}$, since $1 \mathrm{~m}^{3}=10^{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} = \mathbf{0.08775} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
  • (b) The total surface area comprises the two ends (each of area $292.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$), three rectangles and a curved surface (which is half a cylinder). Hence,
    total surface area $= (2 \times 292.5) + 2(12 \times 300) + (16 \times 300) + \frac{1}{2}(2\pi \times 8 \times 300) = 585 + 7200 + 4800 + 2400\pi = \mathbf{20125} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$ or $\mathbf{2.0125} \mathbf{~ m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 17. A pyramid has a rectangular base 3.60 cm by 5.40 cm . Determine the volume and total surface area of the pyramid if each of its sloping edges is 15.0 cm

The pyramid is shown in Figure 27.15. To calculate the volume of the pyramid, the perpendicular height EF is required. Diagonal BD is calculated using Pythagoras' theorem,

i.e. $BD=\sqrt{\left[3.60^{2}+5.40^{2}\right]}=6.490 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, $\quad EB=\frac{1}{2}BD=\frac{6.490}{2}=3.245 \mathrm{~cm}$

Figure 27.15

Using Pythagoras' theorem on triangle BEF gives $BF^{2}=EB^{2}+EF^{2}$

from which $\quad EF=\sqrt{\left(BF^{2}-EB^{2}\right)} = \sqrt{15.0^{2}-3.245^{2}}=14.64 \mathrm{~cm}$

$$\text{Volume of pyramid} = \frac{1}{3}(\text{area of base})(\text{perpendicular height})$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}(3.60 \times 5.40)(14.64)=\mathbf{94.87} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$$

Area of triangle ADF (which equals triangle BCF) $=\frac{1}{2}(AD)(FG)$, where G is the midpoint of AD. Using Pythagoras' theorem on triangle FGA gives

$FG=\sqrt{\left[15.0^{2}-1.80^{2}\right]}=14.89 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, area of triangle ADF $=\frac{1}{2}(3.60)(14.89) = 26.80 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$

Similarly, if H is the mid-point of AB, $FH=\sqrt{15.0^{2}-2.70^{2}}=14.75 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, area of triangle ABF (which equals triangle CDF) $=\frac{1}{2}(5.40)(14.75)=39.83 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$

$$\text{Total surface area of pyramid} = 2(26.80)+2(39.83)+(3.60)(5.40)$$ $$= 53.60+79.66+19.44 = \mathbf{152.7} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$$

üí° Problem 18. Calculate the volume and total surface area of a hemisphere of diameter 5.0 cm

$$\text{Volume of hemisphere} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{volume of sphere})$$ $$= \frac{2}{3}\pi r^{3}=\frac{2}{3}\pi\left(\frac{5.0}{2}\right)^{3} = \mathbf{32.7} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$$
$$\text{Total surface area} = \text{curved surface area} + \text{area of circle}$$ $$= \frac{1}{2}(\text{surface area of sphere}) + \pi r^{2}$$ $$= \frac{1}{2}(4\pi r^{2}) + \pi r^{2}$$ $$= 2\pi r^{2}+\pi r^{2}=3\pi r^{2}=3\pi\left(\frac{5.0}{2}\right)^{2} = \mathbf{58.9} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$$

üí° Problem 19. A rectangular piece of metal having dimensions 4 cm by 3 cm by 12 cm is melted down and recast into a pyramid having a rectangular base measuring 2.5 cm by 5 cm . Calculate the perpendicular height of the pyramid

Volume of rectangular prism of metal $=4 \times 3 \times 12 = 144 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Volume of pyramid $= \frac{1}{3}(\text{area of base})(\text{perpendicular height})$

Assuming no waste of metal, $144=\frac{1}{3}(2.5 \times 5)(\text{height})$

i.e. perpendicular height of pyramid $=\frac{144 \times 3}{2.5 \times 5} = \mathbf{34.56} \mathbf{~ c m}$

üí° Problem 20. A rivet consists of a cylindrical head, of diameter 1 cm and depth 2 mm , and a shaft of diameter 2 mm and length 1.5 cm . Determine the volume of metal in 2000 such rivets

Radius of cylindrical head $=\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{~cm}=0.5 \mathrm{~cm}$ and height of cylindrical head $=2 \mathrm{~mm}=0.2 \mathrm{~cm}$.

Hence, volume of cylindrical head $=\pi r^{2}h=\pi(0.5)^{2}(0.2)=0.1571 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Volume of cylindrical shaft $=\pi r^{2}h=\pi\left(\frac{0.2}{2}\right)^{2}(1.5)=0.0471 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Total volume of 1 rivet $=0.1571+0.0471 = 0.2042 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

Volume of metal in 2000 such rivets $=2000 \times 0.2042 = \mathbf{408.4} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$

üí° Problem 21. A solid metal cylinder of radius 6 cm and height 15 cm is melted down and recast into a shape comprising a hemisphere surmounted by a cone. Assuming that 8% of the metal is wasted in the process, determine the height of the conical portion if its diameter is to be 12 cm
Figure 27.16

Volume of cylinder $=\pi r^{2}h=\pi \times 6^{2} \times 15 = 540\pi \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

If 8% of metal is lost then 92% of $540\pi$ gives the volume of the new shape, shown in Figure 27.16.

Hence, the volume of (hemisphere + cone ) $=0.92 \times 540\pi \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$

i.e. $\quad \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right)+\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h=0.92 \times 540\pi$

Dividing throughout by $\pi$ gives $\frac{2}{3} r^{3}+\frac{1}{3} r^{2} h=0.92 \times 540$

Since the diameter of the new shape is to be 12 cm , radius $r=6 \mathrm{~cm}$,

hence $\frac{2}{3}(6)^{3}+\frac{1}{3}(6)^{2}h = 0.92 \times 540$

$144+12h = 496.8$

i.e. height of conical portion, $\boldsymbol{h}=\frac{496.8-144}{12}=\mathbf{29.4} \mathbf{~ c m}$

üí° Problem 22. A block of copper having a mass of 50 kg is drawn out to make 500 m of wire of uniform cross-section. Given that the density of copper is $8.91 \mathrm{~g}/\mathrm{cm}^{3}$, calculate (a) the volume of copper, (b) the cross-sectional area of the wire and (c) the diameter of the cross-section of the wire

  • (a) A density of $8.91 \mathrm{~g}/\mathrm{cm}^{3}$ means that 8.91 g of copper has a volume of $1 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$, or 1 g of copper has a volume of $(1 \div 8.91) \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$.
    Density $=\frac{\text{mass}}{\text{volume}}$ from which volume $=\frac{\text{mass}}{\text{density}}$
    Hence, 50 kg , i.e. 50000 g , has a volume $=\frac{\text{mass}}{\text{density}} = \frac{50000}{8.91} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}=\mathbf{5612} \mathbf{~ c m}^{3}$
  • (b) Volume of wire = area of circular cross-section $\times$ length of wire
    Hence, $5612 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}=$ area $\times (500 \times 100 \mathrm{~cm})$
    from which, area $=\frac{5612}{500 \times 100} \mathrm{~cm}^{2} = \mathbf{0.1122} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$
  • (c) Area of circle $=\pi r^{2}$ or $\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}$ hence, $0.1122=\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}$
    from which, $d=\sqrt{\left(\frac{4 \times 0.1122}{\pi}\right)}=0.3780 \mathrm{~cm}$
    i.e. diameter of cross-section is 3.780 mm.

üßä 27.5 Volumes and surface areas of frusta of pyramids and cones

The frustum of a pyramid or cone is the portion remaining when a part containing the vertex is cut off by a plane parallel to the base.

The volume of a frustum of a pyramid or cone is given by the volume of the whole pyramid or cone minus the volume of the small pyramid or cone cut off.

The surface area of the sides of a frustum of a pyramid or cone is given by the surface area of the whole pyramid or cone minus the surface area of the small pyramid or cone cut off. This gives the lateral surface area of the frustum. If the total surface area of the frustum is required then the surface area of the two parallel ends are added to the lateral surface area.

There is an alternative method for finding the volume and surface area of a frustum of a cone. With reference to Figure 27.21,

Figure 27.21

$$\text{Volume}=\frac{1}{3}\pi h(R^{2}+Rr+r^{2})$$
$$\text{Curved surface area}=\pi l(R+r)$$
$$\text{Total surface area}=\pi l(R+r)+\pi r^{2}+\pi R^{2}$$

üí° Problem 24. Determine the volume of a frustum of a cone if the diameter of the ends are 6.0 cm and 4.0 cm and its perpendicular height is 3.6 cm

(i) Method 1

A section through the vertex of a complete cone is shown in Figure 27.22.

Figure 27.22

Using similar triangles, $\quad \frac{AP}{DP}=\frac{DR}{BR}$

Hence, $\quad \frac{AP}{2.0}=\frac{3.6}{1.0}$ from which $AP=\frac{(2.0)(3.6)}{1.0} = 7.2 \mathrm{~cm}$

The height of the large cone $=3.6+7.2 = 10.8 \mathrm{~cm}$

$$\text{Volume of frustum of cone} = \text{volume of large cone} - \text{volume of small cone cut off}$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}\pi(3.0)^{2}(10.8)-\frac{1}{3}\pi(2.0)^{2}(7.2)$$ $$= 101.79-30.16=\mathbf{71.6} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

Hence, volume of frustum $=\frac{1}{3}\pi(3.6)[(3.0)^{2}+(3.0)(2.0)+(2.0)^{2}]$

$$= \frac{1}{3}\pi(3.6)(19.0)=\mathbf{71.6} \mathbf{~ c m}^{\mathbf{3}}$$

üßä 27.6 Volumes of similar shapes

Figure 27.27 shows two cubes, one of which has sides three times as long as those of the other.

Figure 27.27

Volume of Figure 27.27(a) $=(x)(x)(x)=x^{3}$

Volume of Figure 27.27(b) $=(3x)(3x)(3x)=27x^{3}$

Hence, Figure 27.27(b) has a volume (3)$^{3}$, i.e. 27, times the volume of Figure 27.27(a).

Summarizing, the volumes of similar bodies are proportional to the cubes of corresponding linear dimensions.

üí° Problem 30. A car has a mass of 1000 kg . A model of the car is made to a scale of 1 to 50 . Determine the mass of the model if the car and its model are made of the same material

$$\frac{\text{Volume of model}}{\text{Volume of car}}=\left(\frac{1}{50}\right)^{3}$$

since the volume of similar bodies are proportional to the cube of corresponding dimensions.

Mass = density $\times$ volume and, since both car and model are made of the same material,

$$\frac{\text{Mass of model}}{\text{Mass of car}}=\left(\frac{1}{50}\right)^{3}$$

Hence, mass of model $= (\text{mass of car})\left(\frac{1}{50}\right)^{3} = \frac{1000}{50^{3}} = \mathbf{0.008} \mathbf{~ k g} \text{ or } \mathbf{8 g}$
