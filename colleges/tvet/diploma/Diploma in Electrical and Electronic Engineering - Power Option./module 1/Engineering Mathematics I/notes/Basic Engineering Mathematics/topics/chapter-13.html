Solving simultaneous equations

üéØ 13.1 Introduction

Only one equation is necessary when finding the value of a single unknown quantity (as with simple equations in Chapter 11). However, when an equation contains two unknown quantities it has an infinite number of solutions. When two equations are available connecting the same two unknown values then a unique solution is possible. Similarly, for three unknown quantities it is necessary to have three equations in order to solve for a particular value of each of the unknown quantities, and so on.

Equations which have to be solved together to find the unique values of the unknown quantities, which are true for each of the equations, are called simultaneous equations.

Two methods of solving simultaneous equations analytically are:

  • (a) by substitution, and
  • (b) by elimination.

(A graphical solution of simultaneous equations is shown in Chapter 19.)

üßÆ 13.2 Solving simultaneous equations in two unknowns

The method of solving simultaneous equations is demonstrated in the following worked problems.

üí° Problem 1. Solve the following equations for $x$ and $y$, (a) by substitution and (b) by elimination
$$x+2y = -1 \quad (1)$$
$$4x-3y = 18 \quad (2)$$

  • (a) By substitution
    From equation (1): $x = -1 - 2y$
    Substituting this expression for $x$ into equation (2) gives
    $$4(-1-2y) - 3y = 18$$
    This is now a simple equation in $y$. Removing the bracket gives
    $$\begin{aligned} -4 - 8y - 3y &= 18 \\ -11y &= 18 + 4 = 22 \\ y &= \frac{22}{-11}=-2 \end{aligned}$$
    Substituting $y=-2$ into equation (1) gives
    $$\begin{aligned} x + 2(-2) &= -1 \\ x - 4 &= -1 \\ x &= -1 + 4 = 3 \end{aligned}$$
    Thus, $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{3}$ and $\boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{-2}$ is the solution to the simultaneous equations.
    Check: in equation (2), since $x=3$ and $y=-2$,
    $$\mathrm{LHS} = 4(3) - 3(-2) = 12 + 6 = 18 = \mathrm{RHS}$$
  • (b) By elimination
    $$x+2y = -1 \quad (1)$$
    $$4x-3y = 18 \quad (2)$$
    If equation (1) is multiplied throughout by 4, the coefficient of $x$ will be the same as in equation (2), giving
    $$4x+8y = -4 \quad (3)$$
    Subtracting equation (3) from equation (2) gives
    $$\begin{aligned} 4x - 3y &= 18 \\ 4x + 8y &= -4 \\ \hline 0 - 11y &= 22 \end{aligned}$$
    Hence, $\boldsymbol{y} = \frac{22}{-11}=\mathbf{-2}$
    (Note: in the above subtraction, $18 - -4 = 18 + 4 = 22$.)
    Substituting $y=-2$ into either equation (1) or equation (2) will give $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{3}$ as in method (a). The solution $x=3, y=-2$ is the only pair of values that satisfies both of the original equations.

üí° Problem 2. Solve, by a substitution method, the simultaneous equations
$$3x-2y = 12 \quad (1)$$
$$x+3y = -7 \quad (2)$$

From equation (2), $x = -7 - 3y$

Substituting for $x$ in equation (1) gives

$$3(-7-3y) - 2y = 12$$

i.e. $-21 - 9y - 2y = 12$

$$-11y = 12 + 21 = 33$$

Hence, $y = \frac{33}{-11}=\mathbf{-3}$

Substituting $y=-3$ in equation (2) gives

$$x + 3(-3) = -7$$

i.e. $x - 9 = -7$

Hence $x = -7 + 9 = 2$

Thus, $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{-3}$ is the solution of the simultaneous equations. (Such solutions should always be checked by substituting values into each of the original two equations.)

üí° Problem 3. Use an elimination method to solve the following simultaneous equations
$$3x+4y = 5 \quad (1)$$
$$2x-5y = -12 \quad (2)$$

If equation (1) is multiplied throughout by 2 and equation (2) by 3, the coefficient of $x$ will be the same in the newly formed equations. Thus,


2 $\times$ equation (1) gives   6x + 8y = 10     (3)
3 $\times$ equation (2) gives   6x - 15y = -36   (4)

Equation (3) - equation (4) gives


    0 + 23y = 46

i.e. $\boldsymbol{y} = \frac{46}{23}=\mathbf{2}$

(Note $+8y - -15y = 8y + 15y = 23y$ and $10 - -36 = 10 + 36 = 46$.)

Substituting $y=2$ in equation (1) gives

$$3x + 4(2) = 5$$

from which $3x = 5 - 8 = -3$

and $x = -1$

Checking, by substituting $x=-1$ and $y=2$ in equation (2), gives

LHS = $2(-1) - 5(2) = -2 - 10 = -12 = \mathrm{RHS}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{-1}$ and $\boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{2}$ is the solution of the simultaneous equations.

The elimination method is the most common method of solving simultaneous equations.

üí° Problem 4. Solve
$$7x-2y = 26 \quad (1)$$
$$6x+5y = 29 \quad (2)$$

When equation (1) is multiplied by 5 and equation (2) by 2, the coefficients of $y$ in each equation are numerically the same, i.e. 10 , but are of opposite sign.


5 $\times$ equation (1) gives    35x - 10y = 130    (3)
2 $\times$ equation (2) gives    12x + 10y = 58     (4)

Adding equations (3) and (4) gives


    47x + 0 = 188

Hence, $x = \frac{188}{47}=\mathbf{4}$

Note that when the signs of common coefficients are different the two equations are added and when the signs of common coefficients are the same the two equations are subtracted (as in Problems 1 and 3).

Substituting $x=4$ in equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 7(4) - 2y &= 26 \\ 28 - 2y &= 26 \\ 28 - 26 &= 2y \\ 2 &= 2y \\ y &= \mathbf{1} \end{aligned}$$

Hence, and

Checking, by substituting $x=4$ and $y=1$ in equation (2), gives

LHS = $6(4)+5(1) = 24+5 = 29 = \mathrm{RHS}$

Thus, the solution is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{4}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{1}$.

‚ûï 13.3 Further solving of simultaneous equations

Here are some further worked problems on solving simultaneous equations.

üí° Problem 5. Solve
$$3p=2q \quad (1)$$
$$4p+q+11=0 \quad (2)$$

Rearranging gives

$$3p-2q = 0 \quad (3)$$ $$4p+q = -11 \quad (4)$$

Multiplying equation (4) by 2 gives

$$8p+2q = -22 \quad (5)$$

Adding equations (3) and (5) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 11p+0 &= -22 \\ p &= \frac{-22}{11}=-2 \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $p=-2$ into equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 3(-2) &= 2q \\ -6 &= 2q \\ q &= \frac{-6}{2}=-3 \end{aligned}$$

Checking, by substituting $p=-2$ and $q=-3$ into equation (2), gives

LHS = $4(-2) + (-3) + 11 = -8 - 3 + 11 = 0 = \mathrm{RHS}$

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{p}=\mathbf{-2}, \boldsymbol{q}=\mathbf{-3}$.

üí° Problem 6. Solve
$$\frac{x}{8}+\frac{5}{2}=y \quad (1)$$
$$13-\frac{y}{3}=3x \quad (2)$$

Whenever fractions are involved in simultaneous equations it is often easier to firstly remove them. Thus, multiplying equation (1) by 8 gives

$$8\left(\frac{x}{8}\right)+8\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)=8y$$

i.e. $x+20 = 8y \quad (3)$

Multiplying equation (2) by 3 gives

$$39-y=9x \quad (4)$$

Rearranging equations (3) and (4) gives

$$x-8y = -20 \quad (5)$$ $$9x+y = 39 \quad (6)$$

Multiplying equation (6) by 8 gives

$$72x+8y = 312 \quad (7)$$

Adding equations (5) and (7) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 73x+0 &= 292 \\ x &= \frac{292}{73}=4 \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $x=4$ into equation (5) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 4-8y &= -20 \\ 4+20 &= 8y \\ 24 &= 8y \\ y = \frac{24}{8} &= \mathbf{3} \end{aligned}$$

Checking, substituting $x=4$ and $y=3$ in the original equations, gives

(1): LHS = $\frac{4}{8}+\frac{5}{2}=\frac{1}{2}+2\frac{1}{2}=3=y=\mathrm{RHS}$

(2): LHS = $13-\frac{3}{3}=13-1=12$

RHS = $3x=3(4)=12$

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{4}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{3}$.

üí° Problem 7. Solve
$$2.5x+0.75-3y=0 \quad (1)$$
$$1.6x=1.08-1.2y \quad (2)$$

It is often easier to remove decimal fractions. Thus, multiplying equations (1) and (2) by 100 gives

$$250x+75-300y = 0 \quad (3)$$ $$160x = 108-120y \quad (4)$$

Rearranging gives

$$250x-300y = -75 \quad (3)$$ $$160x+120y = 108 \quad (4)$$

Multiplying equation (3) by 2 gives

$$500x-600y = -150 \quad (5)$$

Multiplying equation (4) by 5 gives

$$800x+600y = 540 \quad (6)$$

Adding equations (5) and (6) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 1300x+0 &= 390 \\ \boldsymbol{x} &= \frac{390}{1300}=\frac{39}{130}=\frac{3}{10}=\mathbf{0.3} \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $x=0.3$ into equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 250(0.3) + 75 - 300y &= 0 \\ 75 + 75 &= 300y \\ 150 &= 300y \\ y = \frac{150}{300} &= \mathbf{0.5} \end{aligned}$$

Checking, by substituting $x=0.3$ and $y=0.5$ in equation (2), gives

LHS = $160(0.3) = 48$

RHS = $108 - 120(0.5) = 108 - 60 = 48$

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{0.3}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{0.5}$

‚ûï 13.4 Solving more difficult simultaneous equations

Here are some further worked problems on solving more difficult simultaneous equations.

üí° Problem 8. Solve
$$\frac{2}{x}+\frac{3}{y}=7 \quad (1)$$
$$\frac{1}{x}-\frac{4}{y}=-2 \quad (2)$$

In this type of equation the solution is easier if a substitution is initially made. Let $\frac{1}{x}=a$ and $\frac{1}{y}=b$

Thus equation (1) becomes $2a+3b = 7 \quad (3)$

and equation (2) becomes $a-4b = -2 \quad (4)$

Multiplying equation (4) by 2 gives

$$2a-8b = -4 \quad (5)$$

Subtracting equation (5) from equation (3) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 0+11b &= 11 \\ \boldsymbol{b} &= \mathbf{1} \end{aligned}$$

i.e.

Substituting $b=1$ in equation (3) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 2a+3 &= 7 \\ 2a = 7-3 &= 4 \\ a &= \mathbf{2} \end{aligned}$$

i.e.

Checking, substituting $a=2$ and $b=1$ in equation (4), gives

LHS = $2 - 4(1) = 2 - 4 = -2 = \mathrm{RHS}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{a}=\mathbf{2}$ and $\boldsymbol{b}=\mathbf{1}$.

However, since $\quad \frac{1}{x}=a, \quad x=\frac{1}{a}=\frac{1}{2}$ or 0.5

and since $\quad \frac{1}{y}=b, \quad y=\frac{1}{b}=\frac{1}{1}=1$

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{0.5}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{1}$.

üí° Problem 9. Solve
$$\frac{1}{2a}+\frac{3}{5b}=4 \quad (1)$$
$$\frac{4}{a}+\frac{1}{2b}=10.5 \quad (2)$$

Let $\frac{1}{a}=x \quad$ and $\quad \frac{1}{b}=y$

then $\quad \frac{x}{2}+\frac{3}{5}y=4 \quad (3)$

$4x+\frac{1}{2}y=10.5 \quad (4)$

To remove fractions, equation (3) is multiplied by 10, giving

$$10\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)+10\left(\frac{3}{5}y\right)=10(4)$$

i.e. $5x+6y = 40 \quad (5)$

Multiplying equation (4) by 2 gives

$$8x+y = 21 \quad (6)$$

Multiplying equation (6) by 6 gives

$$48x+6y = 126 \quad (7)$$

Subtracting equation (5) from equation (7) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 43x+0 &= 86 \\ \boldsymbol{x} &= \frac{86}{43}=\mathbf{2} \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $x=2$ into equation (3) gives

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{2}{2}+\frac{3}{5}y &= 4 \\ \frac{3}{5}y &= 4-1=3 \\ y &= \frac{5}{3}(3)=\mathbf{5} \end{aligned}$$

Since $\quad \frac{1}{a}=x, a=\frac{1}{x}=\frac{1}{2}$ or 0.5 and since $\frac{1}{b}=y, \quad b=\frac{1}{y}=\frac{1}{5}$ or 0.2

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{a}=\mathbf{0.5}, \boldsymbol{b}=\mathbf{0.2}$, which may be checked in the original equations.

üí° Problem 10. Solve
$$\frac{1}{x+y}=\frac{4}{27} \quad (1)$$
$$\frac{1}{2x-y}=\frac{4}{33} \quad (2)$$

To eliminate fractions, both sides of equation (1) are multiplied by 27 $(x+y)$, giving

$$27(x+y)\left(\frac{1}{x+y}\right)=27(x+y)\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)$$

i.e. $27(1) = 4(x+y)$

i.e. $27 = 4x+4y \quad (3)$

Similarly, in equation (2) $33=4(2x-y)$

i.e. $33 = 8x-4y \quad (4)$

Equation (3) + equation (4) gives

$$60 = 12x \quad \text{and} \quad \boldsymbol{x} = \frac{60}{12}=\mathbf{5}$$

Substituting $x=5$ in equation (3) gives

$$27 = 4(5)+4y$$

from which $4y = 27-20=7$

and $\boldsymbol{y} = \frac{7}{4}=\mathbf{1} \frac{\mathbf{3}}{4}$ or $\mathbf{1.75}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{5}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{1.75}$ is the required solution, which may be checked in the original equations.

üí° Problem 11. Solve
$$\frac{x-1}{3}+\frac{y+2}{5}=\frac{2}{15} \quad (1)$$
$$\frac{1-x}{6}+\frac{5+y}{2}=\frac{5}{6} \quad (2)$$

Before equations (1) and (2) can be simultaneously solved, the fractions need to be removed and the equations rearranged.

Multiplying equation (1) by 15 gives

$$15\left(\frac{x-1}{3}\right)+15\left(\frac{y+2}{5}\right)=15\left(\frac{2}{15}\right)$$

i.e. $5(x-1)+3(y+2) = 2$

$$5x-5+3y+6 = 2$$

i.e. $5x+3y = 2+5-6$

$$5x+3y = 1 \quad (3)$$

Hence,

Multiplying equation (2) by 6 gives

$$6\left(\frac{1-x}{6}\right)+6\left(\frac{5+y}{2}\right)=6\left(\frac{5}{6}\right)$$

i.e. $(1-x)+3(5+y) = 5$

$$1-x+15+3y = 5$$

i.e. $-x+3y = 5-1-15$

$$-x+3y = -11 \quad (4)$$

Hence,

Thus the initial problem containing fractions can be expressed as

$$5x+3y = 1 \quad (3)$$ $$-x+3y = -11 \quad (4)$$

Subtracting equation (4) from equation (3) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 6x+0 &= 12 \\ x &= \frac{12}{6}=2 \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $x=2$ into equation (3) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 5(2)+3y &= 1 \\ 10+3y &= 1 \\ 3y = 1-10 &= -9 \\ y = \frac{-9}{3} &= -3 \end{aligned}$$

Checking, substituting $x=2, y=-3$ in equation (4) gives

LHS = $-2+3(-3)=-2-9=-11=\mathrm{RHS}$

Hence, the solution is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{-3}$.

üí° Problem 12. The law connecting friction $F$ and load $L$ for an experiment is of the form $F=aL+b$ where $a$ and $b$ are constants. When $F=5.6 \mathrm{~N}$, $L=8.0 \mathrm{~N}$ and when $F=4.4 N, L=2.0 \mathrm{~N}$. Find the values of $a$ and $b$ and the value of $F$ when $L=6.5 \mathrm{~N}$

Substituting $F=5.6$ and $L=8.0$ into $F=aL+b$ gives

$$5.6 = 8.0a+b \quad (1)$$

Substituting $F=4.4$ and $L=2.0$ into $F=aL+b$ gives

$$4.4 = 2.0a+b \quad (2)$$

Subtracting equation (2) from equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 1.2 &= 6.0a \\ \boldsymbol{a} &= \frac{1.2}{6.0}=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{5}} \text{ or } \mathbf{0.2} \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $a=\frac{1}{5}$ into equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 5.6 &= 8.0\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)+b \\ 5.6 &= 1.6+b \\ 5.6-1.6 &= b \\ \quad \boldsymbol{b} &= \mathbf{4} \end{aligned}$$

i.e.

Checking, substituting $a=\frac{1}{5}$ and $b=4$ in equation (2), gives

RHS = $2.0\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)+4 = 0.4+4 = 4.4 = \mathrm{LHS}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{a}=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{5}}$ and $\boldsymbol{b}=\mathbf{4}$

When $L=6.5, F=aL+b=\frac{1}{5}(6.5)+4=1.3+4$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{F}=\mathbf{5.30} \mathbf{~ N}$.

üí° Problem 13. The equation of a straight line, of gradient $m$ and intercept on the $y$-axis $c$, is $y=mx+c$. If a straight line passes through the point where $x=1$ and $y=-2$, and also through the point where $x=3.5$ and $y=10.5$, find the values of the gradient and the $y$-axis intercept

Substituting $x=1$ and $y=-2$ into $y=mx+c$ gives

$$-2 = m+c \quad (1)$$

Substituting $x=3.5$ and $y=10.5$ into $y=mx+c$ gives

$$10.5 = 3.5m+c \quad (2)$$

Subtracting equation (1) from equation (2) gives

$$12.5 = 2.5m, \quad \text{from which}, \quad \boldsymbol{m}=\frac{12.5}{2.5}=\mathbf{5}$$

Substituting $m=5$ into equation (1) gives

$$ -2 = 5+c$$

$\boldsymbol{c}=-2-5=\mathbf{-7}$

Checking, substituting $m=5$ and $c=-7$ in equation (2), gives

RHS = $(3.5)(5) + (-7) = 17.5 - 7 = 10.5 = \mathrm{LHS}$

Hence, the gradient $\boldsymbol{m}=5$ and the $\boldsymbol{y}$-axis intercept $c=-7$.

üí° Problem 15. The distance $s$ metres from a fixed point of a vehicle travelling in a straight line with constant acceleration, $a \mathrm{~m}/\mathrm{s}^{2}$, is given by $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^{2}$, where $u$ is the initial velocity in $\mathrm{m}/\mathrm{s}$ and $t$ the time in seconds. Determine the initial velocity and the acceleration given that $s=42 \mathrm{~m}$ when $t=2 \mathrm{~s}$, and $s=144 \mathrm{~m}$ when $t=4 \mathrm{~s}$. Also find the distance travelled after 3 s

Substituting $s=42$ and $t=2$ into $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^{2}$ gives

$$42 = 2u + \frac{1}{2}a(2)^{2}$$

i.e. $42 = 2u+2a \quad (1)$

Substituting $s=144$ and $t=4$ into $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^{2}$ gives

$$144 = 4u + \frac{1}{2}a(4)^{2}$$

i.e. $144 = 4u+8a \quad (2)$

Multiplying equation (1) by 2 gives

$$84 = 4u+4a \quad (3)$$

Subtracting equation (3) from equation (2) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 60 &= 0+4a \\ \boldsymbol{a} &= \frac{60}{4}=\mathbf{15} \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $a=15$ into equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 42 &= 2u+2(15) \\ 42-30 &= 2u \\ \boldsymbol{u} &= \frac{12}{2}=\mathbf{6} \end{aligned}$$

Substituting $a=15$ and $u=6$ in equation (2) gives

RHS = $4(6)+8(15)=24+120=144=\mathrm{LHS}$

Hence, the initial velocity $u=\mathbf{6m}/\mathbf{s}$ and the acceleration $a=\mathbf{15} \mathbf{~ m}/\mathbf{s}^{\mathbf{2}}$.

Distance travelled after 3 s is given by $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^{2}$ where $t=3, u=6$ and $a=15$.

Hence, $s=(6)(3)+\frac{1}{2}(15)(3)^{2}=18+67.5$

i.e. distance travelled after 3s = 85.5 m.

üí° Problem 16. The resistance $R \Omega$ of a length of wire at $t^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is given by $R=R_{0}(1+\alpha t)$, where $R_{0}$ is the resistance at $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistance in $/{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Find the values of $\alpha$ and $R_{0}$ if $R=30 \Omega$ at $50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $R=35 \Omega$ at $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$

Substituting $R=30$ and $t=50$ into $R=R_{0}(1+\alpha t)$ gives

$$30 = R_{0}(1+50\alpha) \quad (1)$$

Substituting $R=35$ and $t=100$ into $R=R_{0}(1+\alpha t)$ gives

$$35 = R_{0}(1+100\alpha) \quad (2)$$

Although these equations may be solved by the conventional substitution method, an easier way is to eliminate $R_{0}$ by division. Thus, dividing equation (1) by equation (2) gives

$$\frac{30}{35} = \frac{R_{0}(1+50\alpha)}{R_{0}(1+100\alpha)} = \frac{1+50\alpha}{1+100\alpha}$$

Cross-multiplying gives

$$\begin{aligned} 30(1+100\alpha) &= 35(1+50\alpha) \\ 30+3000\alpha &= 35+1750\alpha \\ 3000\alpha-1750\alpha &= 35-30 \\ 1250\alpha &= 5 \end{aligned}$$

i.e. $\quad \boldsymbol{\alpha}=\frac{5}{1250}=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{250}} \text{ or } \mathbf{0.004}$

Substituting $\alpha=\frac{1}{250}$ into equation (1) gives

$$\begin{aligned} 30 &= R_{0}\left\{1+(50)\left(\frac{1}{250}\right)\right\} \\ 30 &= R_{0}(1.2) \\ \boldsymbol{R}_{\mathbf{0}} &= \frac{30}{1.2}=\mathbf{25} \end{aligned}$$

Checking, substituting $\alpha=\frac{1}{250}$ and $R_{0}=25$ in equation (2), gives

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{RHS} &= 25\left\{1+(100)\left(\frac{1}{250}\right)\right\} \\ &= 25(1.4)=35=\mathrm{LHS} \end{aligned}$$

Thus, the solution is $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=\mathbf{0.004} /^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $\boldsymbol{R}_{\mathbf{0}}=\mathbf{25} \boldsymbol{\Omega}$.

üí° Problem 17. The molar heat capacity of a solid compound is given by the equation $c=a+bT$, where $a$ and $b$ are constants. When $c=52, T=100$ and when $c=172, T=400$. Determine the values of $a$ and $b$

When $c=52, \quad T=100$, hence

$$52 = a+100b \quad (1)$$

When $c=172, \quad T=400$, hence

$$172 = a+400b \quad (2)$$

Equation (2) - equation (1) gives

$$120 = 300b$$

from which, $\quad \boldsymbol{b}=\frac{120}{300}=\mathbf{0.4}$

Substituting $b=0.4$ in equation (1) gives

$$52 = a+100(0.4)$$

$\boldsymbol{a}=52-40=\mathbf{12}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{a}=\mathbf{12}$ and $\boldsymbol{b}=\mathbf{0} .4$

‚ûï 13.6 Solving simultaneous equations in three unknowns

Equations containing three unknowns may be solved using exactly the same procedures as those used with two equations and two unknowns, providing that there are three equations to work with. The method is demonstrated in the following worked problem.

üí° Problem 18. Solve the simultaneous equations.
$$x+y+z=4 \quad (1)$$
$$2x-3y+4z=33 \quad (2)$$
$$3x-2y-2z=2 \quad (3)$$

There are a number of ways of solving these equations. One method is shown below.

The initial object is to produce two equations with two unknowns. For example, multiplying equation (1) by 4 and then subtracting this new equation from equation (2) will produce an equation with only $x$ and $y$ involved.

Multiplying equation (1) by 4 gives

$$4x+4y+4z = 16 \quad (4)$$

Equation (2) - equation (4) gives

$$-2x-7y = 17 \quad (5)$$

Similarly, multiplying equation (3) by 2 and then adding this new equation to equation (2) will produce another equation with only $x$ and $y$ involved.

Multiplying equation (3) by 2 gives

$$6x-4y-4z = 4 \quad (6)$$

Equation (2) + equation (6) gives

$$8x-7y = 37 \quad (7)$$

Rewriting equation (5) gives

$$-2x-7y = 17 \quad (5)$$

Now we can use the previous method for solving simultaneous equations in two unknowns.

Equation (7) - equation (5) gives

$$10x = 20$$

from which, $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}$

(Note that $8x - -2x = 8x+2x = 10x$)

Substituting $x=2$ into equation (5) gives

$$-4-7y = 17$$

from which, $\quad -7y = 17+4=21$

and $y = -3$

Substituting $x=2$ and $y=-3$ into equation (1) gives

$$2-3+z=4$$

from which, $z = 5$

Hence, the solution of the simultaneous equations is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}, \boldsymbol{y}=\mathbf{-3}$ and $\boldsymbol{z}=\mathbf{5}$.
