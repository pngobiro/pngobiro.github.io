Ratio and proportion

üéØ 6.1 Introduction

Ratio is a way of comparing amounts of something; it shows how much bigger one thing is than the other. Some practical examples include mixing paint, sand and cement, or screen wash. Gears, map scales, food recipes, scale drawings and metal alloy constituents all use ratios.

Two quantities are in direct proportion when they increase or decrease in the same ratio. There are several practical engineering laws which rely on direct proportion. Also, calculating currency exchange rates and converting imperial to metric units rely on direct proportion.

Sometimes, as one quantity increases at a particular rate, another quantity decreases at the same rate; this is called inverse proportion. For example, the time taken to do a job is inversely proportional to the number of people in a team: double the people, half the time.

When we have completed this chapter on ratio and proportion you will be able to understand, and confidently perform, calculations on the above topics.

For this chapter you will need to know about decimals and fractions and to be able to use a calculator.

üìä 6.2 Ratios

Ratios are generally shown as numbers separated by a colon (:) so the ratio of 2 and 7 is written as 2:7 and we read it as a ratio of 'two to seven.'

Some practical examples which are familiar include:

  • ‚Ä¢ Mixing 1 measure of screen wash to 6 measures of water; i.e., the ratio of screen wash to water is 1:6
  • ‚Ä¢ Mixing 1 shovel of cement to 4 shovels of sand; i.e., the ratio of cement to sand is 1:4
  • ‚Ä¢ Mixing 3 parts of red paint to 1 part white, i.e., the ratio of red to white paint is 3:1

Ratio is the number of parts to a mix. The paint mix is 4 parts total, with 3 parts red and 1 part white. 3 parts red paint to 1 part white paint means there is

$$\frac{3}{4} \text{ red paint to } \frac{1}{4} \text{ white paint}$$

Here are some worked examples to help us understand more about ratios.

üí° Problem 1. In a class, the ratio of female to male students is 6:27. Reduce the ratio to its simplest form

  • ‚Ä¢ Both 6 and 27 can be divided by 3.
  • ‚Ä¢ Thus, 6:27 is the same as 2:9.

6:27 and 2:9 are called equivalent ratios.

It is normal to express ratios in their lowest, or simplest, form. In this example, the simplest form is 2:9 which means for every 2 females in the class there are 9 male students.

üí° Problem 2. A gear wheel having 128 teeth is in mesh with a 48 -tooth gear. What is the gear ratio?

$$\text{Gear ratio} = 128:48$$

A ratio can be simplified by finding common factors.

  • ‚Ä¢ 128 and 48 can both be divided by 2, i.e. 128:48 is the same as 64:24
  • ‚Ä¢ 64 and 24 can both be divided by 8, i.e. 64:24 is the same as 8:3
  • ‚Ä¢ There is no number that divides completely into both 8 and 3 so 8:3 is the simplest ratio, i.e. the gear ratio is 8:3

Thus, 128:48 is equivalent to 64:24 which is equivalent to 8:3 and 8:3 is the simplest form.

üí° Problem 3. A wooden pole is 2.08 m long. Divide it in the ratio of 7 to 19

  • ‚Ä¢ Since the ratio is 7:19, the total number of parts is 7+19=26 parts.
  • ‚Ä¢ 26 parts corresponds to 2.08 m = 208 cm, hence, 1 part corresponds to $\frac{208}{26}=8$.
  • ‚Ä¢ Thus, 7 parts corresponds to $7 \times 8=\mathbf{56} \mathbf{~ c m}$ and 19 parts corresponds to $19 \times 8=\mathbf{1 5 2} \mathbf{~ c m}$.

Hence, 2.08 m divides in the ratio of 7:19 as 56 cm to 152 cm .

(Check: 56+152 must add up to 208, otherwise an error would have been made.)

üí° Problem 4. In a competition, prize money of ¬£828 is to be shared among the first three in the ratio 5:3:1

  • ‚Ä¢ Since the ratio is 5:3:1 the total number of parts is 5+3+1=9 parts.
  • ‚Ä¢ 9 parts corresponds to ¬£828.
  • ‚Ä¢ 1 part corresponds to $\frac{828}{9}=\mathbf{¬£92}, 3$ parts corresponds to $3 \times ¬£92=¬£276$ and 5 parts corresponds to $5 \times ¬£92=¬£460$.

Hence, ¬£828 divides in the ratio of 5:3:1 as ¬£460 to ¬£276 to ¬£92. (Check: 460+276+92 must add up to 828, otherwise an error would have been made.)

üí° Problem 5. A map scale is 1:30000. On the map the distance between two schools is 6 cm . Determine the actual distance between the schools, giving the answer in kilometres

$$\text{Actual distance between schools}$$ $$= 6 \times 30000 \mathrm{~cm} = 180000 \mathrm{~cm}$$ $$= \frac{180,000}{100} \mathrm{~m} = 1800 \mathrm{~m}$$ $$= \frac{1800}{1000} \mathrm{~m} = \mathbf{1.80} \mathbf{~ k m}$$

( 1 mile $\approx$ 1.6 km, hence the schools are just over 1 mile apart.)

üí° Problem 6. Express 45 p as a ratio of ¬£7.65 in its simplest form

  • ‚Ä¢ Changing both quantities to the same units, i.e. to pence, gives a ratio of 45:765
  • ‚Ä¢ Dividing both quantities by 5 gives $45:765 \equiv 9:153$
  • ‚Ä¢ Dividing both quantities by 3 gives $9:153 \equiv 3:51$
  • ‚Ä¢ Dividing both quantities by 3 again gives $3:51 \equiv 1:17$

Thus, 45 p as a ratio of ¬£7.65 is 1:17

45:765, 9:153, 3:51 and 1:17 are equivalent ratios and 1:17 is the simplest ratio.

üí° Problem 7. A glass contains 30 ml of whisky which is 40% alcohol. If 45 ml of water is added and the mixture stirred, what is now the alcohol content?

  • ‚Ä¢ The 30 ml of whisky contains 40% alcohol = $\frac{40}{100} \times 30 = 12 \text{ ml}$.
  • ‚Ä¢ After 45 ml of water is added we have $30+45 = 75 \text{ ml}$ of fluid, of which alcohol is 12 ml .
  • ‚Ä¢ Fraction of alcohol present = $\frac{12}{75}$
  • ‚Ä¢ Percentage of alcohol present = $\frac{12}{75} \times 100\% = 16\%$.

üí° Problem 8. 20 tonnes of a mixture of sand and gravel is 30% sand. How many tonnes of sand must be added to produce a mixture which is 40% gravel?

  • ‚Ä¢ Amount of sand in 20 tonnes = 30% of 20 t = $\frac{30}{100} \times 20 = 6 \text{ t}$.
  • ‚Ä¢ If the mixture has 6 t of sand then amount of gravel = $20-6=14 \text{ t}$.
  • ‚Ä¢ We want this 14 t of gravel to be 40% of the new mixture. 1% would be $\frac{14}{40} \text{ t}$ and 100% of the mixture would be $\frac{14}{40} \times 100 \text{ t} = 35 \text{ t}$.
  • ‚Ä¢ If there is 14 t of gravel then amount of sand = $35-14=21 \text{ t}$.
  • ‚Ä¢ We already have 6 t of sand, so amount of sand to be added to produce a mixture with 40% gravel = $21-6=\mathbf{15} \mathbf{~ t}$.

(Note 1 tonne = 1000 kg.)

üìà 6.3 Direct proportion

Two quantities are in direct proportion when they increase or decrease in the same ratio. For example, if 12 cans of lager have a mass of 4 kg , then 24 cans of lager will have a mass of 8 kg ; i.e., if the quantity of cans doubles then so does the mass. This is direct proportion.

In the previous section we had an example of mixing 1 shovel of cement to 4 shovels of sand; i.e., the ratio of cement to sand was 1:4. So, if we have a mix of 10 shovels of cement and 40 shovels of sand and we wanted to double the amount of the mix then we would need to double both the cement and sand, i.e. 20 shovels of cement and 80 shovels of sand. This is another example of direct proportion.

Here are three laws in engineering which involve direct proportion:

  • (a) Hooke's law states that, within the elastic limit of a material, the strain $\varepsilon$ produced is directly proportional to the stress $\sigma$ producing it, i.e. $\varepsilon \propto \sigma$ (note than '$\propto$' means 'is proportional to').
  • (b) Charles's law states that, for a given mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume $V$ is directly proportional to its thermodynamic temperature $T$, i.e. $V \propto T$.
  • (c) Ohm's law states that the current $I$ flowing through a fixed resistance is directly proportional to the applied voltage $V$, i.e. $I \propto V$.

Here are some worked examples to help us understand more about direct proportion.

üí° Problem 9. 3 energy saving light bulbs cost ¬£7.80. Determine the cost of 7 such light bulbs

  • ‚Ä¢ 3 light bulbs cost ¬£7.80
  • ‚Ä¢ Therefore, 1 light bulb costs $\frac{7.80}{3}=¬£2.60$

Hence, 7 light bulbs cost $7 \times ¬£2.60=¬£\mathbf{18.20}$

üí° Problem 10. If 56 litres of petrol costs ¬£59.92, calculate the cost of 32 litres

  • ‚Ä¢ 56 litres of petrol costs ¬£59.92
  • ‚Ä¢ Therefore, 1 litre of petrol costs $\frac{59.92}{56}=¬£1.07$

Hence, 32 litres cost $32 \times 1.07=¬£\mathbf{34.24}$

üí° Problem 11. Hooke's law states that stress, $\sigma$, is directly proportional to strain, $\varepsilon$, within the elastic limit of a material. When, for mild steel, the stress is 63 MPa , the strain is 0.0003 . Determine (a) the value of strain when the stress is 42 MPa , (b) the value of stress when the strain is 0.00072

  • (a) Stress is directly proportional to strain.
    • ‚Ä¢ When the stress is 63 MPa , the strain is 0.0003
    • ‚Ä¢ Hence, a stress of 1 MPa corresponds to a strain of $\frac{0.0003}{63}$
    • ‚Ä¢ Thus, the value of strain when the stress is 42 MPa = $\frac{0.0003}{63} \times 42=\mathbf{0.0002}$
  • (b) Strain is proportional to stress.
    • ‚Ä¢ When the strain is 0.0003, the stress is 63 MPa .
    • ‚Ä¢ Hence, a strain of 0.0001 corresponds to $\frac{63}{3} \text{ MPa}$.
    • ‚Ä¢ Thus, the value of stress when the strain is 0.00072 = $\frac{63}{3} \times 7.2=151.2 \text{ MPa}$.

üí° Problem 12. Charles's law states that for a given mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to its thermodynamic temperature. A gas occupies a volume of 2.4 litres at 600 K . Determine (a) the temperature when the volume is 3.2 litres, (b) the volume at 540 K

  • (a) Volume is directly proportional to temperature.
    • ‚Ä¢ When the volume is 2.4 litres, the temperature is 600 K .
    • ‚Ä¢ Hence, a volume of 1 litre corresponds to a temperature of $\frac{600}{2.4} \text{ K}$.
    • ‚Ä¢ Thus, the temperature when the volume is 3.2 litres = $\frac{600}{2.4} \times 3.2=\mathbf{800} \text{ K}$.
  • (b) Temperature is proportional to volume.
    • ‚Ä¢ When the temperature is 600 K , the volume is 2.4 litres.
    • ‚Ä¢ Hence, a temperature of 1 K corresponds to a volume of $\frac{2.4}{600}$ litres.
    • ‚Ä¢ Thus, the volume at a temperature of 540 K = $\frac{2.4}{600} \times 540=\mathbf{2.16}$ litres.

üí° Problem 13. Some guttering on a house has to decline by 3 mm for every 70 cm to allow rainwater to drain. The gutter spans 8.4 m . How much lower should the low end be?

üìâ 6.4 Inverse proportion

Two variables, $x$ and $y$, are in inverse proportion to one another if $y$ is proportional to $\frac{1}{x}$, i.e. $y \alpha \frac{1}{x}$ or $y=\frac{k}{x}$ or $k=x y$ where $k$ is a constant, called the coefficient of proportionality.

Inverse proportion means that, as the value of one variable increases, the value of another decreases, and that their product is always the same.

For example, the time for a journey is inversely proportional to the speed of travel. So, if at 30 m.p.h. a journey is completed in 20 minutes, then at 60 m.p.h. the journey would be completed in 10 minutes. Double the speed, half the journey time. (Note that $30 \times 20=60 \times 10$.) In another example, the time needed to dig a hole is inversely proportional to the number of people digging. So, if 4 men take 3 hours to dig a hole, then 2 men (working at the same rate) would take 6 hours. Half the men, twice the time. (Note that $4 \times 3=2 \times 6$.)

Here are some worked examples on inverse proportion.

üí° Problem 17. It is estimated that a team of four designers would take a year to develop an engineering process. How long would three designers take?

If 4 designers take 1 year, then 1 designer would take 4 years to develop the process. Hence, 3 designers would take $\frac{4}{3}$ years, i.e. 1 year 4 months.

üí° Problem 18. A team of five people can deliver leaflets to every house in a particular area in four hours. How long will it take a team of three people?

If 5 people take 4 hours to deliver the leaflets, then 1 person would take $5 \times 4 = 20$ hours. Hence, 3 people would take $\frac{20}{3}$ hours, i.e. $6 \frac{2}{3}$ hours, i.e. 6 hours 40 minutes.

üí° Problem 19. The electrical resistance $R$ of a piece of wire is inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area $A$. When $A=5 \mathrm{~mm}^{2}$, $R=7.02$ ohms. Determine (a) the coefficient of proportionality and (b) the cross-sectional area when the resistance is 4 ohms

  • (a) $R \alpha \frac{1}{A}$, i.e. $R=\frac{k}{A}$ or $k=R A$. Hence, when $R=7.2$ and $A=5$, the coefficient of proportionality, $k=(7.2)(5)=\mathbf{36}$
  • (b) Since $k=R A$ then $A=\frac{k}{R}$. Hence, when $R=4$, the cross sectional area, $A=\frac{36}{4}=\mathbf{9} \mathbf{~ m m}^{\mathbf{2}}$

üí° Problem 20. Boyle's law states that, at constant temperature, the volume $V$ of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its absolute pressure $p$. If a gas occupies a volume of $0.08 \mathrm{~m}^{3}$ at a pressure of $1.5 \times 10^{6}$ pascals, determine (a) the coefficient of proportionality and (b) the volume if the pressure is changed to $4 \times 10^{6}$ pascals

  • (a) $V \propto \frac{1}{p}$ i.e. $V=\frac{k}{p}$ or $k=p V$. Hence, the coefficient of proportionality, $k$
    $$=\left(1.5 \times 10^{6}\right)(0.08)=\mathbf{0.12} \times \mathbf{10}^{\mathbf{6}}$$
  • (b) Volume, $V$$=\frac{k}{p} = \frac{0.12 \times 10^{6}}{4 \times 10^{6}}=\mathbf{0.03} \mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}$
