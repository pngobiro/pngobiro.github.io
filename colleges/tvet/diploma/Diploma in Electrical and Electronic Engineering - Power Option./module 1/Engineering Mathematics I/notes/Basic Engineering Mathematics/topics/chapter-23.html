Non-right-angled triangles and some practical applications

üìê 23.1 The sine and cosine rules

To 'solve a triangle' means 'to find the values of unknown sides and angles'. If a triangle is right-angled, trigonometric ratios and the theorem of Pythagoras may be used for its solution, as shown in Chapter 21. However, for a non-right-angled triangle, trigonometric ratios and Pythagoras' theorem cannot be used. Instead, two rules, called the sine rule and the cosine rule, are used.

23.1.1 The sine rule

With reference to triangle ABC of Figure 23.1, the sine rule states

$$\frac{a}{\sin A}=\frac{b}{\sin B}=\frac{c}{\sin C}$$

Figure 23.1

The rule may be used only when

  • (a) 1 side and any 2 angles are initially given, or
  • (b) 2 sides and an angle (not the included angle) are initially given.

23.1.2 The cosine rule

With reference to triangle ABC of Figure 23.1, the cosine rule states

$$a^{2}=b^{2}+c^{2}-2bc \cos A$$ $$\text{or } b^{2}=a^{2}+c^{2}-2ac \cos B$$ $$\text{or } c^{2}=a^{2}+b^{2}-2ab \cos C$$

The rule may be used only when

  • (a) 2 sides and the included angle are initially given, or
  • (b) 3 sides are initially given.

üìê 23.2 Area of any triangle

The area of any triangle such as ABC of Figure 23.1 is given by

  • (a) $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$
  • (b) $\frac{1}{2} ab \sin C$ or $\frac{1}{2} ac \sin B$ or $\frac{1}{2} bc \sin A$
  • (c) $\sqrt{[s(s-a)(s-\boldsymbol{b})(s-c)]}$ where $s=\frac{a+b+c}{2}$

üí° 23.3 Worked problems on the solution of triangles and their areas

üí° Problem 1. In a triangle $X Y Z, \angle X=51^{\circ}, \angle Y=67^{\circ}$ and $Y Z=15.2 \mathrm{~cm}$. Solve the triangle and find its area

The triangle XYZ is shown in Figure 23.2. Solving the triangle means finding $\angle Z$ and sides XZ and XY.

Figure 23.2

Since the angles in a triangle add up to $180^{\circ}$, $Z=180^{\circ}-51^{\circ}-67^{\circ}=62^{\circ}$

Applying the sine rule,

$$\frac{15.2}{\sin 51^{\circ}} = \frac{y}{\sin 67^{\circ}} = \frac{z}{\sin 62^{\circ}}$$

Using $\frac{15.2}{\sin 51^{\circ}} = \frac{y}{\sin 67^{\circ}}$ and transposing gives

$$\boldsymbol{y}=\frac{15.2 \sin 67^{\circ}}{\sin 51^{\circ}} = \mathbf{18.00} \mathbf{~ cm}=X \mathbf{Z}$$

Using $\frac{15.2}{\sin 51^{\circ}} = \frac{z}{\sin 62^{\circ}}$ and transposing gives

$$z=\frac{15.2 \sin 62^{\circ}}{\sin 51^{\circ}} = \mathbf{17.27} \mathbf{~ c m}=\mathbf{X Y}$$

Area of triangle $X Y Z=\frac{1}{2} x y \sin Z$

$$\begin{aligned} &= \frac{1}{2}(15.2)(18.00) \sin 62^{\circ}=\mathbf{120.8} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2} \\ (\text{or area } &= \frac{1}{2}xz \sin Y = \frac{1}{2}(15.2)(17.27) \sin 67^{\circ} \\ &= \mathbf{120.8} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}) \end{aligned}$$

It is always worth checking with triangle problems that the longest side is opposite the largest angle and viceversa. In this problem, Y is the largest angle and XZ is the longest of the three sides.

üí° Problem 2. Solve the triangle $A B C$ given $B=78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}, A C=22.31 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $A B=17.92 \mathrm{~mm}$. Also find its area

Triangle ABC is shown in Figure 23.3. Solving the triangle means finding angles A and C and side BC.

Figure 23.3

Applying the sine rule,$\quad \frac{22.31}{\sin 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}}=\frac{17.92}{\sin C}$

from which $\sin C=\frac{17.92 \sin 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}}{22.31}=0.7881$

Hence, $\quad C=\sin^{-1} 0.7881=52^{\circ} 0^{\prime}$ or $128^{\circ} 0^{\prime}$

Since $B=78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}$, C cannot be $128^{\circ} 0^{\prime}$, since $128^{\circ} 0^{\prime}+ 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}$ is greater than $180^{\circ}$. Thus, only $\boldsymbol{C}=\mathbf{52}^{\circ} \mathbf{0}^{\prime}$ is valid.

Angle $\boldsymbol{A}=180^{\circ}-78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}-52^{\circ} 0^{\prime}=\mathbf{49}^{\circ} \mathbf{9}^{\prime}$.

Applying the sine rule, $\quad \frac{a}{\sin 49^{\circ} 9^{\prime}}=\frac{22.31}{\sin 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}}$

from which $\quad \boldsymbol{a}=\frac{22.31 \sin 49^{\circ} 9^{\prime}}{\sin 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}}=\mathbf{17.20} \mathbf{~ m m}$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{A}=\mathbf{49^{\circ} 9^{\prime}}, \boldsymbol{C}=\mathbf{52}^{\circ} \mathbf{0}^{\prime}$ and $\boldsymbol{BC}=\mathbf{17.20} \mathbf{~ m m}$.

$$\text{Area of triangle } \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{B} \boldsymbol{C} = \frac{1}{2} a c \sin B$$ $$= \frac{1}{2}(17.20)(17.92) \sin 78^{\circ} 51^{\prime}$$ $$= \mathbf{151.2} \mathbf{~ m m}^{2}$$

üí° Problem 3. Solve the triangle $P Q R$ and find its area given that $Q R=36.5 \mathrm{~mm}, P R=29.6 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $\angle Q=36^{\circ}$

Triangle PQR is shown in Figure 23.4.

Figure 23.4

Applying the sine rule, $\frac{29.6}{\sin 36^{\circ}}=\frac{36.5}{\sin P}$ from which $\sin P=\frac{36.5 \sin 36^{\circ}}{29.6}=0.7248$

Hence, $\quad P=\sin^{-1} 0.7248=46.45^{\circ}$ or $133.55^{\circ}$

When $P=46.45^{\circ}$ and $Q=36^{\circ}$ then $R=180^{\circ}-46.45^{\circ}-36^{\circ}=97.55^{\circ}$

When $P=133.55^{\circ}$ and $Q=36^{\circ}$ then $R=180^{\circ}-133.55^{\circ}-36^{\circ}=10.45^{\circ}$

Thus, in this problem, there are two separate sets of results and both are feasible solutions. Such a situation is called the ambiguous case.

Case 1. $P=46.45^{\circ}, Q=36^{\circ}, R=97.55^{\circ}, p=36.5 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $q=29.6 \mathrm{~mm}$

From the sine rule, $\quad \frac{r}{\sin 97.55^{\circ}}=\frac{29.6}{\sin 36^{\circ}}$

from which $\quad r=\frac{29.6 \sin 97.55^{\circ}}{\sin 36^{\circ}}=\mathbf{49.92} \mathrm{~mm}=\boldsymbol{PQ}$

$$\text{Area of } PQR = \frac{1}{2} p q \sin R = \frac{1}{2}(36.5)(29.6) \sin 97.55^{\circ}$$ $$= \mathbf{535.5} \mathbf{~ m m}^{\mathbf{2}}$$

Case 2. $P=133.55^{\circ}, Q=36^{\circ}, R=10.45^{\circ}, p=36.5 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $q=29.6 \mathrm{~mm}$

From the sine rule, $\quad \frac{r}{\sin 10.45^{\circ}}=\frac{29.6}{\sin 36^{\circ}}$

from which $\quad r=\frac{29.6 \sin 10.45^{\circ}}{\sin 36^{\circ}}=\mathbf{9.134} \mathbf{~ m m}=\boldsymbol{PQ}$

$$\text{Area of } PQR = \frac{1}{2} p q \sin R = \frac{1}{2}(36.5)(29.6) \sin 10.45^{\circ}$$ $$= \mathbf{97.98} \mathbf{~ m m}^{\mathbf{2}}$$

üí° 23.4 Further worked problems on the solution of triangles and their areas

üí° Problem 4. Solve triangle $D E F$ and find its area given that $E F=35.0 \mathrm{~mm}, D E=25.0 \mathrm{~mm}$ and $\angle E=64^{\circ}$

Triangle DEF is shown in Figure 23.6. Solving the triangle means finding angles D and F and side DF. Since two sides and the angle in between the two sides are given, the cosine needs to be used.

Figure 23.6

Applying the cosine rule, $\quad e^{2}=d^{2}+f^{2}-2df \cos E$

i.e. $\quad e^{2}=(35.0)^{2}+(25.0)^{2}-[2(35.0)(25.0)\cos 64^{\circ}]$

$$\begin{aligned} &= 1225+625-767.15 \\ &= 1082.85 \end{aligned}$$

from which $\quad e=\sqrt{1082.85} = \mathbf{32.91} \mathbf{~ mm}=\boldsymbol{DF}$

Applying the sine rule, $\quad \frac{32.91}{\sin 64^{\circ}}=\frac{25.0}{\sin F}$

from which $\sin F=\frac{25.0 \sin 64^{\circ}}{32.91}=0.6828$

Thus, $\angle F=\sin^{-1} 0.6828=43^{\circ} 4^{\prime}$ or $136^{\circ} 56^{\prime}$

$F=136^{\circ} 56^{\prime}$ is not possible in this case since $136^{\circ} 56^{\prime}+ 64^{\circ}$ is greater than $180^{\circ}$. Thus, only $\boldsymbol{F}=\mathbf{43}^{\circ} \mathbf{4}^{\prime}$ is valid.

Then $\angle \boldsymbol{D}=180^{\circ}-64^{\circ}-43^{\circ} 4^{\prime}=\mathbf{72}^{\circ} 56^{\prime}$.

Area of triangle $\boldsymbol{DEF}=\frac{1}{2}df \sin E = \frac{1}{2}(35.0)(25.0)\sin 64^{\circ}=\mathbf{393.2} \mathbf{~ m m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 5. A triangle $A B C$ has sides $a=9.0 \mathrm{~cm}, b=7.5 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $c=6.5 \mathrm{~cm}$. Determine its three angles and its area

Triangle ABC is shown in Figure 23.7. It is usual first to calculate the largest angle to determine whether the triangle is acute or obtuse. In this case the largest angle is A (i.e. opposite the longest side).

Figure 23.7

Applying the cosine rule, $a^{2}=b^{2}+c^{2}-2bc \cos A$ from which $2bc \cos A=b^{2}+c^{2}-a^{2}$

and $\cos A=\frac{b^{2}+c^{2}-a^{2}}{2bc} = \frac{7.5^{2}+6.5^{2}-9.0^{2}}{2(7.5)(6.5)} = 0.1795$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{A}=\cos^{-1} 0.1795=\mathbf{79.67^{\circ}}$

(or $280.33^{\circ}$, which is clearly impossible)

The triangle is thus acute angled since $\cos A$ is positive. (If $\cos A$ had been negative, angle A would be obtuse; i.e., would lie between $90^{\circ}$ and $180^{\circ}$.)

Applying the sine rule, $\quad \frac{9.0}{\sin 79.67^{\circ}}=\frac{7.5}{\sin B}$

from which $\quad \sin B=\frac{7.5 \sin 79.67^{\circ}}{9.0}=0.8198$

Hence, $\boldsymbol{B}=\sin^{-1} 0.8198=\mathbf{55.0}^{\circ}$

and $\boldsymbol{C}=180^{\circ}-79.67^{\circ}-55.07^{\circ}=\mathbf{45.26}^{\circ}$

Area $=\sqrt{[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]}$, where $s=\frac{a+b+c}{2}=\frac{9.0+7.5+6.5}{2}=11.5 \mathrm{~cm}$

Hence, area $=\sqrt{[11.5(11.5-9.0)(11.5-7.5)(11.5-6.5)]} = \sqrt{[11.5(2.5)(4.0)(5.0)]}=\mathbf{23.98} \mathbf{~ cm}^{2}$

Alternatively, area $=\frac{1}{2} ac \sin B = \frac{1}{2}(9.0)(6.5) \sin 55.07^{\circ}=\mathbf{23.98} \mathbf{~ cm}^{\mathbf{2}}$

üí° Problem 6. Solve triangle $X Y Z$, shown in Figure 23.8, and find its area given that $Y=128^{\circ}, X Y=7.2 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $Y Z=4.5 \mathrm{~cm}$
Figure 23.8

Applying the cosine rule,

$$\begin{aligned} y^{2} &= x^{2}+z^{2}-2xz \cos Y \\ &= 4.5^{2}+7.2^{2}-[2(4.5)(7.2) \cos 128^{\circ}] \\ &= 20.25+51.84-[-39.89] \\ &= 20.25+51.84+39.89=112.0 \end{aligned}$$

$\boldsymbol{y}=\sqrt{112.0}=\mathbf{10.58} \mathbf{~ cm}=\mathbf{X Z}$

Applying the sine rule, $\frac{10.58}{\sin 128^{\circ}}=\frac{7.2}{\sin Z}$

from which $\sin Z=\frac{7.2 \sin 128^{\circ}}{10.58}=0.5363$

Hence, $Z=\sin^{-1} 0.5363=\mathbf{32.43^{\circ}}$ (or $147.57^{\circ}$ which is not possible)

Thus, $\boldsymbol{X}=180^{\circ}-128^{\circ}-32.43^{\circ}=\mathbf{19.57}^{\circ}$

Area of $X Y Z = \frac{1}{2} x z \sin Y = \frac{1}{2}(4.5)(7.2) \sin 128^{\circ} = \mathbf{12.77} \mathbf{~ c m}^{2}$

‚öôÔ∏è 23.5 Practical situations involving trigonometry

There are a number of practical situations in which the use of trigonometry is needed to find unknown sides and angles of triangles. This is demonstrated in the following worked problems.

üí° Problem 7. A room 8.0 m wide has a span roof which slopes at $33^{\circ}$ on one side and $40^{\circ}$ on the other. Find the length of the roof slopes, correct to the nearest centimetre

A section of the roof is shown in Figure 23.9.

Figure 23.9

Angle at ridge, $B=180^{\circ}-33^{\circ}-40^{\circ}=107^{\circ}$

From the sine rule, $\quad \frac{8.0}{\sin 107^{\circ}}=\frac{a}{\sin 33^{\circ}}$ from which $\quad \boldsymbol{a}=\frac{8.0 \sin 33^{\circ}}{\sin 107^{\circ}}=\mathbf{4.556} \mathrm{m}=\boldsymbol{BC}$

Also from the sine rule, $\quad \frac{8.0}{\sin 107^{\circ}}=\frac{c}{\sin 40^{\circ}}$ from which $\quad \boldsymbol{c}=\frac{8.0 \sin 40^{\circ}}{\sin 107^{\circ}}=\mathbf{5.377} \mathbf{m}=\boldsymbol{A B}$

Hence, the roof slopes are 4.56 m and 5.38 m , correct to the nearest centimetre.

üí° Problem 8. A man leaves a point walking at $6.5 \mathrm{~km}/\mathrm{h}$ in a direction $\mathrm{E} 20^{\circ} \mathrm{N}$ (i.e. a bearing of $70^{\circ}$). A cyclist leaves the same point at the same time in a direction $\mathrm{E} 40^{\circ} \mathrm{S}$ (i.e. a bearing of $130^{\circ}$) travelling at a constant speed. Find the average speed of the cyclist if the walker and cyclist are 80 km apart after 5 hours

After 5 hours the walker has travelled $5 \times 6.5=32.5 \mathrm{~km}$ (shown as AB in Figure 23.10). If AC is the distance the cyclist travels in 5 hours then BC=80 km.

Figure 23.10

Applying the sine rule, $\quad \frac{80}{\sin 60^{\circ}}=\frac{32.5}{\sin C}$

from which $\quad \sin C=\frac{32.5 \sin 60^{\circ}}{80}=0.3518$

Hence, $\quad C=\sin^{-1} 0.3518=20.60^{\circ}$ (or $159.40^{\circ}$, which is not possible)

and $\quad B=180^{\circ}-60^{\circ}-20.60^{\circ}=99.40^{\circ}$

Applying the sine rule again, $\frac{80}{\sin 60^{\circ}}=\frac{b}{\sin 99.40^{\circ}}$

from which $\quad b=\frac{80 \sin 99.40^{\circ}}{\sin 60^{\circ}}=91.14 \mathrm{~km}$

Since the cyclist travels 91.14 km in 5 hours,

$$\text{average speed}=\frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}=\frac{91.14}{5}=\mathbf{18.23} \mathbf{~ k m / h}$$

üí° Problem 9. Two voltage phasors are shown in Figure 23.11. If $V_{1}=40 \mathrm{~V}$ and $V_{2}=100 \mathrm{~V}$, determine the value of their resultant (i.e. length $O A$) and the angle the resultant makes with $V_{1}$
Figure 23.11

Angle $O B A=180^{\circ}-45^{\circ}=135^{\circ}$

Applying the cosine rule,

$$\begin{aligned} O A^{2} &= V_{1}^{2}+V_{2}^{2}-2 V_{1} V_{2} \cos O B A \\ &= 40^{2}+100^{2}-\left\{2(40)(100) \cos 135^{\circ}\right\} \\ &= 1600+10000-\{-5657\} \\ &= 1600+10000+5657=17257 \end{aligned}$$

Thus, resultant, $\boldsymbol{O A}=\sqrt{17257}=131.4 \mathrm{~V}$

Applying the sine rule $\quad \frac{131.4}{\sin 135^{\circ}}=\frac{100}{\sin A O B}$

from which $\quad \sin A O B=\frac{100 \sin 135^{\circ}}{131.4}=0.5381$

Hence, angle $A O B=\sin^{-1} 0.5381=32.55^{\circ}$ (or $147.45^{\circ}$, which is not possible)

Hence, the resultant voltage is 131.4 volts at 32.55$^{\circ}$ to $V_{1}$

üí° Problem 10. In Figure 23.12, $P R$ represents the inclined jib of a crane and is 10.0 m long. $P Q$ is 4.0 m long. Determine the inclination of the jib to the vertical and the length of tie $Q R$.
Figure 23.12

Applying the sine rule, $\quad \frac{P R}{\sin 120^{\circ}}=\frac{P Q}{\sin R}$

from which $\quad \sin R=\frac{P Q \sin 120^{\circ}}{P R}=\frac{(4.0) \sin 120^{\circ}}{10.0} = 0.3464$

Hence, $\angle R=\sin^{-1} 0.3464=20.27^{\circ}$ (or $159.73^{\circ}$, which is not possible)

$\angle \boldsymbol{P}=180^{\circ}-120^{\circ}-20.27^{\circ}=39.73^{\circ}$, which is the inclination of the jib to the vertical

Applying the sine rule, $\frac{10.0}{\sin 120^{\circ}}=\frac{Q R}{\sin 39.73^{\circ}}$

from which length of tie, $\boldsymbol{Q R}=\frac{10.0 \sin 39.73^{\circ}}{\sin 120^{\circ}} = \mathbf{7.38} \mathbf{~ m}$

üí° Problem 11. A vertical aerial stands on horizontal ground. A surveyor positioned due east of the aerial measures the elevation of the top as $48^{\circ}$. He moves due south 30.0 m and measures the elevation as $44^{\circ}$. Determine the height of the aerial

In Figure 23.16, DC represents the aerial, A is the initial position of the surveyor and B his final position.

From triangle ACD, $\tan 48^{\circ}=\frac{DC}{AC}$ from which $AC=\frac{DC}{\tan 48^{\circ}}$

Similarly, from triangle BCD, $BC=\frac{DC}{\tan 44^{\circ}}$

Figure 23.16

For triangle ABC, using Pythagoras' theorem,

$$BC^{2}=AB^{2}+AC^{2}$$ $$\left(\frac{DC}{\tan 44^{\circ}}\right)^{2}=(30.0)^{2}+\left(\frac{DC}{\tan 48^{\circ}}\right)^{2}$$ $$DC^{2}\left(\frac{1}{\tan^{2} 44^{\circ}}-\frac{1}{\tan^{2} 48^{\circ}}\right)=30.0^{2}$$ $$DC^{2}(1.072323-0.810727)=30.0^{2}$$ $$DC^{2}=\frac{30.0^{2}}{0.261596}=3440.4$$

Hence, height of aerial, $\boldsymbol{DC}=\sqrt{3340.4}=\mathbf{58.65} \mathbf{~ m}$.

üí° Problem 12. A crank mechanism of a petrol engine is shown in Figure 23.17. Arm $O A$ is 10.0 cm long and rotates clockwise about $O$. The connecting rod $A B$ is 30.0 cm long and end $B$ is constrained to move horizontally.
Figure 23.17
(a) For the position shown in Figure 23.17, determine the angle between the connecting rod $A B$ and the horizontal, and the length of $O B$.
(b) How far does $B$ move when angle $A O B$ changes from $50^{\circ}$ to $120^{\circ}$ ?

  • (a) Applying the sine rule, $\frac{AB}{\sin 50^{\circ}}=\frac{AO}{\sin B}$
    from which $\sin B=\frac{AO \sin 50^{\circ}}{AB}=\frac{10.0 \sin 50^{\circ}}{30.0} = 0.2553$
    Hence, $B=\sin^{-1} 0.2553=14.79^{\circ}$ (or $165.21^{\circ}$, which is not possible)
    Hence, the connecting rod AB makes an angle of 14.79$^{\circ}$ with the horizontal.
    Angle $O A B=180^{\circ}-50^{\circ}-14.79^{\circ}=115.21^{\circ}$
    Applying the sine rule: $\frac{30.0}{\sin 50^{\circ}}=\frac{O B}{\sin 115.21^{\circ}}$
    from which $\boldsymbol{O B}=\frac{30.0 \sin 115.21^{\circ}}{\sin 50^{\circ}} = \mathbf{35.43} \mathbf{~ cm}$
  • (b) Figure 23.18 shows the initial and final positions of the crank mechanism.
    Figure 23.18
    In triangle $O A^{\prime} B^{\prime}$, applying the sine rule, $\frac{30.0}{\sin 120^{\circ}}=\frac{10.0}{\sin A^{\prime} B^{\prime} O}$
    from which $\sin A^{\prime} B^{\prime} O=\frac{10.0 \sin 120^{\circ}}{30.0}=0.28868$
    Hence, $\quad A^{\prime} B^{\prime} O=\sin^{-1} 0.28868=16.78^{\circ} \quad$ (or $163.22^{\circ}$, which is not possible)
