The circle

üéØ 26.1 Introduction

A circle is a plain figure enclosed by a curved line, every point on which is equidistant from a point within, called the centre.

In Chapter 25, worked problems on the areas of circles and sectors were demonstrated. In this chapter, properties of circles are listed and arc lengths are calculated, together with more practical examples on the areas of sectors of circles. Finally, the equation of a circle is explained.

‚≠ï 26.2 Properties of circles

  • (a) The distance from the centre to the curve is called the radius, $r$, of the circle (see OP in Figure 26.1).

Figure 26.1

  • (b) The boundary of a circle is called the circumference, $c$.
  • (c) Any straight line passing through the centre and touching the circumference at each end is called the diameter, $\boldsymbol{d}$ (see QR in Figure 26.1). Thus, $d=2r$.
  • (d) The ratio $\frac{\text{circumference}}{\text{diameter}}$ is a constant for any circle. This constant is denoted by the Greek letter $\pi$ (pronounced 'pie'), where $\pi=3.14159$, correct to 5 decimal places (check with your calculator). Hence, $\frac{c}{d}=\pi$ or $\boldsymbol{c}=\boldsymbol{\pi} \boldsymbol{d}$ or $\boldsymbol{c}=\mathbf{2} \boldsymbol{\pi} \boldsymbol{r}$.
  • (e) A semicircle is one half of a whole circle.
  • (f) A quadrant is one quarter of a whole circle.
  • (g) A tangent to a circle is a straight line which meets the circle at one point only and does not cut the circle when produced. AC in Figure 26.1 is a tangent to the circle since it touches the curve at point B only. If radius OB is drawn, angle ABO is a right angle.
  • (h) The sector of a circle is the part of a circle between radii (for example, the portion OXY of Figure 26.2 is a sector). If a sector is less than a semicircle it is called a minor sector; if greater than a semicircle it is called a major sector.

Figure 26.2

  • (i) The chord of a circle is any straight line which divides the circle into two parts and is terminated at each end by the circumference. ST, in Figure 26.2, is a chord.
  • (j) Segment is the name given to the parts into which a circle is divided by a chord. If the segment is less than a semicircle it is called a minor segment (see shaded area in Figure 26.2). If the segment is greater than a semicircle it is called a major segment (see the un-shaded area in Figure 26.2).
  • (k) An arc is a portion of the circumference of a circle. The distance SRT in Figure 26.2 is called a minor arc and the distance SXYT is called a major arc.
  • (l) The angle at the centre of a circle, subtended by an arc, is double the angle at the circumference subtended by the same arc. With reference to Figure 26.3,
    Angle AOC = $2 \times$ angle ABC

Figure 26.3

  • (m) The angle in a semicircle is a right angle (see angle BQP in Figure 26.3).

üí° Problem 1. Find the circumference of a circle of radius 12.0 cm

Circumference, $c=2 \times \pi \times \text{radius} = 2\pi r=2\pi(12.0) = \mathbf{75.40} \mathbf{~ cm}$

üí° Problem 2. If the diameter of a circle is 75 mm , find its circumference

Circumference, $c=\pi \times \text{diameter} = \pi d=\pi(75) = \mathbf{235.6} \mathbf{~ mm}$

üí° Problem 3. Determine the radius of a circular pond if its perimeter is 112 m

Perimeter = circumference, $c=2\pi r$

Hence, radius of pond, $r=\frac{c}{2\pi}=\frac{112}{2\pi}=\mathbf{17.83} \mathbf{~ cm}$

üí° Problem 4. In Figure 26.4, AB is a tangent to the circle at B. If the circle radius is 40 mm and AB=150 mm, calculate the length AO
Figure 26.4

A tangent to a circle is at right angles to a radius drawn from the point of contact; i.e., $\boldsymbol{ABO}=\mathbf{90^{\circ}}$. Hence, using Pythagoras' theorem,

$$\begin{aligned} AO^{2} &= AB^{2}+OB^{2} \\ \text{from which, } \quad \boldsymbol{AO} &= \sqrt{AB^{2}+OB^{2}} \\ &= \sqrt{150^{2}+40^{2}}=\mathbf{155.2} \mathbf{~ mm}\end{aligned}$$

üîÑ 26.3 Radians and degrees

One radian is defined as the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc equal in length to the radius. With reference to Figure 26.7, for arc length $s$,

$$\theta \text{ radians} = \frac{s}{r}$$

Figure 26.7

When $s=$ whole circumference ($=2\pi r$) then

$$\theta = \frac{s}{r} = \frac{2\pi r}{r}=2\pi$$

i.e. $2\pi$ radians $= 360^{\circ}$ or $\pi$ radians $= 180^{\circ}$

Thus, $1 \mathbf{r a d}=\frac{\mathbf{1 8 0}^{\circ}}{\boldsymbol{\pi}}=\mathbf{57.30}^{\circ}$, correct to 2 decimal places.

Since $\pi \mathrm{~rad}=180^{\circ}$, then $\frac{\pi}{2}=90^{\circ}, \frac{\pi}{3}=60^{\circ}, \frac{\pi}{4}=45^{\circ}$, and so on.

üí° Problem 5. Convert to radians: (a) $125^{\circ}$ (b) $69^{\circ} 47^{\prime}$

  • (a) Since $180^{\circ}=\pi \mathrm{rad}, 1^{\circ}=\frac{180}{\pi} \mathrm{rad}$, therefore $125^{\circ}=125\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad}=\mathbf{2.182} \text{ radians}$.
  • (b) $69^{\circ} 47^{\prime}=69 \frac{47^{\circ}}{60}=69.783^{\circ}$ (or, with your calculator, enter $69^{\circ} 47^{\prime}$ using ${}^\circ{}''$ function, press = and press ${}^\circ{}''$ again).
    and $69.783^{\circ}=69.783\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad} = \mathbf{1.218} \text{ radians}$

üí° Problem 6. Convert to degrees and minutes:
(a) 0.749 radians (b) $3\pi/4$ radians

  • (a) Since $\pi \mathrm{~rad}=180^{\circ}, 1 \mathrm{~rad}=\frac{180^{\circ}}{\pi}$
    therefore $\quad 0.749 \mathrm{~rad}=0.749\left(\frac{180}{\pi}\right)^{\circ}=42.915^{\circ}$
    $0.915^{\circ}=(0.915 \times 60)^{\prime}=55^{\prime}$, correct to the nearest minute,
    Hence, 0.749 radians = 42$^{\circ}$ 55$^{\prime}$
  • (b) Since $1 \mathrm{~rad}=\left(\frac{180}{\pi}\right)^{0}$ then $\frac{3\pi}{4} \mathrm{~rad}=\frac{3\pi}{4}\left(\frac{180}{\pi}\right)^{\circ}=\frac{3}{4}(180)^{\circ}=\mathbf{135}^{\circ}$

üí° Problem 7. Express in radians, in terms of $\pi$,
(a) $150^{\circ}$ (b) $270^{\circ}$ (c) $37.5^{\circ}$

Since $180^{\circ}=\pi \mathrm{rad}, 1^{\circ}=\frac{\pi}{180} \mathrm{rad}$

  • (a) $150^{\circ}=150\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad}=\frac{5\pi}{\mathbf{6}} \mathbf{~ r a d}$
  • (b) $270^{\circ}=270\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad}=\frac{\mathbf{3\pi}}{\mathbf{2}} \mathbf{~ r a d}$
  • (c) $37.5^{\circ}=37.5\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad}=\frac{75\pi}{360} \mathrm{rad}=\frac{\mathbf{5\pi}}{\mathbf{24}} \mathbf{~ r a d}$

üìè 26.4 Arc length and area of circles and sectors

26.4.1 Arc length

From the definition of the radian in the previous section and Figure 26.7,

arc length, $\boldsymbol{s}=\boldsymbol{r} \boldsymbol{\theta} \quad$ where $\theta$ is in radians

26.4.2 Area of a circle

From Chapter 25, for any circle, area = $\pi \times (\text{radius})^{2}$ i.e.

$$\text{area}=\pi r^{2}$$

Since $r=\frac{d}{2}$, area $=\pi r^{2}$ or $\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}$

26.4.3 Area of a sector

$$\text{Area of a sector}=\frac{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\mathbf{360}}\left(\boldsymbol{\pi} \boldsymbol{r}^{\mathbf{2}}\right) \text{ when } \theta \text{ is in degrees}$$ $$=\frac{\theta}{2\pi}\left(\pi r^{2}\right)$$ $$=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{2}} \boldsymbol{r}^{\mathbf{2}} \boldsymbol{\theta} \quad \text{ when } \theta \text{ is in radians}$$

üí° Problem 8. A hockey pitch has a semicircle of radius 14.63 m around each goal net. Find the area enclosed by the semicircle, correct to the nearest square metre

Area of a semicircle $=\frac{1}{2}\pi r^{2}$

When $r=14.63 \mathrm{~m}, \quad$ area $=\frac{1}{2}\pi(14.63)^{2}$

i.e. area of semicircle = 336 m$^{2}$

üí° Problem 9. Find the area of a circular metal plate having a diameter of 35.0 mm , correct to the nearest square millimetre

Area of a circle $=\pi r^{2}=\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}$

When $d=35.0 \mathrm{~mm}, \quad$ area $=\frac{\pi(35.0)^{2}}{4}$

i.e. area of circular plate = 962 $\mathrm{mm}^{2}$

üí° Problem 10. Find the area of a circle having a circumference of 60.0 mm

Circumference, $c=2\pi r$

from which radius, $r=\frac{c}{2\pi}=\frac{60.0}{2\pi}=\frac{30.0}{\pi}$

Area of a circle $=\pi r^{2}$

i.e. $\quad$ area $=\pi\left(\frac{30.0}{\pi}\right)^{2}=\mathbf{286.5} \mathbf{~ m m}^{2}$

üí° Problem 11. Find the length of the arc of a circle of radius 5.5 cm when the angle subtended at the centre is 1.20 radians

Length of arc, $s=r\theta$, where $\theta$ is in radians.

Hence, arc length, $s=(5.5)(1.20)=\mathbf{6.60} \mathbf{~ c m}$.

üí° Problem 12. Determine the diameter and circumference of a circle if an arc of length 4.75 cm subtends an angle of 0.91 radians

Since arc length, $s=r\theta$ then radius, $r=\frac{s}{\theta}=\frac{4.75}{0.91}=5.22 \mathrm{~cm}$

Diameter $=2 \times \text{radius} = 2 \times 5.22=\mathbf{10.44} \mathbf{~ c m}$

Circumference, $c=\pi d=\pi(10.44)=\mathbf{32.80} \mathbf{~ c m}$

üí° Problem 13. If an angle of $125^{\circ}$ is subtended by an arc of a circle of radius 8.4 cm , find the length of (a) the minor arc and (b) the major arc, correct to 3 significant figures

Since $180^{\circ}=\pi$ rad then $1^{\circ}=\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right)$ rad and $125^{\circ}=125\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \mathrm{rad}$

  • (a) Length of minor arc, $s=r\theta=(8.4)(125)\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right)=\mathbf{18.3} \mathbf{~ c m}$ correct to 3 significant figures
  • (b) Length of major arc = (circumference - minor arc) $=2\pi(8.4)-18.3=\mathbf{34.5} \mathbf{~ c m}$, correct to 3 significant figures.
    (Alternatively, major arc $=r\theta = 8.4(360-125)\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right)=\mathbf{34.5} \mathbf{~ c m}$.)

üí° Problem 14. Determine the angle, in degrees and minutes, subtended at the centre of a circle of diameter 42 mm by an arc of length 36 mm . Calculate also the area of the minor sector formed

Since length of arc, $s=r\theta$ then $\theta=\frac{s}{r}$

Radius, $\quad r=\frac{\text{diameter}}{2}=\frac{42}{2}=21 \mathrm{~mm}$, hence $\quad \theta=\frac{s}{r}=\frac{36}{21}=1.7143 \text{ radians.}$

$1.7143 \mathrm{~rad}=1.7143 \times \left(\frac{180}{\pi}\right)^{\circ}=98.22^{\circ}=\mathbf{98}^{\circ} \mathbf{13}^{\prime}=$ angle subtended at centre of circle.

From equation (2), area of sector $=\frac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta = \frac{1}{2}(21)^{2}(1.7143) = \mathbf{378} \mathbf{~ m m}^{\mathbf{2}}$

üí° Problem 15. A football stadium floodlight can spread its illumination over an angle of $45^{\circ}$ to a distance of 55 m . Determine the maximum area that is floodlit.

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Floodlit area} &= \text{area of sector} = \frac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{2}(55)^{2}\left(45 \times \frac{\pi}{180}\right) \\ &= \mathbf{1188} \mathbf{~ m}^{2} \end{aligned}$$

üí° Problem 16. An automatic garden sprayer produces spray to a distance of 1.8 m and revolves through an angle $\alpha$ which may be varied. If the desired spray catchment area is to be $2.5 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$, to what should angle $\alpha$ be set, correct to the nearest degree?

Area of sector $=\frac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$, hence $2.5=\frac{1}{2}(1.8)^{2}\alpha$

from which, $\quad \alpha=\frac{2.5 \times 2}{1.8^{2}}=1.5432 \text{ radians}$

$1.5432 \mathrm{~rad}=\left(1.5432 \times \frac{180}{\pi}\right)^{\circ}=88.42^{\circ}$

Hence, angle $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=\mathbf{88}^{\circ}$, correct to the nearest degree.

‚≠ï 26.5 The equation of a circle

The simplest equation of a circle, centre at the origin and radius $r$, is given by

$$x^{2}+y^{2}=r^{2}$$

For example, Figure 26.15 shows a circle $x^{2}+y^{2}=9$.

Figure 26.15

More generally, the equation of a circle, centre $(a, b)$ and radius $r$, is given by

$$(x-a)^{2}+(y-b)^{2}=r^{2}$$

Figure 26.16 shows a circle $(x-2)^{2}+(y-3)^{2}=4$.

Figure 26.16

The general equation of a circle is

$$x^{2}+y^{2}+2ex+2fy+c=0$$

Multiplying out the bracketed terms in equation (1) gives

$$x^{2}-2ax+a^{2}+y^{2}-2by+b^{2}=r^{2}$$

Comparing this with equation (2) gives

$2e=-2a$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{a}=-\frac{\mathbf{2e}}{\mathbf{2}}$

and $2f=-2b$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{b}=-\frac{\mathbf{2f}}{\mathbf{2}}$

and $c=a^{2}+b^{2}-r^{2}$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{r}=\sqrt{\boldsymbol{a}^{2}+\boldsymbol{b}^{\mathbf{2}}-\boldsymbol{c}}$

Thus, for example, the equation $x^{2}+y^{2}-4x-6y+9=0$ represents a circle with centre,

$a=-\left(\frac{-4}{2}\right), b=-\left(\frac{-6}{2}\right)$ i.e., at (2,3) and

radius, $r=\sqrt{2^{2}+3^{2}-9}=2$

Hence, $x^{2}+y^{2}-4x-6y+9=0$ is the circle shown in Figure 26.16 (which may be checked by multiplying out the brackets in the equation $(x-2)^{2}+(y-3)^{2}=4$).

üí° Problem 18. Determine (a) the radius and (b) the co-ordinates of the centre of the circle given by the equation $x^{2}+y^{2}+8x-2y+8=0$

$x^{2}+y^{2}+8x-2y+8=0$ is of the form shown in equation (2),

where $\quad a=-\left(\frac{8}{2}\right)=-4, b=-\left(\frac{-2}{2}\right)=1$

and $r=\sqrt{(-4)^{2}+1^{2}-8}=\sqrt{9}=3$

Hence, $x^{2}+y^{2}+8x-2y+8=0$ represents a circle centre (-4,1) and radius 3, as shown in Figure 26.17.

Figure 26.17

Alternatively, $x^{2}+y^{2}+8x-2y+8=0$ may be rearranged as

$(x+4)^{2}+(y-1)^{2}-9 = 0$

i.e. $(x+4)^{2}+(y-1)^{2} = 3^{2}$

which represents a circle, centre (-4,1) and radius 3, as stated above.

üí° Problem 19. Sketch the circle given by the equation $x^{2}+y^{2}-4x+6y-3=0$

The equation of a circle, centre $(a, b)$, radius $r$ is given by

$$(x-a)^{2}+(y-b)^{2}=r^{2}$$

The general equation of a circle is $x^{2}+y^{2}+2ex+2fy+c=0$

From above $a=-\frac{2e}{2}, b=-\frac{2f}{2}$ and $r=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}-c}$

Hence, if $x^{2}+y^{2}-4x+6y-3=0$

then $\quad a=-\left(\frac{-4}{2}\right)=2, b=-\left(\frac{6}{2}\right)=-3 \quad$ and $r=\sqrt{2^{2}+(-3)^{2}-(-3)}=\sqrt{16}=4$

Thus, the circle has centre (2, -3) and radius 4, as shown in Figure 26.18.

Figure 26.18

Alternatively, $x^{2}+y^{2}-4x+6y-3=0$ may be rearranged as

$(x-2)^{2}+(y+3)^{2}-3-13=0$

i.e. $(x-2)^{2}+(y+3)^{2}=4^{2}$

which represents a circle, centre (2,-3) and radius 4, as stated above.
