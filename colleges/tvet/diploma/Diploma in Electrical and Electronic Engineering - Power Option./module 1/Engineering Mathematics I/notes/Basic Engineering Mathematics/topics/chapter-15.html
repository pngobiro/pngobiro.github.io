Logarithms

üéØ 15.1 Introduction to logarithms

With the use of calculators firmly established, logarithmic tables are now rarely used for calculation. However, the theory of logarithms is important, for there are several scientific and engineering laws that involve the rules of logarithms.

From Chapter 7, we know that $\quad 16 = 2^{4}$.

The number 4 is called the power or the exponent or the index. In the expression $2^{4}$, the number 2 is called the base.

In another example, we know that $\quad 64 = 8^{2}$.

In this example, 2 is the power, or exponent, or index. The number 8 is the base.

15.1.1 What is a logarithm?

Consider the expression $16=2^{4}$.

An alternative, yet equivalent, way of writing this expression is $\log_{2} 16=4$.

This is stated as 'log to the base 2 of 16 equals 4'.

We see that the logarithm is the same as the power or index in the original expression. It is the base in the original expression that becomes the base of the logarithm.

$$\text{The two statements } 16=2^{4} \text{ and }$$
$$\log_{2} 16=4 \text{ are equivalent}$$

If we write either of them, we are automatically implying the other.

In general, if a number $y$ can be written in the form $a^{x}$, then the index $x$ is called the 'logarithm of $y$ to the base

of $a'$, i.e.

$$\text{if } y=a^{x} \text{ then } x=\log_{a} y$$

In another example, if we write down that $64=8^{2}$ then the equivalent statement using logarithms is $\log_{8} 64=2$. In another example, if we write down that $\log_{3} 27=3$ then the equivalent statement using powers is $3^{3}=27$. So the two sets of statements, one involving powers and one involving logarithms, are equivalent.

15.1.2 Common logarithms

From the above, if we write down that $1000=10^{3}$, then $3=\log_{10} 1000$. This may be checked using the 'log' button on your calculator.

Logarithms having a base of 10 are called common logarithms and $\log_{10}$ is usually abbreviated to lg. The following values may be checked using a calculator.

$$\lg 27.5 = 1.4393\ldots$$ $$\lg 378.1 = 2.5776\ldots$$ $$\lg 0.0204 = -1.6903\ldots$$

15.1.3 Napierian logarithms

Logarithms having a base of $e$ (where $e$ is a mathematical constant approximately equal to 2.7183 ) are called hyperbolic, Napierian or natural logarithms, and $\log_{e}$ is usually abbreviated to ln. The following values may be checked using a calculator.

$$\ln 3.65 = 1.2947\ldots$$ $$\ln 417.3 = 6.0338\ldots$$ $$\ln 0.182 = -1.7037\ldots$$

Napierian logarithms are explained further in Chapter 16, following.

Here are some worked problems to help understanding of logarithms.

üí° Problem 1. Evaluate $\log_{3} 9$

Let $x=\log_{3} 9 \quad$ then $3^{x}=9$ from the definition of a logarithm,

i.e. $3^{x}=3^{2}$, from which $x=2$

Hence, $\log_{3} 9=2$

üí° Problem 2. Evaluate $\log_{10} 10$

Let $x=\log_{10} 10$ then $10^{x}=10$ from the definition of a logarithm,

i.e. $10^{x}=10^{1}$, from which $x=1$

Hence, $\log_{10} 10=1$ (which may be checked using a calculator).

üí° Problem 3. Evaluate $\log_{16} 8$

Let $x=\log_{16} 8 \quad$ then $16^{x}=8$ from the definition of a logarithm,

i.e. $(2^{4})^{x}=2^{3}$

i.e. $2^{4x}=2^{3}$ from the laws of indices,

from which, $4x=3$ and $x=\frac{\mathbf{3}}{\mathbf{4}}$

Hence, $\log_{16} 8=\frac{3}{4}$

üí° Problem 4. Evaluate $\lg 0.001$

Let $x=\lg 0.001=\log_{10} 0.001$ then $10^{x}=0.001$

i.e. $10^{x}=10^{-3}$

from which, $x=-3$

Hence, $\lg 0.001=-3$ (which may be checked using a calculator)

üí° Problem 5. Evaluate $\ln e$

Let $x=\ln e=\log_{e} e$ then $e^{x}=e$

i.e. $e^{x}=e^{1}$, from which $x=1$

Hence, $\ln \boldsymbol{e}=\mathbf{1}$ (which may be checked by a calculator)

üí° Problem 6. Evaluate $\log_{3} \frac{1}{81}$

Let $x=\log_{3} \frac{1}{81} \quad$ then $\quad 3^{x}=\frac{1}{81}=\frac{1}{3^{4}}=3^{-4}$ from which $x=-4$

Hence, $\quad \log_{3} \frac{1}{81}=-4$

üí° Problem 7. Solve the equation $\lg x=3$

If $\lg x=3$ then $\log_{10} x=3$

and $x=10^{3} \quad$ i.e. $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{1000}$

üí° Problem 8. Solve the equation $\log_{2} x=5$

If $\log_{2} x=5 \quad$ then $\quad \boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}^{\mathbf{5}}=\mathbf{32}$

üí° Problem 9. Solve the equation $\log_{5} x=-2$

If $\log_{5} x=-2 \quad$ then $\quad x=5^{-2}=\frac{1}{5^{2}}=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{25}}$

üìê 15.2 Laws of logarithms

There are three laws of logarithms, which apply to any base:

  • (1) To multiply two numbers:
    $$\log (A \times B)=\log A+\log B$$
    The following may be checked by using a calculator.
    $\lg 10=1$
    Also, $\quad \lg 5+\lg 2=0.69897\ldots+0.301029\ldots=1$
    Hence, $\quad \lg (5 \times 2)=\lg 10=\lg 5+\lg 2$
  • (2) To divide two numbers:
    $$\log \left(\frac{A}{B}\right)=\log A-\log B$$
    The following may be checked using a calculator.
    $\ln \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)=\ln 2.5=0.91629\ldots$
    Also,
    $$\begin{aligned} \ln 5-\ln 2 &= 1.60943\ldots-0.69314\ldots \\ &= 0.91629\ldots \end{aligned}$$
    Hence, $\quad \ln \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)=\ln 5-\ln 2$
  • (3) To raise a number to a power:
    $$\log \mathrm{A}^{n}=n \log \mathrm{A}$$
    The following may be checked using a calculator.
    $\lg 5^{2}=\lg 25=1.39794\ldots$
    Also, $\quad 2 \lg 5=2 \times 0.69897\ldots=1.39794\ldots$
    Hence, $\quad \lg 5^{2}=2 \lg 5$

Here are some worked problems to help understanding of the laws of logarithms.

üí° Problem 10. Write $\log 4+\log 7$ as the logarithm of a single number

$$\log 4+\log 7 = \log (7 \times 4)$$ $$= \log \mathbf{28}$$

üí° Problem 11. Write $\log 16-\log 2$ as the logarithm of a single number

$$\log 16-\log 2 = \log \left(\frac{16}{2}\right) \quad \text{by the second law of logarithms}$$ $$= \log 8$$

üí° Problem 12. Write 2 log 3 as the logarithm of a single number

$$2 \log 3 = \log 3^{2} \quad \text{by the third law of logarithms}$$ $$= \log 9$$

üí° Problem 13. Write $\frac{1}{2} \log 25$ as the logarithm of a single number

$$\frac{1}{2} \log 25 = \log 25^{\frac{1}{2}} \quad \text{by the third law of logarithms}$$ $$= \log \sqrt{25} = \log 5$$

üí° Problem 14. Simplify $\log 64-\log 128+\log 32$

$64=2^{6}, 128=2^{7}$ and $32=2^{5}$

Hence, $\quad \log 64-\log 128+\log 32$

$$\begin{aligned} &= \log 2^{6} - \log 2^{7} + \log 2^{5} \\ &= 6 \log 2 - 7 \log 2 + 5 \log 2 \end{aligned}$$

by the third law of logarithms

$$= \mathbf{4 \log 2}$$

üí° Problem 15. Write $\frac{1}{2}\log 16+\frac{1}{3}\log 27-2\log 5$ as the logarithm of a single number

$$\begin{aligned} &\frac{1}{2} \log 16+\frac{1}{3} \log 27-2 \log 5 \\ &= \log 16^{\frac{1}{2}}+\log 27^{\frac{1}{3}}-\log 5^{2} \end{aligned}$$

by the third law of logarithms

$$= \log \sqrt{16}+\log \sqrt[3]{27}-\log 25$$

by the laws of indices

$$= \log 4+\log 3-\log 25$$
$$= \log \left(\frac{4 \times 3}{25}\right) \text{ by the first and second laws of logarithms}$$ $$= \log \left(\frac{12}{25}\right)=\log 0.48$$

üí° Problem 16. Write (a) $\log 30$ (b) $\log 450$ in terms of $\log 2, \log 3$ and $\log 5$ to any base

  • (a) $\log 30=\log (2 \times 15)=\log (2 \times 3 \times 5) = \log 2+\log 3+\log 5$ by the first law of logarithms
  • (b) $\log 450=\log (2 \times 225)=\log (2 \times 3 \times 75) = \log (2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 25) = \log (2 \times 3^{2} \times 5^{2}) = \log 2+\log 3^{2}+\log 5^{2}$ by the first law of logarithms
    i.e. $\quad \log 450=\log 2+2 \log 3+2 \log 5$ by the third law of logarithms

üí° Problem 17. Write $\log \left(\frac{8 \times \sqrt[4]{5}}{81}\right)$ in terms of $\log 2, \log 3$ and $\log 5$ to any base

$$\log \left(\frac{8 \times \sqrt[4]{5}}{81}\right) = \log 8+\log \sqrt[4]{5}-\log 81$$

by the first and second laws of logarithms

$$= \log 2^{3}+\log 5^{\frac{1}{4}}-\log 3^{4}$$

by the laws of indices

i.e. $\quad \log \left(\frac{8 \times \sqrt[4]{5}}{81}\right)=3 \log 2+\frac{1}{4} \log 5-4 \log 3$ by the third law of logarithms

üí° Problem 18. Evaluate
$$\frac{\log 25-\log 125+\frac{1}{2}\log 625}{3\log 5}$$

$$\frac{\log 25-\log 125+\frac{1}{2} \log 625}{3 \log 5}$$ $$= \frac{\log 5^{2}-\log 5^{3}+\frac{1}{2} \log 5^{4}}{3 \log 5}$$ $$= \frac{2 \log 5-3 \log 5+\frac{4}{2} \log 5}{3 \log 5}$$ $$= \frac{1 \log 5}{3 \log 5}=\frac{\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{3}}$$

üí° Problem 19. Solve the equation
$$\log (x-1)+\log (x+8)=2\log (x+2)$$

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{LHS}=\log (x-1)+\log (x+8)&= \log (x-1)(x+8) \\ &\text{from the first law of logarithms} \\ &= \log (x^{2}+7x-8)\end{aligned}$$
$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{RHS}=2\log (x+2)&= \log (x+2)^{2} \\ &\text{from the first law of logarithms} \\ &= \log (x^{2}+4x+4)\end{aligned}$$

Hence, $\log (x^{2}+7x-8) = \log (x^{2}+4x+4)$

from which, $x^{2}+7x-8 = x^{2}+4x+4$

i.e. $7x-8 = 4x+4$

i.e. $3x = 12$

i.e. $x = 4$

üí° Problem 20. Solve the equation
$$\frac{1}{2}\log 4=\log x$$

$$\frac{1}{2}\log 4 = \log 4^{\frac{1}{2}} \quad \text{from the third law of}$$ $$= \log \sqrt{4} \quad \text{from the laws of indices}$$

Hence, $\frac{1}{2}\log 4 = \log x$

becomes $\log \sqrt{4} = \log x$

i.e. $\log 2 = \log x$

from which, $2 = x$

i.e. the solution of the equation is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{2}$.

üí° Problem 21. Solve the equation
$$\log (x^{2}-3)-\log x=\log 2$$

$$\log (x^{2}-3)-\log x = \log \left(\frac{x^{2}-3}{x}\right) \quad \text{from the second law of logarithms}$$

Hence, $\quad \log \left(\frac{x^{2}-3}{x}\right) = \log 2$

from which, $\quad \frac{x^{2}-3}{x}=2$

Rearranging gives $\quad x^{2}-3=2x$

and $\quad x^{2}-2x-3=0$

Factorizing gives $\quad (x-3)(x+1)=0$

from which, $x=3$ or $x=-1$

$x=-1$ is not a valid solution since the logarithm of a negative number has no real root.

Hence, the solution of the equation is $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{3}$.

üìà 15.3 Indicial equations

The laws of logarithms may be used to solve certain equations involving powers, called indicial equations.

For example, to solve, say, $3^{x}=27$, logarithms to a base of 10 are taken of both sides,

i.e. $\quad \log_{10} 3^{x}=\log_{10} 27$

and $\quad x\log_{10} 3 = \log_{10} 27$ by the third law of logarithms

Rearranging gives $\quad x = \frac{\log_{10} 27}{\log_{10} 3}=\frac{1.43136\ldots}{0.47712\ldots} = \mathbf{3} \quad$ which may be readily checked.

(Note, $\frac{\log 27}{\log 3}$ is not equal to $\log \frac{27}{3}$)

üí° Problem 22. Solve the equation $2^{x}=5$, correct to 4 significant figures

Taking logarithms to base 10 of both sides of $2^{x}=5$ gives

i.e. $\quad \log_{10} 2^{x}=\log_{10} 5$

$x\log_{10} 2 = \log_{10} 5$ by the third law of logarithms

Rearranging gives x $= \frac{\log_{10} 5}{\log_{10} 2}=\frac{0.6989700\ldots}{0.3010299\ldots} = \mathbf{2.322}$, correct to 4 significant figures.

üí° Problem 23. Solve the equation $2^{x+1}=3^{2x-5}$ correct to 2 decimal places

Taking logarithms to base 10 of both sides gives

$$\log_{10} 2^{x+1}=\log_{10} 3^{2x-5}$$

i.e. $\quad (x+1)\log_{10} 2=(2x-5)\log_{10} 3$

$$x\log_{10} 2+\log_{10} 2 = 2x\log_{10} 3-5\log_{10} 3$$

i.e. $0.3010x+0.3010 = 0.9542x-2.3855$

Hence, $2.3855+0.3010 = 0.9542x-0.3010x$

$$2.6865 = 0.6532x$$

from which $x=\frac{2.6865}{0.6532}=\mathbf{4.11}$ correct to 2 decimal places.

üí° Problem 24. Solve the equation $x^{2.7}=34.68$, correct to 4 significant figures

Taking logarithms to base 10 of both sides gives

$$\log_{10} x^{2.7}=\log_{10} 34.68$$
$$2.7\log_{10} x=\log_{10} 34.68$$

Hence, $\log_{10} x = \frac{\log_{10} 34.68}{2.7}=0.57040$

Thus, x $=$ antilog 0.57040 $= 10^{0.57040} = \mathbf{3.719}$ correct to 4 significant figures.

üìà 15.4 Graphs of logarithmic functions

A graph of $y=\log_{10} x$ is shown in Figure 15.1 and a graph of $y=\log_{e} x$ is shown in Figure 15.2. Both can be seen to be of similar shape; in fact, the same general shape occurs for a logarithm to any base.

Figure 15.1

In general, with a logarithm to any base, $a$, it is noted that

  • (a) $\log_{a} 1=0$
    Let $\log_{a} = x$ then $a^{x}=1$ from the definition of the logarithm.
    If $a^{x}=1$ then $x=0$ from the laws of logarithms.
    Hence, $\log_{\boldsymbol{a}} \mathbf{1}=\mathbf{0}$. In the above graphs it is seen that $\log_{10} 1=0$ and $\log_{e} 1=0$.
  • (b) $\log_{a} a=1$
    Let $\log_{a} a=x$ then $a^{x}=a$ from the definition of a logarithm.
    If $a^{x}=a$ then $x=1$.
    Figure 15.2
    Hence, $\log_{a} a=1$. (Check with a calculator that $\log_{10} 10=1$ and $\log_{e} e=1$.)
  • (c) $\log_{a} 0 \rightarrow -\infty$
    Let $\log_{a} 0=x$ then $a^{x}=0$ from the definition of a logarithm.
    If $a^{x}=0$, and a is a positive real number, then $x$ must approach minus infinity. (For example, check with a calculator, $2^{-2}=0.25,2^{-20}= 9.54 \times 10^{-7},2^{-200}=6.22 \times 10^{-61}$, and so on.) Hence, $\log_{\boldsymbol{a}} \mathbf{0} \rightarrow -\infty$
