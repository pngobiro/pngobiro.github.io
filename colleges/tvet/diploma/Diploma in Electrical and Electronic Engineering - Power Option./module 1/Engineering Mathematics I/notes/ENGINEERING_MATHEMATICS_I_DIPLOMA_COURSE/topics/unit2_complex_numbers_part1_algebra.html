COMPLEX NUMBERS

2.1 ALGEBRA OF COMPLEX NUMBERS

Introduction:

Let us consider the quadratic equation \(ax^{2}+bx+c=0\). The solution of this equation is given by the formula \(x=\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^{2}-4ac}}{2a}\) which is meaningful only when \(b^{2}-4ac > 0\). Because the square of a real number is always positive and it cannot be negative. If it is negative, then the solution for the equation extends the real number system to a new kind of number system that allows the square root of negative numbers. The square root of -1 is denoted by the symbol \(i\), called the imaginary unit, which was first introduced in mathematics by the famous Swiss mathematician, Leonhard Euler in 1748. Thus for any two real numbers a and b, we can form a new number \(a+ib\) is called a complex number. The set of all complex numbers denoted by C and the nomenclature of a complex number was introduced by a German mathematician C.F. Gauss.

Definition: Complex Number

A number which is of the form \(a+ib\) where \(a, b \in R\) and \(i^{2}=-1\) is called a complex number and it is denoted by z . If \(z=a+ib\) then a is called the real part of z and b is called the imaginary part of z and are denoted by \(\operatorname{Re}(z)\) and \(\operatorname{Im}(z)\).

For example, if \(z=3+4i\) then \(\operatorname{Re}(z)=3\) and \(\operatorname{Im}(z)=4\).

üí°Note

In the complex number \(z=a+ib\) we have,

  1. (i)If \(a=0\) then z is purely imaginary
  2. (ii)If \(b=0\) then z is purely real.
  3. (iii)\(z=a+ib=(a, b)\) any complex number can be expressed as an ordered pair.

Conjugate of a complex number:

If \(z=a+ib\) then the conjugate of z is defined by \(a-ib\) and it is denoted by \(\bar{z}\). Thus, if \(z=a+ib\) then \(\bar{z}=a-ib\).

Results:

  1. (i) \(\overline{\bar{Z}}=Z\)
  2. (ii) \(a=\operatorname{Re}(z)=\frac{z+\bar{z}}{2} \& b=I_{m}(z)=\frac{z-\bar{z}}{2}\)
  3. (iii) \(\overline{Z_{1}+Z_{2}}=\overline{Z}_{1}+\overline{Z}_{2}\)
  4. (iv) \(\overline{Z_{1}-Z_{2}}=\overline{Z}_{1}-\overline{Z}_{2}\)
  5. (v) \(\overline{Z_{1}Z_{2}}=\overline{Z}_{1}\overline{Z}_{2}\)
  6. (vi) \(\overline{\left(\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right)}=\frac{\bar{Z}_{1}}{\bar{Z}_{2}}\) where \(Z_{2} \neq 0\)
  7. (vii) \(\overline{z^{n}}=(\bar{z})^{n}\)

Algebra of complex numbers:

(i) Addition of two complex numbers:
Let \(z_{1}=a+ib\) and \(z_{2}=c+id\) be any two complex numbers then their sum is defined as
\(z_{1}+z_{2}=a+ib+c+id=(a+c)+i(b+d) \in C\)
\(z+\bar{z}=2a \quad\) Real number.

(ii) Difference of two complex numbers:
Let \(z_{1}=a+ib\) and \(z_{2}=c+id\) be any two complex numbers then their difference is defined as
\(z_{1}-z_{2}=(a+ib)-(c+id)=(a-c)+i(b-d) \in C\)
\(z-\bar{Z}=2ib \quad\) Imaginary number.

(iii) Multiplication of two compelx numbers:
Let \(z_{1}=a+ib\) and \(z_{2}=c+id\) be any two complex numbers then their product is defined as,
\(z_{1}z_{2} =(a+ib)(c+id)\)
\(=ac+i ad+i bc+i^{2}bd\)
\( =(ac-bd)+i(ad+bc) \in C\)
\(z \bar{Z}=(a+ib)(a-ib)=a^{2}+b^{2}\)

(iv) Division of two complex numbers:
Let \(z_{1}=a+ib\) and \(z_{2}=c+id \neq 0\) be any two complex numbers then their quotient is defined as
\(\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=\frac{a+ib}{c+id} \times \frac{c-id}{c-id}=\left[\frac{ac+bd}{c^{2}+d^{2}}\right]+i\left[\frac{bc-ad}{c^{2}+d^{2}}\right]\)

Modulus and Polar Form

Modulus of a complex number:

If \(z=a+ib\) is a complex number then the modulus (or) absolute value of z is defined as \(\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\) and is denoted by \(|z|\). Thus, if \(z=a+ib\) then \(|z|=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\).

üí°Note

  1. (i) \(|\bar{z}|=|z|=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\)
  2. (ii) \(|z|=\sqrt{Z \bar{Z}}=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\)
  3. (iii) \(\operatorname{Re}(z) \leq|z|\) and \(\operatorname{Im}(z) \leq|z|\)

Polar form of a Complex Number:

Let \((r, \theta)\) be the Polar co-ordinates of the point P representing the complex number \(z=a+ib\). Then from the fig. we get,

\(\cos \theta=\frac{OM}{OP}=\frac{a}{r}\) and \(\sin \theta=\frac{PM}{OP}=\frac{b}{r}\)
\(\Rightarrow a=r \cos \theta\) and \(b=r \sin \theta\)

where \(r=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}=|a+ib|\) is called the modulus of \(z=a+ib\).

Also, \(\tan \theta=\frac{b}{a} \Rightarrow \theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)\) is called the amplitude or argument of \(z=a+ib\) and denoted by \(\operatorname{amp}(z)\) or \(\arg (z)\) and is measured as the angle in positive sense. Thus, \(\arg (z)=\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)\).

Hence \(z=a+ib=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)\) is called the Polar form or the modulus amplitude form of the complex number.

Theorems and Euler's Formula

Theorems of Complex numbers:

  1. 1) The product of two complex numbers is a complex number whose modulus is the product of their modulii and whose amplitude is the sum of their amplitudes
    \(\text { i.e, }|z_{1} z_{2}|=|z_{1}||z_{2}|\)
    and \(\arg (z_{1} z_{2})=\arg (z_{1})+\arg (z_{2})\)
  2. 2) The quotient of two complex numbers is a complex number whose modulus is the quotient of their modulii and whose amplitude is the difference of their amplitudes.
    \(\text { i.e }\left|\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right|=\frac{\left|z_{1}\right|}{\left|z_{2}\right|}\) wehre \(Z_{2} \neq 0\) and \(\arg \left(\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right)=\arg (z_{1})-\arg (z_{2})\).

Euler's formula:

The symbol \(e^{i \theta}\) is defined by \(e^{i \theta}=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta\) is known as Euler's formula.
If \(z \neq 0\) then \(z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)=r e^{i \theta}\). This is called the exponential form of the complex number z .

üí°Note

If \(z=re^{i \theta}\) then \(\bar{z}=re^{-i \theta}\).

Multiplication and Division of complex numbers (Geometrical proof not needed)

Let \(z_{1}=r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)\) and \(z_{2}=r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right)\) be any two complex numbers in Polar form then their product is given by

$$z_{1} z_{2}=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)\right]$$

Also the division of the above two complex numbers is given by

$$\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right] \text { where } z_{2} \neq 0 .$$

PART - A

1. If \(z_{1}=2+3 i\) and \(z_{2}=4-5 i\) find \(z_{1}+z_{2}\).

Solution:

Given: \(z_{1}=2+3 \mathrm{i} \quad \& \quad z_{2}=4-5 \mathrm{i}\)
\(z_{1}+z_{2} =(2+3 \mathrm{i})+(4-5 \mathrm{i})\)
\(=2+3 \mathrm{i}+4-5 \mathrm{i}\)
\( =(2+4)+(3 \mathrm{i}-5 \mathrm{i})\)
\(\Rightarrow z_{1}+z_{2} =6-2 \mathrm{i}\)

2. If \(z_{1}=3-4 i\) and \(z_{2}=-2+3 i\) find the value of \(2 z_{1}-3 z_{2}\).

Solution:

Given: \(z_{1}=3-4 \mathrm{i} \& z_{2}=-2+3 \mathrm{i}\)
\(2 z_{1}-3 z_{2} =2(3-4 \mathrm{i})-3(-2+3 \mathrm{i})\)
\(=6-8 \mathrm{i}+6-9 \mathrm{i}\)
\(\Rightarrow 2 z_{1}-3 z_{2} =12-17 \mathrm{i}\)

3. Express: \((3+2 \mathrm{i})(4+2 \mathrm{i})\) in \(a+ib\) form.

Solution:

\((3+2 \mathrm{i})(4+2 \mathrm{i}) =12+6 \mathrm{i}+8 \mathrm{i}+4 \mathrm{i}^{2}\)
\(=12+14 \mathrm{i}-4\)
\(=8+14 \mathrm{i}=\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{ib}\) form.

4. Find the real and imaginary parts of \(\frac{1}{3+2 \mathrm{i}}\).

Solution:

Let \(z=\frac{1}{3+2 \mathrm{i}}=\frac{1}{3+2 \mathrm{i}} \times \frac{3-2 \mathrm{i}}{3-2 \mathrm{i}}\)
\(=\frac{3-2 \mathrm{i}}{(3)^{2}-(2 \mathrm{i})^{2}}\)
\(=\frac{3-2 \mathrm{i}}{9+4}\)
\(=\frac{3-2 \mathrm{i}}{13}\)
\(\Rightarrow z=\frac{3}{13}-\frac{2 \mathrm{i}}{13}\)
\(\therefore \operatorname{Re}(\mathrm{z})=\frac{3}{13} \& \operatorname{Im}(\mathrm{z})=\frac{-2}{13}\)

5. Find the conjugate of \(\frac{1}{1+\mathrm{i}}\).

Solution:

Let \(z=\frac{1}{1+\mathrm{i}}=\frac{1}{1+\mathrm{i}} \times \frac{1-\mathrm{i}}{1-\mathrm{i}}\)
\(=\frac{1-\mathrm{i}}{(1)^{2}-(\mathrm{i})^{2}}\)
\(=\frac{1-\mathrm{i}}{1+1}\)
\(=\frac{1-\mathrm{i}}{2}\)
\(\Rightarrow z=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{\mathrm{i}}{2}\)
\(\therefore \text { Conjugate }: \overline{\mathrm{z}}=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\mathrm{i}}{2}\)

6. Find the modulus and amplitude of \(1+\mathrm{i}\).

Solution:

Let \(z=1+\mathrm{i}\)
Here \(a=1 \quad \& \quad b=1\)
Modulus \(:|z| =\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}=\sqrt{(1)^{2}+(1)^{2}}=\sqrt{1+1}=\sqrt{2}\)
and \(\operatorname{amp}(\mathrm{z}) =\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{1}\right)=\tan ^{-1}(1)\)
\(\Rightarrow \theta =45^{\circ}\)

Argand Diagram

Every complex number \(a+ib\) can be considered as an ordered pair \((a, b)\) of real numbers, we can represent such number by a point in xy-plane called the complex plane and such a representation is also known as the argand diagram. The complex number \(z=a+ib\) represented by \(P(z)\) then the distance between z and the origin is the modulus. i.e \(|z|=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\)

Here the set of real numbers \((x, 0)\) corresponds to the x -axis called real axis and the set of Imaginary numbers \((0, y)\) correponds to the y -axis called the imaginary axis.

Result:

The distance between the two complex numbers \(z_{1}\) and \(z_{2}\) is \(|z_{1}-z_{2}|\). Thus, if \(z_{1}=x_{1}+i y_{1}\) and \(z_{2}=x_{2}+i y_{2}\) then \(|z_{1}-z_{2}|=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}\).

Collinear Points:

If A, B and C are any three points representing the complex numbers \(x_{1}+i y_{1}, x_{2}+i y_{2}\) and \(x_{3}+i y_{3}\) respectively, are collinear then the required condition is, the area of \(\triangle \mathrm{ABC}\) is zero.
i.e. \(\frac{1}{2}\left[x_{1}\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)+x_{2}\left(y_{3}-y_{1}\right)+x_{3}\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)\right]=0\)
i.e \(x_{1}\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)+x_{2}\left(y_{3}-y_{1}\right)+x_{3}\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)=0\)
i.e. \(\left|\begin{array}{lll}x_{1} & y_{1} & 1 \\ x_{2} & y_{2} & 1 \\ x_{3} & y_{3} & 1\end{array}\right|=0\)

Condition for square:

If A, B, C and D are any four complex numbers representing the vertices of a square then the required condition is

  1. (i)\(AB=BC=CD=DA\)
    i.e four sides are equal.
  2. (ii)\(AC=BD\)
    i.e the diagonals are equal

Condition for rectangle

If A, B, C and D are any four complex numbers represents the vertices of a rectangle then the required condition is,

  1. (i) \(AB=CD\) and \(BC=DA\).
    i.e Opposite sides are equal.
  2. (ii) \(AC=BD\)
    i.e the diagonals are equal.

Condition for rhombus

If A, B, C and D are any four complex numbers represents the vertices of a rhombus then the required condition is
\(AB=BC=CD=DA\)
i.e four sides are equal.

Condition for Parallelogram

If A, B, C and D are any four complex numbers represents the vertices of a parallelogram then the required condition is either,
mid-point of the diagonal AC = mid.point of the diagonal BD .
(or) The length of the opposite sides are equal. i.e \(AB=CD \& BC=DA\).

üí°Note

  1. (i) To find length of the sides and diagonals of square, rectangle, rhombus and parallelogram apply the distance formula.
    i.e distance: \(AB=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}\) etc.
  2. (ii) To find the mid.point of the diagonals of the parallelogram apply middle point formula, \((x, y)=\left[\frac{x_{1}+x_{2}}{2}, \frac{y_{1}+y_{2}}{2}\right]\)

Condition for collinear is \(\left|\begin{array}{lll}x_{1} & y_{1} & 1 \\ x_{2} & y_{2} & 1 \\ x_{3} & y_{3} & 1\end{array}\right|=0\)
LHS: \(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 & 1 \\ 6 & -8 & 1\end{array}\right|=1\left|\begin{array}{ll}-2 & 1 \\ -8 & 1\end{array}\right|-2\left|\begin{array}{ll}3 & 1 \\ 6 & 1\end{array}\right|+1\left|\begin{array}{cc}3 & -2 \\ 6 & -8\end{array}\right|\)
\(=1(-2+8)-2(3-6)+1(-24+12)\)
\(=1(6)-2(-3)+1(-12)\)
\(=6+6-12\)
\(=12-12\)
\(=0\)

\(\therefore\) The given complex numebrs are collinear.

2. Show that the complex numbers \(-1+\mathrm{i}, 3+2 \mathrm{i}, 2+2 \mathrm{i}\) and \(-2+\mathrm{i}\) form a parallelogram.

Solution:

Let the given complex numbers are
A : \(-1+\mathrm{i}=(-1,1)\)
B: \(3+2 \mathrm{i}=(3,2)\)
C : \(2+2 \mathrm{i}=(2,2)\)
D : \(-2+\mathrm{i}=(-2,1)\)

Mid-point of \(AC=\left[\frac{x_{1}+x_{3}}{2}, \frac{y_{1}+y_{3}}{2}\right]=\left[\frac{-1+2}{2}, \frac{1+2}{2}\right]=\left[\frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}\right]\) .....(1)
Mid-point of \(BD=\left[\frac{3-2}{2}, \frac{2+1}{2}\right]=\left[\frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}\right]\) .....(2)

From (1) & (2), Mid-point of AC = Mid-point of BD.

PART - C

1. Prove that the points representing the complex numbers \(3+2 \mathrm{i}, 5+4 \mathrm{i}, 3+6 \mathrm{i}\) and \(1+4 \mathrm{i}\) form a square.

Solution:

Let the given complex number be
A : \(3+2 \mathrm{i}=(3,2)\)
B : \(5+4 \mathrm{i}=(5,4)\)
C : \(3+6 \mathrm{i}=(3,6)\)
& D : \(1+4 \mathrm{i}=(1,4)\)

Now,
\(AB=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(3-5)^{2}+(2-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-2)^{2}+(-2)^{2}}=\sqrt{4+4}=\sqrt{8}\)
\(BC=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(5-3)^{2}+(4-6)^{2}}=\sqrt{(2)^{2}+(-2)^{2}}=\sqrt{4+4}=\sqrt{8}\)
\(CD=\sqrt{\left(x_{3}-x_{4}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{3}-y_{4}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(3-1)^{2}+(6-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{(2)^{2}+(2)^{2}}=\sqrt{4+4}=\sqrt{8}\)
and \(DA=\sqrt{\left(x_{4}-x_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{4}-y_{1}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(1-3)^{2}+(4-2)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-2)^{2}+(2)^{2}}=\sqrt{4+4}=\sqrt{8}\)

Also, \(AC=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{3}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(3-3)^{2}+(2-6)^{2}}=\sqrt{(0)^{2}+(-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{16}=4\)
\(BD=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{4}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{4}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(5-1)^{2}+(4-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{(4)^{2}+(0)^{2}}=\sqrt{16}=4\)

Here the sides \(AB=BC=CD=DA=\sqrt{8}\)
& diagonals \(AC=BD=4\)
\(\therefore\) The given complex numbers form a square.

2. Prove that the points representing the complex numbers \(2-2 i, 8+4 i, 5+7 i\) and \(-1+i\) form a rectangle.

Solution:

Let the given complex numbers be,
A: \(2-2 \mathrm{i}=(2,-2)\)
B : \(8+4 \mathrm{i}=(8,4)\)
C: \(5+7 \mathrm{i}=(5,7)\)
& D : \(-1+\mathrm{i}=(-1,1)\)

Now, \(AB=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(2-8)^{2}+(-2-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-6)^{2}+(-6)^{2}}=\sqrt{36+36}=\sqrt{72}\)
Now, \(BC=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(8-5)^{2}+(4-7)^{2}}=\sqrt{(3)^{2}+(-3)^{2}}=\sqrt{9+9}=\sqrt{18}\)
\(CD=\sqrt{\left(x_{3}-x_{4}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{3}-y_{4}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(5-(-1))^{2}+(7-1)^{2}}=\sqrt{(6)^{2}+(6)^{2}}=\sqrt{36+36}=\sqrt{72}\)
& \(DA=\sqrt{\left(x_{4}-x_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{4}-y_{1}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-1-2)^{2}+(1-(-2))^{2}}=\sqrt{(-3)^{2}+(3)^{2}}=\sqrt{9+9}=\sqrt{18}\)

Also, \(AC=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{3}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(2-5)^{2}+(-2-7)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-3)^{2}+(-9)^{2}}=\sqrt{9+81}=\sqrt{90}\)
\(BD=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{4}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{4}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(8-(-1))^{2}+(4-1)^{2}}=\sqrt{(9)^{2}+(3)^{2}}=\sqrt{81+9}=\sqrt{90}\)

Here the sides \(AB=CD=\sqrt{72}\)
\(BC=DA=\sqrt{18}\)
and diagonals \(AC=BD=\sqrt{90}\)
\(\therefore\) The given complex numbers form a rectangle.

3. Prove that the points \(3+4 \mathrm{i}, 9+8 \mathrm{i}, 5+2 \mathrm{i}\) and \(-1-2 \mathrm{i}\) form a rhombus in the argand diagram.

Solution:

Let the given complex numbers be,
A : \(3+4 \mathrm{i}=(3,4)\)
B : \(9+8 \mathrm{i}=(9,8)\)
C: \(5+2 \mathrm{i}=(5,2)\)
& D : \(-1-2 \mathrm{i}=(-1,-2)\)

\(AB=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(3-9)^{2}+(4-8)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-6)^{2}+(-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{36+16}=\sqrt{52}\)
\(BC=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(9-5)^{2}+(8-2)^{2}}=\sqrt{(4)^{2}+(6)^{2}}=\sqrt{16+36}=\sqrt{52}\)
\(CD=\sqrt{\left(x_{3}-x_{4}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{3}-y_{4}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(5-(-1))^{2}+(2-(-2))^{2}}=\sqrt{(6)^{2}+(4)^{2}}=\sqrt{36+16}=\sqrt{52}\)
& \(DA=\sqrt{\left(x_{4}-x_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{4}-y_{1}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-1-3)^{2}+(-2-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{(-4)^{2}+(-6)^{2}}=\sqrt{16+36}=\sqrt{52}\)

Here the sides, \(AB=BC=CD=DA=\sqrt{52}\).
\(\therefore\) The given complex number form a rhombus.
