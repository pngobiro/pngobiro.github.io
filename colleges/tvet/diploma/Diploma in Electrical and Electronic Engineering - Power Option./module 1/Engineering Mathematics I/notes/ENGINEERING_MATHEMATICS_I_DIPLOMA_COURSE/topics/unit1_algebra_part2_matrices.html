üî¢1.2 MATRICES

Introduction:

The term matrix was first introduced by a French mathematician CAYLEY in the year 1857. The theory of matrices is one of the powerful tools of mathematics not only in the field of higher mathematics but also in other branches such as applied sciences, nuclear physics, statistics, economics and eletrical circuits.

Definition: A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in delete rows and columns enclosed by brackets.

Example:
1) \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{array}\right)\);
2) \(B=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 1 & 0 \\ -5 & 6 & 7 \\ 1 & 0 & 8\end{array}\right)\)

Order of a matrix:

If there are ' m ' rows and ' n ' columns in a matrix, then the order of the matrix is \(m \times n\) (read as m by n ).

Example: \(A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}a_{1} & a_{2} & a_{3} \\ b_{1} & b_{2} & b_{3}\end{array}\right)\)
The above matrix A has two rows and three columns. We say that A is a matrix of order \(2 \times 3\).

Types of matrices:

(1) Row matrix:
A matrix having only one row and any number of columns is called a row matrix.
Example: \(A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}1 & 2 & -3\end{array}\right)\)

(2) Column matrix:
A matrix having only one column and any number of rows is called a column matrix.
Example: \(B=\left(\begin{array}{c}3 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{array}\right)\)

(3) Square matrix.
A matrix which has equal number of rows and columns is called a square matrix.
Example: \(C=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 & -1 & 0 \\ 3 & 4 & -2 \\ 1 & 5 & 6\end{array}\right)\) is a square matrix of order 3.

(4) Null matrix (or) Zero matrix:
If all the elements of a matrix are zero, the matrix is called zero or null matrix.
Example: \(O=\left(\begin{array}{lll}0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right)\)

(5) Diagonal matrix:
A square matrix with all the elements equal to zero except those in the leading diagonal is called a diagonal matrix.

Example: \(D=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}-1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2\end{array}\right)\)

(6) Unit matrix:
Unit matrix is a square matrix in which the diagonal elements are all ones and all the other elements are zeros.

Example: \(I=\left(\begin{array}{lll}1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right)\)

(7) Determinant of a matrix:
Let ' A ' be a square matrix. The determinant formed by the elements of A is said to be the determinant of matrix A. This is denoted by \(|A|\).

(8) Singular and Non-Singular matrix:
A square matrix ' A ' is called a singular matrix if \(|A|=0\). If \(|A| \neq 0\), then the matrix A is called a non-singular matrix.

(9) Transpose of a matrix:
If the rows and columns of a matrix are interchanged, then the resultant matrix is called the transpose of the given matrix. It is denoted by \(A^T\).

Algebra of Matrices:

(1) Addition and Subtraction of matrices:
If A and B are any two matrices of the same order, then their sum or difference i.e \(A+B\) or \(A-B\) is of the same order, and is obtained by adding or subtracting the corresponding elements of A and B .

Example:
If \(A =\left(\begin{array}{cc}4 & 3 \\ -1 & 0\end{array}\right) ; B=\left(\begin{array}{cc}2 & 1 \\ -3 & -4\end{array}\right)\) then
\(A+B =\left(\begin{array}{cc}4+2 & 3+1 \\ -1-3 & 0-4\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc}6 & 4 \\ -4 & -4\end{array}\right)\) and
\(A-B =A+(-B) \quad -B=\left(\begin{array}{cc}-2 & -1 \\ 3 & 4\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{cc}4-2 & 3-1 \\ -1+3 & 0+4\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc}2 & 2 \\ 2 & 4\end{array}\right)\)

(2) Multiplication of matrices:
Two matrices A and B are conformable for multiplication if and only if the number of columns in A is equal to the number of rows in B.

üí°Note

If A is of order \(m \times n\) and B is of order \(n \times p\) matrices, then AB exists and is of order \(m \times p\).

Example:
If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 0 & 2\end{array}\right)\) and \(B=\left(\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \\ 4 & -3\end{array}\right)\) then
\(AB=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 0 & 2\end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \\ 4 & -3\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{cc}-1+6+12 & 2+8-9 \\ -3+0+8 & 6+0-6\end{array}\right)\)
\(AB=\left(\begin{array}{cc}17 & 1 \\ 5 & 0\end{array}\right)\)
Generally \(AB \neq BA\)

Matrix Operations and Properties

Co-factor matrix:

In a matrix, if all the elements are replaced by the corresponding co-factors of the elements, then the matrix obtained is called the co-factor matrix.

Example: If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & -4 \\ 8 & 3\end{array}\right)\)
Co-factor of 1 is +3 Co-factor of -4 is -8
Co-factor of 8 is 4 Co-factor of 3 is 1
\(\therefore\) Co-factor matrix is \(\left(\begin{array}{cc}3 & -8 \\ 4 & 1\end{array}\right)\)

Adjoint (or) Adjugate matrix:

The transpose of the co-factor matrix is called the adjoint matrix or adjugate matrix. It is denoted by Adj.A

Example: From the above matrix \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & -4 \\ 8 & 3\end{array}\right)\) and the co-factor matrix of A is \(\left(\begin{array}{cc}3 & -8 \\ 4 & 1\end{array}\right)\), the adjoint of A is \(\left(\begin{array}{cc}3 & 4 \\ -8 & 1\end{array}\right)\).

Inverse of a matrix:

Let A be a non-singular square matrix. If there exists a square matrix B, such that \(AB=BA=I\), where I is the unit matrix of the same order as that of A, then B is called the inverse of A and it is denoted by \(A^{-1}\). This can be determined by using the formula.
\(A^{-1}=\frac{1}{|A|}(\operatorname{adj} A)\)

üí°Note

  1. 1.If \(|A|=0\), then there is no inverse for the matrix A .
  2. 2.\(A^{-1}A=AA^{-1}=I\)
  3. 3.\((AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}\)
  4. 4.\((A^T)^{-1}=(A^{-1})^T\)

Rank of a matrix:

Let ' A ' be any \(m \times n\) matrix. It has square sub-matrices of different orders. The order of the largest square submtrix of A whose determinant has a non-zero value is known as the rank of the matrix A.

Generally the rank is denoted by \(\rho(A)\), \(\rho(A)\) is always minimum of m and n.

üí°Note

The rank of a matrix is said to be r if

  1. 1.It has atleast one non zero minor of order r.
  2. 2.Every minor of A of order higher than r is zero.

Example:

1. Find the rank of the matrix \(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 5 & 6 \\ 2 & 3 & 4 \\ -1 & 2 & 2\end{array}\right|\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left|\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 5 & 6 \\ 2 & 3 & 4 \\ -1 & 2 & 2\end{array}\right|\)
\(|A| =1(6-8)-5(4+4)+6(4+3)\)
\(=1(-2)-5(8)+6(7)\)
\(=-2-40+42\)
\(|A|=0\)

consider the determinant of the second order
\(\left|\begin{array}{ll}1 & 5 \\ 2 & 3\end{array}\right|=-7 \neq 0\)
Hence the rank of A is 2.
i.e \(\rho(A)=2\)

WORKED EXAMPLES

PART - A

1. If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}0 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 4\end{array}\right)\) ; \(B=\left(\begin{array}{lll}7 & 6 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 & 5\end{array}\right)\) find \(A+B\).

Solution:

\(A+B =\left(\begin{array}{lll}0 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 4\end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{lll}7 & 6 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 & 5\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{lll}0+7 & 2+6 & 3+3 \\ 2+1 & 1+4 & 4+5\end{array}\right)\)
\(A+B =\left(\begin{array}{lll}7 & 8 & 6 \\ 3 & 5 & 9\end{array}\right)\)

2. If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 3 \\ 3 & 4\end{array}\right)\) ; \(B=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 4 & 3\end{array}\right)\) find \(A-B\).

Solution:

\(A-B =\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 3 \\ 3 & 4\end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 4 & 3\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1-1 & 3-2 \\ 3-4 & 4-3\end{array}\right)\)
\(A-B =\left(\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1\end{array}\right)\)

3. Prove that the matrix \(\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & -1 \\ 4 & -2\end{array}\right)\) is singular.

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & -1 \\ 4 & -2\end{array}\right)\)
\(|A| =\left|\begin{array}{ll}2 & -1 \\ 4 & -2\end{array}\right|\)
\(=-4+4\)
\(|A| =0 \quad \therefore A\) is singular.

4. Find the rank of \(\left(\begin{array}{cc}3 & -4 \\ -6 & 8\end{array}\right)\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}3 & -4 \\ -6 & 8\end{array}\right)\)
Now, \(|A|=\left|\begin{array}{cc}3 & -4 \\ -6 & 8\end{array}\right|=24-24=0 . \quad \therefore \rho(A) \neq 2\)
Since each element of A is non zero,
\(\rho(A)=1\).

5. Find the rank of \(\left(\begin{array}{lll}1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6\end{array}\right)\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6\end{array}\right)\) and let the sub matrix of \(A=B=\left|\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 4 & 5\end{array}\right|\)
\(|B|=\left|\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 4 & 5\end{array}\right|=5-8=-3 \neq 0\)
\(\therefore \rho(A)=2\)

PART - B

1. If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 3 & 0 \\ 5 & 2 & -1\end{array}\right) ; B=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}3 & -1 & 4 \\ 2 & 6 & 7\end{array}\right)\) find \(3A-2B\).

Solution:

\(3A-2B =3\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 3 & 0 \\ 5 & 2 & -1\end{array}\right)-2\left(\begin{array}{ccc}3 & -1 & 4 \\ 2 & 6 & 7\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}6 & 9 & 0 \\ 15 & 6 & -3\end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{ccc}6 & -2 & 8 \\ 4 & 12 & 14\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}6-6 & 9+2 & 0-8 \\ 15-4 & 6-12 & -3-14\end{array}\right)\)
\(3A-2B =\left(\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 11 & -8 \\ 11 & -6 & -17\end{array}\right)\)

2. If \(f(x)=4x+2\) and \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}2 & -1 \\ 0 & 3\end{array}\right)\) find \(f(A)\).

Solution:

\(f(A) =4A+2\)
\(=4A+2I\)
\(=4\left(\begin{array}{cc}2 & -1 \\ 0 & 3\end{array}\right)+2\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{cc}8 & -4 \\ 0 & 12\end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2\end{array}\right)\)
\(f(A) =\left(\begin{array}{cc}10 & -4 \\ 0 & 14\end{array}\right)\)

3. Find the value of 'a' so that the matrix \(\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 & -2 & 0 \\ 2 & \mathrm{a} & 4 \\ 2 & 1 & 1\end{array}\right)\) is singular.

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 & -2 & 0 \\ 2 & \mathrm{a} & 4 \\ 2 & 1 & 1\end{array}\right)\). The matrix is singular, then \(|A|=0\).
i.e \(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}1 & -2 & 0 \\ 2 & \mathrm{a} & 4 \\ 2 & 1 & 1\end{array}\right|=0\)
expanding through first row
\(1(a-4)+2(2-8)+0=0\)
\(a-4+2(-6)=0\)
\(a-4-12=0\)
\(a-16=0\)
\(a=16\)

4. If \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1\end{array}\right)\) and \(B=\left(\begin{array}{ll}0 & 3 \\ 2 & 4\end{array}\right)\) find \(AB\).

Solution:

\(AB=\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll}0 & 3 \\ 2 & 4\end{array}\right)\)
\(AB=\left(\begin{array}{ll}0+0 & 3+0 \\ 0+2 & 6+4\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc}0 & 3 \\ 2 & 10\end{array}\right)\)

5. Find the inverse of \(\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5\end{array}\right)\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5\end{array}\right) \therefore |A|=\left|\begin{array}{ll}2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5\end{array}\right|=10-12=-2 \neq 0\)
Adj \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}5 & -3 \\ -4 & 2\end{array}\right)\)
\(A^{-1}=\frac{1}{|A|} \text{Adj} A=\frac{1}{-2}\left(\begin{array}{cc}5 & -3 \\ -4 & 2\end{array}\right)\)

6. Find the rank of \(\left(\begin{array}{cc}5 & -15 \\ -6 & 18 \\ 7 & -21\end{array}\right)\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}5 & -15 \\ -6 & 18 \\ 7 & -21\end{array}\right)\)
\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}5 & -15 \\ -6 & 18\end{array}\right|=90-90=0\)
\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}-6 & 18 \\ 7 & -21\end{array}\right|= (-6)(-21) - (18)(7) = 126-126=0\)
\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}5 & -15 \\ 7 & -21\end{array}\right|= (5)(-21) - (-15)(7) = -105+105=0\)
\(\therefore \rho(A) \neq 2\)
Since there are non-zero elements, \(\rho(A)=1\).

PART - C

1. If \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\) and \(B=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-2 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 & -4\end{array}\right]\) show that \(AB=BA\).

Solution:

\(AB=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-2 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 & -4\end{array}\right]\)
\(=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-4-1+0 & 2-2+0 & -2+2+0 \\ 0-2+2 & 0-4-1 & 0+4-4 \\ -2+0+2 & 1+0-1 & -1+0-4\end{array}\right]\)
\(=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-5 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -5\end{array}\right]\)

\(BA=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-2 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 & -4\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\)
\(=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-4+0-1 & 2-2+0 & 0+1-1 \\ 2+0-2 & -1-4+0 & 0+2-2 \\ 4+0-4 & -2+2+0 & 0-1-4\end{array}\right]\)
\(BA=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-5 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -5\end{array}\right]\)
\(\therefore AB=BA\)

2. Show that \(AB \neq BA\) if \(A=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & 2 \\ -1 & 4\end{array}\right]\) and \(B=\left[\begin{array}{cc}3 & 2 \\ 1 & -1\end{array}\right]\).

Solution:

\(AB=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & 2 \\ -1 & 4\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc}3 & 2 \\ 1 & -1\end{array}\right]\)
\(AB=\left[\begin{array}{cc}3+2 & 2-2 \\ -3+4 & -2-4\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}5 & 0 \\ 1 & -6\end{array}\right]\)

\(BA=\left[\begin{array}{cc}3 & 2 \\ 1 & -1\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & 2 \\ -1 & 4\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}3-2 & 6+8 \\ 1+1 & 2-4\end{array}\right]\)
\(BA=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & 14 \\ 2 & -2\end{array}\right]\)
\(\therefore AB \neq BA\)

3. If \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1\end{array}\right]\) and if \(f(x)=3x^{2}-2x+4\) find \(f(A)\).

Solution:

If \(f(x)=3x^{2}-2x+4\)
\(f(A)=3A^{2}-2A+4I\)
\(A^{2} =A \times A = \left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1\end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{ll}1+6 & 2+2 \\ 3+3 & 6+1\end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{ll}7 & 4 \\ 6 & 7\end{array}\right]\)

\(\therefore f(A)=3A^{2}-2A+4I\)
\(=3\left[\begin{array}{ll}7 & 4 \\ 6 & 7\end{array}\right]-2\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1\end{array}\right]+4\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\)
\(=\left[\begin{array}{ll}21 & 12 \\ 18 & 21\end{array}\right]+\left[\begin{array}{cc}-2 & -4 \\ -6 & -2\end{array}\right]+\left[\begin{array}{ll}4 & 0 \\ 0 & 4\end{array}\right]\)
\(f(A) =\left[\begin{array}{cc}21-2+4 & 12-4+0 \\ 18-6+0 & 21-2+4\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}23 & 8 \\ 12 & 23\end{array}\right]\).

4. Find the inverse of the matrix \(\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 3\end{array}\right]\).

Solution:

Let \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 3\end{array}\right]\)
\(|A|=\left|\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 3\end{array}\right| =1(3-0)-1(6-0)-1(4+1)=1(3)-1(6)-1(5) =3-6-5=-8 \neq 0\). \(A^{-1}\) exists.

Co-factors
\(A_{11}=+\left|\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3\end{array}\right|=3-0=3\)
\(A_{12}=-\left|\begin{array}{cc}2 & 0 \\ -1 & 3\end{array}\right|=-(6-0)=-6\)
\(A_{13}=+\left|\begin{array}{cc}2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2\end{array}\right|=(4+1)=5\)
\(A_{21}=-\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\ 2 & 3\end{array}\right|=-(3+2)=-5\)
\(A_{22}=+\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\ -1 & 3\end{array}\right|=(3-1)=2\)
\(A_{23}=-\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & 1 \\ -1 & 2\end{array}\right|=-(2+1)=-3\)
\(A_{31}=+\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0\end{array}\right|=(0+1)=1\)
\(A_{32}=-\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\ 2 & 0\end{array}\right|=-(0+2)=-2\)
\(A_{33}=+\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1\end{array}\right|=(1-2)=-1\)

Co-factor matrix \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}3 & -6 & 5 \\ -5 & 2 & -3 \\ 1 & -2 & -1\end{array}\right]\)
Adj. \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}3 & -5 & 1 \\ -6 & 2 & -2 \\ 5 & -3 & -1\end{array}\right]\)
\(\therefore A^{-1}=\frac{\operatorname{Adj} A}{|A|}=\frac{-1}{8}\left[\begin{array}{ccc}3 & -5 & 1 \\ -6 & 2 & -2 \\ 5 & -3 & -1\end{array}\right]\)

5. If \(A=\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0\end{array}\right] ; B=\left[\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\) show that \((AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}\).

Solution:

\(AB =\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cc}-2 & 7 \\ 1 & -2\end{array}\right]\)
\(|AB| =\left|\begin{array}{cc}-2 & 7 \\ 1 & -2\end{array}\right|= 4 - 7 = -3 \neq 0\). \((AB)^{-1}\) exists.
\(\operatorname{Adj}(AB) =\left(\begin{array}{ll}-2 & -7 \\ -1 & -2\end{array}\right)\)
\((AB)^{-1} =\frac{1}{-3}\left(\begin{array}{ll}-2 & -7 \\ -1 & -2\end{array}\right) = \frac{-1}{3}\left(\begin{array}{ll}-2 & -7 \\ -1 & -2\end{array}\right)\) .....(i)

\(B=\left(\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right) \quad |B|=\left|\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right|=-1 \neq 0\)
\(\operatorname{Adj} B=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & -2 \\ 0 & -1\end{array}\right)\)
\(B^{-1} =\frac{1}{-1}\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & -2 \\ 0 & -1\end{array}\right)\)

\(A =\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0\end{array}\right] \quad |A|=\left|\begin{array}{cc}2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0\end{array}\right|=3 \neq 0\)
Adj \(A =\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & -3 \\ 1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)
\(A^{-1} =\frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{ll}0 & -3 \\ 1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)

\(B^{-1}A^{-1} =-\frac{1}{1}\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & -2 \\ 0 & -1\end{array}\right] \times \frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & -3 \\ 1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)
\(B^{-1}A^{-1} =-\frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -2 \\ 0 & -1\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & -3 \\ 1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)
\(B^{-1}A^{-1} =-\frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{cc}0-2 & -3-4 \\ 0-1 & 0-2\end{array}\right] = -\frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{cc}-2 & -7 \\ -1 & -2\end{array}\right]\) .....(ii)

From (i) and (ii) \((AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}\)

6. Find the rank of the matrix \(\left[\begin{array}{cccc}2 & 3 & 4 & -1 \\ 5 & 2 & 0 & -1 \\ -4 & 5 & 12 & -1\end{array}\right]\).

Solution:

Here the rank of the matrix can not exceed 3, the minimum of 3 and 4.
The value of the each of the determinants.
\(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 3 & 4 \\ 5 & 2 & 0 \\ -4 & 5 & 12\end{array}\right|=2(24-0)-3(60-0)+4(25+8)=48-180+132=0\)
\(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}3 & 4 & -1 \\ 2 & 0 & -1 \\ 5 & 12 & -1\end{array}\right|= 3(0 - (-12)) - 4(-2 - (-5)) + (-1)(24-0) = 3(12) - 4(3) - 24 = 36 - 12 - 24 = 0\)
Similarly we can prove \(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 4 & -1 \\ 5 & 0 & -1 \\ -4 & 12 & -1\end{array}\right|=0\) and \(\left|\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 3 & -1 \\ 5 & 2 & -1 \\ -4 & 5 & -1\end{array}\right|=0\)

While the second order determinant
\(\left|\begin{array}{ll}2 & 3 \\ 5 & 2\end{array}\right|=4-15=-11 \neq 0\)
Hence the rank of the matrix = 2
