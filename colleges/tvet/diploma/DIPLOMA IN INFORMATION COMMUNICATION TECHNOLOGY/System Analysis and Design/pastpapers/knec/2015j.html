Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Explain the term information technology as used in systems analysis and design. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline two characteristics of a knowledge based system. (2 marks)

(b) (i) Outline two types of system users that could be found in organizations. (2 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between open questionnaire and closed questionnaire as used in data collection. (4 marks)

(c) Rita was required to investigate the advantages of a closed loop system. Explain two advantages that she likely to have identified. (4 marks)

(d) With the aid of an example in each case, describe each of the following types of information systems:

(i) MIS; (3 marks)

(ii) TPS. (3 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Information Technology (IT):

  • The use of computers, storage, networking, and other physical devices, infrastructure, and processes to create, process, store, secure, and exchange all forms of electronic data. In SAD, it refers to the hardware and software tools used to build and run information systems.

(a)(ii) Characteristics of Knowledge-Based System (KBS):

  • Uses a knowledge base (facts, rules, heuristics) specific to a domain.
  • Employs an inference engine to reason and derive conclusions or solutions.
  • Often designed to mimic human expert reasoning.
  • Can explain its reasoning process.

(b)(i) Types of System Users:

  • End-Users (Operational Staff): Directly interact with the system to perform daily tasks.
  • Management Users: Use system outputs (reports, summaries) for decision-making and control.
  • System Administrators/Technical Staff: Maintain and manage the system infrastructure.

(b)(ii) Open vs Closed Questionnaire:

Open QuestionnaireClosed Questionnaire
Contains questions that allow respondents to answer freely in their own words.Contains questions with predefined answer choices (e.g., Yes/No, multiple choice, scale).
Provides qualitative, in-depth insights but harder to analyze statistically.Provides quantitative data that is easier to analyze statistically but may lack depth.

(c) Advantages of Closed Loop System:

  • Automatic Control: Can automatically adjust its actions based on feedback to maintain a desired state without human intervention.
  • Stability: Tends to be more stable and less prone to deviations from the set point due to the corrective feedback mechanism.
  • Accuracy: Can achieve higher accuracy by continuously monitoring output and making adjustments.

(d) Types of Information Systems:

  • (i) Management Information System (MIS): Provides routine summary reports and exception reports based on transaction data to middle managers for structured decision-making and control. Example: Monthly sales summary report by region.
  • (ii) Transaction Processing System (TPS): Records and processes daily routine transactions necessary to conduct business. Focuses on efficiency and accuracy at the operational level. Example: Point-of-Sale (POS) system recording sales, Order entry system.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Explain the term data type as applied in system design. (2 marks)

(b) Explain two disadvantages of observation as used in data collection. (4 marks)

(c) (i) Suszy was required to create a TOR for a proposed information system in her company. Outline four reasons that could have necessitated creation of this document. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between a project scope and a project schedule as applied in project management. (4 marks)

(d) Vicky was required to prepare a system training session for a client that would be conduct during the working hours.

(i) Explain the most appropriate training method that she could use to achieve her goal. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain two challenges that could be experienced with the method identified in (i). (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Data Type:

  • A classification that specifies which type of value a variable or data field can hold and what type of mathematical, relational, or logical operations can be applied to it without causing an error (e.g., Integer, String, Date, Boolean).

(b) Disadvantages of Observation:

  • Hawthorne Effect: Individuals may alter their behavior because they know they are being watched.
  • Observer Bias: The observer's interpretations may be subjective or influenced by preconceived notions.
  • Time-Consuming: Can require significant time investment to observe processes thoroughly.
  • Limited Insight: May not reveal the underlying reasons or mental processes behind observed actions.

(c)(i) Reasons for Creating TOR:

  • To clearly define the project's purpose and objectives.
  • To establish the scope and boundaries of the project.
  • To identify key stakeholders and their responsibilities.
  • To outline constraints (budget, time, resources).
  • To serve as a formal agreement and reference point for the project.

(c)(ii) Project Scope vs Project Schedule:

Project ScopeProject Schedule
Defines *what* work is included and excluded in the project and its deliverables.Defines *when* project activities will occur and the overall project timeline.
Focuses on the boundaries and objectives.Focuses on timeframes, milestones, and task sequencing.

(d)(i) Appropriate Training Method:

  • On-the-Job Training (OJT) or Phased Training. Justification: These methods allow training to occur within the normal work environment or in stages, minimizing disruption to ongoing operations. OJT provides context-specific learning.

(d)(ii) Challenges of OJT/Phased Training:

  • Distraction: Trainees might be distracted by their regular work duties.
  • Inconsistency: Quality of training can vary depending on the trainer/mentor.
  • Potential for Errors: Trainees might make mistakes on the live system if not properly supervised.
  • Scheduling: Coordinating training sessions around operational needs can be difficult.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Outline two objectives of systems design phase in SDLC. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain the term system boundary as used in systems theory. (2 marks)

(b) With the aid of a diagram, describe the stages in system development life cycle using a spiral model. (6 marks)

(c) Paul intends to use a use case diagram to model a proposed information system. Outline four advantages he would realize from using the tool. (4 marks)

(d) Leah intends to use functional decomposition to analyze an information system for a client. Explain three benefits he would realize when using the tool. (6 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Objectives of Systems Design Phase:

  • Translate user requirements (from analysis phase) into detailed technical specifications.
  • Define the system architecture, components, modules, interfaces, and data structures.
  • Create a blueprint for system construction/implementation.
  • Ensure the design meets performance, security, and usability requirements.

(a)(ii) System Boundary:

  • The conceptual line or interface that separates a system from its environment. It defines what is considered part of the system and what is external to it.

(b) Spiral Model Stages (Diagram Description):

A spiral model diagram typically shows four quadrants representing key activities performed in each iteration (cycle) of the spiral:

  1. Determine Objectives, Alternatives, Constraints: Planning phase, understanding requirements for this cycle.
  2. Evaluate Alternatives, Identify and Resolve Risks: Risk analysis and prototyping to mitigate identified risks.
  3. Develop, Verify Next-Level Product: Engineering phase - detailed design, coding, testing of the current iteration's product.
  4. Plan Next Phases: Evaluating the results of the current iteration and planning the next cycle.

(A visual diagram would show these quadrants arranged in a spiral expanding outwards, indicating iterative progression and increasing completeness.)

(c) Advantages of Use Case Diagrams:

  • Captures Functional Requirements: Clearly shows the interactions between users (actors) and the system to achieve specific goals.
  • Easy to Understand: Provides a high-level view easily understood by both technical and non-technical stakeholders.
  • Defines System Scope: Helps define the boundaries of the system by showing what actors interact with it and for what purpose.
  • Basis for Testing: Use cases can be used to derive test scenarios.

(d) Benefits of Functional Decomposition:

  • Manages Complexity: Breaks down a large, complex system into smaller, more manageable functional units (sub-systems or modules).
  • Clear Structure: Provides a hierarchical view of the system's functions, showing how they relate to each other.
  • Facilitates Allocation: Makes it easier to assign development tasks for specific functions to different team members.
  • Improved Understanding: Helps analysts and developers understand the different parts of the system and their specific roles.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Outline two technical factors that should be considered when selecting system design methodologies. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline three strengths of object oriented methodology as used in system development. (3 marks)

(b) (i) Outline two roles of a system designer in an organization. (2 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between black box and white box testing methods. (4 marks)

(c) With the aid of a sketch in each case, outline three components of system flow chart. (3 marks)

(d) Brian intends to carry out system testing with users in order to ascertain that it meets their requirements. He prefers to release trial versions to users in order to achieve his objective.

(i) Explain the type of testing strategy employed by Brian. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain two drawbacks of the strategy explained in (i). (4 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Technical Factors for Selecting Methodology:

  • Type of Technology Used: The target platform, database, and programming languages might favor certain methodologies.
  • System Complexity and Size: Large, complex systems might benefit from more structured or iterative approaches.
  • Need for Integration: Requirements for integrating with existing systems can influence methodological choices.

(a)(ii) Strengths of Object-Oriented Methodology:

  • Reusability: Objects and classes can be reused across different parts of the system or in future projects.
  • Maintainability: Encapsulation makes changes within an object less likely to affect other parts of the system.
  • Modularity: Systems are naturally divided into manageable objects/classes.
  • Real-World Modeling: Objects often map well to real-world entities, making the model more intuitive.

(b)(i) Roles of System Designer:

  • Translate functional requirements into technical specifications.
  • Design system architecture, database structure, and user interfaces.
  • Select appropriate hardware and software technologies.

(b)(ii) Black Box vs White Box Testing:

Black Box TestingWhite Box Testing
Testing based on input/output behavior without knowledge of internal code structure.Testing based on knowledge of the internal code structure, logic paths, and conditions.
Focuses on functional requirements ("Does it do what it's supposed to?").Focuses on code coverage and internal logic ("Does the code work correctly?").
Performed by testers often without programming knowledge.Performed by developers or testers with programming knowledge.

(d)(i) Testing Strategy:

  • Beta Testing. Justification: Releasing trial versions to actual users outside the development team to get feedback on usability and functionality in a real-world context before final release.

(d)(ii) Drawbacks of Beta Testing:

  • Loss of Control: Less control over the testing environment and process compared to internal testing.
  • Feedback Quality: Feedback from beta users can be varied, sometimes unstructured or difficult to interpret.
  • Potential for Negative Exposure: If the trial version has significant bugs, it could negatively impact the product's reputation before launch.
  • Resource Intensive: Managing beta testers, collecting feedback, and fixing reported issues requires resources.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Outline two factors that could necessitate system review. (2 marks)

(b) Explain two strategies that could be used to manage risks in system project. (4 marks)

(c) Tana Company Ltd intends to implement a new information system in stages for all its branches in Kenya. Assume you are hired as a systems analyst:

(i) Identify the most appropriate changeover strategy that you could use justifying your answer. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain two advantages of the changeover strategy identified in (i). (4 marks)

(d) Read the following narrative to answer the question that follows. Tangazeni College intends to automate its admission process. Prospective students are required to fill their details in the college website which are vetted and stored in the prospectus file. The applicants that meet requirements are notified through the email provided in their registration form; their details are stored in waiting list file whereas rejected applicants details are stored on the applicant log file for future recommendation. Once the shortlisted applicant receives notification email they are required to attend entry interview in the college and are required to produce their original academic certificates and testimonies. Before the interview, the applicantâ€™s sign-in attendance list and their certificates are also scrutinized to verify their authenticity; the details of attendance and authenticity of the certificate are then stored in the attendance and certificates authenticity files respectively. Successful applicants are assigned admission number and notified through email to pay fees. Details of successful applicant who are yet to pay fee are stored in enroll file for a period of one month. Similarly, applicants that pay their fee in time are assigned class and their details stored in student file and payment file respectively.

Draw a level 1 dataflow diagram to model the narrative into a system. (8 marks)

Answer

(a) Factors Necessitating System Review:

  • Performance Degradation: System response time or throughput falls below acceptable levels.
  • Changes in Business Needs: The system no longer fully supports current or new business processes.
  • Technological Obsolescence: Hardware or software becomes outdated, unsupported, or inefficient.
  • User Dissatisfaction: Users report significant problems with usability, functionality, or reliability.
  • Post-Implementation Evaluation: Scheduled review after deployment to assess if objectives were met.

(b) Risk Management Strategies:

  • Risk Avoidance: Modifying the project plan to eliminate the risk entirely (e.g., changing scope, using different technology).
  • Risk Mitigation: Taking proactive steps to reduce the probability or impact of the risk (e.g., extra testing, staff training, contingency planning).
  • Risk Transfer: Shifting the risk to a third party (e.g., insurance, outsourcing a high-risk component).
  • Risk Acceptance: Acknowledging the risk and deciding not to take action, usually for low-impact/low-probability risks.

(c)(i) Changeover Strategy:

  • Phased Changeover. Justification: Implementing the system branch by branch (in stages) allows the company to manage the rollout across multiple locations, learn from initial implementations, and reduce the risk associated with a simultaneous company-wide switch.

(c)(ii) Advantages of Phased Changeover:

  • Reduced Risk: Problems encountered in early phases can be fixed before implementing in subsequent branches.
  • Resource Management: Allows resources (training staff, technical support) to be focused on one stage at a time.
  • Learning Curve: Experience gained in early phases benefits later implementations.
  • Less Disruption: Limits disruption to one part of the organization at a time.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Outline two measures that could be adopted by organizations to cope with the challenges of the emerging trends in systems analysis and design. (2 marks)

(b) Naomi has designed a database with the following keys clearly defined. Explain the function of each keys:

(i) primary; (2 marks)

(ii) index; (2 marks)

(iii) foreign. (2 marks)

(c) Caleb noted with concern information system project that he supervising was derailing. Outline four possible causes of the derailment. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Gloria was required to prepare a budget for a proposed project in her company. Explain two significances of the budget. (4 marks)

(ii) A certain company advertised for a project manager. Outline four responsibilities that are likely to be part of the advert. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Coping with Emerging Trends Challenges:

  • Continuous Training and Skill Development: Keeping staff updated on new technologies and methodologies.
  • Adopting Agile/Flexible Methodologies: Using development approaches that can adapt to changing requirements and technologies.
  • Investing in Modern Tools and Platforms: Utilizing tools that support new trends (e.g., cloud platforms, DevOps tools).
  • Focusing on Modular/Service-Oriented Architecture: Designing systems that are easier to update or integrate with new components.

(b) Database Key Functions:

  • (i) Primary Key: Uniquely identifies each record (row) within a database table. Ensures entity integrity (no duplicate rows, no null values).
  • (ii) Index: A data structure (separate from the table data itself) that improves the speed of data retrieval operations on a database table at the cost of additional writes and storage space. It allows the database to find rows matching specific criteria quickly without scanning the entire table.
  • (iii) Foreign Key: A field (or collection of fields) in one table that uniquely identifies a row of another table (or the same table). It establishes and enforces a link between two tables, ensuring referential integrity (related data remains consistent).

(c) Causes of Project Derailment:

  • Scope Creep: Uncontrolled changes or additions to the project requirements.
  • Poor Planning/Estimation: Inaccurate estimation of time, cost, or resources needed.
  • Lack of Stakeholder Involvement/Communication: Insufficient communication or buy-in from users or management.
  • Resource Constraints: Insufficient budget, personnel, or tools.
  • Unforeseen Technical Problems: Unexpected difficulties with technology or integration.
  • Poor Risk Management: Failure to identify or mitigate potential risks.

(d)(i) Significance of Project Budget:

  • Financial Planning & Control: Provides a baseline for tracking project expenditures and managing costs.
  • Resource Allocation: Helps determine how financial resources will be allocated across different project tasks and phases.
  • Decision Making: Used as a factor in evaluating project feasibility and making go/no-go decisions.
  • Performance Measurement: Actual costs can be compared against the budget to measure financial performance.

(d)(ii) Project Manager Responsibilities:

  • Develop Project Plan (Scope, Schedule, Budget).
  • Lead and Manage Project Team.
  • Monitor and Control Project Execution (Track progress, manage risks/issues).
  • Manage Stakeholder Communication and Expectations.
  • Ensure Project Deliverables Meet Quality Standards.
  • Manage Project Budget and Resources.

(i) Identify the most appropriate type of maintenance it could use justifying your answer. (2 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Challenges in Developing User Manual:

  • Keeping it Updated: Ensuring the manual accurately reflects the current state of the system as it evolves.

  • Completeness vs Conciseness: Balancing the need to cover all features with keeping the manual easy to navigate and not overwhelming.

  • The degree of interdependence between different software modules. Low coupling (modules are independent) is desirable, as it makes the system easier to understand, modify, and maintain without causing ripple effects. High coupling means changes in one module are likely to require changes in others.

(b)(i) Type of Maintenance:

  • Preventive Maintenance. Justification: This involves performing routine checks and updates to prevent potential problems *before* they occur or are reported, aiming to enhance reliability and longevity.

(b)(ii) Advantages of Preventive Maintenance:

  • Increased System Reliability: Reduces the likelihood of unexpected failures and downtime.
  • Extended System Lifespan: Helps keep the system running efficiently for longer.
  • Reduced Emergency Costs: Prevents costly emergency repairs by addressing potential issues proactively.
  • Improved Performance: Can include optimizations that keep the system running smoothly.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Outline three desirable feature of a functional requirement. (3 marks)

(b) (i) Explain the term prototype as used in system design. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline three examples of stakeholders that could be involved when carrying out a system project in organizations. (3 marks)

(c) Explain each of the following types of decision table:

(i) limited entry; (2 marks)

(ii) extended entry; (2 marks)

(iii) mixed entry. (2 marks)

(d) With the aid of a symbol, outline each of the following type of nodes in a decision tree:

(i) chance/event; (2 marks)

(ii) terminal/edge; (2 marks)

(iii) decision. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Desirable Features of Functional Requirement:

  • Unambiguous: Clearly stated with only one possible interpretation.
  • Testable/Verifiable: Possible to determine whether the requirement has been met.
  • Complete: Fully describes the necessary functionality.
  • Consistent: Does not contradict other requirements.
  • Necessary: Represents a capability truly needed by the user or system.

(b)(i) Prototype:

  • A working model or preliminary version of a system or component, built quickly to test concepts, gather user feedback, or demonstrate functionality before full development.

(b)(ii) Examples of Stakeholders:

  • End Users: People who will directly interact with the system.
  • Management: Sponsors, department heads, executives who have an interest in the project's outcome.
  • Development Team: Analysts, designers, programmers, testers building the system.
  • Customers (if applicable): External parties who will use or benefit from the system.
  • Regulators (if applicable): Government or industry bodies whose rules the system must comply with.

(c) Types of Decision Table:

  • (i) Limited Entry: Conditions have only 'Y' (Yes), 'N' (No), or '-' (Irrelevant) entries. Actions have 'X' (Perform) or blank (Do not perform) entries.
  • (ii) Extended Entry: Conditions use relational operators (e.g., >, <, =) or specific values directly in the condition rows, rather than just Y/N. Actions can specify values or ranges.
  • (iii) Mixed Entry: Combines features of both limited and extended entry tables; some condition rows might use Y/N while others use specific values or ranges.
