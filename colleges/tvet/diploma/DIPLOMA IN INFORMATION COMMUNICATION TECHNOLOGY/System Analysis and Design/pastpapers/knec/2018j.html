DIPLOMA IN INFORMATION COMMUNICATION TECHNOLOGY

MODULE II

SYSTEMS ANALYSIS AND DESIGN

INSTRUCTIONS TO CANDIDATES

This paper consists of EIGHT questions.

Answer any FIVE of the following EIGHT questions in the answer booklet provided.

Candidates should answer the questions in English.

Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Outline two limitations of traditional system development methodologies. (2 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between table of contents and references as used in systems documentation. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Outline two objectives of a walkthrough meeting. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline three advantages of using Jackson Systems development (JSD). (3 marks)

(c) Design a typical examination system using structured charts. (3 marks)

(d) Peter has been requested to develop a training plan for system users. Explain three elements expected in the plan. (6 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Limitations of Traditional Methodologies:

  • Rigidity: Difficult to accommodate changes in requirements once a phase is complete.
  • Late Feedback: User involvement is often limited to the beginning and end, leading to potential misunderstandings discovered late.
  • Slow Delivery: Working software is typically delivered only at the end of the project.

(a)(ii) Table of Contents vs References:

FeatureTable of ContentsReferences
PurposeLists major sections/topics and their page numbers for navigation.Lists external sources (books, articles, websites) cited or consulted in the document.
LocationUsually at the beginning of the document.Usually at the end of the document.
ContentSection titles and page numbers.Bibliographic details (author, title, publisher, year, URL).

(b)(i) Objectives of a Walkthrough Meeting:

  • Error Detection: Identify defects, inconsistencies, or ambiguities in a work product (e.g., design document, code).
  • Improve Quality: Provide feedback to the author to enhance the quality of the work product.
  • Knowledge Sharing: Familiarize team members with the work product.

(b)(ii) Advantages of Jackson Systems Development (JSD):

  • Focus on Data Structure: Models the system based on the structure of the data it processes, leading to stable designs.
  • Good for Real-Time Systems: Handles time-ordered data and concurrent processes effectively.
  • Clear Mapping: Provides a clear link between the real world being modeled and the system implementation.

Note: This is a high-level conceptual chart. A detailed chart would break down each module further.

(d) Elements of a Training Plan:

  • Training Objectives: Clearly define what skills or knowledge users should acquire (e.g., "Users will be able to enter customer orders correctly").
  • Target Audience & Needs: Identify who needs training and their specific learning requirements based on their roles.
  • Training Content & Methods: Outline the topics to be covered and the methods used (e.g., classroom, e-learning, OJT, manuals).
  • Schedule & Logistics: Specify training dates, times, locations, required resources (trainers, rooms, equipment), and duration.
  • Evaluation Strategy: Describe how training effectiveness will be measured (e.g., tests, observation, feedback forms).

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Outline two challenges of adopting software as a service (SaaS) in an organization. (2 marks)

(b) Explain two categories of stakeholders in an Information System project. (4 marks)

(c) Rose used open questions to design a survey to gather detailed user understanding about a problem under investigation. Explain three disadvantages of this type of questions as used in the survey. (6 marks)

(d) (i) With the aid of diagrams, outline two file organization methods that could be used in Information Systems. (4 marks)

(ii) Bonnie opines that the existing Information System is destined for failure. Outline four signs that could have informed his opinion. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Challenges of Adopting SaaS:

  • Security Concerns: Entrusting sensitive organizational data to a third-party provider raises security and privacy risks.
  • Vendor Lock-in: Difficulty in migrating data and processes to a different provider if needed.
  • Dependence on Internet Connectivity: Access to the software relies entirely on a stable internet connection.
  • Limited Customization: SaaS applications often offer less flexibility for customization compared to on-premise solutions.

(b) Categories of Stakeholders:

  • Internal Stakeholders: Individuals or groups within the organization directly involved or affected by the project (e.g., end-users, managers, IT staff, project team, sponsors).
  • External Stakeholders: Individuals or groups outside the organization who have an interest or are affected by the project (e.g., customers, suppliers, government regulators, competitors).

(c) Disadvantages of Open Questions in Surveys:

  • Difficult to Analyze: Responses are qualitative and varied, making statistical analysis and comparison challenging and time-consuming.
  • Lower Response Rate (Potentially): Require more effort from respondents compared to closed questions, potentially leading to fewer completed surveys or skipped questions.
  • Subjectivity in Interpretation: Analyzing and coding open-ended responses can be subjective, introducing potential bias.
  • Irrelevant Information: Respondents might provide lengthy or off-topic answers that are not useful for the research objectives.

(d)(i) File Organization Methods:

  1. Sequential File Organization: Records are stored one after another in a predetermined order (e.g., by key field). Simple but slow for direct access. 
    +----------+    +----------+    +----------+
| Record 1 | -> | Record 2 | -> | Record 3 | ...
+----------+    +----------+    +----------+
  2. Indexed Sequential File Organization (ISAM/VSAM): Records are stored sequentially, but an index is maintained to allow direct access to specific records based on their key. Balances sequential processing and direct access needs. 
    +-------+      +----------+
| Index | ---> | Record X |
+-------+      +----------+
   |           | Record Y |
   +---------> +----------+
               | Record Z |
               +----------+
(Index points to approximate location, then sequential search)
  3. Direct/Random File Organization: Records are stored at addresses calculated from their key field using a hashing algorithm. Very fast for direct access but inefficient for sequential processing. 
    +----------+  Hashing   +-----------------+
| KeyValue | ---------> | Storage Address | ---> [Record Data]
+----------+ Algorithm  +-----------------+

(d)(ii) Signs of Potential System Failure:

  • Frequent Crashes/Errors: System is unstable and unreliable during operation.
  • Poor Performance: System is slow, unresponsive, or cannot handle the required workload.
  • High Maintenance Costs: Requires constant fixes and excessive resources to keep running.
  • User Dissatisfaction: Users find the system difficult to use, inefficient, or not meeting their needs.
  • Data Inconsistency/Corruption: System produces incorrect results or loses data.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Define the term task dependency as used in a project. (2 marks)

(b) Explain two factors that should be considered while creating a term of reference for an Information System project. (4 marks)

(c) (i) Differentiate between a throw-away prototype and evolutionary prototype. (4 marks)

(ii) Paul was required to design a data input form for an Information System. Explain two data accuracy measures that he could implement in the form design. (4 marks)

(d) Luke was required to investigate whether a new Information System addressed all requirements of a client.

(i) Identify the most appropriate testing method that he could use, justifying your answer. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain two advantages that the client would realize after carrying out the testing method identified in (i). (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Task Dependency:

  • A relationship between project tasks where one task must be started or completed before another can begin or finish.
  • Examples: Finish-to-Start (most common), Start-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, Start-to-Finish.

(b) Factors for Creating Terms of Reference (ToR):

  • Clear Objectives: Defining what the project aims to achieve ensures focus and measurability.
  • Defined Scope: Outlining project boundaries (what's included/excluded) prevents scope creep and manages expectations.
  • Stakeholder Identification: Knowing who is involved or affected helps in requirement gathering and communication planning.
  • Constraints: Identifying limitations (budget, time, resources) sets realistic boundaries for the project.

(c)(i) Throw-away vs Evolutionary Prototype:

FeatureThrow-away PrototypeEvolutionary Prototype
PurposeUsed primarily for requirement gathering and clarification; discarded after use.Gradually refined and developed into the final operational system.
DevelopmentBuilt quickly, often with less robust tools or code.Built using the target technology stack; requires more rigorous development.
End ResultDiscarded; serves as a learning tool.Becomes the final system after iterations.

(c)(ii) Data Accuracy Measures for Input Form:

  • Input Validation: Implementing checks to ensure data entered meets predefined criteria (e.g., format checks like email format, range checks for numbers, type checks for data types).
  • Dropdown Lists/Predefined Choices: Using lists or radio buttons for fields with a limited set of valid options reduces typing errors and ensures consistency.
  • Data Verification (Double Entry): Requiring critical data (like passwords or email addresses) to be entered twice to confirm accuracy.

(d)(i) Appropriate Testing Method:

  • Acceptance Testing (specifically User Acceptance Testing - UAT).
  • Justification: This testing is performed by the client/users to verify that the system meets their specified requirements and business needs before final acceptance.

(d)(ii) Advantages of Acceptance Testing for the Client:

  • Confidence Building: Assures the client that the system functions as expected and meets their business requirements.
  • Requirement Validation: Confirms that the developed system aligns with the agreed-upon specifications and user needs.
  • Early Defect Detection (from user perspective): Users might find usability issues or requirement gaps missed during earlier testing phases.
  • Improved User Buy-in: Involving users in testing increases their understanding and acceptance of the new system.

(d) Advantages of State Transition Diagrams:

  • Modeling Dynamic Behavior: Clearly shows how a system or object changes state in response to events over time, essential for online/interactive systems.

  • Communication Tool: Provides an intuitive visual representation of complex system behavior, understandable by both technical and non-technical stakeholders.
  • Basis for Testing: Test cases can be derived directly from the diagram to ensure all states and transitions are covered.

(ii) Explain two methods that could be used to acquire an Information System. (4 marks)

(b) CR-B Company Ltd. intends to review its Information System so as to address new user needs.

(i) Identify the type of system maintenance. (2 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Meta Data:

  • Data about data. It describes the properties, characteristics, context, and structure of other data.
  • Examples: Data type, field length, description, source, creation date, access permissions.

(a)(ii) Methods to Acquire an Information System:

  • In-house Development: Building the system using the organization's own IT staff and resources. Offers maximum customization but can be costly and time-consuming.
  • Purchasing Off-the-Shelf Software (COTS): Buying pre-built software packages from vendors. Faster implementation and potentially lower initial cost, but may require process changes to fit the software and offer limited customization.
  • Outsourcing: Hiring an external company to develop the system. Can leverage external expertise but requires careful vendor management and contract negotiation.
  • End-User Development: Simple applications developed by end-users using tools like spreadsheets or database software. Suitable for small, specific needs but lacks robustness and control.

(b)(i) Type of System Maintenance:

  • Perfective Maintenance. Justification: The review is intended to address new user needs, which involves enhancing or improving the existing system's functionality or performance, fitting the definition of perfective maintenance.

(b)(ii) Precautions for Perfective Maintenance:

  • Thorough Impact Analysis: Assess how the proposed changes will affect other parts of the system to avoid unintended consequences.
  • Regression Testing: After implementing changes, test existing functionalities thoroughly to ensure they haven't been negatively impacted (broken).
  • Version Control: Maintain different versions of the software to allow rollback if the changes introduce critical issues.
  • User Communication & Training: Inform users about the upcoming changes and provide necessary training or documentation.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Describe an entity life history (ELH) as used in system development. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline three benefits of using a work breakdown structure (WBS) in project management. (3 marks)

(b) Outline three conditions that will justify use of a spiral model during system analysis and design. (3 marks)

(c) Margaret was required to use several keys when designing tables in database information system for her company. Explain three types of keys that she could use. (6 marks)

(d) Draw a limited entry decision table to represent the following course selection processes. Wetu Technical College intends to shortlist students applicants for a diploma course in Information Communication Technology. Applicants should have scored at least C- mean grade in KCSE and at least scored grade C in both, Mathematics and English. (6 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Entity Life History (ELH):

  • A modeling technique (often used in SSADM) that describes the sequence of events affecting an entity from its creation to its deletion.
  • It models the dynamic behavior of a single entity type, showing the valid states and transitions caused by events.

(a)(ii) Benefits of Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):

  • Improved Planning: Breaks down large projects into smaller, manageable tasks, making planning and scheduling easier.
  • Clearer Scope Definition: Provides a hierarchical view of all project deliverables and tasks, helping to define project scope clearly.
  • Enhanced Estimation: Facilitates more accurate estimation of time, cost, and resources required for individual tasks.

(b) Conditions Justifying Spiral Model Use:

  • High Risk Projects: When project risks are significant and need careful evaluation and mitigation throughout development.
  • Unclear or Evolving Requirements: Well-suited for projects where requirements are not fully understood initially and are expected to change.
  • Large and Complex Projects: The iterative nature helps manage complexity by breaking the project into smaller cycles.

(c) Types of Database Keys:

  • Primary Key: Uniquely identifies each record in a database table; ensures entity integrity.
  • Foreign Key: Establishes a link between two tables; ensures referential integrity.
  • Composite Key: Primary key made up of multiple attributes to uniquely identify a record.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Outline two typical goals of system design. (2 marks)

(b) Distinguish between contingency and fallback plan as used in project management. (4 marks)

(c) Dema-P Company Ltd. intended to introduce a prototype of a proposed Information System that will be tested with users while they continue doing their work using its legacy Information System.

(i) Identify the changeover strategy used. (2 marks)

(ii) Outline four advantages of the changeover strategy identified in (i). (4 marks)

(d) Read the following narrative to answer the question that follows.

Hazina Technical Institute intends to set up a policy document for malpractice cases in Student’s term reports. A student who is suspected of plagiarizing his/her work would first be summoned by a subject lecturer for a chat to establish the authenticity of sources of references on affected reports.

Based on the outcome of the chat, the lecturer would then decide whether there is an academic dishonesty case to answer. If the lecturer attests that there is an academic dishonesty, there would be a case to answer. Hence, the lecturer would check student academic record for a similar case and if found the student would be referred to the Principal, a week suspension and a subsequent meeting with academic committee on reporting date.

However, if it is a first case the lecturer is supposed to get an approval from Dean of studies for the appropriate punishment that would be endorsed and a letter written to the student. A student would then choose to accept or appeal the finding of the Dean of studies. On acceptance he/she proceeds to carry out the punishment and the case is closed. Otherwise, if he/she chooses to appeal, he will meet the academic committee for the hearing of the case in line with the Institute’s academic policy before the case gets closed.

Draw a program flow chart to model the narrative into a system. (8 marks)

(a) Goals of System Design:

  • Efficiency: Create a system that performs operations efficiently, minimizing resource usage and maximizing performance.
  • User-Friendly: Design an interface that is intuitive and easy to use, reducing training needs and user errors.

(b) Contingency Plan vs Fallback Plan:

(c)(i) Changeover Strategy Used:

  • Parallel Running/Operation - Running both the new and old systems simultaneously.

(c)(ii) Advantages of Parallel Running:

  • Risk Minimization: Business continuity is maintained as the old system acts as a safety net.
  • Comparison of Results: Outputs from both systems can be compared to verify accuracy.
  • Confidence Building: Users gain confidence in the new system while having the security of the old system.
  • Training Opportunity: Staff can learn the new system without time pressure while still using the familiar system.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Outline two types of system users. (2 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between data and information as used in systems analysis and design. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Describe an expert system. (2 marks)

(ii) State two factors that should be considered when acquiring a new Information System, other than cost. (2 marks)

(c) Maria intends to investigate the characteristics of an open loop control system. Outline four characteristics that she could establish. (4 marks)

(d) A lecturer described components of an Information System to a SAD class. Explain three components that he could have mentioned to the class. (6 marks)

Answer

(a)(i) Types of System Users:

  • End Users: People who interact with the system directly to perform their jobs (e.g., data entry clerks, operators).
  • System Administrators: Technical users who maintain and manage the system (e.g., database administrators, network managers).

(a)(ii) Data vs Information:

FeatureDataInformation
DefinitionRaw facts and figures without context.Processed data that has meaning and value for decision making.
FormatUnorganized and unprocessed.Organized and processed into a meaningful form.
ValueLimited value in raw form.High value for decision making and planning.
ExampleSales figures: 500, 600, 700Monthly sales report showing trends and analysis.

(b)(i) Expert System:

  • A computer system that emulates human expert decision-making abilities using artificial intelligence and knowledge base.
  • It uses rules and facts to solve complex problems in a specific domain, similar to how a human expert would approach the problem.

(b)(ii) Factors to Consider When Acquiring an Information System:

  • Technical Compatibility: System must be compatible with existing hardware, software, and network infrastructure.
  • User Requirements: System should meet the functional needs and skill levels of intended users.
  • Vendor Reputation/Support: Vendor's track record, reliability, and availability of technical support.

(c) Characteristics of Open Loop Control System:

  • No Feedback Mechanism: System operates without monitoring its output or results.
  • Fixed Operation: System follows predetermined instructions without adapting to changes.
  • Simple Design: Generally less complex than closed-loop systems due to absence of feedback components.
  • Lower Accuracy: Less precise control due to inability to correct errors based on output.

(d) Components of an Information System:

  • Hardware: Physical equipment like computers, servers, network devices, and peripheral devices.
  • Software: Programs and applications that process data and provide user interface.
  • Data: Raw facts and figures that are processed by the system.
  • People: Users, operators, and technical staff who interact with and maintain the system.
  • Procedures: Guidelines and instructions for using and maintaining the system.
