DIPLOMA IN INFORMATION COMMUNICATION TECHNOLOGY

Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) State four items that could be included in a Terms of Reference (TOR) document. (4 marks)

(b) Differentiate between open and closed system. (4 marks)

(c) Mark, a System Analyst, conducted a technical feasibility study for an Information System. Explain two requirements he could have considered. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Janet developed a database for an Information System. Describe two models she could have used to develop the database. (4 marks)

(ii) Describe two methods that could be used to capture data directly into a database. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Items in Terms of Reference (TOR):

  1. Project objectives and scope
  2. Deliverables and milestones
  3. Budget and resources
  4. Stakeholder roles and responsibilities

(b) Open vs. Closed System:

Open System Closed System
Interacts with its environment Isolated from its environment
Exchanges information/resources Self-contained
Adaptive to external changes Not affected by external changes
Example: Business organization Example: Chemical reaction in a sealed container

(c) Technical Feasibility Requirements:

  1. Hardware and Software Availability:
    • Assessing if the required technology (servers, workstations, specific software) is available or can be acquired.
    • Compatibility checks between new and existing systems.
  2. Technical Expertise:
    • Evaluating if the current IT staff has the necessary skills to develop, implement, and maintain the system.
    • Determining if external expertise or training is needed.

(d) (i) Database Models:

  1. Relational Model:
    • Data organized in tables (relations) with rows and columns.
    • Relationships established through keys (primary/foreign).
    • Widely used, supported by SQL.
  2. Hierarchical Model:
    • Data organized in a tree-like structure.
    • Parent-child relationships (one-to-many).
    • Older model, less flexible than relational.

(ii) Direct Data Capture Methods:

  1. Optical Character Recognition (OCR):
    • Scans printed text and converts it into machine-readable data.
    • Used for digitizing documents, forms.
  2. Barcode Scanners:
    • Reads barcodes to capture product or item information quickly.
    • Common in retail, inventory management.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four rules followed when drawing data flows in a data flow diagram. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Describe two prototyping techniques used during system development. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between limited entry and extended entry decision tables. (4 marks)

(c) Bidii Company intends to develop a new Information System. Outline four reasons that could have led to this consideration. (4 marks)

(d) Joan used questionnaire and interview method to collect data for a new Information System. Describe two methods she should use to verify the accuracy of this data. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Rules for Drawing Data Flows in DFD:

  1. Data flows must originate from or terminate at a process.
  2. Data flows must be labeled with meaningful noun phrases describing the data.
  3. Data cannot flow directly between two external entities without a process.
  4. Data cannot flow directly between two data stores without a process.

(b) (i) Prototyping Techniques:

  1. Throwaway/Rapid Prototyping:
    • Quickly built to understand requirements, then discarded.
    • Focuses on exploring ideas and getting user feedback early.
  2. Evolutionary Prototyping:
    • Prototype is refined iteratively based on feedback.
    • Gradually evolves into the final system.

(ii) Limited vs. Extended Entry Decision Tables:

Limited Entry Extended Entry
Condition stubs allow only 'Y', 'N', or '-' (Yes/No/Irrelevant). Condition stubs allow more descriptive values or ranges.
Action stubs typically use 'X' to indicate action execution. Action entries can specify more detailed actions or values.
Simpler structure, easier for basic logic. More compact for complex conditions, can be harder to read initially.

(c) Reasons for Developing a New Information System:

  1. Outdated Technology: Existing system uses old hardware/software that is inefficient or unsupported.
  2. New Business Requirements: Changes in business processes, regulations, or market demands require new functionalities.
  3. Poor Performance: Current system is slow, unreliable, or cannot handle the volume of data/transactions.
  4. Competitive Advantage: Need for a system that offers better features, efficiency, or customer experience than competitors.

(d) Methods to Verify Data Accuracy:

  1. Cross-referencing:
    • Comparing data collected from questionnaires with data from interviews.
    • Checking consistency across different sources (e.g., documents).
  2. Follow-up Interviews/Clarifications:
    • Contacting respondents to clarify ambiguous or conflicting answers.
    • Conducting targeted follow-up questions based on initial findings.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Describe Structured System Development Methodology. (2 marks)

(b) Explain the importance of system design. (4 marks)

(c) Describe two stages of System Development Life Cycle (SDLC) that are carried out on a fully developed system. (4 marks)

(d) Shujaa Company generates employees’ payslip using a payroll system. The system accepts hours worked and computes the gross pay given a rate of Ksh. 500 per hour. The system then stores this information in a payment file.

(i) Represent this logic in a system flowchart. (6 marks)

(ii) Explain two limitations of using a flowchart to design a system. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Structured System Development Methodology (SSADM):

  • A systematic approach to system analysis and design.
  • Emphasizes clear stages, documentation, and modeling techniques (like DFDs, LDS, ELHs).

(b) Importance of System Design:

  • Blueprint Creation: Provides a detailed plan for building the system, guiding developers.
  • Requirement Translation: Converts user requirements into technical specifications.
  • Quality Foundation: Establishes the architecture for reliability, maintainability, and performance.
  • Cost Estimation: Allows for more accurate estimation of development time and resources.

(c) SDLC Stages on a Fully Developed System:

  1. Maintenance:
    • Ongoing process after deployment.
    • Includes correcting errors (corrective), adapting to new environments (adaptive), and enhancing features (perfective).
  2. Evaluation/Review:
    • Assessing the system's performance against original objectives.
    • Gathering user feedback for future improvements or replacement decisions.

(d) Payroll System Flowchart:

(ii) Limitations of Flowcharts:

  1. Complexity: Can become large and difficult to follow for complex systems.
  2. Modification Difficulty: Making changes often requires redrawing significant portions.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two advantages of decomposing a system into modules. (4 marks)

(b) Techdada Company hired a system analyst. Outline four tasks she is likely to perform. (4 marks)

(c) Masomo College uses the following procedure to process an application for admission. An application is received and the system checks whether an application fee has been paid. If paid, the system checks whether the course applied for has a vacancy. If vacancy is available and fee is paid, an admission letter is generated, else a message to pay is sent to the applicant. Otherwise, a regret letter is generated.

Draw a decision tree to represent this logic. (6 marks)

(d) Felix created a data dictionary for an Information System. Explain the importance of this tool. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Advantages of System Decomposition (Modularity):

  1. Easier Development & Testing: Modules can be developed and tested independently by different teams or individuals, speeding up the process.
  2. Improved Maintainability: Changes or bug fixes in one module are less likely to affect others, making maintenance simpler and safer.
  3. Reusability: Well-defined modules can potentially be reused in other systems or parts of the same system.

(b) Tasks of a System Analyst:

  1. Requirement Gathering: Interviewing users, observing processes, and analyzing documents to understand business needs.
  2. System Modeling: Creating diagrams (DFDs, ERDs, Use Cases) to represent system processes and data.
  3. Feasibility Studies: Assessing the technical, economic, and operational viability of proposed solutions.
  4. System Design: Developing specifications for hardware, software, data structures, and user interfaces.

(c) Admission Process Decision Tree:

      Application Received
           /       \
      Fee Paid?     Fee Paid?
       /    \         /    \
      Y      N       Y      N
     /        \     /        \
Course Vacancy?  Msg: Pay Fee  Course Vacancy?  Msg: Pay Fee
   /      \             /      \
  Y        N           Y        N
 /          \         /          \
Generate     Generate   Generate     Generate
Admission    Regret     Admission    Regret
Letter       Letter     Letter       Letter
*Note: The logic seems slightly ambiguous in the prompt ("If vacancy is available and fee is paid... else a message to pay... Otherwise, a regret letter"). The tree above interprets "Otherwise" as applying if the fee is paid but there's no vacancy.* A clearer interpretation might be needed based on exact rules.

(d) Importance of a Data Dictionary:

  1. Standardization: Ensures consistent naming and definition of data elements across the system, reducing ambiguity.
  2. Documentation: Provides a central repository of metadata (data about data), detailing format, meaning, relationships, and usage.
  3. Communication Aid: Facilitates understanding and communication between analysts, designers, developers, and users regarding data.
  4. Impact Analysis: Helps in assessing the impact of changes to data elements on different parts of the system during maintenance.

(b) Differentiate between formative and summative evaluations as used in system review. (4 marks)

(a) Physical Security Measures:

  1. Access Control: Locks, key cards, biometric scanners to restrict entry to server rooms or data centers.
  2. Surveillance: CCTV cameras to monitor sensitive areas.
  3. Environmental Controls: Fire suppression systems, temperature and humidity controls to protect hardware.
  4. Secure Hardware Disposal: Proper destruction of old hard drives or storage media.

(b) Formative vs. Summative Evaluation:

Formative Evaluation Summative Evaluation
Conducted *during* system development or implementation. Conducted *after* system implementation is complete.
Purpose is to improve the system as it's being built. Purpose is to assess the overall success or effectiveness of the final system.
Focuses on process and identifying areas for immediate change. Focuses on outcomes and impact.
Feedback is used for ongoing adjustments. Results inform decisions about continuation, modification, or replacement.

(c) User Training:

  1. End-Users/Operators: Those who directly interact with the system for daily tasks.
  2. Managers/Supervisors: Those who use system outputs for decision-making or oversee operations.

(ii) Methods of Training:

  1. Instructor-Led Classroom Training: Formal sessions with an instructor guiding a group of users, often with hands-on exercises.
  2. Web-Based Training (WBT) / E-Learning: Online modules, videos, and tutorials that users can access at their own pace.

Answer

(a) Characteristics of MIS:

  1. Management Oriented: Focuses on providing information to support managerial functions (planning, controlling, decision-making).
  2. Integrated System: Combines data and processes from various functional areas of an organization.
  3. Database Driven: Relies on a common, controlled database for data consistency.
  4. Report Focused: Generates predefined reports (summary, exception, detailed) on a regular schedule or on demand.

(b) Leasing vs. Outsourcing:

Leasing Outsourcing
Acquiring the *use* of hardware/software for a period via payments. Contracting an external company to perform specific IT functions or manage systems.
Company typically operates the leased system internally. External provider manages and operates the function/system.
Focus is on acquiring assets temporarily. Focus is on acquiring services or expertise.
Example: Leasing servers instead of buying. Example: Hiring a company to manage payroll processing or IT helpdesk.

(c) System Maintenance:

(i) Type of Maintenance: Perfective Maintenance

(ii) Reasons for Recommendation:

  1. User Requests: Users identified needs for new functionalities to improve usability or efficiency.
  2. Changing Business Needs: The business environment evolved, requiring the system to support new processes or goals.
  3. Performance Enhancement: Adding features could optimize existing workflows or improve overall system performance.

(ii) Disadvantages of NPV Method:

  1. Requires Accurate Forecasts: NPV is highly sensitive to the accuracy of future cash flow predictions.
  2. Discount Rate Sensitivity: Choosing the correct discount rate is crucial and can be subjective or difficult to determine accurately.
  3. Doesn't Consider Non-Monetary Factors: Ignores intangible benefits or strategic importance that don't have a direct cash value.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Explain each of the following terms as used in system documentation:

(i) user manual; (2 marks)

(ii) test data (2 marks)

(b) Differentiate between functional and non-functional requirements as used in system analysis. (4 marks)

(c) A system development team completed an ICT project as planned. Explain three measures taken to ensure this successful completion. (6 marks)

(d) The government intends to automate all its services by registering all its citizens. Adult fill a form with their details and those of dependants which are stored in a citizen file. Details are verified against the national identity register and birth register respectively. A passport photo for each citizen is uploaded and stored. If the details are verified a unique ID number is generated and the adult citizen is notified through an SMS to collect the card. The citizen then signs a digital register and the details are stored in a signature file.

Draw a level 1 data flow diagram to represent this logic. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) System Documentation Terms:

(i) User Manual:

  • A document intended for the end-users of the system.
  • Provides instructions on how to use the system's features, perform tasks, and troubleshoot common issues.

(ii) Test Data:

  • A set of input data specifically created to test the functionality and performance of a system.
  • Includes valid, invalid, and boundary condition data to ensure the system behaves correctly under various scenarios.

(b) Functional vs. Non-Functional Requirements:

Functional Requirements Non-Functional Requirements
Define *what* the system should do. Define *how* the system should perform its functions.
Specify features, processes, inputs, outputs. Specify quality attributes like performance, security, usability, reliability.
Example: "The system shall allow users to register an account." Example: "The system login page must load within 2 seconds."
Verifiable through testing specific functions. Verifiable through performance tests, security audits, usability reviews.

(c) Measures for Successful Project Completion:

  1. Clear Scope Management: Defining project boundaries clearly and managing any changes through a formal process to prevent scope creep.
  2. Effective Project Planning & Tracking: Developing a realistic schedule with milestones and regularly monitoring progress against the plan using tools like Gantt charts.
  3. Stakeholder Communication: Maintaining regular communication with users, sponsors, and team members to manage expectations and address issues promptly.
  4. Risk Management: Identifying potential risks early and having contingency plans in place to mitigate their impact.

(i) Describe two types of relationships depicted in the figure. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Contents of Project Initiation Document (PID):

  1. Project Goals and Objectives: What the project aims to achieve.
  2. Project Scope: Defines boundaries, what is included and excluded.
  3. Key Stakeholders: Identifies individuals or groups involved or affected.
  4. High-Level Requirements: Initial understanding of what the system needs to do.
  5. Initial Risk Assessment: Preliminary identification of potential problems.

(b) System Boundary vs. Environment:

Boundary Environment
Defines the limits or scope of the system. Everything outside the system's boundary.
Separates the system from its surroundings. Interacts with the system through inputs and outputs crossing the boundary.
Determined during system analysis. Includes users, other systems, organizations, regulations, etc.
Example: The components included within a specific software application. Example: The users interacting with the software, the operating system it runs on, connected hardware.

(c) Organizational Levels (Based on Standard Pyramid):

(i) Information Needs:

  • Top Management (Strategic): Summarized, external, future-oriented information (e.g., market trends, competitor analysis, overall financial performance).
  • Middle Management (Tactical): Exception reports, departmental summaries, budget vs. actual comparisons (e.g., sales figures by region, production efficiency).
  • Operational Management (Operational): Detailed, internal, real-time data (e.g., daily transaction logs, inventory levels, individual employee performance).

(ii) Types of Decisions:

  • Top Management: Strategic Decisions (long-term goals, new market entry, major investments). Unstructured.
  • Middle Management: Tactical Decisions (resource allocation, budget setting, process improvement). Semi-structured.
  • Operational Management: Operational Decisions (daily scheduling, handling routine transactions, inventory reordering). Structured.

(d) Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD - Based on Example):

(i) Relationship Types Depicted:

  1. One-to-Many (1..*): Example: One Publisher can publish many Books, but each Book is published by one Publisher. (Represented by crow's foot notation on the 'many' side).
  2. Many-to-Many (M..* or *..*): Example: One Member can borrow many Books, and one Book can be borrowed by many Members (often resolved via an associative entity like 'Borrows').

(ii) Challenge in Implementing ERDs:

  • Complexity Management: For large systems, ERDs can become very complex and difficult to read or maintain, requiring careful layout and potentially breaking down into smaller diagrams.
  • Accurate Representation: Ensuring the relationships and cardinalities accurately reflect the real-world business rules can be challenging and requires thorough analysis.
