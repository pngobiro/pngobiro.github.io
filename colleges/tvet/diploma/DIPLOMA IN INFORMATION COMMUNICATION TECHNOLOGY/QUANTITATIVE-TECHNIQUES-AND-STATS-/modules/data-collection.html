2.3 Data Collection Methods

Introduction

Data collection is a crucial step in statistical analysis. This chapter covers various methods of collecting data and the considerations for choosing appropriate methods based on research objectives.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand different types of data collection methods
  • Learn how to design effective questionnaires and surveys
  • Recognize the importance of sampling techniques
  • Identify potential sources of bias in data collection

Primary Data Collection Methods

Direct Observation

The process of gathering data through direct observation of phenomena, events, or behavior.

Advantages

  • First-hand information
  • Real-time data collection
  • High accuracy potential

Disadvantages

  • Time-consuming
  • Limited to observable phenomena
  • Potential observer bias

Questionnaires

A set of written questions designed to gather information from respondents.

Key Principles of Questionnaire Design

  • Keep questions clear and concise
  • Avoid leading questions
  • Use appropriate response formats
  • Maintain logical question order
  • Include clear instructions

Example: Survey Question Types

Closed-ended: "Do you own a car? □ Yes □ No"

Multiple Choice: "How often do you exercise?
□ Daily □ Weekly □ Monthly □ Never"

Likert Scale: "Rate your satisfaction (1-5):
Very Dissatisfied (1) --- Very Satisfied (5)"

Secondary Data Collection

Using existing data from previous research, publications, or databases.

Sources of Secondary Data

  • Government publications
  • Academic journals
  • Industry reports
  • Online databases
  • Previous research studies

Evaluating Secondary Data

Consider these factors when using secondary data:

  • Reliability of the source
  • Age of the data
  • Relevance to current research
  • Methodology used
  • Potential bias

Sampling Techniques

Types of Sampling

Probability Sampling

  • Simple Random Sampling
  • Systematic Sampling
  • Stratified Sampling
  • Cluster Sampling

Non-Probability Sampling

  • Convenience Sampling
  • Purposive Sampling
  • Quota Sampling
  • Snowball Sampling

Example: Stratified Sampling

A company wants to survey employee satisfaction across different departments:

  • Marketing Department (100 employees) - Sample 20
  • Sales Department (150 employees) - Sample 30
  • IT Department (75 employees) - Sample 15

Total population: 325 employees

Sample size: 65 employees (20% from each stratum)

📝

Key Points

  • •

    Choose data collection methods based on research objectives and available resources.

  • •

    Well-designed questionnaires are crucial for gathering accurate and useful data.

  • •

    Secondary data can save time but must be evaluated for reliability and relevance.

  • •

    Appropriate sampling techniques ensure representative data collection.

  • •

    Consider potential biases and limitations in all data collection methods.
