2.2 Techniques of Counting

Introduction

This section covers various counting techniques essential for solving management decision problems and statistical analysis, focusing on the fundamental counting principle, permutations, and combinations.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply the Fundamental Counting Principle to solve problems
  • Calculate and use permutations in real-world scenarios
  • Understand and apply combinations in practical situations
  • Distinguish between when to use permutations vs combinations

Fundamental Counting Principle

What is the Fundamental Counting Principle?

If one event can occur in 'm' ways and another independent event can occur in 'n' ways, then the two events can occur together in 'm × n' ways.

Example: Menu Combinations

A restaurant offers:

  • 3 types of main dishes
  • 4 types of side dishes
  • 2 types of drinks

How many different meal combinations are possible?

Solution: 3 × 4 × 2 = 24 different combinations

Permutations

A permutation is an ordered arrangement of items in which the order must be strictly observed.

Permutation Formula

The number of permutations of 'r' items taken from a sample of 'n' items is given by:

nPr = n!/(n-r)!

where ! = factorial and 0! = 1

Example: Arranging Books

In how many different ways can 5 different books be arranged on a shelf?

This is a permutation of 5 items, taking all 5 at once.

5P5 = 5!

= 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1

= 120 different arrangements

Example: PIN Numbers

How many different 3-digit PIN numbers can be created using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 (no repetition allowed)?

This is a permutation of 5 items, taking 3 at a time.

5P3 = 5!/(5-3)!

= 5!/(2)!

= (5 × 4 × 3 × 2!)/(2!)

= 60 different PIN numbers

Practice Problems - Permutations

  1. How many different ways can 7 people line up for a photograph?
  2. From a group of 6 candidates, in how many ways can we select a president, secretary, and treasurer?
  3. How many 4-letter words can be formed using the letters of the word "MATH" (no repetition)?

Combinations

A combination is a group of items in which order is not important. For a combination to hold at any given time, it must comprise the same items but if a new item is added to or removed from the group then we have a new combination.

Combination Formula

The following formula is used to determine the number of combinations in a given situation:

nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!)

where n is the total number of items and r is the number of items being chosen

Example 1: Team Selection

In how many ways can a team of 3 players be selected from 8 available players?

This is a combination because order doesn't matter - we just need any 3 players.

8C3 = 8!/(3!(8-3)!)

= 8!/(3!5!)

= (8 × 7 × 6 × 5!)/(3 × 2 × 1 × 5!)

= 56 different possible teams

Example 2: Pizza Toppings

A pizza shop offers 7 different toppings. How many different 3-topping pizzas can be made?

This is a combination because:

  • Order doesn't matter (pepperoni + mushroom = mushroom + pepperoni)
  • We're selecting 3 toppings from 7 available

7C3 = 7!/(3!(7-3)!)

= 7!/(3!4!)

= 35 different pizza combinations

Practice Problems - Combinations

  1. In how many ways can 4 books be chosen from a shelf of 10 books?
  2. A student must answer 6 questions out of 9 on an exam. How many different combinations of questions can be chosen?
  3. From a standard deck of 52 cards, in how many ways can 5 cards be drawn?
📝

Key Points

  • •

    The Fundamental Counting Principle states that if event A can occur in m ways, and event B in n ways, then events A and B can occur together in m × n ways.

  • •

    Permutations are ordered arrangements - use when sequence matters (like arranging people in a line or creating PIN numbers).

  • •

    Combinations are unordered selections - use when sequence doesn't matter (like choosing team members or pizza toppings).

  • •

    For permutations, use the formula nPr = n!/(n-r)! when selecting r items from n items.

  • •

    For combinations, use the formula nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!) when selecting r items from n items.

  • •

    When solving counting problems, first determine if order matters to decide between using permutation or combination.
