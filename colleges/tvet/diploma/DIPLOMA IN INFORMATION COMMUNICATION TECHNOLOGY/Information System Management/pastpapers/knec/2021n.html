DIPLOMA IN INFORMATION COMMUNICATION TECHNOLOGY

MODULE III

MANAGEMENT INFORMATION SYSTEMS

INSTRUCTIONS TO CANDIDATES

This paper consists of EIGHT questions.

Answer any FIVE questions in the answer booklet provided.

Candidates should answer all questions in English.

Question 1

(a) Outline two challenges of emerging trends in Management Information Systems. (2 marks)

(b) Masomo Institute installed a new information system for data processing. Explain three guidelines that could be applied to ensure responsible use of this system. (6 marks)

(c) A County Government intends to establish information centres. Explain three services that are likely to be offered in the centres. (6 marks)

(d) (i) Ujuzi Institute engaged John as their ICT project manager. Explain two roles that he could play. (4 marks)

(d) (ii) Outline two causes of failure to complete an ICT project as scheduled. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Challenges of Emerging Trends in MIS:
  1. Keeping Pace with Rapid Change: Constant evolution of technology (AI, Big Data, Cloud, IoT) requires continuous learning, adaptation, and investment, making it hard to stay current.
  2. Security & Privacy Concerns: New technologies often introduce new vulnerabilities and ethical challenges related to data security, privacy, and potential misuse (e.g., AI bias, IoT security).
  3. Integration Complexity: Integrating new systems (like cloud services or AI tools) with existing legacy systems can be technically challenging and costly.
  4. Skills Gap: Finding and retaining personnel with the necessary skills to manage and leverage new technologies can be difficult.
*(Note: Only two challenges were required)*
(b) Guidelines for Responsible Use of New IS (Masomo Institute):
  1. Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Clearly define how the system should be used for institutional purposes, outlining prohibited activities (e.g., personal use, accessing unauthorized data, installing unapproved software).
  2. Data Confidentiality & Privacy: Emphasize the importance of protecting sensitive student and staff data, adhering to data privacy regulations, and accessing/sharing information only on a need-to-know basis.
  3. Security Practices: Mandate strong password policies, regular password changes, locking workstations when unattended, reporting suspicious activities, and avoiding phishing scams or malware introduction.
(c) Services Offered in County Government Information Centres:
  1. Access to E-Government Services: Providing public access terminals and assistance for citizens to access online government services (e.g., applying for permits, paying taxes/fees, accessing public records).
  2. Digital Literacy Training: Offering basic computer skills training, internet usage guidance, and digital literacy programs to bridge the digital divide.
  3. Public Information Access: Providing access to county information, public notices, development plans, job postings, and other relevant community information via digital platforms.
  4. Business Support Services: Offering resources for local businesses, such as access to online registration portals, market information, or e-procurement systems.
*(Note: Only three services were required)*
(d) (i) Roles of ICT Project Manager (John):
  1. Planning & Scheduling: Defining project scope, objectives, tasks, timelines, resource requirements, and creating the project plan and schedule.
  2. Leading & Coordinating Team: Directing project team members, assigning tasks, facilitating communication, resolving conflicts, and ensuring collaboration towards project goals.
  3. Monitoring & Controlling Progress: Tracking project progress against the plan, managing risks and issues, controlling scope changes, managing the budget, and reporting status to stakeholders.
*(Note: Only two roles were required)*
(d) (ii) Causes of ICT Project Failure to Meet Schedule:
  1. Scope Creep: Uncontrolled changes or additions to the project scope after it has started, requiring more time than originally planned.
  2. Poor Initial Estimation: Underestimating the time required for tasks due to lack of experience, insufficient analysis, or optimism bias.
  3. Resource Unavailability: Lack of required personnel, equipment, or funding when needed during the project lifecycle.
  4. Unforeseen Technical Problems: Encountering unexpected technical challenges or integration issues that take longer to resolve.
*(Note: Only two causes were required)*

(d) Viuvall Company Ltd conducts its business globally. Describe two types of information systems the company could use. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Circumstances for Effective Change Programme via IS:
  1. Clear Business Need & Vision: When there is a well-defined business problem or opportunity that the IS directly addresses, coupled with a clear vision from leadership about how the IS will enable the desired change.
  2. Strong User Involvement & Management Support: When end-users are actively involved in the design and implementation, and top management provides consistent support, resources, and communication, fostering acceptance and managing resistance.
  3. Appropriate Technology & Implementation Strategy: Selecting technology that fits the organizational context and using a suitable implementation approach (e.g., phased rollout, adequate training) that minimizes disruption and maximizes user adoption.
*(Note: Only two circumstances were required)*
(b) (i) Socio-technical View:

The socio-technical view emphasizes that the performance of an information system is optimized only when both the social system (people, structure, tasks, culture) and the technical system (hardware, software, networks) are designed to work harmoniously together. It recognizes the interdependence between technology and people in achieving organizational goals.

(b) (ii) Factors to Consider When Using IS for Decision Making:
  1. Information Quality: The accuracy, timeliness, relevance, completeness, and reliability of the data and information provided by the system are crucial for making sound decisions. Poor quality information leads to poor decisions.
  2. System Capabilities & Limitations: Understanding what the system can and cannot do, the models it uses, its assumptions, and its limitations is essential to interpreting results correctly and avoiding over-reliance on potentially flawed outputs.
  3. User Skills & Interpretation: The decision-maker's ability to understand the information presented, use the system's analytical tools effectively, and apply appropriate judgment and context to the system's output.
*(Note: Only two factors were required)*
(c) Network Diagram & Project Duration:

(i) Network Diagram:

  2. Global Customer Relationship Management (CRM) System: A centralized system to manage customer interactions, sales, and marketing efforts across different geographical markets, providing a consistent customer experience and global view of customer data.
  3. Supply Chain Management (SCM) System: Systems to manage the flow of goods, information, and finances between the company and its global suppliers and distributors, optimizing logistics and inventory across borders.
*(Note: Only two systems were required)*

Question 3

(a) Outline two differences between Critical Path Method (CPM) and Project Evaluation and Review Technique (PERT) as used in project management. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Describe adaptive system maintenance as used in information systems. (2 marks)

(b) (ii) A Technical Institute is undertaking an ICT project. Explain two attributes the project should exhibit. (4 marks)

(c) Tegeo Industries prefers using web-based IS project management. Explain three reasons for this preference. (6 marks)

(d) Differentiate between functional and management activity approaches to information systems classification. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Differences between CPM and PERT:
  1. Time Estimates: CPM typically uses a single, deterministic time estimate for each activity, assuming certainty. PERT uses three time estimates (optimistic, most likely, pessimistic) to calculate an expected time, acknowledging uncertainty (probabilistic).
  2. Focus: CPM is primarily activity-oriented and often focuses on the trade-off between project cost and time. PERT is more event-oriented and better suited for projects with high uncertainty, focusing on time and probability analysis.
  3. Application: CPM is often used in industries with predictable activities (e.g., construction), while PERT is favored for R&D projects or those with novel tasks where time estimates are less certain.
*(Note: Only two differences were required)*
(b) (i) Adaptive System Maintenance:

Adaptive maintenance involves modifying an information system to cope with changes in its external environment. This includes changes in business rules, government regulations, hardware platforms, operating systems, or interfaces with other systems. The goal is to keep the system functional and relevant as its surrounding context evolves.

(b) (ii) Attributes of an ICT Project (Technical Institute):
  1. Defined Scope & Objectives: The project should have clear, specific, and measurable goals outlining what it aims to achieve for the institute (e.g., implement a new student portal, upgrade network infrastructure).
  2. Temporary Endeavor: An ICT project has a defined start and end date, unlike ongoing operations. It aims to create a unique product, service, or result within that timeframe.
  3. Resource Constraints: Projects operate within specific constraints, including budget limitations, available personnel (staff, students, faculty), equipment, and time schedules.
*(Note: Only two attributes were required)*
(c) Reasons for Using Web-Based IS Project Management (Tegeo Industries):
  1. Accessibility & Collaboration: Allows team members and stakeholders to access project information (plans, status, documents) from anywhere with an internet connection, facilitating remote work and collaboration across different locations.
  2. Real-time Updates & Communication: Provides a central platform for real-time updates on task progress, issue tracking, and communication, improving transparency and coordination.
  3. Centralized Document Management: Offers a single repository for storing and sharing project-related documents, ensuring everyone works with the latest versions.
  4. Reduced Software Installation/Maintenance: As a web-based solution, it typically requires no client-side installation and updates are managed centrally by the provider, reducing IT overhead.
*(Note: Only three reasons were required)*
(d) Functional vs. Management Activity Approaches to IS Classification:
  • Functional Approach: Classifies information systems based on the specific business function they support, such as Sales & Marketing IS, Manufacturing & Production IS, Finance & Accounting IS, Human Resources IS. It focuses on departmental areas.
  • Management Activity (or Level) Approach: Classifies information systems based on the level of management activity or decision-making they support: Operational (TPS), Knowledge (OAS, KWS), Management (MIS, DSS), and Strategic (ESS). It focuses on the type of support provided to different management levels.

Question 4

(a) Outline two characteristics of an information system for each of the following dimensions:

  • (i) time (2 marks)
  • (ii) content; (2 marks)
  • (iii) form. (2 marks)

(b) Explain two importance of Management Information System in an organization. (4 marks)

(c) (i) State two criteria used to measure information system performance after a maintenance activity. (2 marks)

(c) (ii) Distinguish between perfective and preventive systems maintenance. (4 marks)

(d) Bidii Institute uses an executive support system. Describe two features of this system. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Characteristics of Information System Dimensions:
  • (i) Time:
    • Timeliness: Information is available when needed for decision-making.
    • Currency: Information is up-to-date and reflects the current state.
    • Frequency: Information is provided as often as needed.
    • Time Period: Information covers the relevant past, present, or future time horizon.
    *(Note: Only two characteristics were required)*
  • (ii) Content:
    • Accuracy: Information is free from errors.
    • Relevance: Information is pertinent and applicable to the task or decision at hand.
    • Completeness: All necessary information required for a specific purpose is present.
    • Conciseness: Information is presented succinctly without unnecessary detail.
    *(Note: Only two characteristics were required)*
  • (iii) Form:
    • Clarity: Information is presented in an unambiguous and easily understandable format.
    • Level of Detail: Information is provided at the appropriate level of summarization or detail for the user.
    • Presentation: Information is presented effectively using appropriate media (text, graphs, tables).
    • Order: Information is arranged logically.
    *(Note: Only two characteristics were required)*
(b) Importance of MIS in an Organization:
  1. Improved Decision Making: Provides managers with timely, accurate, and relevant information to support tactical and operational decision-making, leading to better outcomes.
  2. Enhanced Efficiency & Control: Helps monitor organizational performance against goals, identify deviations, streamline processes, and improve operational efficiency.
  3. Facilitates Planning & Coordination: Provides historical data and reports that aid in forecasting, planning future activities, and coordinating efforts across departments.
*(Note: Only two points were required)*
(c) (i) Criteria to Measure IS Performance After Maintenance:
  • System Uptime / Availability
  • User Satisfaction Surveys
  • Error Rates / Number of Reported Bugs
  • Response Time / Throughput
  • Maintenance Request Backlog
*(Note: Only two criteria were required)*
(c) (ii) Perfective vs. Preventive Maintenance:
  • Perfective Maintenance: Aims to improve the system's performance, efficiency, or usability even if it's functioning correctly. It focuses on making the system *better*. Example: Optimizing code, enhancing user interface.
  • Preventive Maintenance: Aims to reduce the likelihood of future failures or problems by performing proactive tasks. It focuses on *preventing* issues. Example: Applying software patches, hardware diagnostics, code restructuring for better maintainability.
(d) Features of an Executive Support System (ESS) (Bidii Institute):
  1. Graphical Interface & Dashboards: Presents high-level, summarized information visually through charts, graphs, and dashboards for quick comprehension by executives.
  2. Drill-Down Capability: Allows executives to explore summary data and move down to lower levels of detail to investigate exceptions or trends.
  3. Access to External & Internal Data: Integrates data from various internal systems (finance, enrollment, HR) and external sources (e.g., government education policies, competitor data, demographic trends).
  4. Ease of Use: Designed for senior executives with limited time and potentially less technical expertise, featuring intuitive navigation.
*(Note: Only two features were required)*

Question 5

(a) Outline four stages of project management. (4 marks)

(b) Tunz Institute outsourced its information systems functions:

  • (i) Explain two merits of this approach. (4 marks)
  • (ii) Outline three limitations of this approach. (6 marks)

(c) Differentiate between physical and human information systems resources. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Explain the term spoofing as used in information systems ethics. (2 marks)

(d) (ii) The following are types of plans: rules, vision, procedures, mission, policies, regulations and strategy. Classify each of the plans according to the level of management. (3 marks)

Answer

(a) Stages of Project Management:
  1. Initiation: Defining the project at a broad level, identifying objectives, scope, stakeholders, and feasibility. Output: Project Charter.
  2. Planning: Developing the detailed project plan, including defining tasks, creating schedules (WBS, Gantt, PERT/CPM), estimating resources and costs, and planning for risks and communication. Output: Project Management Plan.
  3. Execution: Carrying out the work defined in the project plan, managing resources, coordinating team members, and implementing project deliverables.
  4. Monitoring & Controlling: Tracking project progress against the plan, measuring performance, identifying variances, managing changes, and taking corrective actions. This occurs throughout the execution phase.
  5. Closure: Finalizing all project activities, obtaining formal acceptance of deliverables, archiving project documents, releasing resources, and conducting post-project reviews (lessons learned).
*(Note: Only four stages were required, often Monitoring & Controlling is integrated with Execution)*
(b) Outsourcing IS Functions (Tunz Institute):
  • (i) Merits:
    1. Cost Reduction: Potential savings through vendor's economies of scale, lower labor costs, or predictable contract pricing compared to managing variable in-house costs.
    2. Access to Expertise/Technology: Gain access to specialized skills, advanced technology, and best practices that the institute might not possess internally or afford to develop.
    3. Focus on Core Activities: Allows the institute to concentrate resources and management effort on its core educational mission rather than managing complex IT functions.
    *(Note: Only two merits were required)*
  • (ii) Limitations:
    1. Loss of Control: Reduced direct control over IT operations, data security, system performance, and responsiveness to specific institutional needs.
    2. Vendor Lock-in/Dependency: Difficulty and cost associated with switching vendors if service is poor or prices increase significantly.
    3. Security & Confidentiality Risks: Entrusting sensitive institutional or student data to a third party introduces potential security vulnerabilities and confidentiality risks.
    4. Hidden Costs: Contract complexities, managing the vendor relationship, and potential extra charges for services outside the initial scope can lead to unexpected costs.
    *(Note: Only three limitations were required)*
(c) Physical vs. Human IS Resources:
  • Physical Resources: Tangible components of an IS, including computer hardware (servers, PCs, peripherals), network equipment (routers, cables), storage media, and physical facilities (data centers).
  • Human Resources: The people involved in the development, operation, management, and use of information systems, including IS specialists (analysts, programmers, administrators) and end-users.
(d) (i) Spoofing:

Spoofing is a type of attack where a malicious party impersonates another device, user, or entity on a network to gain unauthorized access, bypass security controls, or trick users into revealing sensitive information. Examples include IP spoofing, email spoofing, and website spoofing (phishing sites).

(d) (ii) Classification of Plans by Management Level:
  • Strategic Level (Top Management): Vision, Mission, Strategy, Long-term Policies, Regulations (External compliance often drives strategy).
  • Tactical Level (Middle Management): Policies (departmental), Medium-term plans derived from strategy.
  • Operational Level (Lower Management/Supervisors): Rules, Procedures.

Note: Classification can overlap; e.g., policies exist at multiple levels. Regulations are external but influence strategic and operational plans.

Question 6

(a) Outline two results of automating workflow procedures. (2 marks)

(b) Explain three types of decisions that could be made at each level of management in a typical organisation. (6 marks)

(c) Janet, an ICT manager, uses a technique in which the start and finish times of a project are known with certainty. Explain three time estimates for the other activities in the project. (6 marks)

(d) (i) State four functions that could be carried out using an Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) in a typical organisation. (2 marks)

(d) (ii) Differentiate between expert and artificial intelligent systems. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Results of Automating Workflow Procedures:
  1. Increased Efficiency & Speed: Tasks are completed faster with less manual intervention, reducing processing times.
  2. Improved Consistency & Accuracy: Automated processes follow predefined rules consistently, reducing errors associated with manual work.
  3. Reduced Operational Costs: Lower labor costs due to automation and reduced errors/rework.
  4. Better Monitoring & Control: Workflow systems often provide visibility into process status, bottlenecks, and performance metrics.
*(Note: Only two results were required)*
(b) Types of Decisions at Each Management Level:
  1. Strategic Level (Top Management): Unstructured decisions concerning long-term goals, overall strategy, resource allocation, and organizational direction. Examples: Entering new markets, major investments, organizational restructuring.
  2. Tactical Level (Middle Management): Semi-structured decisions focused on implementing strategies defined by top management. Examples: Developing departmental budgets, creating marketing plans, deciding on resource allocation within the department, scheduling production.
  3. Operational Level (Lower Management/Supervisors): Structured decisions related to day-to-day operations and ensuring tasks are performed efficiently according to predefined rules. Examples: Handling customer complaints based on policy, managing inventory levels, assigning daily tasks to employees.
(c) Time Estimates for Activities (Certain Start/Finish):

If the start and finish times of a project are known with certainty, this implies a deterministic approach, likely CPM rather than PERT. In traditional CPM, only one time estimate is typically used for each activity:

  1. Duration Estimate: The single, most likely estimate of the time required to complete the activity based on historical data or expert judgment.

However, if the question implies using PERT's framework despite certainty (which is unusual), Janet would still need the three estimates for calculation, even if the range is narrow:

  1. Optimistic Time (to): The minimum possible time.
  2. Most Likely Time (tm): The realistic time under normal conditions.
  3. Pessimistic Time (tp): The maximum possible time if delays occur.

(Context suggests CPM's single estimate is more appropriate if start/finish are certain, but PERT estimates are listed if the question implies that specific technique despite the certainty premise).

(d) (i) Functions Carried Out Using ERP:
  • Financial Accounting (General Ledger, Accounts Payable/Receivable)
  • Human Resources (Payroll, Personnel Administration)
  • Manufacturing & Production Planning
  • Supply Chain Management (Inventory, Order Processing, Procurement)
  • Customer Relationship Management (Sales, Marketing, Service)
*(Note: Only four functions were required)*
(d) (ii) Expert Systems vs. Artificial Intelligent Systems:
  • Expert Systems (ES): A type of AI system designed to emulate the decision-making ability of a human expert in a specific, narrow domain. They typically use a knowledge base (facts and rules) and an inference engine to provide advice or solutions. They capture existing human expertise.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) Systems: A broader field encompassing systems that exhibit intelligent behavior, including learning, problem-solving, perception, and language understanding. AI systems (like machine learning models) can often learn from data and improve over time, potentially surpassing codified human expertise in certain tasks. Expert systems are a subset of AI.

Question 7

(a) (i) State two impacts of computer crimes on a business. (2 marks)

(a) (ii) Outline two criteria for information system acquisition. (2 marks)

(b) Explain each of the following terms as used in information systems ethics:

  • (i) passive attack; (2 marks)
  • (ii) integrity. (2 marks)

(c) A company has two offices interconnected. Explain three advantages of these systems. (6 marks)

(d) The management of a company developed a security policy document. Explain three control measures that could have been included in the document. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) (i) Impacts of Computer Crimes on a Business:
  1. Financial Loss: Direct theft of funds, costs associated with system recovery, legal fees, regulatory fines, loss of revenue due to downtime.
  2. Reputational Damage: Loss of customer trust and public confidence following a data breach or service disruption, leading to customer churn and difficulty attracting new business.
  3. Operational Disruption: System downtime caused by attacks (e.g., ransomware, DDoS) can halt business operations, affecting productivity and service delivery.
  4. Loss of Intellectual Property/Competitive Advantage: Theft of trade secrets, customer data, or R&D information can severely damage a company's competitive position.
*(Note: Only two impacts were required)*
(a) (ii) Criteria for Information System Acquisition:
  1. Functional Requirements Fit: How well the proposed system meets the identified business needs and user requirements.
  2. Cost: Total cost of ownership, including purchase price, implementation costs, training, maintenance, and ongoing operational expenses, compared to the budget and expected benefits (ROI).
  3. Technical Feasibility & Compatibility: Whether the system is compatible with existing infrastructure (hardware, OS, network) and whether the organization has the technical expertise to support it.
  4. Vendor Viability & Support: The financial stability, reputation, track record, and quality of support offered by the system vendor.
*(Note: Only two criteria were required)*
(b) IS Ethics Terms:
  • (i) Passive Attack: An attack where the intruder monitors or intercepts communication or system data without altering it. The goal is typically information gathering or espionage, not disruption. Example: Eavesdropping on network traffic.
  • (ii) Integrity: A security goal ensuring that data and system resources are accurate, complete, and protected from unauthorized modification or destruction. It ensures data trustworthiness.
(c) Advantages of Interconnected Offices (e.g., via WAN/VPN):
  1. Resource Sharing: Allows employees in different offices to access shared central resources like databases, servers, applications, and specialized hardware, reducing duplication.
  2. Improved Communication & Collaboration: Facilitates seamless communication (email, VoIP, video conferencing) and collaboration (shared documents, project management tools) between geographically dispersed teams.
  3. Centralized Management & Control: Enables centralized management of IT resources, data backups, security policies, and software updates across all locations.
(d) Control Measures in an ICT Security Policy:
  1. Access Controls: Measures defining who can access specific systems and data, including user authentication (passwords, multi-factor authentication), authorization levels based on roles, and procedures for granting/revoking access.
  2. Data Security Measures: Guidelines on data handling, including encryption requirements for sensitive data (at rest and in transit), data backup and recovery procedures, and secure data disposal methods.
  3. Network Security Measures: Rules regarding firewall configuration, use of VPNs for remote access, intrusion detection/prevention systems, and wireless network security protocols.
  4. Acceptable Use Guidelines: Defining permitted and prohibited uses of company ICT resources, including internet usage, email etiquette, and software installation rules.
*(Note: Only three measures were required)*

Question 8

(a) State two information systems planning methodologies. (2 marks)

(b) (i) Outline two approaches that could be used to implement virtual offices. (2 marks)

(b) (ii) A system analyst provided a high maintenance cost estimate. Explain three factors that influenced this cost. (6 marks)

(c) Explain two mechanisms that could be used when dealing with personnel in an organisation with respect to information systems ethics. (4 marks)

(d) Newton Company Ltd is planning for a new information system. Outline the steps the company could take for an effective information system plan. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Information Systems Planning Methodologies:
  1. Business Systems Planning (BSP): A top-down approach developed by IBM that identifies key business processes and data classes to define an overall information architecture.
  2. Critical Success Factors (CSF): Focuses on identifying the key areas where an organization must perform well to achieve its strategic goals, and then defines IS needed to support these factors.
  3. Stages of Growth Model (Nolan): Suggests that IS planning and development evolve through predictable stages (initiation, contagion, control, integration, data administration, maturity).
  4. Scenario Planning: Developing various plausible future scenarios and planning IS capabilities needed to respond effectively to each.
*(Note: Only two methodologies were required)*
(b) (i) Approaches to Implement Virtual Offices:
  1. Telecommuting/Remote Work: Employees work primarily from home or remote locations, using technologies like VPNs, cloud applications, video conferencing, and instant messaging to connect and collaborate.
  2. Hot Desking/Hotel-ing: Employees do not have assigned desks but reserve workspace (desks, meeting rooms) as needed, supported by mobile technology and shared resources.
  3. Co-working Spaces: Utilizing shared office spaces that provide infrastructure (internet, desks, meeting rooms) for remote workers or distributed teams.
*(Note: Only two approaches were required)*
(b) (ii) Factors Influencing High Maintenance Cost Estimate:
  1. System Age & Complexity (Legacy Systems): Older systems, especially those with complex, poorly documented code or outdated technology, are often significantly more difficult and expensive to maintain.
  2. Poor Initial Development Quality: Systems developed with poor design, inadequate testing, or insufficient documentation will likely have more bugs and require more effort to modify or enhance later.
  3. Frequency & Scope of Changes: A system requiring frequent updates due to rapidly changing business requirements or regulations will naturally incur higher maintenance costs.
  4. Lack of Skilled Personnel: Difficulty in finding or retaining personnel with expertise in the specific (potentially older) technologies used by the system can drive up maintenance costs.
*(Note: Only three factors were required)*
(c) Mechanisms for Dealing with Personnel & IS Ethics:
  1. Clear Policies & Codes of Conduct: Establishing and communicating clear policies regarding acceptable use, data privacy, intellectual property, and consequences for unethical behavior.
  2. Ethics Training & Awareness Programs: Regularly educating employees about ethical responsibilities, potential risks, security best practices, and relevant laws/regulations.
  3. Monitoring & Auditing: Implementing monitoring systems (with appropriate disclosure) and conducting audits to detect policy violations or unethical activities.
*(Note: Only two mechanisms were required)*
(d) Steps for Effective IS Plan (Newton Company Ltd):
  1. Understand Business Strategy: Clearly define Newton's overall business goals, strategies, and critical success factors.
  2. Assess Current IS Situation: Evaluate the existing IT infrastructure, applications, data resources, and personnel skills (strengths and weaknesses).
  3. Identify IS Opportunities/Needs: Analyze business processes and identify areas where new or improved IS can support business goals, solve problems, or create advantages.
  4. Develop IS Architecture Vision: Define the desired future state for data, applications, and technology infrastructure aligned with business needs.
  5. Formulate IS Strategy & Prioritize Projects: Outline the specific strategies for achieving the architecture vision and prioritize potential IS projects based on criteria like strategic alignment, ROI, risk, and resource availability.
  6. Create Implementation Roadmap & Resource Plan: Develop a high-level timeline, budget, and resource allocation plan for the prioritized projects.
