Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Outline three approaches used in classifying Information Systems in an organisation. (3 marks)

(b) The ICT manager of HeavyTec Company is required to talk to new recruits on ethical issues related to using a modern information system. Explain three points that the manager should include in the talk. (6 marks)

(c) A hospital intends to implement a new information system. Explain three types of information system plans that should be formulated at the strategic level. (6 marks)

(d) A certain organisation is in the process of acquiring a new information system.

  • (i) Outline two factors that could influence the choice of the system to be acquired. (2 marks)
  • (ii) Outline three reasons that would lead to acquisition failure of this information system. (3 marks)

Answer

(a) Approaches to Classifying Information Systems:

  • By Organizational Level Supported: Classifying systems based on the management level they serve (Operational - TPS; Tactical - MIS/DSS; Strategic - ESS).
  • By Functional Area Supported: Classifying systems based on the specific business function they automate or support (e.g., Sales & Marketing Systems, Manufacturing & Production Systems, Finance & Accounting Systems, Human Resources Systems).
  • By Type of Support Provided: Classifying systems based on the nature of the tasks they perform (e.g., Transaction Processing, Management Reporting, Decision Support, Office Automation, Knowledge Management).

(b) Ethical Issues Talk for New Recruits (HeavyTec):

  • Data Privacy and Confidentiality: Emphasize the responsibility to protect sensitive customer and employee data, adhere to privacy policies, and avoid unauthorized access or disclosure.
  • Acceptable Use of Resources: Clearly define policies regarding the appropriate use of company computers, networks, and software for business purposes, prohibiting excessive personal use, illegal activities, or accessing inappropriate content.
  • Intellectual Property Rights: Explain the importance of respecting software licenses, copyrights, and company trade secrets, prohibiting unauthorized copying or distribution of software or proprietary information.

(c) Strategic Level IS Plans (Hospital):

  • IT Infrastructure Plan: Outlining the long-term strategy for hardware, software, networks, and data centers needed to support the hospital's overall goals, including scalability, security, and disaster recovery.
  • Data Management and Analytics Plan: Defining the strategy for collecting, storing, integrating, and analyzing clinical and administrative data to support strategic decision-making, research, and quality improvement initiatives (e.g., implementing a data warehouse or BI tools).
  • Telemedicine/Digital Health Plan: Developing a long-term vision and roadmap for implementing or expanding telehealth services, patient portals, remote monitoring, or other digital health initiatives to improve access and patient care.

(d) IS Acquisition:

  • (i) Factors Influencing Choice:
    1. Functional Requirements: How well the system's features and capabilities match the specific needs and processes of the organization.
    2. Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): Including not just the purchase price but also implementation, training, maintenance, support, and infrastructure costs over the system's lifecycle.
  • (ii) Reasons for Acquisition Failure:
    1. Poor Requirements Definition: Failure to accurately identify and document the organization's needs, leading to acquiring a system that doesn't fit.
    2. Lack of User Involvement/Acceptance: Insufficient input from end-users during selection or implementation, leading to resistance or failure to adopt the new system.
    3. Inadequate Project Management: Poor planning, budget control, scope management, or vendor management during the acquisition and implementation process.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two causes leading to maintenance of an information system. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Outline four problems that ICT managers may face while undertaking their roles in their organisations. (4 marks)

(ii) Outline four impacts caused by implementation of information systems in organisations. (4 marks)

(c) Placet Company intends to commence on a new information system project. Explain two planning methodologies that the company could use. (4 marks)

(d) Waops Company uses information systems in its transportation business. Describe two types of systems that could be ideal for the company. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Causes Leading to IS Maintenance:

  • System Errors/Bugs: Corrective maintenance is required when defects are found in the software or hardware, causing incorrect results or system failures.
  • Environmental Changes: Adaptive maintenance is needed when external factors change, such as new government regulations, changes in business processes, or upgrades to operating systems or hardware that the IS must adapt to.

(b) ICT Manager Roles and IS Impacts:

  • (i) Problems Faced by ICT Managers:
    1. Keeping Up with Technological Change: The rapid pace of IT evolution requires constant learning and strategic decisions about adopting new technologies.
    2. Budget Constraints: Balancing the need for system upgrades and new projects with limited financial resources.
    3. Security Threats: Constantly evolving cyber threats require ongoing vigilance, investment in security measures, and incident response planning.
    4. Aligning IT with Business Goals: Ensuring that IT initiatives directly support the strategic objectives of the organization and demonstrate clear business value.
  • (ii) Impacts of IS Implementation:
    1. Increased Efficiency/Productivity: Automation of tasks and streamlined workflows often lead to faster processing and higher output.
    2. Improved Decision Making: Better access to timely and accurate information can support more informed managerial decisions.
    3. Changes in Organizational Structure/Roles: IS can flatten hierarchies, change job responsibilities, and sometimes lead to job displacement.
    4. Enhanced Customer Service/Competitive Advantage: New systems can enable better customer interaction, faster response times, or innovative products/services.

(c) IS Planning Methodologies (Placet Company):

  • Critical Success Factors (CSF): Focuses on identifying the few key areas (CSFs) where the organization must excel to achieve its goals. IS planning then prioritizes projects that support these CSFs.
  • Business Systems Planning (BSP): A top-down approach developed by IBM that identifies key business processes and data classes, then defines an information architecture to support them, leading to specific IS project recommendations.

(d) Ideal IS for Transportation Business (Waops Company):

  • Fleet Management System (incorporating GPS): To track vehicle locations in real-time, optimize routes, monitor fuel consumption and driver behavior, manage maintenance schedules, and improve dispatching efficiency.
  • Customer Relationship Management (CRM) / Booking System: To manage customer interactions, handle bookings or service requests, track shipments (if applicable), manage billing, and provide customer service support.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two application areas of information societies to developing countries. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Explain two circumstances that would lead an organisation to make decisions of a semi structured nature. (4 marks)

(ii) Connet Ventures Ltd intends to establish an effective decision making process in their business. Explain three characteristics that these decisions should possess. (6 marks)

(c) Nowadays organisations are using cloud-computing technologies for their business processes. Outline two advantages that these organisations accrue from this approach. (2 marks)

(d) Distinguish between knowledge based information system and office automation system as used in management information systems. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Application Areas of Information Societies (Developing Countries):

  • Education (E-Learning): Providing access to educational resources, online courses, and remote learning opportunities, potentially overcoming geographical barriers and teacher shortages.
  • Healthcare (E-Health/Telemedicine): Enabling remote consultations, access to health information, electronic health records, and improved management of healthcare services, especially in rural areas.

(b) Decision Making:

  • (i) Circumstances for Semi-structured Decisions:
    1. Problems where only some variables or procedures are known, requiring managerial judgment alongside data analysis (e.g., setting marketing budgets based on sales data and market intuition).
    2. Situations involving moderate uncertainty or complexity where standard operating procedures are insufficient, but analytical tools can still provide support (e.g., evaluating different investment options).
  • (ii) Characteristics of Effective Decisions (Connet Ventures):
    1. Goal-Oriented: Decisions should be clearly aimed at achieving specific, desired organizational objectives.
    2. Rational/Data-Informed: Based on logical analysis of available information and evidence, rather than solely on emotion or guesswork.
    3. Timely: Made within an appropriate timeframe to be relevant and effective in addressing the problem or seizing the opportunity.

(c) Advantages of Cloud Computing:

  • Scalability: Easily adjust computing resources (storage, processing power) up or down based on business needs without large upfront investments in hardware.
  • Cost Savings: Potential reduction in capital expenditure on infrastructure, lower maintenance costs, and pay-as-you-go pricing models.

(d) Knowledge-Based Information System (KBIS) vs. Office Automation System (OAS):

  • KBIS (e.g., Expert Systems): Designed to capture, store, and apply specialized human knowledge or expertise to solve problems or make decisions within a specific domain. They often use AI techniques like rule-based reasoning. Focus is on replicating expertise.
  • OAS: Focuses on improving the efficiency and productivity of clerical and administrative tasks through tools like word processing, email, scheduling, and document management. Focus is on automating routine office work.

  • Quality of Initial Development: Poorly designed, poorly documented, or bug-ridden systems are significantly more expensive to maintain over their lifecycle.

(b) Functions of Initial Transaction-Oriented Systems:

  • Record Keeping: Capturing and storing basic operational data related to core business transactions (e.g., sales, purchases, payments).
  • Process Automation: Automating routine, high-volume tasks like order entry, billing, or payroll calculation to improve efficiency and reduce manual errors.
  • Basic Reporting: Generating simple operational reports summarizing transaction activities (e.g., daily sales reports, inventory lists).

  • Niche Market Focus: Employing targeted IS (e.g., specialized databases, focused marketing systems) to serve a specific market segment exceptionally well, understanding and meeting their unique needs better than broad-market competitors.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two benefits that an organisation could derive from using Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three circumstances under which prototyping methodology would be preferred during system development. (6 marks)

(c) Explain two ways in which information systems could be used to support production function in a manufacturing company. (4 marks)

(d) Explain three challenges that may be encountered during information system project implementation phase. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Benefits of ERP Systems:

  • Integration of Business Processes: Provides a unified platform integrating data and processes across different functional areas (finance, HR, supply chain, etc.), improving visibility and coordination.
  • Improved Efficiency and Standardization: Standardizes processes based on industry best practices embedded in the ERP software, reducing redundancy and improving operational efficiency.

(b) Circumstances Favoring Prototyping Methodology:

  • Unclear User Requirements: When users are unsure of their exact needs or find it difficult to articulate them, building a working prototype allows them to see and interact with a model, facilitating feedback and clarification.
  • Need for Early User Feedback: When early and frequent user feedback is crucial to ensure the final system meets expectations, especially for user interface design or novel functionalities.
  • High-Risk or Innovative Projects: For projects involving new technologies or complex features, prototyping helps test feasibility and identify potential issues early in the development cycle.

(c) IS Support for Production Function (Manufacturing):

  • Process Control Systems: Using sensors and automated controls (often part of SCADA or DCS) to monitor and adjust physical production processes in real-time, ensuring quality and efficiency (e.g., controlling temperature, pressure, machine speeds).
  • Manufacturing Execution Systems (MES): Tracking and managing work-in-progress on the factory floor, scheduling production orders, monitoring machine availability, and collecting real-time production data.

(d) Challenges During IS Project Implementation Phase:

  • User Resistance to Change: Employees may resist adopting the new system due to fear of the unknown, perceived threats to their jobs, lack of training, or dissatisfaction with the system's usability.
  • Data Conversion Problems: Migrating data accurately and completely from legacy systems to the new system can be complex, time-consuming, and prone to errors.
  • Technical Issues and Bugs: Unexpected technical problems, software bugs, or integration difficulties can arise during implementation, causing delays and requiring troubleshooting.
