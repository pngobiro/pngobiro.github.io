Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two reasons for the increased reluctance by information system managers to adopt cloud computing. (4 marks)

(b) Explain two features of Decision Support Systems. (4 marks)

(c) Malida Enterprises Ltd intends to introduce a quality management information system. Explain three functions that the system could perform. (6 marks)

(d) A commercial bank has installed an information system with a view to gain competitive advantage over its rivals. Explain three ways in which the system could assist the bank achieve their objective. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Reluctance to Adopt Cloud Computing:

  • Security Concerns: Managers may worry about the security of sensitive company data when it's stored off-premises in a third-party provider's data center, fearing potential breaches or unauthorized access.
  • Loss of Control/Vendor Lock-in: Concerns about losing direct control over IT infrastructure and data, and becoming overly dependent on a specific cloud provider, making future migrations difficult or costly.

(b) Features of Decision Support Systems (DSS):

  • Support for Semi-structured/Unstructured Decisions: DSS are designed to assist managers with complex problems that may not have clear-cut solutions or standard procedures, unlike Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) which handle routine tasks.
  • User Interaction and Control: They are typically interactive, allowing users (managers) to explore data, use various models (statistical, optimization, simulation), conduct "what-if" analysis, and control the decision-making process.

(c) Functions of a Quality Management Information System (QMIS) for Malida Enterprises:

  • Data Collection and Monitoring: Tracking key quality metrics throughout processes (e.g., defect rates, customer complaints, supplier performance) to identify trends and areas for improvement.
  • Document Control: Managing quality-related documents like procedures, standards, specifications, and records to ensure consistency and compliance.
  • Corrective and Preventive Actions (CAPA): Facilitating the process of identifying root causes of quality issues, implementing corrective actions, and tracking their effectiveness to prevent recurrence.

(d) Information System for Competitive Advantage (Commercial Bank):

  • Improved Customer Service: Offering convenient online banking, mobile apps, personalized services based on customer data analysis, and faster transaction processing can attract and retain customers.
  • Operational Efficiency/Cost Reduction: Automating processes (like loan applications, account management) reduces manual effort, minimizes errors, and lowers operational costs, allowing for more competitive pricing or higher profits.
  • New Product/Service Innovation: Using data analytics to identify unmet customer needs or market trends, enabling the bank to develop and launch innovative financial products or services ahead of competitors.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four functions of information as a resource in a management information system. (4 marks)

(b) Explain a circumstance under which each of the following system maintenance methods could be used in an organization:

  • (i) preventive; (2 marks)
  • (ii) perfective. (2 marks)

(c) A hospital intends to use a financial and accounting management information system. Explain three relevant functions that the system could be used for. (6 marks)

(d) Explain three functions of information system resources in an organization. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Functions of Information as a Resource in MIS:

  • Decision Support: Providing timely and relevant data to help managers at all levels make informed decisions.
  • Operational Control: Monitoring day-to-day activities and transactions to ensure they align with plans and standards.
  • Communication/Coordination: Facilitating the flow of information between different departments, levels, and individuals within the organization.
  • Knowledge Management: Capturing, storing, and disseminating organizational knowledge and expertise.

(b) System Maintenance Circumstances:

  • (i) Preventive Maintenance: Used proactively to reduce the likelihood of future system failures. Circumstance: Regularly scheduled activities like data backups, hardware checks, software updates/patching, and performance tuning, even if no specific problem has occurred.
  • (ii) Perfective Maintenance: Used to improve system performance, efficiency, or maintainability, even if the system is functioning correctly. Circumstance: Optimizing code, restructuring a database for faster queries, or enhancing user interface based on feedback, without changing core functionality.

(c) Functions of Financial & Accounting MIS in a Hospital:

  • Patient Billing and Accounts Receivable: Generating patient invoices, tracking payments from patients and insurance companies, managing collections, and reporting on outstanding balances.
  • General Ledger and Financial Reporting: Recording all financial transactions, producing financial statements (income statement, balance sheet), managing budgets, and ensuring regulatory compliance.
  • Payroll and Accounts Payable: Managing employee salaries, benefits, and deductions, as well as processing and paying invoices from suppliers and vendors.

(d) Functions of Information System Resources:

  • Input: Capturing or collecting raw data from within the organization or from its external environment (e.g., data entry, scanning documents, sensor readings).
  • Processing: Converting raw input into a more meaningful form (e.g., calculating totals, sorting data, performing analysis, formatting reports).
  • Output: Transferring the processed information to the people who will use it or to the activities for which it will be used (e.g., displaying reports, printing invoices, sending notifications).

Answer

(a) Importance of Information System Characteristics:

  • (i) Relevance: Information must be pertinent and applicable to the specific task or decision at hand. Irrelevant information wastes time and can lead to poor decisions.
  • (ii) User Requirements: The system must meet the specific needs and expectations of the people who will use it. Failure to meet user requirements leads to low adoption, inefficiency, and dissatisfaction.
  • (iii) Accuracy: Information must be correct, precise, and free from errors. Inaccurate information can lead to flawed decisions, financial losses, and damage to reputation.

(b) Ensuring Reliability in a Government HR System:

  • Rigorous Testing: Conduct thorough unit, integration, system, and user acceptance testing (UAT) to identify and fix bugs before deployment.
  • Data Validation Rules: Implement input validation checks to prevent incorrect or inconsistent data from entering the system.
  • Robust Error Handling: Design the system to gracefully handle unexpected errors or exceptions without crashing or corrupting data.
  • Redundancy and Backup: Implement backup procedures and potentially redundant hardware/systems to ensure data availability and recoverability in case of failure.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four strategic decisions which a management information systemâ€™s developer would consider in order to achieve management information system goals and objectives. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Outline two measures that an organization could put in place to control electronic eavesdropping. (2 marks)

(ii) Distinguish between data flow diagrams and data matrices as used in project planning of a management information system. (4 marks)

(c) A large supermarket intends to acquire an inventory control information system. Outline four criteria that the supermarket could use. (4 marks)

(d) Management information systems provide a platform for effective decision-making. Explain three features of the system that could make it provide such a platform. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Strategic Decisions for MIS Developer:

  • Technology Platform: Choosing the underlying hardware, operating systems, database management systems, and network infrastructure that align with long-term goals and scalability needs.
  • Build vs. Buy vs. Outsource: Deciding whether to develop the system in-house, purchase an off-the-shelf package, or outsource development/hosting based on cost, time, expertise, and customization requirements.
  • Data Architecture and Integration: Planning how data will be structured, stored, accessed, and integrated across different systems to ensure consistency and support business intelligence.
  • Security Strategy: Defining the overall approach to system security, including access controls, data encryption, disaster recovery, and compliance with regulations.

(b) Controls and Diagrams:

  • (i) Measures to Control Electronic Eavesdropping:
    1. Encryption: Encrypting sensitive data both in transit (e.g., using TLS/SSL for network communication) and at rest (e.g., encrypting databases or files) makes intercepted data unreadable without the key.
    2. Secure Network Design: Implementing secure network protocols, firewalls, and intrusion detection systems to prevent unauthorized access and monitoring of network traffic. Using wired connections where possible or secured Wi-Fi (WPA2/WPA3).
  • (ii) Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs) vs. Data Matrices:
    • DFDs: Graphical representations showing the flow of data through a system. They illustrate processes, data stores, external entities, and the data flows connecting them. Focus on *how* data moves and is transformed.
    • Data Matrices (e.g., CRUD Matrix): Tabular representations showing the relationship between data entities (or objects) and system processes/functions. They typically indicate which processes Create, Read, Update, or Delete specific data entities. Focus on *what* processes do to *which* data.

(c) Criteria for Supermarket Inventory Control System Acquisition:

  • Functionality: Does the system offer the required features (e.g., real-time stock tracking, purchase order generation, sales integration, reporting, barcode scanning support, expiry date tracking)?
  • Scalability: Can the system handle the current volume of inventory and transactions, and can it grow with the supermarket's future expansion needs?
  • Integration Capabilities: Can the system easily integrate with existing systems, particularly the Point of Sale (POS) system and potentially accounting software?
  • Cost (Total Cost of Ownership): Considering not just the initial purchase price but also implementation costs, training, ongoing maintenance, support fees, and hardware requirements.

(d) MIS Features for Effective Decision-Making Platform:

  • Data Integration: Consolidating data from various sources (sales, inventory, finance, HR) into a unified view, providing a holistic picture for better analysis.
  • Reporting and Analysis Tools: Offering flexible tools to generate standard reports, ad-hoc queries, visualizations (charts, dashboards), and perform analysis (e.g., trend analysis, forecasting) to identify insights.
  • Timeliness and Accessibility: Providing up-to-date information in an easily accessible format (often via web interfaces or dashboards) when managers need it.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Explain two logical components of a management information system. (4 marks)

(b) Management information systems can be classified by their functional areas or by the support provided. Explain the difference between these two classes of management information system. (4 marks)

(c) Explain three ways in which the use of management information system by organizations have resulted to an effective control system. (6 marks)

(d) Most managers are concerned about the use of management information system by employees as a tool for social interaction within the workplace. Explain three reasons that may have influenced the managers concern. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Logical Components of MIS:

  • Data/Information: The raw facts (data) collected and the processed, organized context (information) that the system manages and provides to users for decision-making.
  • Processes/Procedures: The defined steps, rules, and logic that govern how data is captured, processed, stored, and distributed within the system to achieve specific outcomes.

(b) MIS Classification: Functional Areas vs. Support Provided:

  • Classification by Functional Area: Groups MIS based on the specific business department or function they support (e.g., Accounting MIS, Marketing MIS, Human Resources MIS, Manufacturing MIS). Each system is tailored to the unique needs and processes of that area.
  • Classification by Support Provided: Groups MIS based on the type of managerial activity or decision-making level they support (e.g., Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) for operational tasks, Management Information Systems (MIS) for tactical control, Decision Support Systems (DSS) for semi-structured decisions, Executive Support Systems (ESS) for strategic decisions). This classification focuses on the nature of the support rather than the department.

(c) How MIS Leads to Effective Control Systems:

  • Performance Monitoring: MIS provides timely reports and dashboards comparing actual performance against planned targets or standards (e.g., sales quotas, production levels, budget variances), allowing managers to quickly identify deviations.
  • Exception Reporting: Systems can automatically flag significant deviations from norms or thresholds, drawing management attention to critical issues that require intervention, rather than overwhelming them with routine data.
  • Improved Data Accuracy and Consistency: By centralizing data and automating calculations, MIS reduces manual errors and ensures that control decisions are based on reliable, consistent information.

(d) Managers' Concerns about MIS as a Social Interaction Tool:

  • Reduced Productivity: Excessive use of system features like internal messaging, email, or integrated social platforms for non-work-related communication can distract employees and decrease overall productivity.
  • Security Risks: Inappropriate sharing of information (even unintentionally) through social features, or clicking malicious links shared via internal communication, can pose security threats to the organization's data and systems.
  • Potential for Misuse/Harassment: Workplace communication tools can potentially be misused for gossip, bullying, harassment, or spreading misinformation, creating a negative or hostile work environment that managers must address.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Explain why each of the following techniques are used by managers when dealing with resistance to change during systems change-over:

  • (i) communication; (2 marks)
  • (ii) education. (2 marks)

(b) Differentiate between semi-structured and unstructured approaches to a decision making process. (4 marks)

(c) A consultant has been engaged by an insurance company to assist in the determination of the requirements of a proposed information system. Explain three ways in which the consultant could obtain the requirements. (6 marks)

(d) Project managers have lately resorted to software applications as a tool for managing projects. Explain three reasons for their preference of this tool. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Techniques for Dealing with Resistance to Change:

  • (i) Communication: Used to explain the reasons for the change, its benefits (to the organization and employees), the timeline, and how it will impact individuals. Open communication helps reduce uncertainty, address rumors, and build understanding and trust.
  • (ii) Education (and Training): Used to provide employees with the knowledge and skills needed to work with the new system or processes. Education helps build confidence, reduce fear of the unknown, and demonstrate the practical benefits and usability of the change.

(b) Semi-structured vs. Unstructured Decision Approaches:

  • Semi-structured Decisions: Involve problems where only parts of the problem have clear-cut answers provided by accepted procedures. They require some element of judgment and evaluation by the decision-maker, often supported by MIS or DSS providing relevant data and models (e.g., deciding on marketing budgets based on sales forecasts and market analysis).
  • Unstructured Decisions: Involve problems where the decision-maker must provide judgment, evaluation, and insight to solve the problem. There are no standard procedures or agreed-upon methods for reaching a solution. Typically novel, important, and non-routine, often supported by ESS providing high-level summarized data and external information (e.g., deciding whether to enter a new market).

(c) Ways for Consultant to Obtain System Requirements (Insurance Company):

  • Interviews: Conducting structured or unstructured interviews with key stakeholders (managers, end-users, IT staff) to understand their needs, current processes, pain points, and desired system features.
  • Observation: Observing users performing their current tasks to understand workflows, identify inefficiencies, and see how information is actually used in practice.
  • Document Analysis: Reviewing existing documentation, such as procedure manuals, forms, reports, system diagrams, and organizational charts, to understand current processes and information flows.

(d) Reasons for Project Managers Preferring Software Tools:

  • Improved Planning and Scheduling: Tools facilitate creating detailed work breakdown structures (WBS), estimating durations, defining dependencies, and generating visual schedules (like Gantt charts or network diagrams).
  • Enhanced Tracking and Monitoring: Allow managers to easily track task progress, resource allocation, budget expenditure, and identify potential delays or issues against the baseline plan.
  • Better Communication and Collaboration: Many tools provide centralized platforms for communication, document sharing, and status updates, improving collaboration among team members and stakeholders.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four ways in which management information system users could get involved in unethical use of information within an organization. (4 marks)

(b) Explain how management information system has made it easy to carry out each of the following organizational functions:

  • (i) planning; (2 marks)
  • (ii) coordination; (2 marks)

(c) The management of a manufacturing company relies on management information system as a tool for its strategic needs. Explain three reasons why the management looks upon the management information system for such strategic functions. (6 marks)

(d) Rational decisions effectively and efficiently ensure the achievement of the goal for which the decisions are made. Explain three challenges in making such decisions in an organization. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Unethical Use of Information by MIS Users:

  • Unauthorized Access/Disclosure: Accessing confidential data (e.g., employee salaries, customer records, trade secrets) without permission or sharing it with unauthorized parties.
  • Data Manipulation/Falsification: Altering or falsifying data for personal gain or to mislead others (e.g., inflating sales figures, changing records).
  • Misuse of Resources: Using company information systems for personal activities (e.g., excessive personal web browsing, running a side business) or in ways that violate company policy.
  • Software Piracy/IP Theft: Illegally copying software, using unlicensed software, or stealing proprietary code or designs developed using company resources.

(b) How MIS Eases Organizational Functions:

  • (i) Planning: MIS provides access to historical data, forecasting tools, and simulation models, enabling managers to analyze trends, evaluate different scenarios, and develop more informed and realistic plans.
  • (ii) Coordination: By providing shared databases, communication platforms (email, messaging), and workflow systems, MIS facilitates information sharing and coordination of activities across different departments, functions, and locations.

(c) Reasons Manufacturing Management Relies on MIS for Strategic Needs:

  • Market Analysis and Forecasting: MIS can integrate sales data with external market trends, competitor information, and economic indicators to help management identify strategic opportunities, threats, and forecast demand for long-term planning.
  • Performance Monitoring (KPIs): Provides high-level dashboards and reports tracking key performance indicators (KPIs) related to production efficiency, quality, costs, and profitability, enabling strategic assessment of business health.
  • Resource Optimization/Capacity Planning: Analyzing production data, resource utilization, and supply chain information helps management make strategic decisions about investments in new equipment, facility expansion, or optimizing the overall production network.

(d) Challenges in Making Rational Decisions:

  • Bounded Rationality/Cognitive Limits: Managers have limited time, information processing capabilities, and cognitive biases, preventing them from identifying and evaluating all possible alternatives perfectly. They often 'satisfice' (choose the first good-enough option) rather than optimize.
  • Information Imperfections: Decisions are often based on incomplete, inaccurate, or ambiguous information about the problem, alternatives, and future outcomes, increasing uncertainty.
  • Organizational Politics/Conflicting Goals: Decisions can be influenced by political maneuvering, differing departmental goals, or personal agendas rather than purely rational analysis of what's best for the organization as a whole.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four causes of errors on information in a management information system during project implementation. (4 marks)

(b) Explain a circumstance under which each of the following methods could be used during information system acquisition:

  • (i) in-house; (2 marks)
  • (ii) off-the-shelf. (2 marks)

(c) Managers operate in external environments of an organization which have direct impact on the management process. Explain three such external environments that could be considered in the management information system design. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Causes of Information Errors During MIS Implementation:

  • Data Entry Errors: Mistakes made by humans when keying data into the system (typos, incorrect values).
  • Data Conversion Errors: Errors occurring when migrating data from old systems to the new MIS, due to incompatible formats, data loss, or incorrect mapping.
  • System Bugs/Logic Errors: Flaws in the system's programming that cause incorrect calculations, processing, or data manipulation.
  • Lack of Training/Understanding: Users not understanding how to use the system correctly or the meaning of data fields, leading to incorrect inputs or interpretations.

(b) Information System Acquisition Methods Circumstances:

  • (i) In-house Development: Used when the organization has unique requirements that off-the-shelf software cannot meet, possesses the necessary technical expertise and resources, and requires tight control over the development process and intellectual property.
  • (ii) Off-the-shelf Purchase: Used when standard software packages meet most of the organization's needs, when a quick solution is required, when internal expertise is lacking, or when cost is a major factor (often cheaper initially than custom development).

(c) External Environments Considered in MIS Design:

  • Customers: MIS design needs to consider customer expectations regarding service, accessibility (e.g., online portals, mobile access), data privacy, and communication channels. Systems might need to integrate with CRM or e-commerce platforms.
  • Competitors: Understanding competitors' use of technology and information systems can influence MIS design choices to maintain or gain competitive advantage (e.g., matching online features, improving efficiency).
  • Regulatory Environment (Government): MIS must be designed to comply with relevant laws and regulations regarding data privacy (e.g., GDPR, CCPA), financial reporting standards, industry-specific rules, and data security requirements.

(d) The Decision Making Cycle:

The decision-making cycle is a sequence of steps managers typically follow when making decisions. While models vary slightly, a common representation includes:

  1. Intelligence: Identifying and defining the problem or opportunity requiring a decision. Gathering data about the situation.
  2. Design: Developing and analyzing possible alternative courses of action or solutions to the problem.
  3. Choice: Selecting the most suitable alternative based on analysis and criteria.
  4. Implementation: Putting the chosen solution into action and communicating it to those affected.
  5. Monitoring/Feedback: Evaluating the results of the decision and implementation, gathering feedback, and making adjustments as needed. This feedback often loops back to the Intelligence phase for future decisions.
