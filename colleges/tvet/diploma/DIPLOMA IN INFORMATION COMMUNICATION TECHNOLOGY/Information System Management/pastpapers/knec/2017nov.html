MODULE III

MANAGEMENT INFORMATION SYSTEMS

INSTRUCTIONS TO CANDIDATES

This paper consists of EIGHT questions.

Answer any FIVE of the EIGHT questions in the answer booklet provided.

All questions carry equal marks.

Candidates should answer the questions in English.

Question 1

(a) Distinguish between a master file and a transaction file as used in computer systems. (4 marks)

(b) Explain three advantages of using database management systems in an organization. (6 marks)

(c) Describe each of the following as applied in project management:

  • (i) Optimistic time;
  • (ii) Most likely time;
  • (iii) Pessimistic time. (6 marks)

(d) PERT is used to review the progress of projects during project management. Explain two limitations of this technique. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Master File vs. Transaction File:
  • Master File: Contains relatively permanent or semi-permanent data about key entities (e.g., customers, products, employees). It's updated periodically by transaction files. Example: A customer master file holds customer names, addresses, credit limits.
  • Transaction File: Contains data about individual events or business activities that occur over a period. It's used to update master files. Example: A sales transaction file holds details of each sale (customer ID, product sold, quantity, date).

Essentially, master files store the current state, while transaction files record the changes to that state.

(b) Advantages of Database Management Systems (DBMS):
  1. Reduced Data Redundancy: Centralized data storage minimizes duplication of data across different applications, improving consistency.
  2. Improved Data Integrity & Consistency: DBMS enforces data constraints and rules, ensuring data accuracy and reliability.
  3. Enhanced Data Security: Provides mechanisms for access control, user authentication, and backups to protect data.
  4. Data Independence: Separates data storage from application programs, allowing changes to data structure without modifying applications extensively.
  5. Improved Data Sharing & Access: Allows multiple users and applications to access and share data concurrently in a controlled manner.
(c) PERT Time Estimates:
  • (i) Optimistic Time (to): The minimum possible time required to accomplish an activity, assuming everything proceeds exceptionally well.
  • (ii) Most Likely Time (tm): The most realistic time required to accomplish an activity under normal conditions.
  • (iii) Pessimistic Time (tp): The maximum possible time required to accomplish an activity, assuming significant delays or problems occur.

These are used to calculate the Expected Time (te) = (to + 4tm + tp) / 6.

(d) Limitations of PERT Technique:
  1. Subjectivity of Time Estimates: The accuracy of PERT relies heavily on the subjective estimates (optimistic, most likely, pessimistic) provided, which can be biased or inaccurate.
  2. Complexity: Can become complex and unwieldy for very large projects with numerous activities and dependencies.
  3. Focus on Time, Not Cost: PERT primarily focuses on time scheduling and may not adequately integrate cost considerations.
  4. Assumption of Unlimited Resources: The basic PERT model often assumes resources are readily available, which may not be realistic.

Question 2

(a) Define the term early warning mechanism as used in management information systems. (2 marks)

(b) The questionnaire is a method used for data collection. Explain three disadvantages of using this method during data collection. (6 marks)

(c) Dorothy intends to design user documentation for the program she developed. Explain three qualities of such a documentation that she should consider. (6 marks)

(d) Most organizations require their new employees to be trained on the use of management information systems as part of employee orientation. Explain three reasons for this type of training. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Early Warning Mechanism:

An early warning mechanism within an MIS is a feature or process designed to automatically detect and alert managers to potential problems, critical deviations from plans, or emerging risks based on predefined rules or thresholds applied to monitored data. This allows for timely intervention before the issue escalates significantly.

(b) Disadvantages of Using Questionnaires:
  1. Low Response Rate: Often, a significant portion of questionnaires sent out are not returned, leading to potentially non-representative data.
  2. Misinterpretation: Respondents may misunderstand questions due to wording or lack of context, and there is no opportunity for the researcher to clarify.
  3. Inflexibility: Once distributed, the questions cannot be changed or adapted based on respondent answers or emerging themes.
  4. Superficiality/Lack of Depth: Closed-ended questions may not capture the complexity of opinions, and open-ended questions may receive brief or incomplete answers.
  5. Potential for Dishonesty/Bias: Respondents might provide socially desirable answers or deliberately inaccurate information.
(c) Qualities of Good User Documentation:
  1. Clarity and Simplicity: Written in clear, concise language appropriate for the target user, avoiding technical jargon where possible.
  2. Accuracy and Completeness: Provides correct information covering all necessary aspects of the program's features, installation, usage, and troubleshooting.
  3. Accessibility and Organization: Well-structured with a logical flow, clear headings, table of contents, index, and possibly search functionality (if digital) for easy navigation and information retrieval.
  4. Task-Oriented: Focuses on helping users accomplish specific tasks with the software, often using step-by-step instructions and examples.
(d) Reasons for Training New Employees on MIS:
  1. Ensure Correct System Usage: To teach employees the proper procedures for using the system to perform their job tasks accurately and efficiently.
  2. Maintain Data Integrity & Security: To instill correct data entry practices and adherence to security protocols, protecting data quality and preventing unauthorized access.
  3. Maximize Productivity & System Benefits: To enable employees to fully utilize the system's features relevant to their role, thereby maximizing the organization's return on the system investment.
  4. Reduce Errors & Support Needs: Proper training minimizes user errors, reducing the burden on IT support staff and preventing costly mistakes.

Question 3

(a) Define the term milestone as used in project management. (2 marks)

(b) Explain a circumstance that would necessitate a systems analyst to recommend each of the following types of system maintenance:

  • (i) corrective maintenance;
  • (ii) perfective maintenance;
  • (iii) adaptive maintenance. (6 marks)

(c) Most organizations have their management information systems connected to a Local Area Network. Explain three benefits such organizations are likely to enjoy. (6 marks)

(d) Capita Company intends to use the Parallel changeover method to implement its new information system.

  • (i) Explain two advantages of this method to the company.
  • (ii) Explain one disadvantage of using this method. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Milestone (Project Management):

A milestone is a significant point or event in a project schedule, representing the completion of a major phase, deliverable, or a set of related tasks. It serves as a marker to track progress, has zero duration, and often triggers reviews or payments.

(b) Circumstances for Different Maintenance Types:
  • (i) Corrective Maintenance: Required when a system defect, bug, or error is identified that causes incorrect processing, system failure, or inaccurate output. Example: Fixing a software crash that occurs under specific conditions.
  • (ii) Perfective Maintenance: Undertaken to enhance system performance, usability, or maintainability, even if the system is functioning correctly according to specifications. Example: Optimizing database queries for faster report generation or improving the user interface design based on user feedback.
  • (iii) Adaptive Maintenance: Necessary when changes in the system's external environment require modifications. Example: Updating the system to comply with new government tax regulations, adapting it to run on a new operating system version, or modifying it to interface with a newly acquired system.
(c) Benefits of Connecting MIS to a LAN:
  1. Resource Sharing: Enables sharing of expensive hardware (printers, scanners), software licenses, and centralized data storage, reducing costs and improving resource utilization.
  2. Improved Communication & Collaboration: Facilitates faster and easier communication (email, messaging) and collaboration among users through shared access to files and information.
  3. Centralized Data Management: Allows data to be stored centrally, improving data integrity, consistency, security, and simplifying backup procedures.
(d) Parallel Changeover Method (Capita Company):
  • (i) Advantages:
    1. Lower Risk / Safety Net: The old system continues to operate alongside the new one, providing a reliable fallback if the new system fails or encounters significant problems, thus minimizing disruption to operations.
    2. Verification & Comparison: Allows outputs from both systems to be compared directly, helping to verify the accuracy and completeness of the new system before fully committing to it.
  • (ii) Disadvantage:
    1. High Cost & Effort: Running two systems concurrently requires double the resources (staff time for potentially double data entry, hardware resources, operational costs), making it the most expensive changeover method.

Question 4

(a) Outline four ways in which a Transaction Processing System improves operation in an organization. (4 marks)

(b) Managers rely on decision support systems to assist them in decision making. Describe three components of this system. (6 marks)

(c) Implementation of information systems in organizations may sometimes fail. Explain three measures that could be taken to prevent such failures. (6 marks)

(d) Management information systems could be classified according to the type of support provided. Describe this type of classification. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) How Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) Improve Operations:
  1. Automation of Routine Tasks: Handles high volumes of repetitive transactions (e.g., order entry, billing, payroll) efficiently, reducing manual labor and associated costs.
  2. Improved Accuracy & Consistency: Standardizes data entry and processing, reducing human errors common in manual systems.
  3. Real-time Data Capture & Availability: Records transactions as they happen, providing up-to-date operational data for monitoring and decision-making.
  4. Enhanced Speed & Efficiency: Speeds up the processing cycle for key business events like order fulfillment or payment processing.
(b) Components of a Decision Support System (DSS):
  1. Database: Contains the data relevant to the decision-making context, often extracted from TPS, MIS, and external sources.
  2. Model Base: A collection of quantitative models (statistical, optimization, simulation, financial) that users can apply to analyze data and evaluate alternatives.
  3. User Interface (Dialog Management): Allows the decision-maker to interact with the DSS, input parameters, select data and models, run analyses, and view results in an understandable format (e.g., graphs, reports).
(c) Measures to Prevent IS Implementation Failures:
  1. Secure Top Management Support: Ensure visible commitment, funding, and leadership from senior executives throughout the project.
  2. Ensure User Involvement & Participation: Actively involve end-users in requirements definition, design reviews, testing, and training to foster ownership and ensure the system meets real needs.
  3. Effective Change Management & Communication: Proactively manage the human side of change by communicating benefits, addressing concerns, providing adequate training, and managing resistance.
  4. Realistic Project Planning & Management: Define clear scope, realistic timelines and budgets, break down the project into manageable phases, and use robust project management methodologies.
(d) Classification of MIS by Type of Support Provided:

Information systems are often classified based on the organizational level and type of decision-making they support:

  • Operational Level Systems: Support operational managers by keeping track of elementary activities and transactions (e.g., TPS).
  • Knowledge Level Systems: Support knowledge and data workers (e.g., OAS, KWS).
  • Management Level Systems: Support middle managers for monitoring, controlling, decision-making, and administrative activities (e.g., MIS, DSS).
  • Strategic Level Systems: Support senior management with long-term strategic planning and non-routine decision making (e.g., ESS/EIS).

Question 5

(a) Outline four features of an executive information system. (4 marks)

(b) Describe each of the following types of systems:

  • (i) office automation system;
  • (ii) GIS;
  • (iii) virtual reality. (6 marks)

(c) Distinguish between structured and unstructured decisions. (4 marks)

(d) Debris Company has installed a biometric system to control access to its offices. Explain three examples of biometric data that could be used in such a system. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Features of an Executive Information System (EIS) / Executive Support System (ESS):
  1. High-Level Summary Information: Presents summarized data from internal and external sources, focusing on key performance indicators (KPIs) and critical success factors (CSFs).
  2. Graphical User Interface (GUI): Employs user-friendly interfaces, often with dashboards, charts, and graphs for easy visualization of trends and patterns.
  3. Drill-Down Capabilities: Allows executives to explore summary data in greater detail, moving from high-level overviews to more specific information.
  4. Access to External Data: Integrates external data sources (e.g., market trends, competitor information, economic indicators) alongside internal data.
  5. Customizable & User-Friendly: Tailored to the specific needs and preferences of senior executives, requiring minimal technical expertise to operate.
(b) System Descriptions:
  • (i) Office Automation System (OAS): Systems designed to increase the productivity of data workers and office staff by supporting administrative and clerical tasks. Examples include word processing, spreadsheets, email, desktop publishing, and scheduling software.
  • (ii) Geographic Information System (GIS): A system designed to capture, store, manipulate, analyze, manage, and present spatial or geographic data. It links location data with descriptive information (e.g., demographics, infrastructure) for mapping and analysis.
  • (iii) Virtual Reality (VR): Computer-generated simulation of a three-dimensional image or environment that can be interacted with in a seemingly real or physical way by a person using special electronic equipment, such as a helmet with a screen inside or gloves fitted with sensors.
(c) Structured vs. Unstructured Decisions:
  • Structured Decisions: Repetitive and routine decisions for which definite procedures have been established. They involve clear objectives, known inputs/outputs, and well-defined solution methods. Often made at lower organizational levels. Example: Deciding whether to grant overtime pay based on established rules.
  • Unstructured Decisions: Non-routine decisions where the decision-maker must provide judgment, evaluation, and insight to solve the problem. They involve novel, complex situations with unclear objectives, alternatives, or outcomes. Typically made at senior management levels. Example: Deciding whether to enter a new market.
(d) Examples of Biometric Data for Access Control:
  1. Fingerprint Recognition: Analyzing the unique patterns of ridges and valleys on a person's fingertip.
  2. Facial Recognition: Measuring and analyzing unique facial features and contours from a digital image or video frame.
  3. Iris/Retinal Scan: Analyzing the unique patterns in the colored part of the eye (iris) or the pattern of blood vessels at the back of the eye (retina).
  4. Voice Recognition: Analyzing unique vocal characteristics like pitch, tone, and frequency patterns.

Question 6

(a) Information technology has been considered as an agent of organizational change. Explain three ways in which it has been applied to bring about this change. (6 marks)

(b) The following features describe a certain method used during the procurement process in an organization. Use them to answer the questions that follow:

  • - There is only one person or entity who can supply the goods or services being procured
  • - There is no reasonable alternative or substitute for the goods or service.
  • - There is an urgent need for the goods or services being procured
  • (i) Identify the method of procurement described by these features. (1 mark)
  • (ii) Outline three disadvantages of this method. (3 marks)

(c) Explain two reasons that would necessitate an organization to embrace cloud computing technology in their operations. (4 marks)

(d) Describe the decision making process, using the Herbert Simons’ model. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) IT as an Agent of Organizational Change:
  1. Process Automation: IT automates manual tasks (e.g., data entry, calculations, report generation), streamlining workflows, reducing errors, and potentially changing job roles.
  2. Improved Communication & Collaboration: Tools like email, intranets, video conferencing, and collaborative platforms break down geographical barriers, flatten hierarchies, and enable faster information sharing and teamwork.
  3. Enhanced Decision Making: Systems like DSS and ESS provide managers with better access to data, analytical tools, and insights, leading to more informed and data-driven decision-making processes.
  4. New Business Models: IT enables entirely new ways of doing business, such as e-commerce, online services, and digital platforms, fundamentally changing organizational structures and strategies.
(b) Procurement Method:
  • (i) Identification: Single-Source Procurement (or Sole-Source Procurement).
  • (ii) Disadvantages:
    1. Lack of Competition / Higher Costs: The absence of competition gives the supplier significant bargaining power, potentially leading to higher prices and unfavorable terms.
    2. Supplier Dependency / Risk: The organization becomes highly dependent on a single supplier, increasing vulnerability to supply disruptions or price hikes.
    3. Reduced Innovation: The supplier may have less incentive to innovate or improve service quality due to the lack of competitive pressure.
(c) Reasons to Embrace Cloud Computing:
  1. Cost Savings: Reduces the need for significant upfront investment in hardware and infrastructure (CapEx) and lowers ongoing maintenance costs, shifting to a pay-as-you-go operational expense (OpEx) model.
  2. Scalability & Flexibility: Allows organizations to easily scale computing resources (storage, processing power) up or down based on demand, providing greater flexibility.
  3. Accessibility & Collaboration: Enables users to access applications and data from anywhere with an internet connection, facilitating remote work and collaboration.
(d) Herbert Simon's Model of Decision Making:

Simon described decision-making as a three-phase process:

  1. Intelligence Phase: Identifying and understanding the problem or opportunity. Involves gathering data, scanning the environment, and recognizing that a decision is needed.
  2. Design Phase: Developing and analyzing possible alternative courses of action to solve the identified problem. Involves exploring potential solutions and evaluating their feasibility and potential outcomes.
  3. Choice Phase: Selecting the most suitable course of action from the alternatives developed in the design phase, based on certain criteria or objectives.

(Note: Some models add an Implementation phase after Choice).

Question 7

(a) Explain two reasons for managing information resources. (4 marks)

(b) Computer users sometimes engage in unethical use of information in the course of duty. Explain three such unethical behavior. (6 marks)

(c) Distinguish between a hacker and a cracker as used in computer crime. (4 marks)

(d) With the aid of a diagram, outline the steps involved in information system planning. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Reasons for Managing Information Resources:
  1. Strategic Value: Information is a critical organizational asset that provides competitive advantage, supports decision-making, and enables operations. Managing it ensures its quality, availability, and security.
  2. Compliance & Risk Management: Organizations need to manage information to comply with legal and regulatory requirements (e.g., data privacy laws) and mitigate risks associated with data breaches or loss.
(b) Unethical Use of Information by Computer Users:
  1. Unauthorized Access/Disclosure: Accessing confidential company or customer data without permission, or sharing it with unauthorized parties.
  2. Data Tampering/Modification: Altering or deleting data inappropriately for personal gain or malicious intent.
  3. Misuse of Resources: Using company computer systems and networks for excessive personal activities, downloading illegal software, or engaging in activities that violate company policy.
  4. Plagiarism/Copyright Infringement: Using or distributing copyrighted software, documents, or media without proper licensing or attribution.
(c) Hacker vs. Cracker:
  • Hacker: Originally referred to a skilled computer expert who explores computer systems and networks, often to understand them better or test security, sometimes without malicious intent (though unauthorized access is often illegal). The term is now widely used, often incorrectly, to describe malicious actors.
  • Cracker: An individual who breaks into computer systems with malicious intent, such as to steal or destroy data, disrupt services, or commit fraud. This term more accurately describes cybercriminals.
(d) Steps in Information System Planning:

Information system planning typically involves the following steps (often iterative):

  1. Align with Corporate Strategy: Understand the overall business goals and strategies to ensure IS plans support them.
  2. Identify IS Opportunities/Needs: Analyze business processes and identify areas where information systems can provide solutions or competitive advantage.
  3. Assess Current IS Resources: Evaluate the existing hardware, software, network infrastructure, and human resources.
  4. Develop IS Architecture: Define the overall structure for the organization's information systems, including data, applications, and technology infrastructure.
  5. Create IS Project Portfolio: Prioritize and select specific IS projects to be undertaken based on strategic alignment, feasibility, and potential benefits.
  6. Develop Resource Allocation Plan: Outline the budget, staffing, and timeline for the selected IS projects.

Question 8

(a) Define the term guarantee as applied in management information systems. (2 marks)

(b) Management information systems assist many organizations in marketing their products and services. Explain three ways in which an information system can be used to achieve this. (6 marks)

(c) Computers are prone to attacks by viruses from various sources.

  • (i) outline three types of computer viruses; (3 marks)
  • (ii) outline three ways of protecting computer systems against such viruses. (3 marks)

(d) The management of a certain company has acquired a new management information system for use and some staff are resisting this move. Other than training, explain three measures the company could adopt to minimize the resistance. (6 marks)

Answer

(a) Guarantee (in MIS context):

In the context of MIS, a guarantee typically refers to a commitment or assurance, often from a vendor or service provider, regarding the performance, availability, reliability, or security of a system or service. It might be part of a Service Level Agreement (SLA) specifying uptime percentages, response times, or data recovery capabilities.

(b) How MIS Assists in Marketing:
  1. Market Analysis & Segmentation: Systems analyze customer data (demographics, purchase history) to identify market segments, trends, and customer preferences, enabling targeted marketing campaigns.
  2. Customer Relationship Management (CRM): CRM systems manage customer interactions, track sales leads, personalize communication, and improve customer service, fostering loyalty.
  3. Sales Forecasting & Planning: MIS provides data and analytical tools to forecast sales, plan marketing budgets, and evaluate the effectiveness of marketing campaigns.
  4. Product Management: Helps track product performance, gather customer feedback, and support decisions related to product development or pricing.
(c) Computer Viruses:
  • (i) Types:
    1. File Infectors: Attach themselves to executable files (.exe, .com) and activate when the program is run.
    2. Macro Viruses: Infect files created using applications that support macros (e.g., Microsoft Word, Excel), executing when the document is opened.
    3. Worms: Self-replicating malware that spreads across networks without needing to attach to an existing program, often exploiting security vulnerabilities.
    4. Trojan Horses: Disguise themselves as legitimate software but contain malicious payload that executes when the program is run.
    5. Boot Sector Viruses: Infect the master boot record (MBR) or boot sector of a hard drive or removable media, executing when the system starts up.
    *(Only three were required)*
  • (ii) Protection Methods:
    1. Install & Update Antivirus Software: Use reputable antivirus software and keep its virus definitions regularly updated to detect and remove known threats.
    2. Be Cautious with Email Attachments & Downloads: Avoid opening attachments or downloading files from unknown or suspicious sources. Scan attachments before opening.
    3. Keep Software Updated: Regularly apply security patches and updates for the operating system and applications to fix known vulnerabilities exploited by viruses.
    4. Use Firewalls: Implement network and personal firewalls to block unauthorized access and potentially malicious traffic.
    5. Regular Data Backups: Maintain regular backups of important data so it can be restored if compromised by a virus.
    *(Only three were required)*
(d) Measures to Minimize Resistance to New MIS (Other than Training):
  1. User Involvement & Participation: Involve staff in the selection, design, and testing phases of the new system. This fosters a sense of ownership and ensures the system addresses their needs.
  2. Clear Communication & Education: Clearly communicate the reasons for the change, the benefits of the new system (for both the organization and the employees), the implementation timeline, and how it will impact their roles. Address concerns openly.
  3. Top Management Support & Endorsement: Demonstrate strong and visible commitment from senior leadership regarding the importance and benefits of the new system.
  4. Phased Implementation & Support: Consider implementing the system in stages (if feasible) to reduce disruption. Provide adequate post-implementation support (help desk, champions) to assist users as they adapt.
