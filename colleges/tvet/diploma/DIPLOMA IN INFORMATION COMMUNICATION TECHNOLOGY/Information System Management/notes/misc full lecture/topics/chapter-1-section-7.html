Data Quality

üèÖ Data Quality

Data quality refers to the state of qualitative or quantitative pieces of information. There are many definitions of data quality but data is generally considered high quality if it is "fit for [its] intended uses in operations, decision making and planning".

Alternatively, data is deemed of high quality if it correctly represents the real-world construct to which it refers.

Furthermore, apart from these definitions, as data volume increases, the question of internal consistency of data becomes significant, regardless of fitness for use for any specific external purpose. People's views on data quality can often be in disagreement, even when discussing the same set of data used for the same purpose.

Data quality involves several dimensions, including:

  • üéØAccuracy: The degree to which data correctly reflects the real-world object or event being described.
  • üíØCompleteness: The proportion of stored data against the potential of "100% complete".
  • üîÑConsistency: Ensuring data is the same across different systems or reports.
  • ‚è±Ô∏èTimeliness: The degree to which data represents reality from the required point in time.
  • ‚úÖValidity: Data conforms to the syntax (format, type, range) of its definition.
  • üíéUniqueness: Nothing will be recorded more than once based upon how that thing is identified.

üîç Data Quality Audit

A data quality audit is a systematic process of examining data to assess its quality against predefined standards and metrics. It helps organizations understand the state of their data and identify areas for improvement.

The audit typically involves:

  1. 1.Defining Scope and Objectives: Determining which data sets and quality dimensions to focus on.
  2. 2.Data Profiling: Analyzing the data to understand its structure, content, and relationships.
  3. 3.Defining Quality Rules: Establishing specific rules and thresholds for each quality dimension (e.g., "Customer email must be a valid format").
  4. 4.Executing Audit: Applying the rules to the data to identify errors, inconsistencies, and gaps.
  5. 5.Reporting Findings: Summarizing the results, highlighting key issues, and quantifying the impact of poor data quality.
  6. 6.Recommending Actions: Suggesting steps for data cleansing, process improvement, and ongoing monitoring.

Regular data quality audits are crucial for maintaining reliable information systems and supporting effective decision-making.
