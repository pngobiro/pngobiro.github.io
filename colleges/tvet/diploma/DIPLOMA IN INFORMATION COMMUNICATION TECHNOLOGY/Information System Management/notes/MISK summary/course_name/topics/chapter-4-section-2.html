Decision Making Steps

üìã Drucker's Scientific Method of Decision Making

Peter Drucker first strongly advocated the scientific method of decision-making in his book 'The Practice of Management' published in 1955. The process is rational and scientific, involving several steps that need to be taken in a logical manner.

  1. Problem Identification

    Defining and identifying the managerial problem. It is rightly said that a problem well-defined is a problem half-solved.

  2. Problem Analysis

    Analyzing the problem in depth to understand its nature and implications.

  3. Alternative Development

    Developing alternative solutions to address the identified problem.

  4. Solution Selection

    Selecting the best solution out of the available alternatives.

  5. Implementation

    Converting the decision into action.

  6. Follow-up

    Ensuring feedback for follow-up and monitoring results.

üí° Important Note:

Decision-making is an iterative process, and although it is useful to separate out the various phases to discuss them, very few decisions are taken in this neat, logical sequence. There is feedback and interrelationships between decisions; there is flair, intuition, judgment and creativity involved.
