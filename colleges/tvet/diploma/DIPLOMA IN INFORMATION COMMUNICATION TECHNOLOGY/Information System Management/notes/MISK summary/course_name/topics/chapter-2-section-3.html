Information Systems and Organizational Structure

üè¢ Organizational Information Systems

üìö Information System Definition

An Information System is a composition of people, procedures and equipment that provide information to the management to be able to make appropriate decisions.

üìä Levels of Information Systems

üéØ

Strategic Level Systems

These support the long-range planning activities of the senior level management both from the environment and internally. Their main concern is maintaining changes in the external environment with the existing information and activities.

  • ‚è≥ Planning Horizon: 5-10 years depending on the project
  • üìä Decision Type: Non-structured & subject to many changes
  • üìà Source: Mostly from outside the organization and internal
‚öñÔ∏è

Management/Tactical Level Systems

They support the monitoring, controlling and decision making and administrative activities of middle level managers. These systems produce reports rather than instant information on the operations. They may focus on the less structured decisions for which information requirements aren't always clear and attempt to answer the 'what if' questions.

  • ‚è≥ Planning Horizon: Usually 3 years
  • üìä Decision Type: Semi-structured and based on managerial experience
  • üìà Source: Mixture of internal and external sources
üß†

Knowledge Level Systems

This supports knowledge and data workers in the organization. It may help businesses to integrate new knowledge and to control the flow of paper work in the organization.

‚öôÔ∏è

Operational Systems

They monitor elementary activities and transactions of the organization e.g. sales, receipts, cash deposits, payroll and flow of materials in the factory. The main purpose of these systems is to answer routine questions and track the flow of information in the organization.

  • ‚è≥ Planning Horizon: Usually a week or less, day to day
  • üìä Decision Type: Structured and hardly require human intervention
  • üìà Source: Captured from source documents

üíª Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)

These are the basic business systems serving the operational level of the organization. A TPS is a computerized system that performs and records the daily routine transactions necessary to conduct the business.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Role:

TPS are central to an organization such that if there is a failure it can bring the organization to a standstill. The information inputs for the TPS are in the form of transactions and events.

Objectives of TPS

  • üéØ To provide all information needed by law or by the organization policy
  • ‚è∞ Provide timely documents and reports
  • üìà Increase the competitiveness of the organization
  • üìä Provide necessary data for tactical and strategic systems
  • ‚úÖ To assure accuracy and integration of data and information
  • üîí To safeguard assets and security of information

Major Characteristics

  • üìä Involves large amounts of data to be processed
  • üîÑ Process information regularly (Daily or Weekly)
  • üíæ Requires higher storage capacity
  • ‚ö° Needs high processing speeds
  • üìà Monitors and collects current and past data
  • üîí Requires high level of accuracy, data integrity and security
üîë

Key Points to Remember

  • üìå

    Information systems exist at different organizational levels with varying purposes and characteristics.

  • üìå

    Strategic systems focus on long-term planning, while operational systems handle day-to-day transactions.

  • üìå

    TPS are fundamental operational systems critical to organization's daily functions.

  • üìå

    Each system level has distinct planning horizons, decision types, and information sources.
