Introduction to Probability

Probability is the ratio of the number of favorable cases to the total number of equally likely cases. It is a crucial concept in business management as it helps quantify and manage risks in various business situations.

Key Concepts:

  • Probability values range between 0 and 1
  • 0 indicates impossibility of an event
  • 1 indicates certainty of an event
  • Used to describe degree of uncertainty in business outcomes

Basic Probability Formula:

Probability (x) = Favorable outcomes/Total outcomes

Business Applications of Probability

  1. Business games of chance (e.g., Raffles, Lotteries)
  2. Insurance firms:
    • Assessing new clients
    • Evaluating property insurance risks
    • Calculating chances of insured risks occurring
  3. Business decision making:
    • Evaluating project viability
    • Assessing investment opportunities
    • Risk assessment

Example: Ball Selection Probability

A bag contains 80 balls: 20 red, 25 blue, and 35 white.

Find the probability of selecting:

  1. Red ball: 
    P(Red) = 20/80 = 1/4
  2. Black ball: 
    P(Black) = 0/80 = 0
  3. Red or Blue ball: 
    P(Red or Blue) = (20 + 25)/80 = 45/80 = 9/16

Note: In probability, "or" is represented by addition (+) of probabilities

Common Terms in Probability

Events

An event is a possible outcome of an experiment, a result of a trial, or an observation.

Mutually Exclusive Events

A set of events is said to be mutually exclusive if the occurrence of any one event precludes the occurrence of any other event in the set.

Example:

When tossing a coin, getting heads and tails are mutually exclusive events - if you get heads, you cannot get tails in the same toss.

Venn Diagram Representation:

For mutually exclusive events:

  • Events are completely separate
  • No overlap between events
  • E₁ ∩ E₂ = Ø (empty set)

Non-Mutually Exclusive Events (Independent Events)

Example:

In a voter survey, being a "KANU supporter" and being a "woman" are not mutually exclusive - a person can be both.

Venn Diagram Representation:

For non-mutually exclusive events:

  • Events can overlap
  • Same outcome can belong to multiple events
  • E₁ ∩ E₂ ≠ Ø (intersection exists)

Independent Events

Events are considered independent when the occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of other events occurring.

Example:

The outcome of tossing a coin is independent of the outcome of rolling a die - getting heads on the coin toss doesn't affect what number you'll get on the die.

Important Points:
  • Events can be independent but not mutually exclusive
  • Independence refers to probability relationship
  • Used in calculating compound probabilities

Example: Playing Cards Probability

Using a standard deck of playing cards to demonstrate different types of events.

Calculate the probability of:

1. Picking either a Diamond or a Heart (Mutually Exclusive Events)

P(Diamond or Heart) = P(Diamond) + P(Heart)

= 13/52 + 13/52
= 26/52
= 0.5

Note: Simple addition as events are mutually exclusive

2. Picking either a Flower or an Ace (Independent Events)

P(Flower or Ace) = P(Flower) + P(Ace) - P(Flower and Ace)

= 13/52 + 4/52 - 1/52
= 16/52 - 1/52
= 15/52
≈ 0.31

Note: Must subtract intersection for independent events

Schools of Thought in Probability

1. Classical Approach

  • Assumes all possible outcomes are mutually exclusive and equally likely
  • Formula: P(E) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes
  • Also known as "prior probability" approach

2. Relative Frequency (Empirical) Approach

Based on actual observations and experimental data rather than theoretical assumptions.

3. Axiomatic Approach

  • Attempts to address inadequacies in classical and empirical approaches
  • Provides mathematical foundation for probability theory

4. Personalistic Approach

  • Based on subjective assessment
  • Probability reflects individual's degree of confidence
  • Considers available evidence and personal judgment

Rules of Probability

Addition Rule

Used to calculate the probability of two or more mutually exclusive events occurring.

Example: Rolling a Die

What is the probability of throwing either a 3 or a 6 with a die?

P(throwing a 3 or a 6) = P(3) + P(6)

= 1/6 + 1/6
= 2/6
= 1/3

Multiplication Rule

Used for calculating probability of a string of independent events occurring together.

Example: Two Dice Throws

What is the probability of throwing a 3 and then a 6 with two throws of a die?

P(throwing a 3 and 6) = P(3) × P(6)

= 1/6 × 1/6
= 1/36
Important Notes:
  • In probability, 'and' is represented by multiplication (×)
  • P(x) and P(y) ≠ P(x and y) when order matters
  • For unordered events: P(3 and 6) = P(3 followed by 6) + P(6 followed by 3) 
    = 1/36 + 1/36 = 1/18

Conditional Probability

The probability of an event occurring, given that another event has already occurred.

Notation:

P(x|y) = Probability of x given that y has already occurred

This concept is particularly useful when events are not independent and the occurrence of one event affects the probability of another.

Formula:
P(x|y) = P(x∩y)/P(y)
Example: Competitive Examination

From 600 candidates:

  • 100 will be called for interview
  • 30 candidates to be selected finally

Let:

  • Event A = called for interview
  • Event B = selected
  1. Probability of being called for interview: 
    P(A) = 100/600 = 1/6
  2. Probability of selection if called for interview: 
    P(B|A) = 30/100 = 3/10
  3. Probability of being called and selected: 
    P(AB) = P(A) × P(B|A)
      = 1/6 × 3/10
      = 3/60
      = 1/20
Example: Machine Setup

A machine has following probabilities:

  • 90% chance of correct setup
  • If setup correctly: 95% chance of good parts
  • If setup incorrectly: 30% chance of good parts
Probability Tree:
CS (0.9) ─┬─ GP (0.95) → CS∩GP
          └─ BP (0.05) → CS∩BP

IS (0.1) ─┬─ GP (0.3)  → IS∩GP
          └─ BP (0.7)  → IS∩BP

Where:
CS = Correct Setting
IS = Incorrect Setting
GP = Good Product
BP = Bad Product
