Techniques of Counting

Permutations

This is an ordered arrangement of items in which the order must be strictly observed.

Example

Let x, y and z be any three items. Arrange these in all possible permutations:

1st 2nd 3rd
X Y Z
X Z Y
Y X Z
Y Z X
Z Y X
Z X Y

Six different permutations

NB: The above 6 permutations are the maximum one can ever obtain in a situation where there are only 3 items but if the number of items exceeds 3 then determining the number of permutations by outlining as done above may be cumbersome. Therefore we use a special formula to determine such permutations.

Formula for Permutations

The number of permutations of 'r' items taken from a sample of 'n' items is given by:

nPr = n!/(n-r)!

where ! = factorial and 0! = 1

Examples Using the Formula

Example 1

3P3 = 3!/(3-3)!
    = (3 √ó 2 √ó 1)/(0)!
    = 6/1
    = 6

Example 2

5P3 = 5!/(5-3)!
    = (5 √ó 4 √ó 3 √ó 2 √ó 1)/(2 √ó 1)
    = 60

Example 3

7P5 = 7!/(7-5)!
    = (7 √ó 6 √ó 5 √ó 4 √ó 3 √ó 2 √ó 1)/(2 √ó 1)
    = 5040/2
    = 2520

Real-World Example

There are 6 contestants for the post of chairman, secretary, and treasurer. These positions can be filled by any of the 6. Find the possible number of ways in which the 3 positions may be filled.

Solution

For each position:

  • Chairman: 6 choices
  • Secretary: 5 choices (after chairman is selected)
  • Treasurer: 4 choices (after chairman and secretary are selected)

Using the formula:

6P3 = 6!/(6-3)!
    = (6 √ó 5 √ó 4 √ó 3 √ó 2 √ó 1)/(3 √ó 2 √ó 1)
    = 720/6
    = 120

Therefore, the three positions can be filled in 120 different ways.

Key Points

  • A permutation is an ordered arrangement of items
  • The order matters in permutations
  • For 3 items, there are 6 possible permutations
  • For larger sets, a formula is needed to calculate the number of permutations

Combinations

A combination is a group of items in which order is not important. For a combination to hold at any given time it must comprise of the same items but if a new item is added to the group or removed from the group then we have a new combination.

For example: 3 items x, y and z will have 6 different permutations but only one combination.

Formula for Combinations

The following formula is used to determine the number of combinations in a given situation:

nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!)

Examples Using the Formula

Example 1

8C7 = 8!/(7!(8-7)!)
    = 8!/(7! √ó 1!)
    = (8 √ó 7!)/(7! √ó 1)
    = 8

Example 2

6C4 = 6!/(4!(6-4)!)
    = 6!/(4! √ó 2!)
    = (6 √ó 5 √ó 4!)/(4! √ó 2 √ó 1)
    = 15

Example 3

8C3 = 8!/(3!(8-3)!)
    = 8!/(3! √ó 5!)
    = (8 √ó 7 √ó 6 √ó 5!)/(3 √ó 2 √ó 1 √ó 5!)
    = 56

Real-World Application

There is a committee to be selected comprising of 5 people from a group of 5 men and 6 women. If the selection is randomly done, find the possibility of having three men and two women.

Solution
  • Committee size = 5 people
  • Total group size = 5 men + 6 women = 11 people
  • Need to select 3 men from 5 men: 5C3
  • And 2 women from 6 women: 6C2

Formula and Solution:

P(committee with 3m and 2w) = (5C3 √ó 6C2)/11C5
                            = [(5!/(3!2!)) √ó (6!/(4!2!))] / [11!/(5!6!)]
                            = (10 √ó 15)/462
                            = 27/77

Additional Committee Questions

i) Probability of having at least one man and one woman

We can solve this using:

P(at least one man and one woman) = 1 - [P(no man) + P(no woman)]

P(committee of five men only) = 5C5/11C5 = 1/462
P(committee of five women only) = 6C5/11C5 = 6/462 = 1/77

P(at least one man and one woman) = 1 - (1/462 + 1/77)
                                  = 1 - (1 + 6)/462
                                  = 1 - 7/462
                                  = 455/462
ii) Probability when one particular man and one particular woman must not be in the committee

Given:

  • Original group size = 5m + 6w
  • Committee size = 5 people
  • Actual group size for selection = 4m + 5w (after excluding specified man and woman)
  • Committee composition = 1m + 4w

Solution:

Total possible selections = 9C5

One man can be selected in 4C1 ways
Four women can be selected in 5C4 ways

P(committee of 4w1m) = (5C4 √ó 4C1)/9C5
                     = [(5!/(4!1!)) √ó (4!/(1!3!))] / [9!/(4!5!)]
                     = (5 √ó 4)/(9 √ó 8 √ó 7 √ó 6 √ó 5/120)
                     = 10/63

Summary of Counting Techniques

Permutations Combinations
Order matters Order does not matter
Formula: nPr = n!/(n-r)! Formula: nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!)
Used when arrangement is important Used when selection is important

Key Points

  • In combinations, order does not matter (unlike permutations)
  • The formula nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!) is used to calculate combinations
  • Combinations are useful in solving real-world selection problems
  • Complex probability problems can be solved using combinations
