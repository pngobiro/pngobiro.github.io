Data Collection

Data Collection and Presentation

Specific Objectives

At the end of this topic, the trainee should be able to:

  • Discuss the basic consideration for data collection
  • Classify collected data into various categories
  • Tabulate collected data
  • Diagrammatically and graphically present data

Introduction to Statistics

Statistics viewed as a subject is a process of:

  • Collecting
  • Tabulating
  • Analyzing numerical data

upon which significant conclusions are drawn.

Multiple Views of Statistics

  1. As numerical data collected from a given source for a particular purpose:
    • Population statistics from the ministry of planning
    • Agricultural statistics from the ministry of Agriculture
  2. As values obtained from statistical calculations:
    • Mean
    • Mode
    • Range
    • etc.

Applications of Statistics

1. Quality Control

Quality control departments in industries use statistical methods to monitor and maintain product quality. For example:

  • Kenya Bureau of Standards (KeBS) inspects products to ensure they meet customer specifications
  • Control departments develop and use quality control charts to check product standards

2. Economic Order Quantities (EOQ)

Statistics helps business managers determine optimal order quantities by considering:

  • Storage costs for large quantities
  • Capital tied up in inventory
  • Risk of stock shortages with small orders
  • Customer satisfaction levels

EOQ helps balance storage costs with customer service by determining the right quantity to order for a specific period.

3. Forecasting

Business managers use statistics for predicting business futures by:

  • Analyzing relationships between dependent and independent variables
  • Developing predictive equations
  • Making informed business decisions based on data

4. Human Resource Management

Statistics aids in HR management through:

  • Employee questionnaires to identify management weaknesses
  • Analysis of resignation patterns and causes
  • Evaluation of whether departures are due to frustration or choice

Basics of Data Collection

Stages of Statistical Investigation

  1. Definition of the problem or issue
  2. Collection of relevant data
  3. Classification and analysis of the collected data
  4. Presentation of the results

Careful planning is essential before beginning data collection. Various factors must be considered during the preliminary stage of a statistical investigation.

Preliminary Considerations

1. Exact Definition of the Problem

This is necessary to:

  • Ensure nothing important is omitted from the enquiry
  • Avoid wasting effort collecting irrelevant data
  • Specify general problems precisely before work begins

2. Definition of Units

Important because:

  • Results must appear in comparable units for valid analysis
  • Data must be in same units for comparisons
  • Different units (e.g., numbers, weights, batches) can cause confusion

3. Scope of the Enquiry

Define the field to be covered:

  • Which departments are included?
  • Is it limited to our business or includes others?
  • What are the boundaries of the investigation?

4. Accuracy of the Data

Consider:

  • Degree of accuracy in recording (e.g., ages to nearest year or month)
  • Accuracy of measuring instruments
  • Relationship between precision and sample size

Data Sources and Types

Data constitute the foundation of statistical analysis and interpretation. Data can be obtained from three sources:

  1. Primary source
  2. Secondary source
  3. Internal records

Primary Data

Primary data are measurements, observations, and records that are part of an original study. These are raw data collected specifically to solve the problem at hand and must be classified and processed using appropriate statistical methods.

Methods of Obtaining Primary Data:

  • Questionnaires
  • Observation
  • Interviews
  • Sampling

Secondary Data

Secondary data can be obtained from:

  • Journals
  • Reports
  • Government publications
  • Research organization publications
  • Trade and professional bodies

Important Considerations

Before using secondary data, examine:

  1. Whether the data are suitable for the purpose of investigation
  2. Whether the data are adequate for the purpose of investigation
  3. Whether the data are reliable

Internal Data

Internal data refers to measurements that are by-products of routine business record keeping, such as:

  • Accounting
  • Finance
  • Production personnel
  • Quality control
  • Sales
  • R&D

Since internal data originates within the business, collecting the desired information is usually straightforward. The procedure depends largely upon the nature of the facts being collected and their existing form.

Data Collection Methods and Limitations

a. Questionnaire

A formal list of questions designed to collect data by asking people who are thought to have the desired information. The respondent fills in the form themselves.

b. Observation

The investigator directly observes the object or action of interest. This can be done by:

  • Individual observation
  • Mechanical device observation

Note: Observation method does not automatically produce accurate data. Physical difficulties in the observation situation or observer limitations may result in errors.

Classification of Data

Classification is grouping of related facts into different classes. Facts in one class differ from another class with respect to some characteristics called a basis of classification.

Cross-classification refers to sorting facts on one basis of classification and then another basis.

Rules of Classification

  1. The number of classes should preferably be between 5 and 15
  2. As far as possible, avoid odd values of class intervals
  3. The starting point (lower limit of first class) should be zero or 5 or multiple of 5
  4. To ensure continuity and correct interval, adopt exclusive method of classification
  5. Whenever possible, all classes should be of the same size

Types of Classification

1. Geographical Classification

Data are classified on the basis of geographical or location differences between various items.

2. Chronological Classification

When data are observed over a period of time, this type of classification is used.

3. Qualitative Classification

Data are classified based on attributes or qualities such as:

  • Sex
  • Color of hair
  • Literacy
  • Religion

4. Quantitative Classification

Classification based on numerical values or quantities.

Classification Functions

Classification of data is similar to sorting letters in a post office. The process:

  • Gives prominence to important information
  • Drops unnecessary details
  • Facilitates comparison
  • Enables statistical treatment of collected material

Limitations of Statistics

Despite the usefulness of statistics in many fields, it should not be viewed as a magical device that always provides correct solutions. Important limitations include:

  • Statistics does not deal with isolated measurements
  • Statistics deals only with quantitative characteristics
    • Qualitative characteristics like honesty, efficiency, intelligence cannot be studied directly
  • Statistical results are true only on average
  • Statistics is only a means, not an end
  • Statistics can be misused

Formation of Frequency Distribution

In frequency distribution, we count the number of times a particular value is repeated (called the frequency). To facilitate counting:

  1. Prepare a column of "tally" marks
  2. List all possible values of variables from lowest to highest
  3. Put a bar (vertical line) opposite each value
  4. Group tally marks in blocks of five for easier counting

Classification According to Class Intervals

Important technical terms when data are classified according to class intervals:

a) Class Limits

The lowest and highest values that can be included in the class. For example, in class 20-40:

  • Lower limit: 20
  • Upper limit: 40

The lower boundary is the value below which there can be no value in that class, while the upper limit is the value above which no value can belong to that class.

b) Class Interval

The span of a class (difference between upper and lower limits). For example:

  • Class: 20-40
  • Interval = 40 - 20 = 20

c) Class Frequency

The number of observations corresponding to a particular class. Adding all class frequencies gives the total frequency.

d) Class Mid-point

The value lying halfway between the lower and upper class limits.

Mid-point = (Upper limit + Lower limit) ÷ 2

Data Tabulation

A table is a systematic arrangement of statistical data in columns and rows where:

  • Rows are horizontal arrangements
  • Columns are vertical arrangements

Purpose of Tables

  • To simplify the presentation of data
  • To facilitate comparisons
  • To enable quick location of desired information through clear and systematic arrangement

Essential Parts of a Table

  1. Table Number: Each table should be numbered
  2. Title: Every table must have a suitable title
  3. Caption: Column headings representing what each column contains
  4. Stub: Row headings at the extreme left, designating the contents of each row
  5. Body: Contains the numerical information arranged according to captions and stubs
  6. Headnote: Brief explanatory statement placed below the title in brackets
  7. Footnote: Additional notes placed directly below the body of the table

Diagrammatic and Graphical Presentation

Rules for Construction of Diagrams

  1. There must be a title for the diagram
  2. Proper proportion between width and height should be maintained
  3. The scale selected should be appropriate
  4. Footnotes should be given at the bottom of the diagram when necessary
  5. Diagram should be absolutely neat and clean
  6. Include an index illustrating different types of lines or shades for easy interpretation
  7. Diagram should be as simple as possible

Types of Diagrams

  • One dimensional diagrams (e.g., bar diagrams)
  • Two dimensional diagrams (e.g., squares)
  • Pictograms and cartograms

One Dimensional Diagrams (Bar Diagrams)

Bar diagrams are the most common type, where a bar is a thick line whose width is shown merely for attention. They are called one-dimensional because only the length matters, not the width.

Merits of Bar Diagrams
  • Readily understood even by those unaccustomed to reading charts
  • Simplest and easiest to make
  • Most effective when comparing large number of observations
Points to Consider When Constructing Bar Diagrams
  • Width of bars should be uniform throughout
  • Gap between bars should be uniform
  • Vertical bars are preferred as they:
    • Give better visual appearance
    • Facilitate comparison
  • Write respective figures at the end of each bar for precise value reference
Types of Bar Diagrams
a) Simple Bar Charts

Used to represent only one variable or show totals.

Limitation: Can present only one classification or category of data. For example, while showing population for five decades, it can only show total population, not sex-wise distribution.

b) Component Bar Charts

Used to represent various parts of the total. Various components in each bar should be clearly distinguished.

Best Practices:

  • Keep components in the same order across bars
  • Arrange components from largest (at base) to smallest (at top)
  • Use different shades or colors to distinguish components
  • Show both component totals and overall totals
c) Multiple Bar Charts

Used to represent two or more sets of interrelated data. Similar to simple bar charts, but:

  • Multiple phenomena are represented together
  • Different shades, colors, dots, or patterns distinguish between bars
  • Usually shows only component totals
d) Percentage Component Bar Charts

Particularly useful for showing relative changes in data:

  • All bars have equal length (100%)
  • Segments represent components as percentages of the total

Two Dimensional Diagrams

Unlike one-dimensional diagrams, these consider both length and width of the bars. The area of the bar represents the given data. Also known as surface diagrams.

Examples include:
  • Rectangles
  • Squares
  • Circles

Pie Charts or Diagrams

Shows the partitioning of a total into component parts.

Steps in Constructing a Pie Chart:
  1. Calculate percentage for each component using:

    Component angle = (Component value ÷ Total) × 360°

  2. Draw a circle of appropriate size with a compass
  3. Measure points on the circle representing each component using a protractor

Data Presentation

After data collection, the next step is to present them in a suitable form. One of the most convincing and appealing ways to present data is through charts.

Types of Charts

  • Diagrams
    • One dimensional diagrams (e.g., bar graphs)
    • Two dimensional diagrams (e.g., rectangles, squares)
    • Pictograms and cartograms (circles)
  • Graphs

Pictograms

Smoothed Frequency Curve

Important points to consider when smoothing a frequency graph:

  • Only frequency distributions based on samples should be smoothed
  • Only continuous series should be smoothed
  • The total area under the curve should equal the area under the original histogram or polygon

Cumulative Frequency Curves (Ogives)

Used to answer questions like "how many workers earn more than a certain amount?" There are two methods:

1. Less Than Method

Start with upper limits of classes and add frequencies progressively. Results in a rising curve.

2. More Than Method

Start with upper limits and subtract frequencies from total progressively. Results in a declining curve.

When frequencies are plotted using this method, we get a declining curve as values decrease with higher limits.

Graphs

A large variety of graphs are used in practice. They can be divided into the following categories:

  • Time series
  • Z-Charts
  • Scatter graphs
  • Semi-logarithmic graphs
  • Graphs of frequency distribution

Time Series Graphs

These show values of a variable at different points in time. The technique is extremely helpful in analyzing changes over time.

Time series graphs can be constructed on either:

  • Natural scale
  • Ratio scale

Graphs of One Variable

When one variable is represented, time is measured on x-axis and the variable on y-axis. Points are plotted and joined by straight lines, with fluctuations showing variations in the variable.

  • Cumulative frequency curve
