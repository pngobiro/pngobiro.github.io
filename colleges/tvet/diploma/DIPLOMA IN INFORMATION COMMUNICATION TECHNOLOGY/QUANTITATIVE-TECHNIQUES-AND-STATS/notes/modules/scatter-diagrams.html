Scatter Diagrams

Understanding Scatter Diagrams

A scatter diagram (also called dotogram or scatter graph) is the simplest device for studying correlation between two variables. It's a special type of dot chart where:

  • Points are plotted on a coordinate system
  • Points represent pairs of values (x, y)
  • Points are not connected by lines
  • The pattern of points reveals the type of relationship between variables

Types of Correlation Patterns

1. Linear Correlation

When the amount of change in one variable bears a constant ratio to the amount of change in the other variable. When plotted, points tend to fall along a straight line.

  • Perfect Positive Linear: Points form an exact upward straight line
  • Perfect Negative Linear: Points form an exact downward straight line

2. Non-Linear Correlation

When the amount of change in one variable does not bear a constant ratio to the amount of change in the other variable. Also called curvilinear correlation.

  • Points form a curved pattern
  • Relationship exists but isn't linear

Real-World Examples of Correlation Patterns

1. Perfect Positive Correlation (Sales and Profits)

When measuring the relationship between volumes of sales and profits in a company:

  • Points fall exactly on an upward straight line
  • As company sales increase, profits increase proportionally
  • Can be represented by a single line graph

2. Perfect Negative Correlation (Price and Sales Volume)

When examining the relationship between price and quantity sold:

  • Points fall exactly on a downward straight line
  • As price increases, sales volume decreases proportionally
  • Example: The cheaper the goods, the bigger the sales

Price Range: 10 → 20

Effect: Sales quantity decreases as price increases

3. High Positive Correlation

A more realistic business scenario where:

  • Points closely follow an upward pattern
  • Some scatter exists around the trend line
  • Strong but not perfect relationship

4. High Negative Correlation

Example showing strong inverse relationship:

  • Points follow a downward pattern
  • Some scatter around the trend line
  • Strong negative relationship but not perfect

5. No Correlation Pattern

When there's no relationship between variables:

  • Points scattered randomly
  • No clear pattern or direction
  • Example scale: x-axis (10-50), y-axis (0-600)

6. Spurious Correlations

Special case where data shows correlation but no logical relationship exists:

  • Data points may show a pattern
  • No convincing real-world relationship
  • Example: High student pass rates showing correlation with increased accidents
  • Relationship exists only in theory, not in reality
  • Also called "nonsense correlations"

Important: Always verify if correlations make logical sense in real-world context!

Interpreting Scatter Diagrams

  • Positive Correlation: As one variable increases, the other tends to increase
  • Negative Correlation: As one variable increases, the other tends to decrease
  • No Correlation: No clear pattern in the scatter of points
  • Perfect Correlation: All points lie exactly on a line
  • Strong Correlation: Points closely follow a pattern
  • Weak Correlation: Points loosely follow a pattern with much scatter
📝

Key Points

  • Scatter diagrams provide a visual representation of correlation
  • The pattern of points indicates the type and strength of relationship
  • Correlation can be linear or non-linear
  • Direction can be positive, negative, or none
