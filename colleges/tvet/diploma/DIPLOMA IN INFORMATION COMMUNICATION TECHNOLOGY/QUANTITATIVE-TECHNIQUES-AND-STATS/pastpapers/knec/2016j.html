Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Outline three methods of collecting statistical data. (3 marks)

(b) Explain each of the following scales of measurement as used in the classification of statistical data:

(i) nominal scale; (2 marks)

(ii) ordinal scale; (2 marks)

(iii) interval scale; (2 marks)

(iv) ratio scale. (2 marks)

(c) An educational researcher was interested in studying the relationship between the performance in Mathematics and English. He took a random sample of 12 students who sat for KCSE mock examination and the marks scored in the two subjects are as shown in the table below:

Student A B C D E F G H I J K L

(i) Compute the Spearman's rank co-efficient of correlation between the marks scored in the two subjects. (7 marks)

(ii) Evaluate the consistency in performance in the two subjects. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Methods of collecting statistical data:

  1. Observation: Watching and recording behavior or events as they occur.
  2. Interviews: Asking questions directly to respondents (face-to-face, phone).
  3. Questionnaires: Using a set of written questions for respondents to answer.
  4. Experimentation: Manipulating variables to observe effects.
  5. Using existing records/secondary data: Analyzing data already collected by others.

(b) Scales of measurement:

(i) Nominal scale: Categorical data without any order or ranking (e.g., gender, colors).

(ii) Ordinal scale: Categorical data with a meaningful order or rank, but differences between ranks are not quantifiable (e.g., satisfaction levels: low, medium, high).

(iii) Interval scale: Ordered data where differences between values are meaningful, but there is no true zero point (e.g., temperature in Celsius, years).

(iv) Ratio scale: Ordered data with meaningful differences and a true zero point, allowing for ratio comparisons (e.g., height, weight, income).

(a) Differentiate between the terms census and sampling as used in Statistics. (4 marks)

Fertilizer (bags) 45 40 32 36 30 50 55 30 55 60

Answer

(a) Census vs. Sampling:

  • Census: A study that collects data from every member of the population. It provides complete information but is often expensive, time-consuming, and impractical for large populations.
  • Sampling: A study that collects data from a subset (sample) of the population. It is generally faster, cheaper, and more practical than a census, but the results are estimates and subject to sampling error.

(b) Project Network Analysis Terms:

(i) Activity: A specific task or operation within a project that consumes time and resources.

(ii) Dummy Activity: An activity with zero duration and zero resource consumption, used to maintain correct precedence relationships in a network diagram (often shown as a dashed arrow).

(iii) Dangling Activity: An activity (other than the final project activity) that does not connect to any subsequent activity, potentially indicating an error in network logic.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Explain three importance of constructing a time series graph in the analysis of time series data. (6 marks)

(b)

(i) State two models used in the analysis of time series data. (2 marks)

(ii) Explain the circumstance under which each time series model is used. (4 marks)

(c) A research study conducted on the off-the-shelf software in Kenya found out that only 30% of the software procured meet the user requirements. Based on this finding, determine the probability that among a random sample of 8 off-the-shelf software selected in Kenya, the following meet the user requirements:

(i) exactly 3 software; (2 marks)

(ii) between 4 and 6 inclusive; (3 marks)

(iii) at least 3 software. (3 marks)

Answer

(a) Importance of Time Series Graphs:

  1. Trend Identification:
    • Helps visualize long-term upward or downward movements
    • Makes pattern recognition easier
  2. Seasonal Pattern Detection:
    • Shows regular fluctuations within fixed time periods
    • Helps in planning for seasonal variations
  3. Comparative Analysis:
    • Allows comparison of multiple time periods
    • Helps identify relationships between different variables over time

(b) Time Series Models:

(i) Two models:

  1. Additive Model: Y = T + S + C + R
  2. Multiplicative Model: Y = T × S × C × R

(ii) Circumstances for each model:

Additive Model is used when:

  • Seasonal variations are constant over time
  • Components are independent of each other
  • Variations are measured in absolute terms

Multiplicative Model is used when:

  • Seasonal variations increase with trend
  • Components are dependent on each other
  • Variations are measured in relative terms

(c) Probability Calculations:

Given: p = 0.3 (probability of meeting requirements)

q = 1 - p = 0.7 (probability of not meeting requirements)

n = 8 (sample size)

(i) P(X = 3):

Using Binomial Probability: P(X = r) = ⁿCᵣ p^r q^(n-r)

P(X = 3) = ⁸C₃ (0.3)³ (0.7)⁵

= 56 × 0.027 × 0.16807

= 0.2541 or 25.41%

(ii) P(4 ≤ X ≤ 6):

= P(X = 4) + P(X = 5) + P(X = 6)

P(X = 4) = ⁸C₄ (0.3)⁴ (0.7)⁴ = 70 × 0.0081 × 0.2401 = 0.1361

P(X = 5) = ⁸C₅ (0.3)⁵ (0.7)³ = 56 × 0.00243 × 0.343 = 0.0466

P(X = 6) = ⁸C₆ (0.3)⁶ (0.7)² = 28 × 0.000729 × 0.49 = 0.0100

Total = 0.1361 + 0.0466 + 0.0100 = 0.1927 or 19.27%

(iii) P(X ≥ 3):

= P(X = 3) + P(X = 4) + P(X = 5) + P(X = 6) + P(X = 7) + P(X = 8)

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Determine the second order derivative of each of the following polynomial functions with respect to x:

y = 4x⁴ - 3x³ - 5x + 8

y = 4x³ - 5x² + 6x - 4

(4 marks)

(b) Integrate each of the following polynomial functions with respect to x:

y = 5x³ - 5x² - 9x + 6

y = 2x³ - 5x² + 7x - 3

(4 marks)

(c) Research findings on computers suggest that the life span of personal computers fit a normal distribution with a mean of 4.2 years and a standard deviation of 0.8 years.

(i) Determine the proportion of personal computers that will have a life span less than 4 years. (3 marks)

(ii) Determine the proportion of personal computers that will have a life span of between 3.5 years and 5.8 years. (5 marks)

(iii) Vendors of personal computers give a warranty of 2 years. Determine the proportion of personal computers that will require replacement within this warranty period. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Second Order Derivatives:

(i) For y = 4x⁴ - 3x³ - 5x + 8:

First derivative: dy/dx = 16x³ - 9x² - 5

Second derivative: d²y/dx² = 48x² - 18x

(ii) For y = 4x³ - 5x² + 6x - 4:

First derivative: dy/dx = 12x² - 10x + 6

Second derivative: d²y/dx² = 24x - 10

(b) Integration:

(i) For y = 5x³ - 5x² - 9x + 6:

∫y dx = (5x⁴/4) - (5x³/3) - (9x²/2) + 6x + C

(ii) For y = 2x³ - 5x² + 7x - 3:

∫y dx = (x⁴/2) - (5x³/3) + (7x²/2) - 3x + C

(c) Normal Distribution Calculations:

Given: μ = 4.2 years, σ = 0.8 years

(i) P(X < 4):

Z = (X - μ)/σ = (4 - 4.2)/0.8 = -0.25

Using standard normal table:

P(Z < -0.25) = 0.4013

Therefore, 40.13% of PCs will have a life span less than 4 years.

(ii) P(3.5 < X < 5.8):

For X = 3.5: Z₁ = (3.5 - 4.2)/0.8 = -0.875

For X = 5.8: Z₂ = (5.8 - 4.2)/0.8 = 2

P(3.5 < X < 5.8) = P(-0.875 < Z < 2)

= P(Z < 2) - P(Z < -0.875)

= 0.9772 - 0.1909

= 0.7863

Therefore, 78.63% of PCs will have a life span between 3.5 and 5.8 years.

(iii) P(X < 2):

Z = (2 - 4.2)/0.8 = -2.75

Using standard normal table:

P(Z < -2.75) = 0.003

Therefore, 0.3% of PCs will require replacement within the warranty period.

(ii) exactly two defective components if three components are drawn at random one at a time. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Maximum number of vehicle registrations:

Step 1: Break down the constraints

  • First character: Must be K (1 possibility)
  • Second and third characters: Letters except I and O (24 letters available)
  • Fourth to sixth characters: Any digit (10 possibilities each)
  • Seventh character: Letters except I and O (24 possibilities)

Total = 1 (K) × 24 (2nd letter) × 24 (3rd letter) × 10 (4th digit) × 10 (5th digit) × 10 (6th digit) × 24 (7th letter)

KAF = 1 (K) × 1 (A) × 1 (F)

Possibilities = 1 × 1 × 1 × 10 × 10 × 10 × 24

= 1 × 1000 × 24 = 24,000

Step 4: Subtract 'KAF' possibilities from total

Maximum registrations = Total possibilities - KAF possibilities

= 13,824,000 - 24,000

= 13,800,000

(ii) Lorry depreciation equation:

(i) stratified random sampling; (3 marks)

(i) Laspeyre's price index; (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Sampling Techniques:

(i) Stratified Random Sampling:

  • Population is divided into subgroups (strata) based on shared characteristics (e.g., age, department).
  • A random sample is then drawn from each stratum, often proportionally.
  • Ensures representation from all important subgroups.

(ii) Systematic Random Sampling:

  • A starting point is selected randomly from a list of the population.
  • Every kth element is then selected (k = population size / sample size).
  • Simpler than simple random sampling but requires an ordered list and can be biased if there's a pattern in the list.

(iii) Multi-stage Random Sampling:

  • Involves selecting samples in stages.
  • Population is divided into clusters (e.g., counties), some clusters are randomly selected.
  • Within selected clusters, smaller units (e.g., towns) are randomly selected, and so on, until the final sample elements (e.g., households) are chosen.
  • Used for large, geographically dispersed populations.

(b) Index Number Bases:

(i) Fixed Base:

  • A specific period (the base period) is chosen, and all subsequent periods are compared to this single base.
  • The base period value is typically set to 100.
  • Useful for long-term comparisons against a benchmark year.

(ii) Chain Base:

  • Each period is compared to the immediately preceding period.
  • Shows period-to-period changes more clearly.
  • Allows for changes in the items or weights included in the index over time.

(b) Properties of the Mean (μ or x̄):

  1. Sum of deviations from the mean is zero: Σ(xᵢ - x̄) = 0
  2. Sum of squared deviations is minimum: Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² is minimized.
  3. Affected by every value: Changing any value changes the mean.
  4. If yᵢ = axᵢ + b, then ȳ = ax̄ + b (Linear transformation property).
