(c) A shopkeeper sells sugar in packets labelled 1000 grams. A random sample of 25 similar packets was taken and found to have a mean weight of 996 grams and a standard deviation of 8.5 grams. Test whether this shopkeeper exploits customers by selling underweight sugar at the following levels of significance:

  • (i) 5% level;
  • (ii) 1% level.

(8 marks)

Answer

(a) Bar Charts:

Note: Data for Nairobi, Mombasa, Nakuru branches is missing from Table 1. Using only Kisumu data for demonstration. Complete charts require full data.

Kisumu Data: Savings=18, Current=22, Fixed=30, Junior=10. Total = 80 million Ksh.

(i) Simple Bar Chart (Kisumu Branch):

A chart with four bars representing each account type for Kisumu. The height of each bar corresponds to the profit:

  • Savings: Bar height 18
  • Current: Bar height 22
  • Fixed: Bar height 30
  • Junior: Bar height 10

(A visual bar chart would be drawn here).

(ii) Percentage Component Bar Chart (Kisumu Branch):

A single bar representing 100% of Kisumu's profit, divided into segments based on the percentage contribution of each account type:

  • Savings % = (18 / 80) * 100 = 22.5%
  • Current % = (22 / 80) * 100 = 27.5%
  • Fixed % = (30 / 80) * 100 = 37.5%
  • Junior % = (10 / 80) * 100 = 12.5%

The bar would be segmented proportionally: 22.5% (Savings), 27.5% (Current), 37.5% (Fixed), 12.5% (Junior).

(A visual segmented bar chart would be drawn here).

(b) Compound Interest:

Principal (P) = 10,000,000 Ksh

Annual Rate (r) = 12% = 0.12

Compounding Frequency (m) = Quarterly = 4 times per year

Time (t) = 6 years

(i) Actual Percentage Rate (APR) / Effective Annual Rate (EAR):

EAR = (1 + r/m)m - 1

EAR = (1 + 0.12/4)4 - 1

EAR = (1 + 0.03)4 - 1

EAR = (1.03)4 - 1

EAR = 1.12550881 - 1

EAR = 0.12550881 ≈ 12.55%

(ii) Amount Accrued after 6 years:

Amount (A) = P * (1 + r/m)mt

A = 10,000,000 * (1 + 0.12/4)(4*6)

A = 10,000,000 * (1.03)24

A = 10,000,000 * 2.032794

A ≈ Ksh 20,327,940

(c) Hypothesis Test (Underweight Sugar):

Claim: Shopkeeper sells underweight sugar (μ < 1000g)

Sample size (n) = 25

Sample mean (x̄) = 996g

Sample standard deviation (s) = 8.5g

Hypothesized mean (μ0) = 1000g

Hypotheses:

  • H0: μ ≥ 1000g (The mean weight is 1000g or more)
  • H1: μ < 1000g (The mean weight is less than 1000g - shopkeeper exploits)

This is a left-tailed test. Since n < 30, we use the t-distribution.

Degrees of freedom (df) = n - 1 = 25 - 1 = 24

Test Statistic (t):

t = (x̄ - μ0) / (s / √n)

t = (996 - 1000) / (8.5 / √25)

t = -4 / (8.5 / 5)

t = -4 / 1.7 ≈ -2.353

(i) Test at 5% level (α = 0.05):

  • Critical t-value (left-tailed, df=24, α=0.05) from t-table ≈ -1.711
  • Decision Rule: Reject H0 if tcalculated < tcritical.
  • Conclusion: Since -2.353 < -1.711, we reject H0.
  • At the 5% significance level, there is sufficient evidence to suggest the shopkeeper exploits customers by selling underweight sugar.

(ii) Test at 1% level (α = 0.01):

  • Critical t-value (left-tailed, df=24, α=0.01) from t-table ≈ -2.492
  • Decision Rule: Reject H0 if tcalculated < tcritical.
  • Conclusion: Since -2.353 is not less than -2.492, we fail to reject H0.
  • At the 1% significance level, there is not sufficient evidence to suggest the shopkeeper exploits customers by selling underweight sugar.

Answer

(a) Statistical Measures Defined:
  • (i) Standard Deviation: A measure of the amount of variation or dispersion of a set of values. A low standard deviation indicates that the values tend to be close to the mean (also called the expected value) of the set, while a high standard deviation indicates that the values are spread out over a wider range. It is the square root of the variance.
  • (ii) Coefficient of Determination (R²): A statistical measure in regression analysis that represents the proportion of the variance for a dependent variable that's explained by an independent variable or variables in a regression model. It ranges from 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%), with higher values indicating that the model explains more of the variability in the dependent variable.
(b) Slack vs. Surplus Variables:
  • Slack Variable: Added to a 'less than or equal to' (≤) constraint in a linear programming problem to convert it into an equality (=). It represents the unused amount of a resource or the amount by which the left-hand side of the constraint falls short of the right-hand side (the limit). Slack variables must be non-negative.
  • Surplus Variable: Subtracted from a 'greater than or equal to' (≥) constraint in a linear programming problem to convert it into an equality (=). It represents the amount by which the left-hand side of the constraint exceeds the minimum requirement (the right-hand side). Surplus variables must be non-negative.

(a) Characteristics of Normal Distribution Curve:
  1. Bell-Shaped: The curve has a distinct symmetrical bell shape.
  2. Symmetry: It is perfectly symmetrical around its center, which is the mean (μ).

(a) Time Series Components Defined:
  • (i) Trend (T): The long-term underlying movement or general direction (upward, downward, or stable) in the time series data, observed over a significant period, ignoring short-term fluctuations.
  • (ii) Seasonal Variation (S): Patterns or fluctuations in the data that repeat regularly over a specific period, usually a year (e.g., higher sales of ice cream in summer, lower in winter). Caused by seasons, weather, holidays, or social customs.
  • (iii) Cyclical Variation (C): Long-term oscillations or wave-like movements in the data that occur over periods longer than a year (often several years). Associated with business cycles (boom, recession, depression, recovery). Less regular than seasonal variations.
  • (iv) Random Variation (Irregular or Residual Variation, I): Unpredictable, non-systematic fluctuations in the data that remain after accounting for trend, seasonal, and cyclical components. Caused by chance events like strikes, natural disasters, or measurement errors.

(a) Types of Data Collection Errors:
  • (i) Frame Error: Occurs when the sampling frame (the list from which the sample is drawn) does not accurately represent the target population. This can lead to undercoverage (excluding parts of the population) or overcoverage (including elements not in the target population), resulting in a biased sample.
  • (ii) Sampling Error: The inherent error that arises because a sample is used instead of the entire population. It's the difference between a sample statistic (e.g., sample mean) and the true population parameter (e.g., population mean). It occurs due to random chance in selecting the sample and can be reduced by increasing sample size.
  • (iii) Response Error: Occurs when respondents provide inaccurate or incorrect answers. This can be due to misunderstanding the question, social desirability bias (giving answers perceived as favorable), recall bias (inability to remember accurately), or deliberate misreporting. Interviewer bias can also contribute to response error.

Answer

(a) Financial Calculation Terms:
  • (i) Lead Time: In inventory management, lead time is the duration between placing an order for goods and receiving them. It includes order processing time, supplier production/preparation time, and shipping/transit time. Understanding lead time is crucial for determining reorder points.
  • (ii) Safety Stock (or Buffer Stock): Extra inventory held by a company to mitigate the risk of stockouts caused by uncertainties in supply (e.g., longer lead times) or demand (e.g., unexpected surges). It acts as a cushion against variability.
(b) CPA vs. PERT:
  • Critical Path Analysis (CPA): A project management technique used when activity durations are known with certainty (deterministic). It focuses on identifying the sequence of activities (the critical path) that determines the minimum project completion time. Activities on the critical path have zero slack or float.
  • Program Evaluation and Review Technique (PERT): A project management technique used when activity durations are uncertain (probabilistic). It uses three time estimates (optimistic, most likely, pessimistic) for each activity to calculate an expected duration and variance. PERT allows for calculating the probability of completing the project by a certain date.

Key Difference: CPA uses deterministic time estimates, while PERT uses probabilistic time estimates.

Answer

(a) Hypothesis Testing Terms:
  • (i) Point Estimate vs. Interval Estimate:
    • Point Estimate: A single value calculated from sample data used to estimate an unknown population parameter (e.g., using the sample mean x̄ to estimate the population mean μ).
    • Interval Estimate (Confidence Interval): A range of values calculated from sample data within which the true population parameter is likely to lie, with a certain level of confidence (e.g., a 95% confidence interval for μ). It provides a measure of the uncertainty associated with the point estimate.
  • (ii) One-Tailed Test vs. Two-Tailed Test:
    • One-Tailed Test: A hypothesis test where the alternative hypothesis (H1) specifies a direction (e.g., μ < 100 or μ > 100). The critical region for rejecting the null hypothesis (H0) is located entirely in one tail of the sampling distribution.
    • Two-Tailed Test: A hypothesis test where the alternative hypothesis (H1) does not specify a direction, only that the parameter is different from a certain value (e.g., μ ≠ 100). The critical region is split between both tails of the sampling distribution.

Answer

(a) Non-Binding Constraint:

In linear programming, a non-binding constraint is one where, at the optimal solution, the resources it represents are not fully utilized (for a ≤ constraint) or the minimum requirement is exceeded (for a ≥ constraint). This means the constraint does not limit the achievement of the optimal objective function value. Changing the right-hand side of a non-binding constraint (within certain limits) will not change the optimal solution or the objective function value. In the Simplex method, a non-binding constraint corresponds to a slack or surplus variable having a positive value in the final optimal tableau.
