Answer

(a) Functions of Statistics:
  1. Simplification of Data: Statistics simplifies complex and large datasets into understandable forms through measures like averages (mean, median, mode), dispersion (range, standard deviation), and graphical representations (charts, graphs).
  2. Comparison: It provides tools and techniques (like averages, ratios, percentages, coefficients) to compare different datasets or different groups within a dataset, enabling meaningful analysis of variations.
  3. Forecasting/Prediction: Statistical methods like time series analysis and regression analysis help in analyzing past data to predict future trends and outcomes, aiding in planning and decision-making.
  4. Hypothesis Testing: Statistics provides a framework for formulating hypotheses about populations and testing them based on sample data, allowing for drawing scientific conclusions and making informed decisions.
  5. Formulation and Testing of Policies: Governments and organizations use statistical data and analysis to formulate policies (e.g., economic, social) and evaluate their effectiveness.
*(Note: Only four functions were required)*
(b) Primary vs. Secondary Data:
  • Primary Data: Data collected first-hand by the researcher specifically for the purpose of the current study or investigation. It is original data gathered directly from the source through methods like surveys, interviews, observations, or experiments. It is often more relevant and reliable for the specific research question but can be time-consuming and expensive to collect.
  • Secondary Data: Data that has already been collected by someone else for a different purpose but is available for use by other researchers. Sources include published reports (government, industry), books, journals, websites, and internal company records. It is generally cheaper and quicker to obtain but may not perfectly fit the researcher's needs or could have issues with accuracy, relevance, or timeliness.

Answer

(a) Limitations of Observation Method:
  1. Observer Bias: The observer's presence or personal biases can influence the behavior of those being observed (Hawthorne effect) or affect the interpretation of what is observed.
  2. Limited Scope: Observation can only capture overt behavior or physical phenomena. It cannot directly measure attitudes, opinions, intentions, or past events.
  3. Time-Consuming and Expensive: Depending on the scope and duration, observation can require significant time and resources, especially if continuous or long-term observation is needed.
  4. Ethical Concerns: Observing individuals, especially without their knowledge or consent (covert observation), raises ethical issues regarding privacy and informed consent.
  5. Difficulty in Quantifying Data: Recording and quantifying observed behaviors systematically can be challenging, potentially leading to subjective interpretations.
*(Note: Only four limitations were required)*
(b) Skewness vs. Kurtosis:
  • Skewness: Measures the asymmetry or lack of symmetry in a probability distribution or dataset.
    • Positive Skew (Right-skewed): The tail on the right side is longer or fatter than the left side; the bulk of the data is on the left. Mean > Median > Mode.
    • Negative Skew (Left-skewed): The tail on the left side is longer or fatter than the right side; the bulk of the data is on the right. Mean < Median < Mode.
    • Zero Skew: The distribution is perfectly symmetrical (like a normal distribution). Mean = Median = Mode.
  • Kurtosis: Measures the "tailedness" or peakedness of a distribution relative to a normal distribution. It describes how heavily the tails of a distribution differ from the tails of a normal distribution.
    • Leptokurtic (Positive Excess Kurtosis): Sharper peak and heavier/fatter tails than a normal distribution. Indicates more outliers. Kurtosis > 3.
    • Mesokurtic (Zero Excess Kurtosis): Same kurtosis as a normal distribution. Kurtosis = 3.
    • Platykurtic (Negative Excess Kurtosis): Flatter peak and lighter/thinner tails than a normal distribution. Indicates fewer outliers. Kurtosis < 3.

(a) Explain four circumstances under which sampling method of data collection may be preferred over census method. (8 marks)

(b) Distinguish between correlation and regression as used in statistics. (4 marks)

(c) Table 3 shows the marks scored by eight students in Quantitative Methods (X) and Business Law (Y) examinations. Use it to answer the question that follows.

Table 3

Calculate the Spearman's rank correlation coefficient for the data. (8 marks)

Answer

(a) Sampling vs. Census:

Sampling method may be preferred over census method under the following circumstances:

  1. Large Population: When the population size is very large or infinite, conducting a census becomes impractical due to time, cost, and logistical constraints. Sampling provides a feasible alternative.
  2. Limited Resources: If resources such as time, budget, and trained personnel are limited, sampling is more economical and efficient than a census.
  3. Destructive Testing: When the process of measuring or testing the units involves destroying them (e.g., testing the breaking strength of materials, lifespan of bulbs), sampling is necessary as a census would destroy the entire population.
  4. Need for Quick Results: If results are needed quickly, sampling allows for faster data collection and analysis compared to the lengthy process of a census.
  5. Higher Accuracy Potential (Non-sampling errors): Sometimes, the focused effort possible with a smaller sample (better training, supervision, measurement) can lead to lower non-sampling errors compared to a large-scale census where controlling such errors is more difficult, potentially resulting in more accurate overall findings despite the presence of sampling error.
*(Note: Only four circumstances were required)*
(b) Correlation vs. Regression:
  • Correlation: Measures the strength and direction (positive, negative, or none) of the linear association between two quantitative variables. It indicates how closely the variables move together, typically represented by a correlation coefficient (like Pearson's r) ranging from -1 to +1. It does not distinguish between independent and dependent variables and does not imply causation.
  • Regression: A statistical method used to model and quantify the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. It aims to find the best-fitting line (or curve) to describe how the dependent variable changes as the independent variable(s) change. It allows for prediction of the dependent variable based on the independent variable(s) and estimates the magnitude of the relationship (slope).

Question 4

(a) Explain four properties of a normal distribution curve. (8 marks)

(b) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in probability:

  • (i) independent events; (2 marks)
  • (ii) conditional probability. (2 marks)

(c) A bag contains 6 red balls and 4 green balls. Two balls are picked at random one after the other with replacement. Determine the probability that the two balls picked are of:

  • (i) the same colour; (4 marks)
  • (ii) different colours. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Properties of Normal Distribution Curve:
  1. Bell-Shaped and Symmetrical: The curve has a distinct symmetrical bell shape, centered around the mean. The left and right sides are mirror images.
  2. Mean, Median, and Mode Coincide: In a perfect normal distribution, the mean, median, and mode are all equal and located at the center (peak) of the distribution.
  3. Asymptotic: The curve approaches the horizontal axis asymptotically, meaning it gets closer and closer but never actually touches it on either side, extending infinitely in both directions.
  4. Total Area = 1: The total area under the curve is equal to 1 (or 100%), representing the total probability of all possible outcomes.
  5. Empirical Rule (Approximate): About 68% of the data falls within ±1 standard deviation (σ) of the mean (μ), about 95% falls within ±2σ, and about 99.7% falls within ±3σ.
*(Note: Only four properties were required)*
(b) Probability Terms:
  • (i) Independent Events: Two events are independent if the occurrence or non-occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other event occurring. Example: Tossing a coin twice; the outcome of the first toss does not influence the outcome of the second toss. Mathematically, P(A and B) = P(A) * P(B).
  • (ii) Conditional Probability: The probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It is denoted as P(A|B), read as "the probability of A given B". Mathematically, P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B), assuming P(B) > 0.
(c) Probability (Picking Balls with Replacement):

Total balls = 6 Red (R) + 4 Green (G) = 10 balls

Picking two balls with replacement (probabilities remain constant for each pick).

P(R) = 6/10 = 0.6

P(G) = 4/10 = 0.4

(i) Probability of Same Colour: P(RR) or P(GG)

  • P(RR) = P(1st is R) * P(2nd is R) = 0.6 * 0.6 = 0.36
  • P(GG) = P(1st is G) * P(2nd is G) = 0.4 * 0.4 = 0.16
  • P(Same Colour) = P(RR) + P(GG) = 0.36 + 0.16 = 0.52

(ii) Probability of Different Colours: P(RG) or P(GR)

  • P(RG) = P(1st is R) * P(2nd is G) = 0.6 * 0.4 = 0.24
  • P(GR) = P(1st is G) * P(2nd is R) = 0.4 * 0.6 = 0.24
  • P(Different Colours) = P(RG) + P(GR) = 0.24 + 0.24 = 0.48
  • Alternatively: P(Different) = 1 - P(Same) = 1 - 0.52 = 0.48

Answer

(a) Components of a Time Series:
  1. Secular Trend (T): The long-term underlying movement or general direction (upward, downward, or stable) in the time series data, observed over a significant period, ignoring short-term fluctuations.
  2. Seasonal Variation (S): Patterns or fluctuations in the data that repeat regularly over a specific period, usually a year (e.g., higher sales in summer, lower in winter). Caused by seasons, weather, holidays, or social customs.
  3. Cyclical Variation (C): Long-term oscillations or wave-like movements in the data that occur over periods longer than a year (often several years). Associated with business cycles (boom, recession, depression, recovery). Less regular than seasonal variations.
  4. Irregular/Random Variation (I): Unpredictable, non-systematic fluctuations in the data that remain after accounting for trend, seasonal, and cyclical components. Caused by chance events like strikes, natural disasters, or measurement errors.
(b) Network Analysis Terms:
  • (i) An Activity: A specific task or operation within a project that consumes time and resources. In a network diagram, it is typically represented by an arrow.
  • (ii) An Event: A specific point in time that marks the start or completion of one or more activities. It does not consume time or resources itself. In a network diagram, it is typically represented by a node (circle).

Question 6

(a) Explain four advantages of using questionnaires in data collection. (8 marks)

(b) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in hypothesis testing:

  • (i) null hypothesis; (2 marks)
  • (ii) level of significance. (2 marks)

(c) The mean lifetime of a sample of 100 light bulbs produced by a company is found to be 1,580 hours with a standard deviation of 90 hours. Test the hypothesis that the mean lifetime of the bulbs produced by the company is 1,600 hours at 5% level of significance. (8 marks)

Answer

(a) Advantages of Questionnaires:
  1. Cost-Effective: Generally less expensive to administer than interviews, especially for large samples spread over a wide geographical area (e.g., postal or online questionnaires).
  2. Anonymity and Reduced Bias: Can offer anonymity, potentially encouraging more honest responses to sensitive questions. Reduces interviewer bias as the respondent answers directly.
  3. Convenience for Respondents: Respondents can complete the questionnaire at their own pace and convenience, potentially leading to more thoughtful answers.
  4. Standardization: All respondents receive the exact same questions in the same order, ensuring consistency and comparability of data.
  5. Easy Analysis (for closed-ended questions): Data from closed-ended questions is often easier and faster to code, enter, and analyze statistically.
*(Note: Only four advantages were required)*
(b) Hypothesis Testing Terms:
  • (i) Null Hypothesis (H₀): A statement about a population parameter that is assumed to be true for the purpose of testing. It typically represents the status quo, a baseline, or a statement of "no effect" or "no difference". The goal of hypothesis testing is to determine if there is enough statistical evidence to reject this assumption.
  • (ii) Level of Significance (α): The probability threshold below which the null hypothesis will be rejected. It represents the maximum acceptable risk of making a Type I error (rejecting a true null hypothesis). Common values are 0.05 (5%) and 0.01 (1%). If the p-value of the test statistic is less than α, the result is considered statistically significant, and H₀ is rejected.
(c) Hypothesis Test (Light Bulbs):

1. Hypotheses:

  • H₀: μ = 1600 hours (The mean lifetime is 1600 hours)
  • H₁: μ ≠ 1600 hours (The mean lifetime is different from 1600 hours) - Two-tailed test

2. Significance Level:

  • α = 0.05

3. Test Statistic:

  • Sample size (n) = 100 (large sample, n ≥ 30, so Z-test is appropriate)
  • Sample mean (x̄) = 1580 hours
  • Population mean (μ₀) = 1600 hours
  • Sample standard deviation (s) = 90 hours (use as estimate for σ since n is large)
  • Z = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s / √n)
  • Z = (1580 - 1600) / (90 / √100)
  • Z = -20 / (90 / 10)
  • Z = -20 / 9 ≈ -2.22

4. Critical Value / Rejection Region:

  • For a two-tailed test at α = 0.05, the critical Z-values are ±1.96.
  • Reject H₀ if |Z| > 1.96.

5. Decision:

  • Calculated Z = -2.22
  • |-2.22| = 2.22
  • Since 2.22 > 1.96, we reject the null hypothesis (H₀).

6. Conclusion:

At the 5% level of significance, there is sufficient statistical evidence to conclude that the mean lifetime of the light bulbs produced by the company is different from 1,600 hours.

Question 7

(a) Explain four circumstances under which a personal interview method may be the most appropriate method of data collection. (8 marks)

(b) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in inventory control:

  • (i) buffer stock; (2 marks)
  • (ii) re-order level. (2 marks)

(c) A company requires 10,000 units of an item annually. The cost of placing an order is Ksh 400 per order and the inventory holding cost is 20% of the purchase price per unit. The purchase price per unit is Ksh 80.

Determine the:

  • (i) economic order quantity (EOQ); (4 marks)
  • (ii) total annual inventory cost. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Circumstances Favoring Personal Interview:
  1. Complex or Sensitive Topics: When the subject matter is complex, requires detailed explanation, or deals with sensitive personal issues, an interviewer can clarify questions, probe for deeper answers, and build rapport to encourage honest responses.
  2. Low Literacy Levels: If the target population has low literacy levels or language barriers, an interviewer can read questions and record answers, ensuring participation from individuals who might struggle with a written questionnaire.
  3. Need for High Response Rate: Personal interviews generally achieve higher response rates compared to mail or online surveys, as it's harder for respondents to refuse participation face-to-face.
  4. Observation Opportunity: Allows the interviewer to observe the respondent's non-verbal cues and environment, which can provide additional context or insights.
  5. Control over Environment: The interviewer can ensure the interview is conducted in a suitable environment, free from distractions, and can verify the identity of the respondent.
*(Note: Only four circumstances were required)*
(b) Inventory Control Terms:
  • (i) Buffer Stock (Safety Stock): Extra inventory held to mitigate the risk of stockouts caused by uncertainties in demand (unexpectedly high sales) or supply (delays in lead time). It acts as a cushion against variability.
  • (ii) Re-order Level (ROL): The inventory level that triggers a new order to be placed. It is set to ensure that new stock arrives just as the buffer stock is about to be used, preventing a stockout during the lead time. ROL = (Demand during lead time) + Buffer Stock.
(c) Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) and Total Cost:

Given:

  • Annual Demand (D) = 10,000 units
  • Ordering Cost (S) = Ksh 400 per order
  • Purchase Price (P) = Ksh 80 per unit
  • Holding Cost (H) = 20% of Purchase Price per unit per year = 0.20 * 80 = Ksh 16 per unit per year

(i) Economic Order Quantity (EOQ):

EOQ = √[(2 * D * S) / H]

EOQ = √[(2 * 10000 * 400) / 16]

EOQ = √[8,000,000 / 16]

EOQ = √500,000 ≈ 707.11 units

Since we can't order fractional units, we might round to 707 units.

(ii) Total Annual Inventory Cost (TIC):

TIC = Annual Purchase Cost + Annual Ordering Cost + Annual Holding Cost

  • Annual Purchase Cost = D * P = 10000 * 80 = Ksh 800,000
  • Number of Orders = D / EOQ = 10000 / 707.11 ≈ 14.14 orders
  • Annual Ordering Cost = (Number of Orders) * S = 14.14 * 400 ≈ Ksh 5,656
  • Average Inventory = EOQ / 2 = 707.11 / 2 ≈ 353.56 units
  • Annual Holding Cost = (Average Inventory) * H = 353.56 * 16 ≈ Ksh 5,657

TIC = 800,000 + 5,656 + 5,657 = Ksh 811,313

(Note: Ordering and Holding costs are approximately equal at the EOQ, slight difference due to rounding EOQ or number of orders).

Alternatively, using the EOQ formula directly in the cost formula:

TIC = (D * P) + (D/EOQ * S) + (EOQ/2 * H)

TIC = (10000*80) + (10000/707.11 * 400) + (707.11/2 * 16)

TIC = 800000 + 5656.85 + 5656.88 ≈ Ksh 811,313.73

Answer

(a) Benefits of Index Numbers:
  1. Measure Changes Over Time: Index numbers are primarily used to measure the relative change in the magnitude of a variable (like price, quantity, or value) over time compared to a base period.
  2. Comparison: They facilitate comparison between related variables or groups of variables, even if they are measured in different units (e.g., comparing price changes of different commodities).

  5. Policy Formulation: Governments and businesses use index numbers (e.g., CPI for wage adjustments, cost of living analysis) to inform policy decisions.

(b) Decision Theory Terms:

Rows typically represent alternatives, and columns represent states of nature.
  • (ii) Decision Tree: A graphical representation of the decision-making process. It uses nodes (squares for decision points, circles for chance/state of nature points) and branches (representing alternatives or outcomes) to map out the sequence of decisions, possible states of nature, probabilities, and payoffs, helping to visualize and analyze complex decisions.
