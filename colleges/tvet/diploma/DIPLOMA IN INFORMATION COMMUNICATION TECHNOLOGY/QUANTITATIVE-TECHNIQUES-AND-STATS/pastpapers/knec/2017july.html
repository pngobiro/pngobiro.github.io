Question 1

(a) Explain four advantages of using sampling method over census method in data collection. (8 marks)

(b) Table 1 shows the distribution of marks scored by students in a Quantitative Methods examination. Use it to answer the questions that follow.

  • (i) median mark; (3 marks)
  • (ii) standard deviation. (5 marks)

(c) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in probability:

  • (i) mutually exclusive events; (2 marks)
  • (ii) independent events. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Advantages of Sampling over Census:
  1. Reduced Cost: Collecting data from a sample is significantly cheaper than collecting data from the entire population, especially for large populations. Fewer resources (personnel, materials, travel) are needed.
  2. Increased Speed: Data collection and analysis are much faster with a sample, allowing for quicker results and timely decision-making.
  3. Improved Accuracy/Quality: With a smaller workload, more resources can be dedicated to training data collectors, using better measurement tools, and carefully supervising the process, potentially leading to higher quality data and fewer non-sampling errors (like measurement or processing errors) than a large-scale census.
  4. Feasibility for Destructive Testing: In cases where testing an item destroys it (e.g., testing the lifespan of light bulbs), sampling is the only feasible option as a census would destroy the entire population.
  5. Greater Scope: Sampling allows for gathering more detailed information on a wider range of topics from each respondent, which might be impractical in a census due to time and cost constraints.

(c) Probability Terms:
  • (i) Mutually Exclusive Events: Events that cannot occur at the same time. If one event happens, the other(s) cannot. Example: When rolling a die once, getting a '1' and getting a '6' are mutually exclusive. P(A and B) = 0.

(b) Table 2 shows the prices and quantities of three commodities consumed in the years 2010 and 2015. Use it to answer the questions that follow.

Calculate each of the following weighted price index numbers:

  • (i) Laspeyre's; (3 marks)
  • (ii) Paasche's; (3 marks)
  • (iii) Fisher's Ideal. (2 marks)

(c) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in network analysis:

  • (i) critical path; (2 marks)
  • (ii) activity float. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Circumstances for Using Mode:
  1. Categorical Data: When dealing with nominal data (categories without inherent order, like favorite color or brand preference), the mode is the only meaningful measure of central tendency as mean and median cannot be calculated.
  2. Identifying the Most Frequent Value: When the primary goal is to find the most common or popular value in a dataset (e.g., most common shoe size sold, most frequent customer complaint).
  3. Quick Estimate: The mode can often be quickly identified by inspection, especially in simple frequency distributions, providing a rapid estimate of central tendency.
  4. Distribution Shape: For distributions that are clearly unimodal (have one distinct peak), the mode represents the point of highest concentration of data.

(c) Network Analysis Terms:
  • (i) Critical Path: The sequence of activities in a project network that determines the longest path from the start to the end of the project. Activities on the critical path have zero float or slack, meaning any delay in these activities will delay the entire project completion time.
  • (ii) Activity Float (or Slack): The amount of time an activity can be delayed without affecting the overall project completion time. It is calculated as the difference between the Latest Start Time (LS) and Earliest Start Time (ES), or the difference between the Latest Finish Time (LF) and Earliest Finish Time (EF). Float = LS - ES = LF - EF.

  • (i) the same colour; (4 marks)
  • (ii) different colours. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Limitations of Using Diagrams:
  1. Lack of Precision: Diagrams provide a visual overview but often lack the precision of numerical data. Exact values can be difficult to read accurately from charts like bar graphs or pie charts.
  2. Potential for Misinterpretation/Distortion: The choice of scale, axis manipulation, or chart type can intentionally or unintentionally distort the data's representation, leading to misleading conclusions.
  3. Limited Information: Diagrams typically summarize data and may omit important details or nuances present in the raw data or detailed tables. They are not suitable for showing large amounts of detailed information.
  4. Complexity for Multiple Variables: Representing relationships between multiple variables (more than 2 or 3) using diagrams can become very complex and difficult to interpret effectively.

  • P(GG) = P(1st is G) * P(2nd is G | 1st was G)

(ii) Probability of Different Colours: P(RG) or P(GR)

  • P(RG) = P(1st is R) * P(2nd is G | 1st was R)

  • P(GR) = P(1st is G) * P(2nd is R | 1st was G)
    = (3/8) * (5/7) = 15/56
  • P(Different Colours) = P(RG) + P(GR)
    = 15/56 + 15/56 = 30/56 = 15/28
  • Alternatively: P(Different) = 1 - P(Same) = 1 - 13/28 = 15/28

Answer

(a) Properties of Binomial Distribution:
  1. Fixed Number of Trials (n): The experiment consists of a fixed number of identical trials.
  2. Independent Trials: The outcome of each trial is independent of the outcomes of other trials.
  3. Two Outcomes: Each trial has only two possible outcomes, typically classified as "success" or "failure".
  4. Constant Probability of Success (p): The probability of success (p) remains the same for each trial. The probability of failure is q = 1 - p.

(c) Hypothesis Testing Errors:
  • (i) Type I Error (α): Occurs when we reject a true null hypothesis (H₀). It's the probability of concluding there is an effect or difference when, in reality, there isn't one. Also known as a "false positive". The significance level (α) of a test is the maximum acceptable probability of making a Type I error.
  • (ii) Type II Error (β): Occurs when we fail to reject a false null hypothesis (H₀). It's the probability of concluding there is no effect or difference when, in reality, there is one. Also known as a "false negative". The power of a test (1 - β) is the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis.

Question 5

(a) Explain four applications of queueing theory in business organizations. (8 marks)

(b) The number of vehicles arriving at a toll station follows a Poisson distribution with a mean rate of 6 vehicles per minute. Determine the probability that:

  • (i) exactly 4 vehicles will arrive in a given minute; (3 marks)
  • (ii) at most 2 vehicles will arrive in a given minute; (3 marks)
  • (iii) at least 3 vehicles will arrive in a given minute. (2 marks)

(c) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in inventory control:

  • (i) lead time; (2 marks)
  • (ii) re-order level. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Applications of Queueing Theory:
  1. Customer Service Centers: Determining the optimal number of service agents (e.g., call center operators, bank tellers, cashiers) to balance customer waiting times and operational costs.
  2. Telecommunications: Designing network capacity (e.g., bandwidth, number of channels) to handle call or data traffic efficiently, minimizing dropped calls or network congestion.
  3. Traffic Management: Optimizing traffic light timings, designing highway lane configurations, or managing airport runway usage to minimize vehicle or aircraft delays.
  4. Manufacturing & Production: Scheduling jobs on machines, managing workflow between workstations, or determining optimal buffer sizes to minimize production bottlenecks and work-in-progress inventory.
  5. Healthcare Systems: Managing patient flow in emergency rooms, scheduling appointments, or determining the optimal number of beds or staff to minimize patient waiting times and resource idle time.
*(Note: Only four applications were required)*
(b) Poisson Distribution (Vehicle Arrivals):

Mean arrival rate (λ) = 6 vehicles per minute.

Poisson formula: P(X=k) = (e-λ * λk) / k!

e-6 ≈ 0.002479

(i) P(X = 4):

P(X=4) = (e-6 * 64) / 4!

P(X=4) = (0.002479 * 1296) / 24

P(X=4) = 3.2128 / 24 ≈ 0.1339

(ii) P(X ≤ 2) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) + P(X=2):

  • P(X=0) = (e-6 * 60) / 0! = (0.002479 * 1) / 1 = 0.002479
  • P(X=1) = (e-6 * 61) / 1! = (0.002479 * 6) / 1 = 0.014874
  • P(X=2) = (e-6 * 62) / 2! = (0.002479 * 36) / 2 = 0.044622

P(X ≤ 2) = 0.002479 + 0.014874 + 0.044622 ≈ 0.0620

(iii) P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - P(X ≤ 2):

P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - 0.0620 = 0.9380

(c) Inventory Control Terms:
  • (i) Lead Time: The time interval between placing an order for inventory and receiving that order. It includes order processing time, supplier's production/handling time, and shipping/transit time. Accurate lead time estimation is crucial for determining when to reorder.
  • (ii) Re-order Level (ROL): The inventory level at which a new order should be placed to replenish stock before it runs out. It is typically calculated as: ROL = (Average daily usage * Average lead time in days) + Safety Stock.

(a) Advantages of Observation Method:

(c) Hypothesis Testing Terms:
  • (i) Null Hypothesis (H₀): A statement about a population parameter that is assumed to be true until evidence suggests otherwise. It typically represents the status quo or a statement of "no effect" or "no difference". Hypothesis testing aims to determine if there is enough evidence to reject H₀.
  • (ii) Alternative Hypothesis (H₁ or Ha): A statement that contradicts the null hypothesis. It represents what the researcher is trying to find evidence for (e.g., an effect, a difference, a relationship). If H₀ is rejected, the evidence supports H₁.

(c) Explain the meaning of each of the following terms as used in decision theory:

  • (i) payoff; (2 marks)
  • (ii) state of nature. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Characteristics of a Good Questionnaire:
  1. Clear and Unambiguous Questions: Questions should be worded simply and directly, avoiding jargon, technical terms, or double negatives, so that all respondents interpret them in the same way.
  2. Relevant Questions: All questions included should be directly relevant to the research objectives. Avoid unnecessary questions that increase respondent burden.
  3. Logical Flow and Sequence: Questions should be arranged in a logical order, typically starting with simple, general questions and moving towards more specific or sensitive ones. Group related questions together.
  4. Appropriate Question Types: Use a mix of question types (e.g., multiple-choice, rating scales, open-ended) appropriate for the information being sought and the respondents' ability to answer.
  5. Avoid Leading or Biased Questions: Questions should be neutral and not suggest a preferred answer or influence the respondent's opinion.
  6. Concise: The questionnaire should be as short as possible while still covering the necessary topics, to maintain respondent engagement and minimize fatigue.
  7. Pre-tested (Piloted): It should be tested on a small sample similar to the target population before full deployment to identify any confusing questions, formatting issues, or problems with flow.
*(Note: Only four characteristics were required)*

(c) Decision Theory Terms:
  • (i) Payoff: The outcome or consequence (often expressed in monetary terms like profit or cost) resulting from a specific combination of a decision alternative chosen by the decision-maker and a state of nature that occurs. Payoffs are typically presented in a payoff table.
  • (ii) State of Nature: A future event or condition that is outside the control of the decision-maker but affects the outcome (payoff) of the decision made. The decision-maker is uncertain about which state of nature will actually occur. Examples: High demand vs. Low demand, Competitor introduces new product vs. No new product.
