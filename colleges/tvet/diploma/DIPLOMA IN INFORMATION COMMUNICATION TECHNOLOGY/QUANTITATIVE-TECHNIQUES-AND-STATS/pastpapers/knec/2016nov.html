(a) Quantitative Methods & Data Classification:

(i) Types of Quantitative Methods:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Methods used to summarize and describe the main features of a dataset (e.g., calculating mean, median, mode, standard deviation, creating charts).
  • Inferential Statistics: Methods used to make inferences or draw conclusions about a larger population based on data collected from a sample (e.g., hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, regression analysis).

(ii) Temporal vs. Spatial Classification:

  • Temporal Classification: Grouping or arranging data based on time of occurrence (chronological order). Examples: sales per month, population per year, temperature per hour.
  • Spatial Classification: Grouping or arranging data based on geographical location or area. Examples: population by county, sales by region, rainfall by district.
(b) Sampling Terms:
  • (i) Population: The entire group of individuals, items, or data points that share one or more characteristics relevant to a study, about which the researcher wants to draw conclusions.
  • (ii) Statistic: A numerical value or measure calculated from sample data that describes a characteristic of the sample (e.g., sample mean, sample proportion). It is used to estimate the corresponding population parameter.
  • (iii) Parameter: A numerical value or measure that describes a characteristic of the entire population (e.g., population mean, population proportion). Parameters are usually unknown and are estimated using sample statistics.

(i) Qualities of a Good Average:

  1. Rigidly Defined: The definition should be clear and unambiguous, leading to one interpretation.

  3. Easy to Understand and Calculate: It should be relatively simple to comprehend and compute.

  5. Least Affected by Sampling Fluctuations: It should be stable, meaning that averages from different samples drawn from the same population should not vary widely.
  6. Not Unduly Affected by Extreme Values: It should not be significantly skewed by very large or very small values in the dataset (the mean is sensitive to this, while the median is less so).

(a) Index Numbers:

(i) Simple Index Number: An index number calculated from a single variable or commodity. It compares the value (e.g., price or quantity) of that single item at one point in time relative to its value at a base period. Example: Simple Price Index = (Current Price / Base Price) * 100.

(ii) Factors in Index Number Construction:

  1. Purpose of the Index: The specific goal (e.g., measuring inflation, cost of living, production changes) dictates the type of data needed, the formula, and the interpretation.
  2. Selection of Items (Basket of Goods): Items included should be representative of the group or phenomenon being measured and should remain relevant over time. The number of items affects accuracy and cost.
  3. Choice of Base Period: The base period should be a relatively normal or stable period, not too distant from the current period, to allow for meaningful comparisons.
  4. Selection of Weights: Assigning appropriate weights (based on relative importance, e.g., expenditure) to different items is crucial for composite indices to reflect their true impact (e.g., Laspeyres uses base period quantities, Paasche uses current period quantities).
  5. Choice of Formula/Average: Different formulas (e.g., Laspeyres, Paasche, Fisher, simple average, weighted average) have different properties and potential biases. The choice depends on the purpose and data availability.

Answer

(a) Advantages and Disadvantages of Moving Averages:

Advantages:

  • Simplicity: Relatively easy to understand and calculate compared to more complex methods like regression.
  • Smoothing: Effectively smooths out short-term fluctuations (random and seasonal variations), making the underlying trend easier to identify visually.

Disadvantages:

  • Loss of Data: Moving averages cannot be calculated for the beginning and end periods of the time series (e.g., a 3-point average loses one point at each end).
  • Inflexibility/Lag: It lags behind turning points in the trend. It may not accurately represent trends that change direction rapidly or are highly non-linear. The choice of the period (e.g., 3-point vs 5-point) affects the degree of smoothing and lag.
(b) Correlation vs. Regression:

(i) Differentiation:

  • Correlation: Measures the strength and direction (positive or negative) of the linear relationship between two quantitative variables. It indicates how closely the variables move together. The correlation coefficient (e.g., Pearson's r) ranges from -1 to +1. It does not imply causation.
  • Regression: A statistical method used to model the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. It aims to find the best-fitting line (or curve) through the data points to predict the value of the dependent variable based on the independent variable(s). It describes the nature of the relationship (e.g., slope) and can be used for prediction, but also does not inherently prove causation.

(ii) Interpretation of Correlation Coefficient = 0.994:

A Karl Pearson's correlation coefficient of 0.994 indicates a very strong positive linear relationship between the amount of fertilizers used and the crop yield per unit area. As the amount of fertilizer increases, the crop yield tends to increase in a highly consistent linear pattern.

(c) Sampling Methods:
  • (i) Stratified Sampling: The population is first divided into mutually exclusive subgroups (strata) based on relevant characteristics (e.g., age, gender, location). Then, a simple random sample is drawn independently from each stratum. This ensures representation from all subgroups and can provide more precise estimates if strata are homogeneous within but heterogeneous between.
  • (ii) Multi-stage Sampling: Involves selecting samples in multiple stages, typically used for large and geographically dispersed populations. For example: Stage 1: Randomly select counties. Stage 2: Randomly select towns within those counties. Stage 3: Randomly select households within those towns. Stage 4: Randomly select individuals within those households. It is more practical and cost-effective than simple random sampling for large populations but can be less precise and more complex to analyze.

Question 7

(a) Explain the meaning of quantitative method as applied in statistics. (2 marks)

(b) The revenue function of a product is r = 28q — q², the unit variable cost is v=q-8 and q is the quantity while the fixed cost is Ksh 64. Determine each of the following:

  • (i) total cost function; (2 marks)
  • (ii) profit function; (2 marks)
  • (iii) Show that the output for maximum profit is not necessarily the same as the output for maximum revenue. (4 marks)

(c) A committee of 8 lecturers is to be formed out of 6 Science, 8 Arts teachers and a Physical instructor. Determine the number of ways the committee can be formed if:

  • (i) any teacher can be included in the committee; (2 marks)
  • (ii) there should be 3 science and 4 arts teachers in the committee. (4 marks)

(d) Rogers intends to invest a sum of Ksh 10,000 at an interest rate of 8.5 % per year. Determine the amount of money that he will have after 3 years. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Quantitative Method:

A quantitative method in statistics refers to a systematic approach that uses numerical data and mathematical techniques to analyze, interpret, and draw meaningful conclusions about phenomena. It involves collecting measurable data, applying mathematical/statistical procedures, and making objective decisions based on the results. Examples include hypothesis testing, regression analysis, time series analysis, and other mathematical modeling techniques.

(b) Cost and Profit Functions:

(i) Total Cost Function:

  • Given: Unit variable cost (v) = q - 8
  • Fixed cost (F) = 64
  • Total Cost (TC) = Fixed Cost + (Variable Cost × Quantity)
  • TC = 64 + (q - 8)q
  • TC = 64 + q² - 8q
  • Therefore, TC = q² - 8q + 64

(ii) Profit Function:

  • Given: Revenue function (r) = 28q - q²
  • Profit (P) = Revenue - Total Cost
  • P = (28q - q²) - (q² - 8q + 64)
  • P = 28q - q² - q² + 8q - 64
  • P = 36q - 2q² - 64
  • Therefore, P = -2q² + 36q - 64

(iii) Maximum Revenue vs Maximum Profit:

For Maximum Revenue:

  • Revenue function: r = 28q - q²
  • dr/dq = 28 - 2q
  • Set dr/dq = 0: 28 - 2q = 0
  • 2q = 28
  • q = 14
  • d²r/dq² = -2 (negative, confirms maximum)
  • Maximum revenue occurs at q = 14

For Maximum Profit:

  • Profit function: P = -2q² + 36q - 64
  • dP/dq = -4q + 36
  • Set dP/dq = 0: -4q + 36 = 0
  • 4q = 36
  • q = 9
  • d²P/dq² = -4 (negative, confirms maximum)
  • Maximum profit occurs at q = 9

Since maximum revenue occurs at q = 14 and maximum profit occurs at q = 9, this shows that the output for maximum profit (q = 9) is not the same as the output for maximum revenue (q = 14).

(c) Committee Formation:

(i) Any Teacher:

  • Total number of teachers = 6 + 8 + 1 = 15 teachers
  • Need to select 8 from 15
  • Number of ways = C(15,8) = 15! / (8! × 7!)
  • = (15 × 14 × 13 × 12 × 11 × 10 × 9 × 8) / (8 × 7 × 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1)
  • = 6435 ways

(ii) 3 Science and 4 Arts Teachers:

  • Ways to select 3 Science teachers from 6 = C(6,3) = 20
  • Ways to select 4 Arts teachers from 8 = C(8,4) = 70
  • Total ways = C(6,3) × C(8,4)
  • = 20 × 70
  • = 1400 ways
(d) Interest Calculation:

Principal (P) = Ksh 10,000

Rate (r) = 8.5% = 0.085 per year

Time (t) = 3 years

Using Simple Interest formula: A = P(1 + rt)

  • A = 10000(1 + 0.085 × 3)
  • A = 10000(1 + 0.255)
  • A = 10000 × 1.255
  • A = 12,550

After 3 years, he will have Ksh 12,550.

It is defined as: GM = ⁿ√(x₁ × x₂ × ... × xₙ), where n is the number of values and x represents individual values. It is useful for finding average rates of growth or calculating average returns in financial applications.
