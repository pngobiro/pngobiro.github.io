  • (i) 5% level;
  • (ii) 1% level.

Answer

(i) Simple Bar Chart:

(A bar chart would be drawn here showing four bars for Savings (18M), Current (22M), Fixed (30M), and Junior (0M) accounts)

(ii) Percentage Component Bar Chart:

Total = 18 + 22 + 30 + 0 = 70 million

  • Savings: (18/70) × 100 = 25.71%
  • Current: (22/70) × 100 = 31.43%
  • Fixed: (30/70) × 100 = 42.86%
  • Junior: (0/70) × 100 = 0%

(A percentage component bar chart would be drawn here showing these percentages)

(b) Compound Interest Calculations:

(i) Annual Percentage Rate (APR):

  • Nominal rate = 12% per year
  • Compounding frequency = 4 times per year (quarterly)
  • Effective Annual Rate (APR) = (1 + r/n)ⁿ - 1
    where r = nominal rate (0.12) and n = number of compounding periods per year (4)
  • APR = (1 + 0.12/4)⁴ - 1
  • APR = (1.03)⁴ - 1
  • APR = 1.12550881 - 1
  • APR = 12.55%

(ii) Amount After 6 Years:

  • Using A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
    where P = principal (10,000,000)
    r = nominal rate (0.12)
    n = compounding frequency (4)
    t = time in years (6)
  • A = 10,000,000(1 + 0.12/4)^(4×6)
  • A = 10,000,000(1.03)^24
  • A = 10,000,000 × 2.0334
  • A = 20,334,000
(c) Hypothesis Testing for Sugar Weight:

Given:

  • Sample size (n) = 25
  • Sample mean (x̄) = 996g
  • Population mean (μ₀) = 1000g (labeled weight)
  • Standard deviation (s) = 8.5g

Test Statistic:

  • t = (x̄ - μ₀)/(s/√n)
  • t = (996 - 1000)/(8.5/√25)
  • t = -4/(8.5/5)
  • t = -4/1.7
  • t = -2.353

(i) At 5% level:

  • Degrees of freedom = n - 1 = 24
  • Critical value at α = 0.05 (one-tailed) = -1.711
  • Since -2.353 < -1.711, reject H₀
  • Conclusion: There is sufficient evidence at 5% level to conclude that the shopkeeper is selling underweight sugar.

(ii) At 1% level:

  • Critical value at α = 0.01 (one-tailed) = -2.492
  • Since -2.353 > -2.492, do not reject H₀
  • Conclusion: There is insufficient evidence at 1% level to conclude that the shopkeeper is selling underweight sugar.

(a) Statistical Measures:

(i) Standard Deviation: A measure of dispersion that quantifies the average deviation of data points from their mean. It is calculated as the square root of the variance, which is the average of squared deviations from the mean. The formula is: σ = √[Σ(x - μ)²/N] for population, or s = √[Σ(x - x̄)²/(n-1)] for sample.

(ii) Coefficient of Determination (R²): A statistical measure that represents the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s). It ranges from 0 to 1, where 1 indicates perfect prediction and 0 indicates no predictive power. It is the square of the correlation coefficient (r).

(b) Slack vs. Surplus Variables:

Slack Variables: Added to inequality constraints of ≤ type to convert them to equations. They represent unused resources in maximization problems. For example, if x₁ + x₂ ≤ 10, we add slack variable s to get x₁ + x₂ + s = 10.

Surplus Variables: Added to inequality constraints of ≥ type to convert them to equations. They represent excess over the minimum requirement in minimization problems. For example, if x₁ + x₂ ≥ 10, we subtract surplus variable s to get x₁ + x₂ - s = 10.

  1. Symmetrical: The curve is bell-shaped and perfectly symmetrical about the mean. The mean, median, and mode are equal and located at the center.

    • 99.7% within ±3 standard deviations

  1. Trend: The long-term movement or general direction in a time series data that shows the underlying pattern or tendency over an extended period. It can be upward (increasing), downward (decreasing), or horizontal (stationary).
  2. Seasonal Variation: Regular patterns of change that occur within a fixed period (usually within a year) due to seasonal factors such as weather, holidays, or business cycles. These variations repeat at regular intervals.
  3. Random Variation: Unpredictable fluctuations in time series data that cannot be explained by trend, seasonal, or cyclical components. These are irregular changes caused by unexpected events or random factors.

    • Memory errors
    • Interviewer bias

  • (ii) one tailed test and two tailed test.

(6 marks)

(b) A flour milling firm produces two types of flour; maize flour and wheat flour, which have a sale price of Ksh 50 and Ksh 80 per kilogram respectively. The resources used to produce each type of flour and the maximum time available for each task per week is as shown in Table 7.

Answer

(a) Statistical Hypothesis Testing Terms:

(i) Point Estimate vs. Interval Estimate:

  • Point Estimate: A single value used to estimate a population parameter. Example: Sample mean (x̄) as an estimate of population mean (μ).
  • Interval Estimate: A range of values likely to contain the population parameter, expressed as a confidence interval. Example: 95% confidence interval for the mean (x̄ ± 1.96σ/√n).

(ii) One-Tailed vs. Two-Tailed Test:

  • One-Tailed Test: Tests for the possibility of a relationship in one direction. The rejection region is entirely in one tail of the distribution. Example: Testing if μ > μ₀ (right-tailed) or μ < μ₀ (left-tailed).
  • Two-Tailed Test: Tests for the possibility of a relationship in both directions. The rejection region is split between both tails of the distribution. Example: Testing if μ ≠ μ₀.
(b) Linear Programming Problem:

(i) LP Model Formulation:

Let:

  • x = number of kg of maize flour
  • y = number of kg of wheat flour

Subject to constraints:

  • Transport: 2x + 3y ≤ 600 (hours)
  • Milling: x + y ≤ 240 (hours)
  • Storage: x + 2y ≤ 300 (hours)
  • Non-negativity: x, y ≥ 0

(ii) Simplex Method Solution:

Step 1: Convert to standard form by adding slack variables

  • 2x + 3y + s₁ = 600
  • x + y + s₂ = 240
  • x + 2y + s₃ = 300

Answer

(a) Non-binding Constraint:

A non-binding constraint is a constraint in linear programming that does not affect the optimal solution because it is automatically satisfied by the solution that optimizes the objective function while satisfying other constraints. In other words, there is "slack" or unused capacity in this constraint at the optimal solution. For example, if a resource constraint allows up to 100 units but only 80 units are needed in the optimal solution, the constraint is non-binding.
