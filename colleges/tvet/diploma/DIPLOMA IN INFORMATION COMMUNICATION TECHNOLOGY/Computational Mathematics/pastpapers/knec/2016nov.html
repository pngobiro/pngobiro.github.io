Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Define each of the following terms as used in mathematics:

(i) interpolation; (2 marks)

(ii) extrapolation; (2 marks)

(iii) mode. (2 marks)

(b) Use the matrix method to solve the following system of simultaneous equations:

$$\begin{gathered} 4x-3y=18 \\ 2y-x=1 \end{gathered}$$

(6 marks)

(c) Using the decimal number 3.14159265, perform the following instructions:

(i) truncate the number to 4 significant figures; (2 marks)

(ii) round off the number to 4 decimal places. (2 marks)

(d) (i) Explain the term infinite difference table as used in numerical analysis. (2 marks)

(ii) Table 1 shows values for $x_i$ and $y_i$. Use it to answer the question that follows.

Table 1
$x_i$123456
$y_i$92571162313539

Using the Newton forward interpolation method, construct the forward difference table. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Mathematical Terms:

(i) Interpolation: The process of estimating unknown values that fall between known data points within the range of a given set of data points.

(ii) Extrapolation: The process of estimating values that lie outside the range of known data points by extending the pattern or trend observed within the known data.

(iii) Mode: The value that appears most frequently in a dataset. A dataset can have one mode (unimodal), two modes (bimodal), multiple modes (multimodal), or no mode.

(b) Matrix Method Solution:

Convert equations to matrix form:

$$\begin{aligned} 4x - 3y &= 18 \\ -x + 2y &= 1 \end{aligned}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 18 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix}4 & -3\\-1 & 2\end{bmatrix}$

$|A| = (4)(2) - (-3)(-1) = 8 - 3 = 5$

$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}2 & 3\\1 & 4\end{bmatrix}$

Solution:

$$\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}2 & 3\\1 & 4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}18\\1\end{bmatrix}$$

$$= \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}(2)(18) + (3)(1)\\(1)(18) + (4)(1)\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}39\\22\end{bmatrix}$$

Therefore: $x = \frac{39}{5} = 7.8$ and $y = \frac{22}{5} = 4.4$

(c) Number Operations:

(i) Truncating to 4 significant figures: 3.142

(ii) Rounding to 4 decimal places: 3.1416

(d) Infinite Difference Table:

(i) An infinite difference table is a table that shows successive differences between consecutive terms in a sequence, continuing indefinitely or until a pattern emerges. It's used to identify the degree of a polynomial that fits the data.

(b) Explain three assumptions in interpolation. (6 marks)

(c) Distinguish between permutation and combination as used in mathematics. (4 marks)

(d) (i) Figure 1 shows three straight line graphs. Identify the line graph that corresponds to the following equations:

(ii) Using the Gaussian elimination method, solve the following simultaneous equations:

$$\begin{aligned} z-3x-2y &= 6 \\ 2z-4x+2y &= 18 \\ -3z+8x+9y &= -9 \end{aligned}$$

(3 marks)

Answer

(a) Advantages of Median over Arithmetic Mean:

  1. The median is not affected by extreme values (outliers) in the data set, making it a more robust measure of central tendency.
  2. The median is more representative of the typical value when the data is skewed, as it's not influenced by the magnitude of extreme values.

(b) Assumptions in Interpolation:

  1. The data points are accurate and reliable.
  2. There is continuity between the known data points (the function is continuous in the interval).
  3. The relationship between points follows a regular pattern that can be approximated by the chosen interpolation method.

(c) Permutation vs. Combination:

Permutation refers to an arrangement of objects where the order matters. It counts the number of ways to arrange objects in a specific sequence.

Combination refers to a selection of objects where the order does not matter. It counts the number of ways to select objects regardless of their sequence.

For example, when selecting 3 letters from ABC:

  • Permutations: ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA (6 ways)
  • Combination: ABC (only 1 way, as ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA are considered the same selection)

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Define each of the following types of measures as used in statistics:

(i) absolute; (2 marks)

(ii) relative. (2 marks)

(b) Describe three ways in which statistical data can be classified. (6 marks)

(c) Solve each of the following:

(i) using 2's complement, subtract $37_{10}$ from $52_{10}$; (2 marks)

(ii) convert the binary number $10101.11_{2}$ to its octal equivalent; (2 marks)

(iii) convert $1359_{10}$ to its octal equivalent; (2 marks)

(iv) convert $6AD_{16}$ to its decimal equivalent. (2 marks)

(d) A line passes through the points $(0,3)$ and $(9,6)$. Derive the equation of the line. (4 marks)

Answer

(a) Types of Statistical Measures:

(i) Absolute Measure: A measure expressed in the original units of measurement without reference to any other value. For example, the actual height in meters or weight in kilograms.

(ii) Relative Measure: A measure that expresses one quantity in relation to another, often as a ratio, percentage, or rate. For example, percentage increase or ratio of males to females.

(b) Statistical Data Classification:

  1. Chronological Classification: Data arranged according to time sequence (e.g., yearly sales, monthly rainfall).
  2. Geographical Classification: Data arranged according to location or spatial distribution (e.g., sales by region, population by country).
  3. Qualitative Classification: Data grouped based on non-numeric characteristics or attributes (e.g., gender, color, profession).

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Define each of the following terms as used in matrices:

(i) identity matrix; (2 marks)

(ii) singular matrix. (2 marks)

(b) The following are examples of the probability of an event occurring. Classify them as dependent, mutually exclusive or independent:

(i) Drawing a red ball when a ball is drawn at random from a bag that contains 4 red and 5 green balls with replacement; (2 marks)

(ii) Selecting a male at random from a group of 15 males and 20 females; (2 marks)

(iii) Winning a prize in a raffle by buying 5 tickets when there are 6 prizes and a total of 200 tickets are sold; (2 marks)

(iv) A couple giving birth to a baby girl as their second born if their first born is a boy. (2 marks)

(c) Given two input binary signals: $A=100101$ and $B=110110$, use truth tables to determine the output $C$ when applied to each of the following gates:

(i) OR; (2 marks)

(ii) NAND; (2 marks)

(iii) XNOR. (2 marks)

(d) Show that: $^{2n}C_2=2 \times {^n}C_2+n^2$. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Matrix Terms:

(i) Identity Matrix: A square matrix in which all elements on the main diagonal are 1 and all other elements are 0. Denoted as I. For example, a 2×2 identity matrix is $\begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 1\end{bmatrix}$.

(ii) Singular Matrix: A square matrix whose determinant is zero. As a result, a singular matrix does not have an inverse.

(b) Probability Event Classification:

(i) Independent - Drawing with replacement means the probability of drawing a red ball remains constant (4/9) for each draw, unaffected by previous draws.

(ii) Mutually Exclusive - A person can be either male or female, not both. These events cannot occur simultaneously.

(iii) Dependent - The probability of winning each prize changes as tickets are drawn and prizes are awarded.

(iv) Independent - The gender of the second child is not influenced by the gender of the first child.

(c) Truth Tables:

Given: $A=100101$ and $B=110110$

(i) OR Gate:

A:    1 0 0 1 0 1
B:    1 1 0 1 1 0
C(OR): 1 1 0 1 1 1

(ii) NAND Gate:

A:      1 0 0 1 0 1
B:      1 1 0 1 1 0
AND:    1 0 0 1 0 0
C(NAND): 0 1 1 0 1 1

(iii) XNOR Gate:

A:       1 0 0 1 0 1
B:       1 1 0 1 1 0
C(XNOR): 1 0 1 1 0 0

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Use a mathematical notation to state the binomial theorem. (4 marks)

(b) With the aid of a diagram in each case, differentiate between kurtosis and skewness as used in statistics. (6 marks)

(c) Use an illustration to describe the code formats of each of the following computer coding systems:

(i) ASCII; (3 marks)

(ii) EBCDIC. (3 marks)

(d) A football team scores an average of 3 goals per match in 45 matches. Assuming a Poisson distribution, determine in how many matches they would expect to score each of the following:

(i) 4 goals; (2 marks)

(ii) less than 2 goals. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Binomial Theorem:

For any real numbers $x$ and $y$ and a non-negative integer $n$:

$(x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k$

where $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$ is the binomial coefficient.

(b) Kurtosis vs Skewness:

Skewness:

    Normal Distribution (Symmetric)
         ___
        /   \
    ___/     \___

    Positive Skew           Negative Skew
         ___                    ___
        /   \___            ___/   \
    ___/        \        /         \___

Skewness measures asymmetry of the distribution. Positive skew has a longer right tail, negative skew has a longer left tail.

Kurtosis:

    Normal (Mesokurtic)     Leptokurtic        Platykurtic
         ___                    /\                 ___
        /   \                 /  \              _/   \_
    ___/     \___        ___/    \___      ___/       \___

Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" or peakedness of the distribution compared to a normal distribution.

(c) Code Formats:

(i) ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange):

7-bit format: 0110001 (Example for character '1')
- First bit: Always 0 (for standard ASCII)
- Next 3 bits: Group identifier
- Last 3 bits: Character position

(ii) EBCDIC (Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code):

8-bit format: 11110001 (Example for character '1')
- First 4 bits: Zone bits
- Last 4 bits: Numeric bits

(d) Poisson Distribution:

Using Poisson formula: $P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$ where $\lambda = 3$

(i) For 4 goals:

$P(X=4) = \frac{e^{-3}3^4}{4!} = \frac{e^{-3}81}{24} \approx 0.1680$

Expected number of matches = $0.1680 \times 45 \approx 7.56$ matches

(ii) For less than 2 goals:

$P(X<2) = P(X=0) + P(X=1)$

$P(X=0) = \frac{e^{-3}3^0}{0!} = e^{-3} \approx 0.0498$

$P(X=1) = \frac{e^{-3}3^1}{1!} = 3e^{-3} \approx 0.1494$

$P(X<2) = 0.0498 + 0.1494 = 0.1992$

Expected number of matches = $0.1992 \times 45 \approx 8.96$ matches

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Explain each of the following terms as used in statistics:

(i) Sample space; (2 marks)

(ii) Event. (2 marks)

(b) Given that Matrix $Q=\left[\begin{array}{rrr}2 & 8 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 & -2 \\ 3 & 4 & -6\end{array}\right]$, determine each of the following:

(i) 3Q; (2 marks)

(ii) $3Q^{-1}$ using the cofactor method. (8 marks)

(c) Figure 2 shows a certain logic gate. Use it to answer the questions that follow.

  A ---+
       | NAND Gate --> C
  B ---+

Figure 2

(i) write the Boolean equation for the output C; (2 marks)

(ii) determine the value of C when $A=1$ and $B=0$. (2 marks)

Answer

(a) Statistical Terms:

(i) Sample Space: The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment or trial. For example, when rolling a die, the sample space is {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

(ii) Event: A subset of the sample space, representing one or more possible outcomes of an experiment. For example, rolling an even number ({2, 4, 6}) is an event.

(b) Matrix Operations:

(i) 3Q:

$3Q = 3 \times \left[\begin{array}{rrr}2 & 8 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 & -2 \\ 3 & 4 & -6\end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rrr}6 & 24 & 6 \\ 3 & 6 & -6 \\ 9 & 12 & -18\end{array}\right]$

(ii) $3Q^{-1}$ using Cofactor Method:

First, find the determinant of Q:

$|Q| = 2( (2)(-6) - (-2)(4) ) - 8( (1)(-6) - (-2)(3) ) + 2( (1)(4) - (2)(3) )$

$|Q| = 2(-12 + 8) - 8(-6 + 6) + 2(4 - 6)$

$|Q| = 2(-4) - 8(0) + 2(-2) = -8 - 0 - 4 = -12$

Since $|Q| \neq 0$, the inverse exists.

Find the matrix of minors:

$M = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} (2)(-6)-(-2)(4) & (1)(-6)-(-2)(3) & (1)(4)-(2)(3) \\ (8)(-6)-(2)(4) & (2)(-6)-(2)(3) & (2)(4)-(8)(3) \\ (8)(-2)-(2)(2) & (2)(-2)-(2)(1) & (2)(2)-(8)(1) \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 0 & -2 \\ -56 & -18 & -16 \\ -20 & -6 & -4 \end{array}\right]$

Find the matrix of cofactors (apply checkerboard pattern +, -, +):

$C = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 0 & -2 \\ 56 & -18 & 16 \\ -20 & 6 & -4 \end{array}\right]$

Find the adjugate (transpose of cofactor matrix):

$Adj(Q) = C^T = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 56 & -20 \\ 0 & -18 & 6 \\ -2 & 16 & -4 \end{array}\right]$

Find the inverse $Q^{-1} = \frac{1}{|Q|} Adj(Q)$:

$Q^{-1} = \frac{1}{-12} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 56 & -20 \\ 0 & -18 & 6 \\ -2 & 16 & -4 \end{array}\right]$

Finally, find $3Q^{-1}$:

$3Q^{-1} = \frac{3}{-12} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 56 & -20 \\ 0 & -18 & 6 \\ -2 & 16 & -4 \end{array}\right] = -\frac{1}{4} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -4 & 56 & -20 \\ 0 & -18 & 6 \\ -2 & 16 & -4 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & -14 & 5 \\ 0 & 4.5 & -1.5 \\ 0.5 & -4 & 1 \end{array}\right]$

(c) Logic Gate:

(i) Boolean Equation: The gate shown is a NAND gate. The equation is $C = \overline{A \cdot B}$.

(ii) Value of C: When $A=1$ and $B=0$, $A \cdot B = 1 \cdot 0 = 0$. Therefore, $C = \overline{0} = 1$.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Describe each of the following models as used in statistical data representation:

(i) histograms; (2 marks)

(ii) frequency polygons. (2 marks)

(b) The probability of three events occurring are $\frac{1}{8}$ for event $X, \frac{1}{5}$ for event Y and $\frac{2}{7}$ for event $Z$. Determine the probability of each of the following:

(i) event X and Y occurring but not event Z; (3 marks)

(ii) event Y occurring but not event X and not event Z; (3 marks)

(iii) event X or event Y happening but not event Z. (3 marks)

(c) A certain examination carries a total of 10 questions and the student is required to answer 7 questions only. The paper is divided into Section A; comprising the first five questions and Section B; comprising the last five questions. Determine the number of ways the student can answer the examination:

(i) If the student must answer 3 questions from the Section A and four questions from the Section B; (2 marks)

(ii) If at least three questions are selected from the Section A. (5 marks)

Answer

(a) Statistical Data Representation Models:

(i) Histograms: A graphical representation of the distribution of numerical data. It uses adjacent rectangles erected over discrete intervals (bins), with an area proportional to the frequency of the observations in the interval. The bars touch each other, indicating continuous data.

(ii) Frequency Polygons: A graph constructed by using lines to join the midpoints of each interval (or bar top in a histogram). The heights of the points represent the frequencies. It's useful for comparing distributions.

(b) Probability Calculations:

Given: $P(X) = 1/8$, $P(Y) = 1/5$, $P(Z) = 2/7$.

Then: $P(\neg X) = 1 - 1/8 = 7/8$, $P(\neg Y) = 1 - 1/5 = 4/5$, $P(\neg Z) = 1 - 2/7 = 5/7$.

(i) X and Y but not Z: $P(X \cap Y \cap \neg Z)$

Assuming independence: $P(X) \times P(Y) \times P(\neg Z) = \frac{1}{8} \times \frac{1}{5} \times \frac{5}{7} = \frac{5}{280} = \frac{1}{56}$

(ii) Y but not X and not Z: $P(\neg X \cap Y \cap \neg Z)$

Assuming independence: $P(\neg X) \times P(Y) \times P(\neg Z) = \frac{7}{8} \times \frac{1}{5} \times \frac{5}{7} = \frac{35}{280} = \frac{1}{8}$

(iii) (X or Y) but not Z: $P((X \cup Y) \cap \neg Z)$

$P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y) - P(X \cap Y)$

$P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \times P(Y) = \frac{1}{8} \times \frac{1}{5} = \frac{1}{40}$

$P(X \cup Y) = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{40} = \frac{5 + 8 - 1}{40} = \frac{12}{40} = \frac{3}{10}$

$P((X \cup Y) \cap \neg Z) = P(X \cup Y) \times P(\neg Z) = \frac{3}{10} \times \frac{5}{7} = \frac{15}{70} = \frac{3}{14}$

(c) Examination Question Selection:

Total Q = 10, Answer = 7. Section A = 5 Q, Section B = 5 Q.

(i) 3 from A and 4 from B:

Ways = (Ways to choose 3 from A) $\times$ (Ways to choose 4 from B)

Ways = $\binom{5}{3} \times \binom{5}{4} = \frac{5!}{3!2!} \times \frac{5!}{4!1!} = 10 \times 5 = 50$ ways.

(ii) At least 3 from Section A:

This means (3 from A and 4 from B) OR (4 from A and 3 from B) OR (5 from A and 2 from B).

Case 1: (3A, 4B) = $\binom{5}{3} \times \binom{5}{4} = 10 \times 5 = 50$

Case 2: (4A, 3B) = $\binom{5}{4} \times \binom{5}{3} = 5 \times 10 = 50$

Case 3: (5A, 2B) = $\binom{5}{5} \times \binom{5}{2} = 1 \times \frac{5!}{2!3!} = 1 \times 10 = 10$

Total ways = 50 + 50 + 10 = 110 ways.
