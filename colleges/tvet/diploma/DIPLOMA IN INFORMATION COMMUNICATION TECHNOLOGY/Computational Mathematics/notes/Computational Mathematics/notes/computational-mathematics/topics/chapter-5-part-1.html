CHAPTER 5: PROBABILITY

üé≤ Introduction to probability

Probability is the science of studying the outcomes of random phenomena.

A phenomenon is called random if individual outcomes are uncertain but the long-term pattern of many outcomes is predictable.

To determine the probability of a specific outcome of a probability experiment (random phenomenon) the experiment is repeated a large number of times and we look at the ratio of times that the outcome occurred.

  • ‚Ä¢Example: The outcome of the experiment of tossing a coin is a random phenomenon. The probability that the outcome is "Heads" can be found by tossing the coin a large number of times.

üìä Probability Models

A probability model is a mathematical representation of a random phenomenon. It is defined by its sample space, events within the sample space, and probabilities associated with each event. The sample space $S$ for a probability model is the set of all possible outcomes.

Terminology:

For any probability experiment (or a random phenomenon)

  • ‚úìThe collection of all the possible outcomes of the experiment is called the Sample Space.
  • ‚úìAn event is any sub-collection of outcomes in the sample space.
  • ‚úìA simple event is any single outcome.
  • ‚úìThe complement of an event $A$, denoted $\bar{A}$, is the set of all outcomes not in $A$.
  • ‚úìA probability model (or a probability distribution) is a process of assigning to the outcomes and events in a sample space a value that represents the probability for that outcome to occur. If $x$ is an outcome, the probability of $x$ will be denoted by $P(x)$. If $E$ is an event, the probability of $E$ will be denoted by $P(E)$.
  • ‚úìThe assignment in a probability model must satisfy the following rules:
    • ‚Ä¢For any event or outcome, the probability is a number between 0 and 1.
    • ‚Ä¢The probability of the sample space is 1.
    • ‚Ä¢If two events A and B do are disjoint, i.e. have no outcome in common, then the probability $\mathbf{P}(\mathbf{A}$ or $\mathbf{B})$ that one or the other occurs is the sum $\boldsymbol{P}(\boldsymbol{A})+\boldsymbol{P}(\boldsymbol{B})$ of the individual probabilities of the events.
    • ‚Ä¢The probability of an impossible (empty) event is 0 .

For a finite (discrete) probability space, these rules guarantee that the probabilities of the individual events must add up to 1 .

If a probability experiment has N outcomes, and all are equally likely to occur, the probability of an individual outcome is $1 / \mathrm{N}$.

The probability of an event A is

$\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{A})=($ The number of outcomes that satisfy A$)) / ($ Total number of outcomes $)$

‚ûï The Sum Rule

If two events have no elements in common, they follow the rule: $P(\operatorname{Aor} B)=P(A)+P(B)$

Definition: If two events have no elements in common, they are called disjoint

Example

In the example of rolling two die and recording the pair of numbers, let $A$ be the event "the sum of the two numbers is a 3 ", and let $B$ be the event "the sum of the two numbers is a 5 ". List the elements in $A$; list the elements in $B$; and list all the elements in the event " the sum of the two numbers is a 3 or a 5 ". Find $P(A), P(B)$, and the probability that the sum is a 3 or a 5 .

But if the events have elements in common this doesn't work.

Example:

This illustrates the sum rule: $P($ Aor $B)=P(A)+P(B)-P($ AandB $)$, where $P($ AandB $)$ is the probability that both A and B will happen.

‚ùì Random Variables

A random variable gives a value to each outcome in a sample space. A probability model for a random variable assigns a probability value to each variable value.

Examples:

  1. 1)In the dice rolling experiment: If we are only interested in the sum of the die, the random variable would be the sum of the two numbers.
  2. 2)In the experiment of rolling one die, the random variable could be the number of dots that lands on top.
  3. 3)In an experiment of tossing a coin, a random variable could assign the number 1 to heads and 0 to tails.

A probability model (or probability distribution) satisfies the rules:

  • ‚úìFor any variable, the probability is a number between 0 and 1.
  • ‚úìThe probability of the sum of all the variables is 1 .

Œº The Mean (Expected Value) of a Probability model

Suppose that the possible values for the random variables are $s_{1}, s_{2}, s_{3}, \ldots, s_{k}$; and suppose that the probability of the variable $s_{j}$ is $p_{j}$. Then the mean (also called expected value) $\boldsymbol{F}$ of the probability distribution is

$$F=s_{1} p_{1}+s_{2} p_{2}+\ldots+s_{k} p_{k}$$
