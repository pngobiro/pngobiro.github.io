üìè Rows and Columns (Matrix Multiplication)

To show how many rows and columns a matrix has we often write rows $\times$ columns. Example: This matrix is $2 \times 3$ ( 2 rows by 3 columns):

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 6 & 4 & 24 \\ 1 & -9 & 8 \end{array}\right]$$

When we do multiplication:

  • ‚Ä¢The number of columns of the 1st matrix must equal the number of rows of the 2nd matrix.
  • ‚Ä¢And the result will have the same number of rows as the 1st matrix, and the same number of columns as the 2nd matrix.

Example:

In that example we multiplied a $1 \times 3$ matrix by a $3 \times 4$ matrix (note the 3 s are the same), and the result was a $1 \times 4$ matrix.

In General:

To multiply an $\mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{n}$ matrix by an $\mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{p}$ matrix, the $\mathbf{n s}$ must be the same, and the result is an $\mathbf{m \times p}$ matrix.

$$m \times n \times n \times p \Rightarrow m \times p$$

‚ÜîÔ∏è Order of Multiplication

In arithmetic we are used to:

$$3 \times 5=5 \times 3$$

(The Commutative Law of Multiplication)

But this is not generally true for matrices (matrix multiplication is not commutative):

$$\mathrm{AB} \neq \mathrm{BA}$$

When we change the order of multiplication, the answer is (usually) different.

Example:

See how changing the order affects this multiplication:

$$\begin{aligned} & {\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{array}\right] \times\left[\begin{array}{ll} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc} 4 & 4 \\ 10 & 8 \end{array}\right]} \\ & {\left[\begin{array}{ll} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 \end{array}\right] \times\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc} 2 & 4 \\ 7 & 10 \end{array}\right]} \end{aligned}$$

üÜî Identity Matrix

The "Identity Matrix" is the matrix equivalent of the number " 1 ":

$$I=\left[\begin{array}{lll} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$

A 3x3 Identity Matrix

  • ‚Ä¢It is "square" (has same number of rows as columns),
  • ‚Ä¢It has $\mathbf{1}$ s on the diagonal and $\mathbf{0}$ s everywhere else.
  • ‚Ä¢Its symbol is the capital letter $\mathbf{I}$.

It is a special matrix, because when we multiply by it, the original is unchanged:

$$\begin{aligned} & \mathrm{A} \times \mathrm{I}=\mathrm{A} \\ & \mathrm{I} \times \mathrm{A}=\mathrm{A} \end{aligned}$$

üî¢ Determinant of a Matrix

The determinant of a matrix is a special number that can be calculated from a square matrix.

A Matrix is an array of numbers:

A Matrix

(This one has 2 Rows and 2 Columns)

$$A \equiv\left[\begin{array}{ll} a & b \\ c & d \end{array}\right]$$

The determinant of that matrix is (calculations are explained later):

$3 \times 6-8 \times 4=18-32=-\mathbf{1 4}$

What is it for?

The determinant tells us things about the matrix that are useful in systems of linear equations, helps us find the inverse of a matrix, is useful in calculus and more.

Symbol

The symbol for determinant is two vertical lines either side.

Example:

$|\mathbf{A}|$ means the determinant of the matrix $\mathbf{A}$

(Exactly the same symbol as absolute value.)

Calculating the Determinant

First of all the matrix must be square (i.e. have the same number of rows as columns). Then it is just basic arithmetic. Here is how:

For a $\mathbf{2 \times 2}$ Matrix

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix ( 2 rows and 2 columns):

The determinant is:

$$|\mathrm{A}|=\mathrm{ad}-\mathrm{bc}$$

"The determinant of A equals a times $d$ minus $b$ times $c$ "

It is easy to remember when you think of a cross:

  • ‚Ä¢Blue means positive (+ad),
  • ‚Ä¢Red means negative (-bc)

Example:

$$\begin{aligned} B & =\left[\begin{array}{ll} 4 & 6 \\ 3 & 8 \end{array}\right] \\ |B| & =4 \times 8-6 \times 3 \\ & =32-18 \\ & =14 \end{aligned}$$

üìê Cramer's Rule

Cramer's Rule, is used to solve systems with matrices. Cramer's Rule was named after the Swiss mathematician Gabriel Cramer, who also did a lot of other neat stuff with math.

Cramer's rule is all about getting determinants of the square matrices that are used to solve systems.

üîÑ Inverse of a Matrix

What is the Inverse of a Matrix?

This is the reciprocal of a Number:

Reciprocal of a Number

The Inverse of a Matrix is the same idea but we write it $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$

Why not ${ }^{1} /$ A ? Because we don't divide by a Matrix! And anyway $1 / 8$ can also be written $8^{-1}$ And there are other similarities:

When you multiply a number by its reciprocal you get $\mathbf{1}$

$$8 \times(1 / 8)=1$$

When you multiply a Matrix by its Inverse you get the Identity Matrix (which is like "1" for Matrices):

$$A \times A^{-1}=\mathbf{I}$$

Same thing when the inverse comes first:

$$\begin{aligned} & (1 / 8) \times 8=\mathbf{1} \\ & \mathrm{A}^{-1} \times \mathrm{A}=\mathbf{I} \end{aligned}$$

We just mentioned the "Identity Matrix". It is the matrix equivalent of the number " 1 ":

$$I=\left[\begin{array}{lll} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$

A 3x3 Identity Matrix

  • ‚Ä¢It is "square" (has same number of rows as columns),
  • ‚Ä¢It has $\mathbf{1} \mathrm{s}$ on the diagonal and $\mathbf{0}$ s everywhere else.
  • ‚Ä¢It's symbol is the capital letter I.

The Identity Matrix can be $2 \times 2$ in size, or $3 \times 3, 4 \times 4$, etc $\ldots$

üìñ Definition (Inverse Matrix)

Here is the definition:

The Inverse of A is $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ only when:

$$\mathrm{A} \times \mathrm{A}^{-1}=\mathrm{A}^{-1} \times \mathrm{A}=\mathbf{I}$$

Sometimes there is no Inverse at all.

üî¢ 2x2 Matrix Inverse

OK, how do we calculate the Inverse?

Well, for a $2 \times 2$ Matrix the Inverse is:

$$\left[\begin{array}{ll} a & b \\ c & d \end{array}\right]^{-1}=\underset{\text { determinant }}{\frac{1}{a d-b c}}\left[\begin{array}{cc} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{array}\right]$$

In other words: swap the positions of $a$ and $d$, put negatives in front of $b$ and $c$, and divide everything by the determinant (ad-bc).

Let us try an example:

$$\begin{aligned} {\left[\begin{array}{ll} 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 6 \end{array}\right]^{-1} } & =\frac{1}{4 \times 6-7 \times 2}\left[\begin{array}{cc} 6 & -7 \\ -2 & 4 \end{array}\right] \\ & =\frac{1}{10}\left[\begin{array}{cc} 6 & -7 \\ -2 & 4 \end{array}\right] \\ & =\left[\begin{array}{cc} 0.6 & -0.7 \\ -0.2 & 0.4 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned}$$

How do we know this is the right answer?

Remember it must be true that: $\mathrm{A} \times \mathrm{A}^{-1}=\mathbf{I}$

So, let us check to see what happens when we multiply the matrix by its inverse:

$$\begin{aligned} {\left[\begin{array}{ll} 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 6 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc} 0.6 & -0.7 \\ -0.2 & 0.4 \end{array}\right] } & =\left[\begin{array}{ll} 4 \times 0.6+7 \times-0.2 & 4 \times-0.7+7 \times 0.4 \\ 2 \times 0.6+6 \times-0.2 & 2 \times-0.7+6 \times 0.4 \end{array}\right] \\ & =\left[\begin{array}{ll} 2.4-1.4 & -2.8+2.8 \\ 1.2-1.2 & -1.4+2.4 \end{array}\right] \\ & =\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned}$$

And, hey!, we end up with the Identity Matrix! So it must be right.

It should also be true that: $\mathrm{A}^{-1} \times \mathrm{A}=\mathbf{I}$

Why don't you have a go at multiplying these? See if you also get the Identity Matrix:

$$\left[\begin{array}{cc} 0.6 & -0.7 \\ -0.2 & 0.4 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ll} 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 6 \end{array}\right]=[ \quad ]$$

‚ùì Why Would We Want an Inverse?

Because with Matrices we don't divide! Seriously, there is no concept of dividing by a Matrix.

But we can multiply by an Inverse, which achieves the same thing.

Imagine you couldn't divide by numbers, and someone asked "How do I share 10 apples with 2 people?"

But you could take the reciprocal of 2 (which is 0.5 ), so you could answer:

$$10 \times 0.5=5$$

They get 5 apples each

The same thing can be done with Matrices:

Say that you know Matrix A and B, and want to find Matrix X:

$$\mathrm{XA}=\mathrm{B}$$

It would be nice to divide both sides by A (to get $\mathrm{X}=\mathrm{B} / \mathrm{A}$ ), but remember we can't divide.

But what if we multiply both sides by $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ ?

$$\mathrm{XAA}^{-1}=\mathrm{BA}^{-1}$$

And we know that $\mathrm{AA}^{-1}=\mathrm{I}$, so:

$$\mathrm{XI}=\mathrm{BA}^{-1}$$

We can remove I (for the same reason we could remove "1" from $1 \mathrm{x}=\mathrm{ab}$ for numbers):

$$\mathrm{X}=\mathrm{BA}^{-1}$$

And we have our answer (assuming we can calculate $\mathrm{A}^{-1}$ )

In that example we were very careful to get the multiplications correct, because with Matrices the order of multiplication matters. AB is almost never equal to BA.
