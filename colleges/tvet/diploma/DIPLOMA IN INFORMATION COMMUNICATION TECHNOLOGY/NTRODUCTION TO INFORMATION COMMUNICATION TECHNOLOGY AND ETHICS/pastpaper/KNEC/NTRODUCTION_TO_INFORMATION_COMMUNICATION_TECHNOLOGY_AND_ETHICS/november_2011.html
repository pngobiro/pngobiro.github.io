Exam Code: 2920/101

Duration: 3 hours

Period: November 2011

1. (a) (i) (3 marks)

Outline three advantages of using materials stored on a CD ROM over the Internet.

Answer

Offline Accessibility: Materials on a CD-ROM are accessible offline, meaning users do not require an internet connection to access the stored content. This is advantageous in situations where internet access is limited, unreliable, or costly. Users can access information anytime, anywhere, without dependency on network availability.

Data Security and Control: CD-ROMs offer enhanced data security and control as the content is physically stored on the disc and is not vulnerable to online threats such as hacking or cyber-attacks. Organizations or individuals have complete control over the distribution and access to the materials, reducing the risk of unauthorized access or data breaches associated with online storage and transmission.

Portability and Durability: CD-ROMs are portable and durable, making them convenient for distributing and preserving materials. They can be easily transported and shared physically, and they are less susceptible to data loss due to digital corruption or server failures compared to online storage. CD-ROMs provide a tangible and reliable medium for distributing and archiving information.

1. (b) (i) (4 marks)

Mamlaka TTI received computers of varied specifications from well-wishers. The school computer technician is required to install necessary software in the computers.

(i) Outline four factors that should be considered before installation.

Answer

System Compatibility: Ensure the software is compatible with the operating system and hardware specifications of each computer. Verify that the software supports the OS version (e.g., Windows 10, Linux distribution), processor type (e.g., Intel Core i5, AMD Ryzen), RAM capacity, and available hard disk space of the target computers. Incompatibility can lead to installation failures or software malfunction.

Software Licensing: Confirm that the school has acquired valid software licenses for the number of computers where the software will be installed. Check the licensing terms to ensure compliance and avoid copyright infringement. Different software licenses may have restrictions on the number of installations or concurrent users allowed. Proper license management is essential for legal and ethical software use.

Installation Prerequisites: Identify and fulfill any software prerequisites before attempting installation. Some software applications require specific libraries, frameworks (e.g., .NET Framework, Java Runtime Environment), or other software components to be installed beforehand. Failure to meet prerequisites can cause installation errors or software instability. Review software documentation for prerequisite details.

User Needs and Functionality: Consider the intended users and their functional requirements when selecting and installing software. Determine the specific tasks students and staff will perform on these computers (e.g., word processing, programming, graphic design). Install software that aligns with these needs and provides the necessary functionalities. Avoid installing unnecessary software that consumes resources and complicates the system.

2. (a) (4 marks)

2. (a) Outline four main characteristics of the fourth generation computers.

Answer

Microprocessor Technology: Fourth-generation computers are characterized by the use of microprocessors, which are Very Large Scale Integrated (VLSI) circuits. A microprocessor integrates thousands of transistors onto a single silicon chip, making computers smaller, more powerful, and more affordable. The microprocessor is the central processing unit (CPU) on a chip, enabling complex computations in a compact form factor.

Graphical User Interfaces (GUIs): The fourth generation saw the widespread adoption of Graphical User Interfaces (GUIs). GUIs replaced command-line interfaces with user-friendly visual environments, using icons, menus, and windows. This made computers more accessible and easier to use for non-technical users, expanding computer usage beyond specialists to the general public and various industries.

Networking and Internet Connectivity: Fourth-generation computers are designed with built-in networking capabilities, facilitating easy connection to local area networks (LANs) and wide area networks (WANs), including the Internet. Networking enabled resource sharing, communication, and data exchange between computers globally. The rise of the Internet during this era transformed computing and communication paradigms.

Portability and Personal Computing: Fourth-generation computers led to the development of personal computers (PCs), which are compact, affordable, and designed for individual use. The miniaturization of components and reduced costs made computers accessible to homes and small businesses. Portable computers like laptops emerged, offering mobility and convenience, further popularizing personal computing and its applications.

2. (d) (ii) (4 marks)

(ii) State two differences between an Intranet and the Internet.

Answer

Intranet:

An intranet is a private network that is accessible only to authorized members within an organization. It is typically used for internal communication, collaboration, and sharing of information and resources within the organization. Access to an intranet is restricted and controlled, ensuring that only employees or designated users can access its content and services. Intranets enhance internal organizational efficiency and security.

Internet:

The Internet is a global, public network that is accessible to anyone with an internet connection. It is a vast network of networks, connecting billions of devices worldwide. The Internet is used for global communication, information access, e-commerce, and a wide range of online services and applications. Access to the Internet is generally open and unrestricted, facilitating global connectivity and information sharing.

Key Differences:

The primary difference lies in accessibility and scope. An intranet is private, restricted to organizational members, and used for internal purposes, while the Internet is public, globally accessible, and used for worldwide communication and information exchange. Intranets provide internal organizational networking, whereas the Internet offers global connectivity and public access.

4. (b) (4 marks)

(b) Outline four requirements of the Data Protection Act.

Answer

Lawfulness, Fairness and Transparency: The Data Protection Act requires that personal data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently. Processing must have a legal basis, be conducted in a manner that is fair to the data subject, and data subjects must be informed about how their data is being processed in a clear and accessible way. Transparency builds trust and ensures individuals are aware of data processing activities.

Purpose Limitation: Personal data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes. Data should only be used for the reasons stated at the time of collection, and any new uses must be compatible with the original purpose or require new consent. Purpose limitation prevents function creep and ensures data is used as intended.

Data Minimization: The Act mandates data minimization, meaning that personal data collected should be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed. Organizations should only collect and retain the minimum amount of data needed to achieve their specified purposes, reducing the risk of data breaches and privacy intrusions. Data minimization promotes efficiency and data protection.

Security and Confidentiality: The Data Protection Act requires organizations to implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure the security and confidentiality of personal data. This includes protecting data against unauthorized or unlawful processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage. Security measures must be proportionate to the risks and include safeguards to maintain data integrity and confidentiality, safeguarding individuals' privacy rights.

4. (d) (6 marks)

(d) Distinguish between magnetic and optical media devices giving an example in each case.

Answer

Magnetic Media Devices:

Magnetic media devices store data by magnetizing a ferromagnetic material. Data is recorded and retrieved using magnetic read/write heads that detect and alter the magnetic orientation on the media surface. Magnetic media devices are typically non-volatile, retaining data even when power is off. They are characterized by their storage capacity, access speed, and durability.

Example: Hard Disk Drive (HDD): A hard disk drive is a magnetic storage device that uses spinning platters coated with magnetic material to store data. Read/write heads move across the platters to access and modify data. HDDs are commonly used for primary storage in computers due to their high capacity and relatively low cost per gigabyte. They are examples of magnetic media devices.

Optical Media Devices:

Optical media devices store and retrieve data using lasers to read and write patterns on reflective surfaces of optical discs. Data is encoded in the form of pits and lands on the disc surface, which are detected by laser beams. Optical media devices are also typically non-volatile and are known for their portability and archival capabilities.

Example: Blu-ray Disc (BD): A Blu-ray disc is an optical storage device that uses a blue laser to read and write data on a disc. Blu-ray discs offer high storage capacities and are used for storing high-definition video, large software applications, and data backups. Blu-ray technology utilizes a shorter wavelength laser, allowing for smaller pits and higher data density compared to CDs and DVDs, making them an example of optical media devices.

Key Differences:

The fundamental difference lies in the data storage mechanism. Magnetic media uses magnetic fields to store data, while optical media uses lasers and reflective surfaces. Magnetic media devices like HDDs are known for high capacity and speed, whereas optical media like Blu-ray discs are valued for portability and archival properties. The technology and methods of data encoding and retrieval differ significantly between magnetic and optical storage devices.

(ii) With the aid of a diagram, describe a mesh network topology.

Answer

Mesh Network Topology:

A mesh network topology is a network setup where devices (nodes) are interconnected directly and dynamically, with each node acting as a relay for the network. In a full mesh topology, every node is connected to every other node in the network, providing multiple paths for data transmission. In a partial mesh, some nodes are connected to multiple but not all other nodes. Mesh networks are characterized by redundancy, self-healing capabilities, and robust communication paths.

Soft-Copy Output:

Soft-copy output is not physically tangible and exists in digital format. Examples include documents viewed on a computer monitor, e-books, digital images, and files displayed on a smartphone screen. Soft-copy output is easily modifiable, shareable electronically, and does not require physical media.

Hard-Copy Output:

Hard-copy output refers to physical, tangible copies of data or information printed on paper or other physical media. Hard-copy output is produced by output devices such as printers and plotters. Examples include printed documents, reports, photographs, and graphics on paper. Hard-copy output is permanent, physically portable, and can be directly handled and viewed without electronic devices.

Key Differences:

The fundamental difference is tangibility. Soft-copy output is digital and viewed electronically, while hard-copy output is physical and printed on paper or other media. Soft copies are easily editable and shareable electronically, whereas hard copies are permanent and physically distributable. Soft copies are viewed on screens, and hard copies are physically handled and read.

7. (c) (i) (2 marks)

(c) (i) Explain the reason for storing firmware in ROM rather than in the hard disk.

Answer

Firmware is stored in ROM (Read-Only Memory) rather than a hard disk because ROM is non-volatile and retains its contents even when power is turned off. Firmware, such as BIOS or UEFI, contains essential boot instructions needed to start a computer. Storing it in ROM ensures that these critical instructions are always available and cannot be accidentally erased or modified, guaranteeing the system can reliably boot up every time it is powered on. Hard disks are volatile and require an operating system to be loaded first, making them unsuitable for storing initial boot firmware.

7. (c) (ii) (2 marks)

(ii) Outline two functions of the control unit in a computer system.

Answer

Instruction Decoding: The control unit decodes instructions fetched from memory to understand the operation to be performed. It interprets the opcode and operand fields of each instruction to determine what actions the CPU needs to execute. Instruction decoding is crucial for translating program instructions into a sequence of control signals that direct other CPU components.

Control Signal Generation: The control unit generates control signals that coordinate and manage the activities of various components within the CPU and computer system. These control signals direct the ALU, registers, memory, and I/O devices to perform specific operations in a synchronized manner, ensuring proper execution of instructions and data flow within the system. Control signal generation is essential for orchestrating computer operations.
