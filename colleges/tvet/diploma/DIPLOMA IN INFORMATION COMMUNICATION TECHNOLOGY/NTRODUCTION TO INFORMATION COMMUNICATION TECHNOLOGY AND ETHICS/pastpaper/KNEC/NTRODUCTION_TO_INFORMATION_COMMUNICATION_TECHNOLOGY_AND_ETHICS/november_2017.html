Exam Code: 2920/101

Duration: 3 hours

Period: November 2017

1. (a) (6 marks)

Outline six limitations of the first generation computers.

Answer

First generation computers, using vacuum tubes, were characterized by significant limitations.

Large Size and Bulkiness: Vacuum tubes were bulky components, leading to very large and cumbersome computers that occupied significant space, often entire rooms.

High Power Consumption and Heat Generation: Vacuum tubes consumed substantial amounts of power and generated excessive heat, requiring extensive cooling systems and leading to high operational costs.

Limited Reliability and Frequent Failures: Vacuum tubes were prone to frequent burnout and failures, resulting in low reliability and requiring constant maintenance and component replacement.

Slow Processing Speed: Compared to later generations, first generation computers had very slow processing speeds, limiting their computational capabilities and the complexity of tasks they could handle.

Limited Memory Capacity: Memory in first generation computers was limited and expensive, restricting the size of programs and data they could process.

Machine Language Programming: Programming was done in machine language, which was complex, tedious, and error-prone, requiring specialized skills and making software development very challenging.

1. (b) (4 marks)

Describe each of the following types of computers, stating an area of application for each: (i) super computers; (ii) mini computers.

Answer

Supercomputers and minicomputers represent different scales of computing power and application.

(i) Supercomputers: Supercomputers are the most powerful type of computers, designed for extremely high-speed computation and processing vast amounts of data.

Area of Application: They are primarily used for complex scientific and engineering tasks requiring massive calculations, such as weather forecasting, climate modeling, nuclear research, fluid dynamics simulations, and cryptography.

(ii) Minicomputers: Minicomputers are mid-range computers, smaller and less powerful than mainframes and supercomputers but more powerful than microcomputers.

Area of Application: They are typically used in small to medium-sized businesses, departments within large organizations, and for tasks such as industrial process control, data processing for multiple users, and running departmental servers.

1. (c) (4 marks)

Distinguish between multiprogramming and multiprocessing as used in computer systems.

Answer

Multiprogramming and multiprocessing are both techniques for enhancing computer efficiency but differ in their execution approach.

Multiprogramming: Multiprogramming is an operating system technique that allows multiple programs to reside in main memory simultaneously. The CPU switches between these programs, executing a portion of each in turn. However, at any given instant, only one program is actively being executed by a single CPU. The goal is to maximize CPU utilization by keeping it busy when one program is waiting for I/O operations.

Multiprocessing: Multiprocessing is a hardware technique that uses multiple CPUs (processors) within a single computer system. These CPUs can execute multiple processes or threads concurrently and simultaneously. This allows for true parallel processing, where different parts of a program or different programs can run at the exact same time, leading to significant performance improvements for computationally intensive tasks and overall system throughput.

2. (a) (4 marks)

Describe each of the following types of computer memories used in a computer system: (i) SRAM: (ii) EPROM.

Answer

SRAM and EPROM are distinct types of semiconductor memory used in computer systems.

(i) SRAM (Static Random Access Memory): SRAM is a type of volatile memory that uses flip-flops to store each bit of data. It is known for its speed and low power consumption in idle state. SRAM does not require periodic refreshing to maintain data as long as power is supplied.

(ii) EPROM (Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory): EPROM is a type of non-volatile memory that can be programmed and erased multiple times. Data is stored by trapping charge in an insulated gate region. EPROM can be erased by exposing it to ultraviolet (UV) light, and then reprogrammed electrically. It retains data even when power is off, making it suitable for firmware storage.

2. (c) (6 marks)

Explain three circumstances that would necessitate an organization to purchase an impact printer.

Answer

Despite advancements in printing technology, impact printers remain necessary in certain organizational circumstances.

Multi-part Forms Printing: When an organization needs to print on multi-part forms (carbon or carbonless forms) to create multiple copies simultaneously, impact printers are essential. Impact printers use physical impact to transfer ink to paper, allowing them to print through multiple layers to produce carbon copies. This is crucial for invoices, receipts, shipping documents, and other forms requiring immediate multiple copies.

Harsh or Dirty Environments: In industrial, warehouse, or other harsh environments where dust, extreme temperatures, or humidity are prevalent, impact printers are more durable and reliable than laser or inkjet printers. Impact printers are mechanically simpler and less sensitive to environmental factors, making them suitable for operational settings where other printer types might malfunction or require frequent maintenance due to environmental conditions.

Low Cost Per Page for High Volume Printing: For organizations with very high-volume printing needs, especially for text-based documents, impact printers, particularly dot-matrix printers, can offer a lower cost per page compared to laser or inkjet printers. Ribbons for dot-matrix printers are relatively inexpensive, and the printers themselves are designed for high duty cycles and longevity, making them economically advantageous for bulk printing of reports, invoices, or data logs where print quality is less critical than cost and volume.

3. (a) (4 marks)

Explain the function of each of the following keyboard keys: (i) Num Lock; (ii) Escape.

Answer

Num Lock and Escape are common keyboard keys with distinct functions in computer interaction.

(i) Num Lock Key: The Num Lock key (Numeric Lock) is a toggle key that, when activated, enables the numeric keypad section of a keyboard to function for numeric input (digits 0-9, decimal point, arithmetic operators). When Num Lock is deactivated, these keys often serve alternative functions such as cursor control (arrow keys), page navigation (Page Up, Page Down), and editing (Insert, Delete).

(ii) Escape Key: The Escape key (Esc) is typically used to interrupt or cancel the current operation or action. Its function varies depending on the application or context. Commonly, it is used to close dialog boxes, exit full-screen mode, stop a loading web page, or cancel a command in a command-line interface.

3. (b) (4 marks)

Describe each of the following terms as used in computer systems: (i) plug and play; (ii) BIOS.

Answer

Plug and play and BIOS are fundamental concepts in computer systems functionality.

(i) Plug and Play: Plug and Play (PnP) is a technology that allows a computer system to automatically detect, configure, and install hardware devices without requiring manual user intervention. When a PnP-compatible device is connected to a computer, the operating system automatically recognizes the device, loads the necessary drivers, and configures system resources so that the device can be used immediately. This simplifies hardware installation and configuration for users.

(ii) BIOS (Basic Input/Output System): BIOS is firmware embedded on a computer's motherboard. It is the first software to run when a computer is powered on. The BIOS performs essential startup routines, including the Power-On Self-Test (POST) to check hardware components, initializes hardware, and loads the operating system from a storage device into memory. The BIOS also provides a basic interface for configuring hardware settings and boot options.

4. (a) (4 marks)

Describe each of the following types of computer buses: (i) data bus; (ii) control bus.

Answer

Data bus and control bus are essential components of a computer's system bus, facilitating communication within the system.

(i) Data Bus: The data bus is a bidirectional pathway that carries actual data between the CPU, memory, and peripheral devices. It determines the amount of data that can be transferred at one time. The width of the data bus (e.g., 32-bit, 64-bit) affects the system's performance; a wider data bus allows for faster data transfer rates and improved overall system speed.

(ii) Control Bus: The control bus is a unidirectional or bidirectional pathway that transmits control signals between the CPU and other components of the computer system. Control signals manage and coordinate the activities of different components, such as memory read/write operations, interrupt requests, and device selection. The control bus ensures proper timing and synchronization of data transfers and operations within the computer system.

4. (c) (4 marks)

Distinguish between analogue and digital computers.

Answer

Analogue and digital computers differ fundamentally in how they represent and process data.

Analogue Computers: Analogue computers represent data as continuous physical quantities, such as voltage, current, or mechanical motion. They operate by directly manipulating these continuous quantities to perform calculations. Analogue computers are specialized for specific types of problems, particularly those involving physical systems and continuous data. Examples include slide rules, mechanical calculators, and early electronic analogue computers used for simulations.

Digital Computers: Digital computers represent data in discrete form, using binary digits (bits) 0 and 1. They perform calculations using digital logic circuits and arithmetic operations on these discrete values. Digital computers are general-purpose and versatile, capable of performing a wide range of tasks by executing programs written in software. Modern computers, from smartphones to supercomputers, are digital computers, known for their accuracy, programmability, and ability to process complex information.

5. (a) (6 marks)

Explain each of the following terms as used in ICT and ethics: (i) phishing; (ii) firewall; (iii) service pack.

Answer

Phishing, firewall, and service pack are important terms in ICT and related ethical considerations.

(i) Phishing: In ICT and ethics, phishing refers to a deceptive cybercrime where attackers impersonate legitimate entities (e.g., banks, organizations) to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, credit card details, or personal data. Phishing is typically carried out through fraudulent emails, websites, or messages that appear genuine but are designed to steal information for malicious purposes like identity theft or financial fraud. Ethically, phishing is a serious violation of trust and privacy.

(ii) Firewall: In ICT security, a firewall is a network security system that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules. It acts as a barrier between a trusted internal network and untrusted external networks (like the internet). Firewalls can be hardware or software-based and help to protect computer systems and networks from unauthorized access, malware, and cyberattacks by blocking or allowing specific network traffic based on security policies. Ethically, firewalls are essential tools for maintaining data security and network integrity.

(iii) Service Pack: In ICT, a service pack (SP) is a cumulative update package that includes a collection of bug fixes, security updates, performance improvements, and sometimes new features for a software application or operating system. Service packs are released by software vendors to address known issues, enhance software stability, and improve overall user experience. Applying service packs is ethically important for maintaining software security, reliability, and protecting systems from vulnerabilities. Neglecting to install service packs can leave systems exposed to security risks and operational problems.

5. (c) (4 marks)

Distinguish between fax machine and modem as used in ICT and ethics.

Answer

Fax machines and modems are both communication technologies but serve different purposes in ICT, with distinct ethical implications.

Fax Machine: A fax machine is a device used to transmit and receive documents over telephone lines. It scans a physical document, converts it into an electronic signal, and sends it to another fax machine, which then prints a physical copy of the document. In ICT ethics, fax machines raise privacy concerns if confidential documents are sent to shared or unsecured fax machines, potentially leading to unauthorized access. Secure handling and transmission of sensitive information via fax are ethical considerations.

Modem (Modulator-Demodulator): A modem is a device that modulates digital signals from a computer into analogue signals for transmission over communication media (like telephone lines or cable TV lines) and demodulates incoming analogue signals back into digital signals for computer processing. Modems are essential for internet access and data communication. Ethically, modems are related to issues of internet access and digital divide. Ensuring equitable access to internet and communication technologies facilitated by modems is an ethical concern, as is responsible use of internet access and data transmission.

6. (b) (4 marks)

Differentiate between dumb terminal and intelligent terminal as applied in computer systems.

Answer

Dumb terminals and intelligent terminals represent different levels of processing capability in computer terminal systems.

Dumb Terminal: A dumb terminal is a basic input/output device that relies entirely on a central server or mainframe computer for processing. It has minimal or no processing capabilities of its own. Dumb terminals primarily function to display information received from the server and transmit user input (keyboard, mouse) back to the server for processing. Examples include basic text-based terminals used to access mainframe systems.

Intelligent Terminal: An intelligent terminal, also known as a smart terminal or workstation, has its own processing capabilities and memory. It can perform some processing tasks independently, reducing the workload on the central server. Intelligent terminals can run local applications, process data locally, and communicate with the central server for specific tasks or data access. Modern PCs, laptops, and workstations functioning in a client-server environment can be considered intelligent terminals.

6. (d) (6 marks)

With the aid of a diagram in each case, describe the following types of network topologies (i) ring; (ii) bus.

Answer

Ring and bus topologies are fundamental network architectures with distinct connection patterns and operational characteristics.

(i) Ring Topology:

Diagram: (Visual representation would show devices connected in a closed loop, each device connected to exactly two others, forming a ring. Data flows in one direction around the ring.)

Description: In a ring topology, each device (node) is connected to exactly two other devices, forming a closed loop or ring. Data travels in one direction (unidirectionally or bidirectionally in some variations) around the ring from one device to the next until it reaches its destination. Each device in the ring acts as a repeater, forwarding the signal to the next device. Ring topologies were historically used in Token Ring networks.

(ii) Bus Topology:

Diagram: (Visual representation would show a single central cable (bus) with all devices connected directly to this cable. Data is transmitted along the bus and can be received by all devices.)

Description: In a bus topology, all devices (nodes) are connected to a single central cable, called the bus or backbone. Data is transmitted along the bus and is available to be received by all devices connected to the bus. Devices listen to the bus traffic and only respond to messages addressed to them. Bus topologies are relatively simple to set up but can suffer from performance issues with increased network traffic and are vulnerable to cable breaks disrupting the entire network. Ethernet networks historically used bus topologies before switching to star topologies.

7. (c) (6 marks)

Jacob intends to secure the company's data from being lost. Explain three software measures that he could put in place.

Answer

To secure company data from loss, Jacob can implement several software-based measures focused on backup, recovery, and data integrity.

Regular Data Backup Software and Schedules: Implement robust data backup software and establish regular backup schedules (daily, weekly, etc.) to create copies of critical company data. Backup software automates the backup process, ensuring data is consistently backed up to secondary storage locations (external drives, network storage, cloud). Regular backups enable data recovery in case of hardware failure, data corruption, or accidental deletion, minimizing data loss.

Data Versioning and Recovery Software: Utilize software with data versioning capabilities to track changes to files and maintain multiple versions of data over time. Versioning software allows for restoring previous versions of files, enabling recovery from accidental modifications, data corruption, or ransomware attacks. DLP software can identify and block unauthorized data transfers (e.g., email attachments, USB drives, cloud uploads) and enforce data security policies. DLP helps prevent data leakage due to employee negligence or malicious intent, reducing the risk of data loss and breaches. Enforce clear data handling policies and train employees on data security best practices to complement DLP software.

7. (d) (4 marks)

Distinguish between confidentiality and authenticity as applied in ICT and ethics.

Answer

Confidentiality and authenticity are distinct but crucial concepts in ICT security and ethics, addressing different aspects of data protection.

Confidentiality: Confidentiality in ICT and ethics refers to protecting sensitive information from unauthorized access and disclosure. It ensures that only authorized individuals or systems can access and view confidential data. Measures to maintain confidentiality include encryption, access controls, data masking, and secure storage practices. Ethically, confidentiality is about respecting privacy and protecting sensitive information from unauthorized eyes.

Authenticity: Authenticity in ICT and ethics refers to verifying the genuineness and origin of information or data. It ensures that data is from a trusted source and has not been tampered with or falsified. Authentication mechanisms include digital signatures, certificates, and secure authentication protocols. Ethically, authenticity is about trust and reliability of information, ensuring that data is verifiable and not misrepresented or forged.

8. (a) (3 marks)

Outline three advantages of USB over serial ports as used in computer systems.

Answer

USB (Universal Serial Bus) has superseded serial ports in computer systems due to several significant advantages.

Higher Data Transfer Rates: USB offers significantly higher data transfer rates compared to serial ports. USB standards (USB 2.0, USB 3.0, USB 3.1, USB 4) provide much faster data communication speeds, enabling quicker data transfer for peripherals like external drives, printers, and multimedia devices. Serial ports have limited bandwidth, making them unsuitable for high-speed data transfer.

Plug and Play Capability and Ease of Use: USB is designed for plug and play functionality, allowing for easy connection and disconnection of devices without needing to restart the computer. USB devices are automatically detected and configured by the operating system, simplifying user experience. Serial ports often require manual configuration and are less user-friendly for device connection and removal.

Versatility and Power Delivery: USB is a versatile interface that can handle various types of devices, including data transfer, video, audio, and power delivery. USB ports can provide power to connected devices, eliminating the need for separate power adapters for some peripherals. Serial ports are primarily designed for data communication and do not offer power delivery capabilities, limiting their versatility.

8. (b) (4 marks)

Describe each of the following types of disk storage technologies as used in computer systems: (i) optical; (ii) magnetic.

Answer

Optical and magnetic storage technologies are fundamental methods for data storage in computer systems.

(i) Optical Storage: Optical storage technologies use lasers to read and write data on optical discs, such as CDs, DVDs, and Blu-ray discs. Data is stored as microscopic pits and lands on the disc surface, which reflect light differently when read by a laser. Optical storage is known for its portability, durability, and relatively high storage capacity. It is commonly used for software distribution, multimedia content, and data archiving.

(ii) Magnetic Storage: Magnetic storage technologies store data by magnetizing ferromagnetic material on storage media like hard disk drives (HDDs) and floppy disks. Data is read and written using magnetic heads that detect and alter the magnetic orientation of the storage medium. Magnetic storage is characterized by high storage capacity, relatively fast access speeds (especially in HDDs), and cost-effectiveness. HDDs are the primary storage medium in most computers for operating systems, applications, and user data.

8. (c) (8 marks)

Computer operators are exposed to health problems due to prolonged working for long hours with computers. Explain four of these health problems.

Answer

Prolonged computer use exposes computer operators to various health problems related to ergonomics, posture, and extended screen time.

Repetitive Strain Injuries (RSIs): RSIs are a common health problem for computer operators, particularly affecting the wrists, hands, arms, neck, and shoulders. Repetitive tasks like typing and mouse use can strain muscles, tendons, and nerves, leading to conditions like carpal tunnel syndrome, tendonitis, and tenosynovitis. Symptoms include pain, numbness, tingling, and reduced hand function, impacting work ability and requiring medical intervention and ergonomic adjustments.

Eye Strain and Vision Problems (Computer Vision Syndrome - CVS): Prolonged screen time can cause eye strain, fatigue, dry eyes, blurred vision, and headaches, collectively known as Computer Vision Syndrome (CVS). Focusing on screens for extended periods reduces blink rate, leading to dry eyes and eye fatigue. Glare from screens, improper lighting, and incorrect monitor distance contribute to eye strain. CVS can reduce visual comfort, concentration, and work efficiency, requiring ergonomic monitor setups, regular eye breaks, and eye care.

Back and Neck Pain (Musculoskeletal Disorders - MSDs): Poor posture, prolonged sitting, and non-ergonomic workstations contribute to back and neck pain and other Musculoskeletal Disorders (MSDs). Incorrect chair height, lack of back support, and improper monitor placement lead to strained muscles and spinal stress. MSDs from prolonged computer work can cause chronic pain, reduced mobility, and decreased quality of life, necessitating ergonomic assessments, posture correction, and regular breaks for movement and stretching.

Sedentary Lifestyle and Related Health Risks: Prolonged computer work often involves a sedentary lifestyle with extended periods of sitting and minimal physical activity. A sedentary lifestyle increases the risk of obesity, cardiovascular diseases, type 2 diabetes, and other health problems. Lack of physical activity, combined with prolonged sitting, negatively impacts overall health and well-being. Encouraging regular breaks, promoting physical activity, and incorporating movement into the workday are essential to mitigate these risks for computer operators.

Answer

(ii) Explain two advantages of this interface to the user.
