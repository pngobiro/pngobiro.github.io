1. (a) (i) (3 marks)

State three benefits that can be realised by an organisation through the implementation of procurement policies for its computer hardware.

Answer

Cost Savings: Procurement policies ensure competitive bidding and bulk purchasing, leading to lower prices for computer hardware. Standardized procurement processes prevent overspending and ensure budget adherence, optimizing hardware investments.

Quality Assurance: Procurement policies establish quality standards and vendor selection criteria, ensuring that the organization acquires reliable and high-performance computer hardware. Policies include quality checks and warranties, minimizing risks associated with faulty or substandard equipment.

Efficient Asset Management: Procurement policies streamline the acquisition, tracking, and disposal of computer hardware assets. Standardized procedures for inventory management, lifecycle management, and disposal ensure efficient utilization of resources and compliance with regulatory requirements.

1. (a) (ii) (2 marks)

Outline two issues addressed by the Computer Misuse Act.

Answer

Unauthorized Access to Computer Systems: The Computer Misuse Act addresses unauthorized access to computer systems and networks. It criminalizes activities such as hacking, gaining unauthorized entry into computer systems, and accessing data without permission, aiming to protect digital assets and prevent cybercrime.

Data Interference and System Disruption: The Act addresses data interference and system disruption by making it illegal to impair the operation of computers, alter data without authorization, or introduce malware. It aims to safeguard the integrity and availability of computer systems and data, preventing malicious activities that can disrupt services or cause damage.

1. (c) (i) (4 marks)

Differentiate between a microcomputer and a minicomputer.

Answer

Microcomputer:

A microcomputer is a small, relatively inexpensive computer with a microprocessor as its central processing unit (CPU). Designed for individual use, microcomputers are commonly used in homes, offices, and small businesses. Examples include desktop computers, laptop computers, and personal digital assistants (PDAs). Microcomputers are characterized by their compact size, affordability, and suitability for personal productivity and general-purpose computing tasks.

Minicomputer:

A minicomputer is a mid-range computer that is more powerful and capable than a microcomputer but less powerful than a mainframe computer. Minicomputers are designed to serve multiple users simultaneously and support more complex applications. Historically used in departments and medium-sized organizations, minicomputers bridge the gap between personal computers and large-scale systems. They are known for their multi-user capabilities and enhanced processing power compared to microcomputers.

Key Differences:

The primary differences lie in processing power, user capacity, and scale of application. Microcomputers are for individual use with lower processing power, while minicomputers are multi-user systems with greater processing capabilities. Minicomputers historically served organizational departments, whereas microcomputers are for personal or small business use. Minicomputers represent a mid-range computing solution, larger and more powerful than microcomputers but smaller and less powerful than mainframes.

1. (c) (ii) (4 marks)

Distinguish between general and special purpose computers, giving one application area in each case.

Answer

General Purpose Computers:

General-purpose computers are designed to perform a wide variety of tasks and are programmable for many different applications. They are versatile and flexible, capable of executing diverse software programs and handling various types of data processing. General-purpose computers are used across numerous domains for tasks ranging from word processing and internet browsing to software development and data analysis.

Application Area: Office Productivity and Internet Browsing: General-purpose computers, such as desktop PCs and laptops, are widely used in office environments for productivity tasks like document creation, spreadsheet management, email communication, and presentations. They are also essential for internet browsing, accessing online resources, and running a broad range of software applications for various purposes.

Special Purpose Computers:

Special-purpose computers are designed to perform a specific task or set of tasks very efficiently. They are dedicated to a particular application and are optimized for that purpose, often sacrificing versatility for performance in their designated area. Special-purpose computers are used in scenarios requiring high efficiency and dedicated functionality for narrowly defined tasks.

Application Area: Industrial Control Systems: Special-purpose computers are crucial in industrial control systems for automating and managing manufacturing processes, machinery, and equipment in factories and plants. These systems are designed for real-time control, data acquisition, and precise operation of industrial processes, ensuring efficiency and reliability in specific industrial applications.

Key Differences:

The primary distinction lies in their intended use and flexibility. General-purpose computers are versatile and can perform many tasks, while special-purpose computers are designed for specific, dedicated tasks. General-purpose computers prioritize flexibility and broad applicability, whereas special-purpose computers focus on efficiency and high performance in a narrow application domain.

2. (a) (6 marks)

2. (a) A firm intends to recruit a database administrator, a network administrator and a computer operator in its ICT department. Outline two roles of each of these ICT personnel in the company.

Answer

Database Administrator (DBA):

Database Design and Maintenance: A database administrator is responsible for designing, implementing, and maintaining databases. Roles include creating database schemas, setting up database systems, managing database security, and ensuring database performance and reliability. They optimize database structures, manage user access, and perform backup and recovery operations to maintain data integrity and availability.

Data Management and Security: DBAs manage data integrity, security, and accessibility within the organization. Roles involve implementing data security measures, controlling data access permissions, ensuring data backup and recovery procedures are in place, and monitoring database performance for optimal operation. They enforce data policies and ensure compliance with data governance standards.

Network Administrator:

Network Infrastructure Management: A network administrator manages the organization's network infrastructure, including LANs, WANs, and wireless networks. Roles encompass setting up and configuring network hardware (routers, switches, firewalls), managing network cabling and connectivity, and monitoring network performance. They ensure network availability, security, and optimal functionality for organizational communication and data transfer.

Network Security and Troubleshooting: Network administrators implement and maintain network security measures and troubleshoot network issues. Roles include configuring firewalls and intrusion detection systems, managing network access controls, monitoring for security breaches, and diagnosing and resolving network connectivity problems. They ensure network security, stability, and efficient operation.

Computer Operator:

System Operation and Monitoring: A computer operator oversees the daily operations of computer systems, including servers, mainframes, and peripherals. Roles involve monitoring system performance, running scheduled jobs, managing batch processing, and responding to system alerts or errors. They ensure smooth system operation and address routine operational tasks.

Data Backup and Recovery Operations: Computer operators perform data backup and recovery operations to safeguard organizational data. Roles include executing scheduled data backups, verifying backup integrity, managing backup media, and performing data recovery in case of system failures or data loss incidents. They ensure data protection and business continuity through effective backup and recovery procedures.

2. (b) (4 marks)

(b) State two characteristics of each of the following generations of computers in terms of hardware technology:

(i) first generation;

Answer

(i) First Generation Computers (1940s-1950s):

Vacuum Tubes: First-generation computers used vacuum tubes as their primary electronic components. Vacuum tubes were bulky, unreliable, generated significant heat, and consumed large amounts of power. Their use resulted in large-sized, power-intensive, and heat-generating computers.

Magnetic Drum Memory: Memory in first-generation computers was typically magnetic drum memory. Magnetic drums provided primary storage but were slow, had limited capacity, and were less reliable compared to later memory technologies. Memory limitations and slow access speeds constrained processing capabilities.

2. (b) (4 marks)

(ii) second generation.

Answer

(ii) Second Generation Computers (1950s-1960s):

Transistors: Second-generation computers replaced vacuum tubes with transistors. Transistors were smaller, more reliable, generated less heat, consumed less power, and were faster than vacuum tubes. Transistor technology led to smaller, more energy-efficient, and more reliable computers compared to first-generation machines.

Magnetic Core Memory: Second-generation computers used magnetic core memory as primary storage. Magnetic core memory was faster, more reliable, and had larger capacity compared to magnetic drums. Improved memory technology enhanced processing speeds and allowed for more complex programs and data handling.

3. (b) (i) (3 marks)

3. (b) (i) A hardware shop intends to acquire a printer to be used for printing invoices in triplicate using carbon paper. Suggest the most appropriate type of printer that is suitable for the task justifying your answer.

Answer

The most appropriate type of printer for printing invoices in triplicate using carbon paper is a Dot Matrix Printer. Dot matrix printers use impact technology, physically striking an ink ribbon against the paper to create characters and images. This impact mechanism is essential for printing through multiple layers of carbon paper to produce triplicate copies simultaneously. Dot matrix printers are reliable for multipart forms and offer cost-effective printing for high-volume invoice generation.

3. (c) (ii) (3 marks)

(ii) State three circumstances under which a plotter may be preferred over a printer.

Answer

Large Format Printing: Plotters are preferred over printers when large format output is required, such as architectural blueprints, engineering drawings, posters, and banners. Plotters can handle wide paper rolls and create prints in sizes that exceed the capabilities of standard printers, making them suitable for large-scale graphics and technical documents.

Precision and Line Accuracy: Plotters excel in producing highly precise lines and accurate vector graphics, essential for technical drawings, CAD designs, and detailed maps. Plotters use pens or cutting tools to create continuous lines with high precision, whereas printers typically rasterize images, which may result in less sharp lines for intricate drawings.

Specialized Media Handling: Plotters can handle a wider range of media types and thicknesses compared to standard printers. They can print on thick paper, vinyl, canvas, and other specialized media used in signage, technical drawings, and graphic arts. Plotters' media handling versatility makes them suitable for diverse output requirements beyond regular paper printing.

4. (c) (i) (1 mark)

4. (c) (i) Name a network topology which contains a central node for message switching between the attached computers.

Answer

Star topology.

4. (c) (ii) (4 marks)

(ii) Explain two weakness of the topology in (i).

Answer

Central Point of Failure: Star topology has a central point of failure in the central node (e.g., hub or switch). If the central node fails, the entire network collapses, and communication between all connected devices is disrupted. This dependency on a single point makes the network vulnerable to complete network outages if the central node malfunctions.

Scalability Limitations: While star topology is generally scalable, very large star networks can face scalability limitations. As more devices are added to the central node, it can become a bottleneck, potentially leading to network congestion and reduced performance. The central node's capacity to handle increasing traffic and connections can limit the network's scalability in very large deployments.

4. (c) (iii) (6 marks)

(iii) Describe three types of networks based on geographic scope.

Answer

Local Area Network (LAN): A Local Area Network (LAN) is a network that connects computers and devices within a limited geographical area, such as a home, office, school, or building. LANs are typically used to share resources like printers, files, and internet access among users in close proximity. They are characterized by high-speed data transfer rates and are commonly implemented using Ethernet or Wi-Fi technologies.

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN): A Metropolitan Area Network (MAN) covers a larger geographic area than a LAN, typically spanning a city or metropolitan region. MANs connect multiple LANs together, enabling communication and resource sharing across a wider urban area. They are often used by large organizations, universities, or government agencies to connect facilities across a city. MANs utilize technologies like fiber optics and microwave links to cover metropolitan distances.

Wide Area Network (WAN): A Wide Area Network (WAN) spans a large geographical area, often covering countries, continents, or even the entire globe. WANs connect geographically dispersed networks, enabling long-distance communication and data sharing. The Internet is the largest example of a WAN, connecting billions of devices worldwide. WANs utilize technologies like leased lines, satellite links, and the internet infrastructure to facilitate global connectivity.

5. (b) (4 marks)

(b) Distinguish between typical and custom software installation.

Answer

Typical Software Installation:

Typical software installation, also known as standard or default installation, is a common method where software is installed with pre-defined settings and options, suitable for most users. It usually involves minimal user input, following a straightforward wizard or setup program that automatically configures installation paths, components, and settings based on common preferences. Typical installation is designed for ease of use and quick setup for general users.

Custom Software Installation:

Custom software installation allows users to select specific components, features, and installation paths for the software. It provides flexibility for users to tailor the installation to their specific needs and preferences. Users can choose which modules to install, specify installation directories, configure advanced settings, and customize the software setup based on their requirements. Custom installation is preferred for users with specific needs or advanced technical knowledge.

Key Differences:

The main distinction is the level of user customization and control. Typical installation is automated with default settings for ease of use, while custom installation offers users the flexibility to choose specific components and settings. Typical installation is quicker and simpler, whereas custom installation is more detailed and tailored to individual user preferences or advanced configurations.

7. (c) (6 marks)

(c) With the aid of a block diagram, represent the basic elements of a computer system, indicating clearly the flow of data and control signals.

Answer

Block Diagram of Basic Elements of a Computer System:

(Unfortunately, I cannot create visual diagrams in this text-based format. However, I can describe the components and data flow for you to visualize or sketch.)

Components:

1. Input Unit: Devices like keyboard, mouse, scanner, etc., used to enter data and instructions into the computer.

2. Central Processing Unit (CPU): The brain of the computer, consisting of:

a. Control Unit (CU): Manages and coordinates all computer operations, fetches instructions, decodes them, and generates control signals.

b. Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU): Performs arithmetic calculations and logical operations on data.

c. Registers: High-speed storage locations within the CPU used for temporary data and instruction storage.

3. Memory Unit: Stores data and instructions. Includes:

a. Primary Memory (RAM): Volatile memory for temporary storage of data and instructions being actively processed.

b. Secondary Storage: Non-volatile memory (Hard Disk, SSD) for long-term data storage.

4. Output Unit: Devices like monitor, printer, speakers, etc., used to display or output processed information to the user.

Data Flow:

1. Data and instructions are entered into the computer through the Input Unit.

2. Data and instructions are stored in the Memory Unit (primarily RAM).

3. The Control Unit fetches instructions from memory and decodes them.

4. The Control Unit sends control signals to the ALU and Memory Unit to process data according to instructions.

5. The ALU performs arithmetic and logical operations on data.

6. Processed data is stored back in the Memory Unit.

7. The Output Unit receives processed data from memory and presents it to the user.

Control Signals Flow: The Control Unit sends control signals to all other units (Input, Memory, ALU, Output) to coordinate their operations. Control signals dictate data flow, operation execution, and synchronization of components.

Data Signals Flow: Data flows between Input Unit, Memory Unit, CPU (ALU and Registers), and Output Unit as per instructions and control signals. Data moves from input to memory, from memory to CPU for processing, back to memory for storage, and finally from memory to output for presentation.

8. (b) (4 marks)

(b) Distinguish between integrated software and software suites.

Answer

Integrated Software:

Integrated software refers to a single application that combines multiple functionalities and tools from different software categories into one unified program. It is designed as a single, cohesive unit where various modules or components are tightly integrated and share data seamlessly within the application. Integrated software aims to provide a comprehensive solution for diverse tasks within a single application environment.

Software Suites:

Software suites are collections of separate, standalone software applications that are bundled together and sold as a package. Each application within a software suite is a distinct program designed for a specific purpose (e.g., word processing, spreadsheet, presentation). While applications in a suite are designed to work together and may offer some level of integration, they remain separate programs that are launched and operated independently.

Key Differences:

The core difference lies in the level of integration and structure. Integrated software is a single application with combined functionalities, whereas software suites are bundles of separate applications. Integrated software offers seamless data sharing within one program, while suites consist of distinct programs that may have interoperability but are not inherently unified as a single application. Integrated software is a singular, comprehensive tool, while software suites are collections of individual applications bundled for convenience and sometimes cost-effectiveness.

8. (c) (4 marks)

(c) Outline four differences between primary and secondary storage in a computer system.

Answer

Volatility: Primary storage (RAM) is volatile memory, meaning it loses stored data when power is turned off. Secondary storage (Hard Disk, SSD) is non-volatile, retaining data even when power is removed. Volatility is a key distinction in how they retain data; primary storage is temporary, while secondary storage is permanent.

Access Speed: Primary storage (RAM) offers much faster data access speeds compared to secondary storage. RAM allows for rapid reading and writing of data, enabling quick CPU access for processing. Secondary storage devices have slower access times due to mechanical components (HDDs) or interface limitations, resulting in slower data retrieval and storage operations.

Cost per Gigabyte: Primary storage (RAM) is significantly more expensive per gigabyte compared to secondary storage. RAM is designed for high-speed performance, which increases its manufacturing cost. Secondary storage, especially HDDs, offers much lower cost per gigabyte, making it more economical for storing large volumes of data.

Purpose and Usage: Primary storage (RAM) is used for temporary storage of data and instructions that the CPU is actively processing. It serves as the main working memory of the computer. Secondary storage is used for long-term, persistent storage of data, applications, and files, even when the computer is powered off. Primary storage is for active processing, while secondary storage is for data archiving and long-term retention.

8. (d) (ii) (4 marks)

(ii) Describe the stages that the technician should follow in order to install the operating system successfully. Assume the hard disks had very large disk spaces.

Answer

Boot from Installation Media: The technician should first boot the computer from the operating system installation media, such as a DVD or USB drive. This typically involves changing the boot order in the BIOS/UEFI settings to prioritize booting from the installation media. Booting from the installation media initiates the OS setup process.

Partition and Format Hard Disk: During OS installation, the technician should partition and format the hard disk. With large disk spaces, creating multiple partitions (e.g., for OS, applications, data) may be advisable for better organization. The partition where the OS will be installed needs to be formatted with a compatible file system (e.g., NTFS for Windows) to prepare it for OS files.

Install Operating System Files: The OS installation process will copy necessary operating system files to the selected hard disk partition. This stage involves extracting installation files, configuring system settings, and setting up core OS components. The technician should follow on-screen prompts to provide required information like language, time zone, and user account details.

Configure System and Install Drivers: After OS file installation, the technician should configure system settings, such as network configurations, device settings, and user preferences. Install necessary hardware drivers for devices like graphics cards, network adapters, and peripherals to ensure proper hardware functionality and compatibility with the newly installed operating system.

8. (d) (iii) (2 marks)

(iii) During the installation of an operating system in one of the computers, the technician observed the following error message;

"Searching for Boot Record from CDROM..OK

Press any key to boot from CD..... Error loading operating system_"

Explain two possible causes of this error.

Answer

Corrupted or Incompatible Installation Media: The error "Error loading operating system" can be caused by corrupted or incompatible OS installation media (CD/DVD or USB drive). Damaged installation media may lack necessary boot files or have corrupted OS installation files, leading to failure in loading the operating system during setup.

Incorrect Boot Order or BIOS Settings: Incorrect boot order settings in BIOS/UEFI or improper configuration can cause the error. If the computer is not correctly set to boot from the installation media (CD/DVD or USB), or if BIOS settings are misconfigured, the system may fail to load the OS installation files, resulting in the "Error loading operating system" message.
