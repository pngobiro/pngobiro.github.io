DIPLOMA IN COMMUNICATION STUDIES

Exam Code: COMMUNICATION MODULE I

Duration: 3 hours

Period: November 2013

1. (3 marks)

Give three types of information that a supermarket may communicate to its clients.

Answer

Promotional offers and discounts to attract customers and boost sales, including weekly flyers, coupons, and special deals.

Product information such as new arrivals, product features, nutritional facts, and usage guidelines to inform purchasing decisions.

Operational information like store hours, locations, holiday schedules, and changes in services to facilitate customer visits and shopping experiences.

2. (3 marks)

State three forms of oral communication that may be used in an organisation.

Answer

Face-to-face meetings for direct discussions, team collaborations, and immediate feedback exchange among employees.

Telephone conversations for quick information sharing, clarifications, and remote communication with colleagues or clients.

Presentations and speeches for addressing larger groups, conveying important information, and motivating or informing employees.

3. (3 marks)

Give the meaning of each of the following forms of non-verbal communication.

Answer

**(i) Sitting with arms folded across the chest:**

This posture often indicates defensiveness, resistance, or a closed-off attitude. It can suggest that the person is not receptive to new ideas or is feeling insecure or uncomfortable in the situation.

**(ii) Looking at someone directly in the eye:**

Direct eye contact generally conveys confidence, sincerity, and attentiveness. In many cultures, it is seen as a sign of respect and honesty, indicating that the person is engaged and interested in the communication.

**(iii) Speaking calmly and distinctly at a reasonable pace:**

This verbal cue suggests composure, clarity of thought, and confidence. Speaking in a calm and distinct manner ensures that the message is easily understood and taken seriously, projecting professionalism and control.

4. (3 marks)

Identify three situations when it would be most appropriate to use the telephone in the office.

Answer

For urgent matters requiring immediate attention and real-time interaction, such as resolving a critical issue or conveying time-sensitive information.

When needing to have a detailed discussion or explanation that would be too lengthy or complex for a quick email or text message.

To establish a personal connection or build rapport with a client or colleague, as phone calls allow for a more human and interactive communication experience.

5. (4 marks)

State four items that may be contained in the appendices of a report.

Answer

Detailed statistical data or raw data sets that support the findings and analysis presented in the main body of the report, but are too extensive to include directly.

Questionnaires or survey instruments used for data collection, providing transparency on the methodology and allowing for scrutiny of the research process.

Transcripts of interviews or focus group discussions, offering verbatim records of qualitative data that informed the report's conclusions.

Supplementary documents such as maps, charts, diagrams, or photographs that provide additional context or visual support to the report's content but are not essential for the main narrative flow.

6. (4 marks)

State four advantages of storing information in electronic form.

Answer

Enhanced accessibility, allowing multiple users to retrieve and view information simultaneously from various locations, improving collaboration and efficiency.

Improved searchability, enabling quick and efficient retrieval of specific information using keywords and indexing, saving time and effort compared to manual searches.

Reduced physical storage space requirements, minimizing the need for bulky filing cabinets and physical archives, freeing up office space and lowering storage costs.

Easier sharing and distribution, facilitating rapid dissemination of information via email, networks, or cloud platforms, enhancing communication and knowledge sharing within and outside the organization.

7. (3 marks)

Outline three reasons that make it necessary for the notice and agenda of a meeting to be circulated to members in good time.

Answer

To allow members sufficient time to prepare for the meeting by reviewing the agenda items, gathering necessary information, and formulating their contributions.

To ensure members can make arrangements to attend the meeting, especially if travel or schedule adjustments are required, maximizing participation and representation.

To enable members to suggest additions or modifications to the agenda if needed, promoting inclusivity and ensuring that all relevant topics are addressed during the meeting.

8. (3 marks)

Highlight three qualities of a good questionnaire.

Answer

Clarity and simplicity in language, using straightforward and unambiguous questions that are easy for respondents to understand, avoiding jargon or complex phrasing.

Logical flow and structure, organizing questions in a coherent sequence that is easy to follow, starting with general questions and progressing to more specific or sensitive topics.

Objectivity and neutrality, phrasing questions in a non-leading and unbiased manner to avoid influencing responses, ensuring that the questionnaire accurately captures respondents' views without imposing researcher bias.

9. (3 marks)

State three types of interviews that may be conducted in an organization.

Answer

Employment interviews, used to assess job applicants' suitability for various roles within the organization, evaluating skills, experience, and cultural fit.

Performance appraisal interviews, conducted to review employee performance, provide feedback, set goals, and discuss career development opportunities.

Exit interviews, held with employees leaving the organization to understand their reasons for departure and gather feedback for organizational improvement.

10. (3 marks)

Identify three situations when graphs may be used in communication.

Answer

Presenting sales trends over time to illustrate growth, decline, or seasonal patterns, making it easier to understand performance at a glance.

Comparing market share of different products or companies to visually represent competitive positions and market dynamics.

Showing budget allocations across departments or projects to provide a clear overview of resource distribution and spending priorities.

12. (16 marks)

In about 250 words, write an essay on: "The Benefits of Improved Road Networks to the Kenyan Economy".

Answer

Improved road networks are critical infrastructure for Kenya's economic development, offering multifaceted benefits that stimulate growth across various sectors. Firstly, enhanced road infrastructure significantly boosts trade and commerce by facilitating faster and more efficient transportation of goods. Reduced travel times and lower transportation costs enable businesses to access wider markets, both domestically and regionally, fostering increased trade volumes and economic activity. This improved connectivity is particularly vital for agricultural sectors, allowing farmers to transport perishable goods to markets promptly, minimizing post-harvest losses and enhancing food security.

Secondly, better roads are essential for tourism, a key revenue earner for Kenya. Accessible and well-maintained roads enable tourists to reach diverse attractions, including national parks, coastal resorts, and cultural sites, more comfortably and safely. This enhanced accessibility expands tourism circuits, encourages repeat visits, and supports the growth of tourism-related businesses such as hotels, tour operators, and local crafts. Furthermore, improved infrastructure enhances Kenya's attractiveness as a regional tourism hub, attracting international visitors and boosting foreign exchange earnings.

Thirdly, investments in road infrastructure create employment opportunities, both directly in construction and maintenance and indirectly in sectors that benefit from improved connectivity. Construction projects generate jobs for engineers, laborers, and support staff, while improved transportation networks stimulate business expansion, leading to further job creation in manufacturing, retail, and services. Moreover, better roads improve access to education and healthcare, particularly in rural areas, enhancing human capital development and overall quality of life, which are fundamental for sustained economic progress. In conclusion, improved road networks are a catalyst for economic growth in Kenya, driving trade, tourism, employment, and overall socio-economic development.

13. (16 marks)

You work as the Communications Assistant at Utawala College. The students' performance in the recent national examinations was poor and the reputation of the college is at stake. The Principal has requested you to investigate the matter and write a report. Assuming that you have completed the investigations, write the report.

Answer

**UTAWAALA COLLEGE - REPORT ON POOR STUDENT PERFORMANCE IN RECENT NATIONAL EXAMINATIONS**

**TO:** The Principal, Utawala College

**FROM:** [Your Name], Communications Assistant

**DATE:** [Date of Report]

**SUBJECT:** Investigation into Decline in Student Performance in National Examinations

**1. INTRODUCTION**

This report presents the findings of an investigation into the recent decline in student performance in the national examinations. The investigation was initiated by the Principal to identify the underlying causes of this poor performance and to recommend actionable strategies for improvement.

**2. METHODOLOGY**

The investigation employed a mixed-methods approach, including:

Analysis of student performance data in national examinations for the past three years.

Surveys administered to students to gather feedback on their learning experiences and challenges.

Interviews conducted with faculty members to obtain their perspectives on factors contributing to the performance decline.

Review of college resources, including learning materials, facilities, and support services.

**3. FINDINGS**

**Performance Decline:** Data analysis confirms a significant decline in overall student pass rates and average scores in the recent national examinations compared to previous years. [Quantify the decline with specific percentages or figures].

**Student Survey Feedback:** Key concerns raised by students include:

**Inadequate Preparation:** Insufficient time allocated for revision and exam preparation.

**Resource Limitations:** Limited access to updated textbooks, online learning resources, and library materials.

**Teaching Quality Concerns:** Some students expressed concerns about the effectiveness of teaching methodologies in certain subjects and the level of individual attention received.

**Exam Stress and Anxiety:** High levels of exam-related stress and anxiety reported by a significant portion of students.

**Faculty Interviews Insights:** Faculty members highlighted the following contributing factors:

**Increased Curriculum Demands:** Perception of increased syllabus content and complexity in recent years.

**Student Engagement Challenges:** Difficulties in maintaining student engagement and motivation throughout the academic year.

**Need for Professional Development:** Identified a need for enhanced professional development opportunities for faculty in modern teaching methodologies and student support strategies.

**4. ANALYSIS**

The decline in student performance appears to be a multifaceted issue stemming from a combination of factors related to student preparation, resource availability, teaching quality, and student well-being. External factors such as curriculum changes may also play a role.

**5. RECOMMENDATIONS**

**Enhanced Academic Support:** Implement structured revision programs, extra tutorial sessions, and peer-to-peer learning initiatives to improve student preparedness.

**Resource Upgrade:** Invest in updating textbooks, expanding online learning resources, and enhancing library facilities to ensure students have access to comprehensive learning materials.

**Teaching Quality Enhancement:** Provide professional development workshops for faculty focusing on innovative teaching methodologies, student-centered learning approaches, and effective assessment techniques.

**Student Well-being Initiatives:** Introduce stress management workshops, counseling services, and exam anxiety reduction programs to support student mental and emotional well-being.

**Curriculum Review:** Conduct a review of the curriculum in light of national examination requirements to ensure alignment and adequate coverage of key topics.

**6. CONCLUSION**

Addressing the identified issues through the recommended actions is critical to reversing the trend of poor student performance and safeguarding the reputation of Utawala College. A collaborative effort involving students, faculty, and administration is essential for successful implementation and sustained improvement. Regular monitoring and evaluation of these interventions will be necessary to track progress and make further adjustments as needed.

Passage

Kenya could be losing billions of shillings annually as the repatriation of profits from the local tourism sector by multinational firms hits a record high. According to economic and tourism analysts, financial leakages from the industry could now be among the highest in the world as foreign tour operators devise self-serving modes of operation. This fear comes against a backdrop of an ever-rising domination of the lucrative industry by the trans-national players, most of whom are believed to be exploiting weak government policies to scoop maximum profits.

Financial leakages in tourism occur when revenues arising from tourism-related economic activities in destination countries are not available for re-investment or consumption of goods and services in the same countries. Financial resources "leak away" from the destination country to another country. particularly when the tourism company is based abroad and when tourism-related goods and services are being imported to the destination country.

Experts advise that while Kenya raised Ksh73.7 billions from tourism in 2010, the revenue could be tripled if loopholes that have facilitated unrestrained repatriation of profits are sealed. Financial leakages have over past decades affected diverse economic sectors in Kenya and many other developing countries. However, the analysts pinpoint the sudden increase in all-inclusive holiday packages to Kenya and transfer pricing as the main factors behind the escalating leakage levels in the tourism industry. The Kenya Association of Tour Operators membership list shows that up to 90 per cent of the 37 Category A and B tour firms in the country are owned and mostly managed by non-Kenyans. In contrast, Kenyans mainly run companies in categories D and E. Nearly all four and five star accommodation facilities in the country are also part of international chains headquartered overseas. The same applies to majority of airline owners in the country. As a result, Kenya could be getting as little as 20 per cent of the total revenue generated from her tourism.

Most of the foreign-owned tour companies are now predominantly offering all-inclusive tour packages in deals that make tourists pay for all their expenses to to the the f firms' agents abroad. These deals have reduced the revenue that Kenya, as a destination country, gets from the sector. The deals have further locked local tour firms out of subcontracts in the sector. Some of the Some of the companies are quite financially stable, such that they now own or are hiring private chartered planes and cruise ships that transport clients directly to and from Kenya. With Kenya being a long-haul destination where flights normally constitute about 40 to 50 per cent of the total package cost, half the total sum paid is thus lost even before tourists enter the country. This would be a different scenario if the tourists primarily relied on Kenyan airlines. Further, unlike in the past when foreign firms used to subcontract destination travel services to locals, most of them no longer outsource these services. They now own diverse fleets of vehicles to cater for safaris and transfers.

The package tours have also made it unnecessary for tourists to spend money outside the hotels and lodges. Most of the tourists now come to Kenya with petty cash to spend on tips, souvenirs and emergencies. Some European families pay up to Ksh2 million for a package tour as part of the initial quotation but only spend less than Ksh300,000 in Kenya. There are cases where some of the tour firms pay Maasai households as little as Ksh300 per night to have tourists spend nights in their manyattas when the guests have paid up to Ksh20,000 each for accommodation daily. There is also a sudden increase in the number of foreigners evading taxation. This is done by hosting tourists in cottages and villas at the coast by accommodating them as friends and relatives, thereby denying the economy a lot of money.

In essence, many local tour operators cannot compete with the financial strength, marketing and logistics of internationally operating tourism companies based in developed countries. This is why some international firms serve thousands of guests all year round while Kenyan firms only do business during the high season.

Adapted from: "The Standard on Saturday", November 12, 2011

14. (a) (12 marks)

In about 150 words, and according to the passage, explain why Kenya is not enjoying the full benefits of the tourism revenue.

Answer

According to the passage, Kenya is not fully benefiting from tourism revenue due to significant financial leakages. These leakages occur when revenues generated from tourism activities are not reinvested or consumed within Kenya but are instead transferred out of the country. This is primarily attributed to multinational tourism firms dominating the sector, employing "self-serving modes of operation" to maximize profits, often exploiting weak government policies (Paragraph 1). The passage highlights all-inclusive holiday packages and transfer pricing as key mechanisms for these leakages (Paragraph 3). These packages ensure tourists pay most expenses to foreign agents, minimizing spending within Kenya. Furthermore, a large portion of tour firms and high-end accommodation are foreign-owned, leading to profit repatriation (Paragraph 3). Local Kenyan firms, mainly in lower categories, and Kenyan-owned airlines are underutilized. Consequently, despite Kenya generating substantial tourism revenue, a disproportionately small percentage, potentially as low as 20%, remains within the country (Paragraph 3), hindering its economic benefits.

14. (b) (6 marks)

Explain the meaning of each of the following words and phrases as used in the passage.

Answer

**(i) lucrative:**

In the context of the passage, "lucrative" means highly profitable or producing a great deal of wealth. The tourism industry is described as "lucrative," indicating its potential to generate significant income and profits.

**(ii) leak away:**

"Leak away" refers to the phenomenon of financial resources escaping or draining out of the Kenyan economy due to practices in the tourism sector. It signifies the loss of revenue that should ideally benefit Kenya's economy but is instead flowing out of the country.

**(iii) diverse:**

"Diverse" is used in the context of foreign firms owning "diverse fleets of vehicles." Here, it means varied or a wide range of. These firms possess a wide variety of vehicles to cater to different tourism needs like safaris and transfers, indicating their comprehensive control over tourism services.

**(iv) revenue generated:**

"Revenue generated" refers to the total income produced or created by the tourism sector in Kenya. The passage discusses how much of this total revenue actually benefits Kenya, highlighting the disparity between generated revenue and retained revenue.

**(v) primarily:**

"Primarily" means mainly or for the most part. In the passage, it's used to describe tourists primarily relying on Kenyan airlines, suggesting that if tourists mainly used Kenyan airlines, more revenue would stay within the country.

**(vi) high season:**

"High season" refers to the peak tourist season when the number of visitors is highest, typically associated with favorable weather or holidays. Kenyan firms are described as mainly operating during the "high season," implying they miss out on revenue during off-peak periods when international firms operate year-round.
