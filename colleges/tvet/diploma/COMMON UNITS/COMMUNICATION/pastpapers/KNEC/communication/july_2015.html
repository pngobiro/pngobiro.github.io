July 2015

DIPLOMA IN COMMUNICATION STUDIES

Exam Code: COMMUNICATION MODULE I

Duration: 3 hours

Period: July 2015

1. (3 marks)

State three reasons why some organizations hold meetings through teleconferencing.

Answer

Reduced travel costs and time for participants as they can attend from any location with teleconferencing facilities.

Increased accessibility for remote employees or individuals in different geographical locations, fostering broader participation.

Enhanced efficiency in decision-making and problem-solving due to quicker meeting setup and information sharing.

2. (3 marks)

List three barriers to communication that may be caused by a manager in an organization.

Answer

Lack of clarity in instructions or messages, leading to misunderstandings and confusion among subordinates.

Poor listening skills, failing to pay attention to employee concerns or feedback, creating a communication breakdown.

Creating a culture of fear or intimidation, discouraging open communication and honest feedback from team members.

3. (3 marks)

Outline three advantages of using a flip chart when making a presentation.

Answer

Flip charts are inexpensive and readily available, making them a cost-effective visual aid for presentations.

They facilitate real-time interaction and brainstorming with the audience, as presenters can write and draw spontaneously.

Flip charts are simple to use and require no technical expertise or equipment, ensuring ease of setup and operation.

4. (4 marks)

State four non-verbal signals that may indicate attentiveness in an audience.

Answer

Maintaining eye contact with the speaker, showing engagement and focus on the presentation.

Nodding in agreement or understanding, indicating active listening and comprehension of the message.

Leaning forward slightly, demonstrating interest and attentiveness to the speaker and the content.

Taking notes, signifying a desire to retain information and engage with the material presented.

5. (3 marks)

State three uses of a report in an organization.

Answer

Reports provide documented information for decision-making, offering data and analysis to guide strategic choices.

They serve as a record of activities, projects, or events, ensuring accountability and historical reference.

Reports facilitate communication of findings and recommendations to stakeholders, promoting transparency and shared understanding.

6. (4 marks)

List four types of interviews that may be held in an organization.

Answer

Selection interviews, used for evaluating candidates during the recruitment process to fill job vacancies.

Performance appraisal interviews, conducted to review employee performance, provide feedback, and set future goals.

Exit interviews, held with departing employees to gather insights into their reasons for leaving and organizational improvement areas.

Disciplinary interviews, used to address employee misconduct or performance issues and to determine appropriate corrective actions.

7. (3 marks)

Outline three reasons why it is important to have an agenda for a meeting.

Answer

Agendas provide structure and direction to meetings, ensuring discussions stay focused and productive.

They help participants prepare for the meeting by outlining topics to be covered, allowing for informed contributions.

Agendas aid in time management during meetings, allocating specific time slots to each item to ensure efficient use of meeting duration.

8. (2 marks)

List two functions of paragraphs in an essay.

Answer

Paragraphs organize ideas logically, grouping related sentences to develop a single point or aspect of the essay's topic.

They enhance readability by breaking down text into manageable chunks, making it easier for the reader to follow the writer's train of thought.

9. (4 marks)

Jacinta, a personal secretary, has received a telephone call with vital information on behalf of her boss who is away from the office. Outline four advantages of writing this information on a telephone message form.

Answer

Written message forms provide a permanent record of the call details, reducing the risk of forgetting or misinterpreting information.

They ensure accurate and complete information capture, including caller's name, number, time, and the message itself.

Message forms facilitate efficient message delivery to the boss upon return, ensuring timely communication and action.

Using a standardized form improves professionalism and organization in handling telephone communications within the office.

10. (3 marks)

Outline three reasons why many managers prefer to use oral communication in an organization.

Answer

Oral communication allows for immediate feedback and clarification, enabling quicker understanding and resolution of issues.

It builds rapport and personal connection between managers and employees, fostering stronger working relationships.

Oral communication is often faster and more efficient for conveying urgent or complex information compared to written methods.

12. (a) (8 marks)

Explain four challenges of using e-mail as a means of communication.

Answer

**Information Overload:** The sheer volume of emails received daily can lead to information overload, causing important messages to be overlooked or missed amidst the clutter. This reduces communication effectiveness and can delay responses to critical issues.

**Misinterpretation of Tone:** Email lacks non-verbal cues such as tone of voice and body language, making it difficult to convey emotions and intentions accurately. This can easily lead to misinterpretations and misunderstandings, potentially causing conflict or damaging relationships.

**Security Risks:** Emails are vulnerable to security threats such as phishing, malware, and data breaches. Sensitive information transmitted via email can be intercepted, leading to privacy violations and potential financial or reputational damage for individuals and organizations.

**Impersonal Communication:** Email communication can feel impersonal and less engaging compared to face-to-face or phone conversations. This can hinder the development of strong interpersonal relationships and reduce the sense of connection and collaboration within teams or organizations.

12. (b) (10 marks)

Explain five factors that may make a meeting fail to achieve its objectives.

Answer

**Lack of Clear Objectives:** Meetings without clearly defined objectives lack direction and focus. Participants may be unsure of the meeting's purpose, leading to unfocused discussions and failure to achieve desired outcomes. A well-defined objective is crucial for meeting success.

**Poor Agenda Management:** A poorly structured or non-existent agenda results in disorganized meetings. Without a clear agenda, discussions can drift off-topic, time may be wasted on irrelevant issues, and key items may be overlooked, preventing objective attainment.

**Ineffective Facilitation:** Weak meeting facilitation can derail progress. If the facilitator fails to manage discussions, control dominant personalities, or encourage participation from all attendees, the meeting can become unproductive and fail to reach its intended goals.

**Lack of Follow-Up Actions:** Meetings often fail when agreed-upon actions are not documented and followed up on. Without clear action items, assigned responsibilities, and deadlines, decisions made during the meeting may not be implemented, rendering the meeting ineffective in achieving its objectives.

**Dominant Participants:** When a few participants dominate the discussion, it stifles contributions from others and limits diverse perspectives. This imbalance can lead to decisions that are not well-rounded or fully considered, hindering the meeting's ability to achieve comprehensive and effective outcomes.

13. (16 marks)

The number of staff members at Utendeti Limited taking meals and refreshments at the staff restaurant has been declining for the past three months. As the Assistant Operations Manger, you have been requested by the Human Resource Manager to investigate the matter. Assume that you have completed the investigation and write a report using the schematic format.

Answer

**UTENDETI LIMITED - STAFF RESTAURANT UTILIZATION REPORT**

**TO:** Human Resource Manager, Utendeti Limited

**FROM:** [Your Name], Assistant Operations Manager

**DATE:** [Date of Report]

**SUBJECT:** Investigation into Declining Staff Restaurant Utilization

**1. INTRODUCTION**

This report presents the findings of an investigation into the recent decline in staff restaurant utilization at Utendeti Limited over the past three months. The investigation was initiated at the request of the Human Resource Manager to identify the reasons for this trend and recommend appropriate actions.

**2. METHODOLOGY**

The investigation involved:

Review of staff restaurant sales data for the past six months.

Distribution of a staff survey to gather feedback on restaurant services.

Informal interviews with a sample of staff members across different departments.

Benchmarking against utilization rates of staff restaurants in similar organizations (where possible).

**3. FINDINGS**

**Decline in Utilization:** Sales data confirms a consistent decline in staff restaurant usage over the last three months, with a [quantify percentage or approximate decline] decrease compared to the previous period.

**Survey Results:** Staff survey feedback indicates the following key concerns:

**Price:** Perceived high prices of meals compared to external options.

**Menu Variety:** Limited menu variety and repetition of dishes.

**Quality:** Concerns about the quality and freshness of food in some instances.

**Service Speed:** Slow service during peak lunch hours.

**Interview Insights:** Interviews corroborated survey findings, with staff mentioning the availability of cheaper and more diverse food options outside the company premises as a significant factor influencing their choice.

**4. ANALYSIS**

The decline in staff restaurant utilization appears to be driven by a combination of factors related to price, menu, quality, and service. The availability of external food options that are perceived as more attractive in terms of cost and variety is a key competitive factor.

**5. RECOMMENDATIONS**

**Price Review:** Conduct a cost analysis and consider adjusting meal prices to be more competitive with external options while maintaining quality and profitability.

**Menu Enhancement:** Introduce a more varied and rotating menu to offer greater choice and appeal to staff preferences. Gather staff input on menu suggestions.

**Quality Improvement:** Implement stricter quality control measures for food preparation and sourcing of ingredients. Conduct regular food quality checks and solicit staff feedback.

**Service Optimization:** Review restaurant service processes to identify and address bottlenecks. Consider strategies to improve service speed, especially during peak hours, such as additional staff or streamlined ordering systems.

**Promotion and Communication:** Promote the staff restaurant through internal communication channels, highlighting menu changes, special offers, and any improvements made based on staff feedback.

**6. CONCLUSION**

Addressing the identified issues related to price, menu, quality, and service is crucial to improve staff restaurant utilization. Implementing the recommendations outlined in this report is expected to enhance the attractiveness of the staff restaurant and encourage greater staff patronage. Regular monitoring of utilization rates and ongoing staff feedback should be incorporated to ensure continuous improvement.

Prepared by:

[Your Name]
Assistant Operations Manager

Passage

The housing sector in Kenya faces a number of challenges. In deed, owning a house remains a dream of many. There is a high demand for formal housing which has fuelled price hikes hence locking out potential buyers. This has, in turn, denied the housing sector much needed funds to- re-invest in order to increase the supply which has resulted in low supply of affordable houses. Real estate analysts and economists are warning that house prices will continue to rise as lenders shy away from the business.

A report by the African Development Bank (AfDB) shows that high prices have locked Kenya's housing sector out of fast growth. The AfDB is warning of a worsening supply gap because only a handful of private developers in the country can afford to invest in medium to large scale developments that target the middle to low income earners. According to the Centre for Affordable Housing Finance in Africa, the annual increase in the demand for housing in Kenya is 206,000 units. The inability of private developers to execute well-done projects at a reasonable cost is becoming a big concern not only in Kenya, but the entire East African region. With the exception of Zambia and to some extent Tanzania, AfDB claims that many regional developers lack experience. In Kenya there are only a few developers who have an average of 5.5 years of experience and are able to put up large developments of 200 and 250 units at a time.

According to data from the Central Bank of Kenya, the average size of a home loan in Kenya has been rising at the rate of about 15 per cent per year. This means that a home loan that was Ksh 5 million in one year would rise to Ksh 5.75 million the following year. The lack of innovative financing options and high interest on mortgages makes affordability of houses a challenge. A research economist at the AfDB found out that interest rates on home loans range between 11 and 25 per cent per year. This means that a borrower of Ksh 6.4 million for a 15-year house loan at an interest rate of 18 per cent per year will end up paying Ksh 17.3 million. While currently about 80 per cent of new houses target high and upper middle income earners, the AfDB report shows that the greatest demand – estimated at 83 per cent – is among the middle and low income earners.

, According to property firm Hass Consult, this state of affairs means that 80 per cent of Kenyans living in urban areas do not live in their own houses. Another challenge is that these urban dwellers who live in rented houses have to contend with fast rising rent hence hindering their potential to save and buy a house of their own. Since 2001, rent in major urban centres has increased by about 300 per cent. For example, a house that was being rented for Ksh 10,000 in 2001 cost about Ksh 30,000 per month in 2013. The lack of innovative mortgage financing solutions is a further challenge. This is because even companies that specialize in developing cheaper houses require their clients to source for financing independently. There is also lack of government support in Kenya especially in the development of infrastructure such as road networks, water and sanitation without which the projects become unattractive to buyers. The market estimates that the cost of privately developed infrastructure adds another 20-30 per cent to the price of houses in Kenya.

The problems facing the Kenya housing sector are multi-dimensional and require a holistic approach if the majority of urban dwellers are to afford houses. The market needs to be educated to accept different building solutions that are more cost effective such as prefabricated houses. Such houses are cheaper and can reduce construction time by up to 50 per cent and the cost of construction by more than 30 per cent.

14. (a) (12 marks)

In about 150 words and according to the passage, explain the challenges facing the housing sector in Kenya.

Answer

According to the passage, the Kenyan housing sector faces significant challenges primarily driven by high demand and limited supply of affordable housing. The passage highlights that "owning a house remains a dream of many" due to "high demand for formal housing which has fuelled price hikes" (Paragraph 1). This price inflation locks out potential buyers and reduces funds for reinvestment, further exacerbating the supply shortage. The African Development Bank (AfDB) report cited in the passage emphasizes a "worsening supply gap" because only "a handful of private developers…can afford to invest in medium to large scale developments" (Paragraph 2). Additionally, rising interest rates on home loans, ranging from 11% to 25% annually, combined with limited "innovative financing options" (Paragraph 3 & 4), make affordability a major obstacle. The passage also notes that "80 per cent of Kenyans living in urban areas do not live in their own houses" (Paragraph 4), indicating a severe housing deficit. Furthermore, rapidly increasing rents, which have risen by "about 300 per cent" since 2001 (Paragraph 4), hinder saving capacity for home purchase. The lack of government support for infrastructure development, as mentioned in paragraph 4, adds to the cost and unattractiveness of housing projects, compounding these challenges. In conclusion, the passage portrays a housing sector struggling with affordability, supply constraints, and financial barriers.

14. (b) (6 marks)

Explain the meaning of the following words and phrases as used in the passage.

Answer

**(i) lenders:**

In the context of the passage, "lenders" refers to financial institutions, primarily banks and mortgage providers, that offer loans to individuals or developers for housing purposes. The passage mentions that "house prices will continue to rise as lenders shy away from the business," indicating that reduced lending activity contributes to the housing crisis.

**(ii) handful:**

"Handful" is used to describe the limited number of private developers in Kenya capable of undertaking large-scale housing projects. The AfDB report states "only a handful of private developers in the country can afford to invest in medium to large scale developments," highlighting the scarcity of developers with sufficient capacity and resources.

**(iii) put up:**

"Put up" in the phrase "able to put up large developments" means to construct or build. The passage indicates that only a few developers in Kenya "are able to put up large developments of 200 and 250 units at a time," emphasizing the limited construction capacity for large housing projects.

**(iv) innovative:**

"Innovative" describes the need for new and creative approaches to mortgage financing. The passage mentions "lack of innovative mortgage financing solutions" as a further challenge, suggesting that traditional mortgage products are insufficient and there is a need for more creative and accessible financial instruments to improve housing affordability.

**(v) hindering:**

"Hindering" means impeding or obstructing progress. The passage states that rising rents are "hindering their potential to save and buy a house of their own," meaning that escalating rental costs are preventing urban dwellers from accumulating savings necessary for homeownership.

**(vi) cost effective:**

"Cost effective" refers to solutions that are economical and provide good value for money. The passage suggests educating the market to accept "more cost effective…prefabricated houses," indicating that prefabricated housing is presented as a more affordable alternative to traditional construction methods.
