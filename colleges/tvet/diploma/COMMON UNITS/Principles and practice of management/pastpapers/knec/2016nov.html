Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Give five reasons that make it necessary for organizations to carry out the function of planning. (10 marks)

(b) Outline five functions of the top level management in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Necessity of Planning Function:
  1. Provides Direction: Sets objectives and defines the path to achieve them, guiding actions and decisions.
  2. Reduces Uncertainty: Forces managers to anticipate the future, consider potential changes, and prepare responses.
  3. Minimizes Waste & Redundancy: Coordinates activities and resources, ensuring efficient utilization and avoiding duplication.
  4. Establishes Standards for Control: Planning sets the goals and standards against which actual performance is measured and evaluated.
  5. Facilitates Decision Making: Provides a framework and criteria for making choices that align with organizational goals.
(b) Functions of Top-Level Management:
  1. Setting Overall Objectives & Strategy: Defining the organization's mission, vision, and long-term strategic goals.
  2. Policy Formulation: Establishing broad policies and guidelines that govern organizational operations.
  3. Organizing: Designing the overall organizational structure and appointing key executives.
  4. Resource Allocation: Making major decisions regarding the allocation of financial, human, and physical resources.
  5. External Relations & Representation: Representing the organization to external stakeholders (e.g., investors, government, public).
  6. Overall Control & Evaluation: Monitoring the organization's overall performance against strategic goals.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five benefits that an organization may derive from effective coordination of its activities. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five criticisms that are levelled against the use of the scientific theory in modern management practice. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Benefits of Effective Coordination:
  1. Synergy: Ensures that the combined output of different units is greater than the sum of individual efforts.
  2. Unity of Action: Aligns the activities of various departments towards common organizational goals.
  3. Efficiency: Minimizes duplication of effort, conflict, and wasted resources.
  4. Improved Relationships: Fosters better communication and collaboration between individuals and departments.
  5. Organizational Effectiveness: Contributes to the overall smooth functioning and achievement of organizational objectives.
(b) Criticisms of Scientific Management Theory:
  1. Dehumanizing Effect: Treats workers like machines, ignoring psychological and social needs, leading to monotony and dissatisfaction.
  2. Over-Specialization: Extreme division of labor can lead to narrow skills and lack of flexibility in the workforce.
  3. Neglect of Human Relations: Focuses primarily on efficiency and ignores the impact of group dynamics, morale, and worker attitudes.
  4. Authoritarian Approach: Assumes managers know best and workers are primarily motivated by money, potentially leading to resistance.
  5. Limited Applicability: Primarily suited for simple, repetitive manufacturing tasks and less applicable to complex, knowledge-based work.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five measures that the management of an organization should take to ensure that selection interviews achieve their intended purpose. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five consequences of an ineffective control system in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Measures for Effective Selection Interviews:
  1. Structured Interview Format: Use a standardized set of job-related questions asked in the same order for all candidates to ensure consistency and fair comparison.
  2. Trained Interviewers: Ensure interviewers are skilled in questioning techniques, active listening, note-taking, and avoiding personal biases (e.g., halo effect, stereotyping).
  3. Job-Relevant Questions: Focus questions on assessing specific skills, knowledge, experience, and behavioral competencies directly related to the job requirements (e.g., behavioral or situational questions).
  4. Panel Interviews: Use multiple interviewers (e.g., HR, hiring manager, potential peer) to get diverse perspectives and reduce individual bias in evaluation.
  5. Clear Evaluation Criteria: Establish clear, objective criteria beforehand for rating candidate responses against the job requirements, using a scoring guide if possible.
(b) Consequences of Ineffective Control Systems:
  1. Failure to Achieve Objectives: Deviations from plans go unnoticed or uncorrected, leading to missed targets and organizational goals.
  2. Resource Wastage: Inefficient use of financial, human, or material resources due to lack of monitoring and accountability.
  3. Poor Quality Products/Services: Lack of quality control measures can result in defects, customer dissatisfaction, and damage to reputation.
  4. Increased Costs & Lower Profits: Errors, inefficiencies, waste, and potential fraud lead to higher operational costs and reduced profitability.
  5. Employee Demotivation & Unethical Behavior: Unfair, unclear, or overly punitive control systems can lower morale, while lack of controls might encourage unethical actions.
  6. Poor Decision Making: Management decisions based on inaccurate, untimely, or incomplete information from the control system will likely be flawed.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five benefits that an organization may derive from sourcing for employees through the Internet. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five factors that may influence the span of control in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Benefits of Sourcing Employees via the Internet:
  1. Wider Reach: Access to a larger and more diverse pool of candidates globally or nationally compared to traditional methods.
  2. Cost-Effectiveness: Often cheaper than print advertising or recruitment agencies, especially for reaching many candidates.
  3. Speed: Faster posting of vacancies and quicker response times from applicants.
  4. Convenience: Easy for both employers to post jobs and candidates to apply online 24/7.
  5. Targeting Capabilities: Ability to target specific skills, industries, or locations through specialized job boards or platforms.
(b) Factors Influencing Span of Control:
  1. Complexity of Work: More complex, varied, and non-routine tasks require closer supervision, thus a narrower span.
  2. Subordinate Training & Experience: Highly trained, experienced, and self-motivated subordinates require less supervision, allowing a wider span.
  3. Manager's Abilities & Workload: More capable and experienced managers can handle wider spans; a heavy workload limits the span.
  4. Degree of Interaction Required: Jobs needing frequent communication and interaction between manager and subordinates necessitate a narrower span.
  5. Clarity of Plans & Procedures: Well-defined goals, clear instructions, and standardized procedures enable wider spans.
  6. Geographical Dispersion: Subordinates located far apart geographically usually require a narrower span.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Explain six external factors that may positively influence an organization's employee recruitment process. (12 marks)

(b) Outline four characteristics that an autocratic leader may possess. (8 marks)

Answer

(a) Positive External Influences on Recruitment:
  1. Favorable Labor Market Conditions: High availability of skilled workers in the relevant market makes it easier to find suitable candidates.
  2. Positive Economic Climate: Economic growth often leads to a larger pool of active job seekers and greater willingness among employed individuals to switch jobs.
  3. Government Policies & Incentives: Supportive labor laws, training subsidies, or employment incentives can make recruitment more attractive or less costly.
  4. Technological Advancements: The prevalence of online job boards, professional networking sites (like LinkedIn), and recruitment software broadens reach and streamlines the application/screening process.
  5. Positive Company Image/Reputation: A strong employer brand and positive public perception attract more and often better-quality candidates voluntarily.
  6. Educational System Output: A strong local education system producing graduates with relevant skills and qualifications increases the supply of potential hires.
(b) Characteristics of an Autocratic Leader:
  1. Centralized Decision Making: Makes decisions unilaterally with little or no input from subordinates.
  2. Directive & Controlling: Gives specific orders and instructions, closely supervises work, and expects strict compliance.
  3. One-Way Communication: Communication flows primarily downwards from the leader to subordinates.
  4. Low Trust in Subordinates: Often assumes employees are not self-motivated or capable without close direction.
  5. Focus on Task Accomplishment: Primarily concerned with achieving tasks and results, sometimes at the expense of employee morale or relationships.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five challenges that may be encountered by managers during an organization's plan implementation stage. (10 marks)

(b) Outline five assumptions of Theory Y as it was advanced by McGregor in the motivation of employees in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Challenges During Plan Implementation:
  1. Resistance to Change: Employees or stakeholders resisting new procedures, roles, or objectives outlined in the plan.
  2. Lack of Resources: Insufficient budget, personnel, time, or necessary tools allocated for successful execution.
  3. Poor Communication: Failure to clearly communicate the plan, individual responsibilities, and progress updates.
  4. Unforeseen Environmental Changes: Unexpected shifts in the market, economy, technology, or competitive landscape disrupting the plan.
  5. Lack of Commitment/Support: Insufficient buy-in or active support from top management or key employees.
  6. Inadequate Monitoring & Control: Failure to track progress effectively and take timely corrective actions when deviations occur.
(b) Assumptions of McGregor's Theory Y:
  1. Work is Natural: Employees view work as natural as rest or play and do not inherently dislike it.
  2. Self-Direction & Self-Control: People will exercise self-direction and self-control towards objectives they are committed to.
  3. Commitment to Objectives: Commitment is a function of the rewards associated with achievement (including intrinsic rewards like satisfaction).
  4. Acceptance of Responsibility: Under proper conditions, the average person learns not only to accept but also to seek responsibility.
  5. Creativity & Ingenuity: The capacity for imagination, ingenuity, and creativity in solving organizational problems is widely distributed in the population, not just among managers.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Outline four ways through which an organization can meet the safety needs of its employees in accordance with Abraham Maslow’s theory of hierarchy of needs. (8 marks)

(b) Explain six personal factors that are likely to influence the choice of leadership style adopted by a manager in an organization. (12 marks)

Answer

(a) Meeting Employee Safety Needs (Maslow):
  1. Safe Working Conditions: Providing physically safe premises, well-maintained equipment, adequate lighting, ventilation, and necessary protective gear. Implementing clear safety procedures and training.
  2. Job Security: Offering stable employment through fair contracts, clear performance expectations, and transparent grievance/disciplinary processes. Avoiding arbitrary layoffs.
  3. Financial Security: Providing a fair wage, timely salary payments, benefits like health insurance, retirement plans (pension/provident fund), and potentially severance packages.
  4. Predictable & Fair Work Environment: Ensuring consistent application of rules and policies, protection from harassment or bullying, and fair treatment by supervisors and management.
(b) Personal Factors Influencing Leadership Style:
  1. Personality Traits: A manager's inherent characteristics like confidence level, dominance, sociability, risk aversion, and need for control strongly influence their tendency towards directive or participative styles.
  2. Values & Beliefs (Philosophy): Personal values regarding trust in employees, belief in participation, importance of control vs. autonomy (Theory X vs. Theory Y assumptions) shape leadership approach.
  3. Past Experience & Background: Previous leadership experiences, successes, failures, cultural background, and prior training significantly influence a manager's comfort level and preference for certain styles.
  4. Skills & Competencies: A manager's own proficiency in communication, delegation, motivation, conflict resolution, and technical expertise affects their ability and willingness to use different styles effectively.
  5. Confidence in Subordinates: The manager's level of trust and confidence in the skills, experience, motivation, and reliability of their team members impacts how much control they retain or delegate.
  6. Stress Levels & Situation Urgency: Under high pressure, tight deadlines, or during crises, managers might naturally revert to a more directive, autocratic style, even if their usual preference is different.
