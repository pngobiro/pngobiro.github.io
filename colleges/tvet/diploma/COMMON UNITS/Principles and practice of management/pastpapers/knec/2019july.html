Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five indicators of effective management in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Highlight five contributions of the contingency approach to management practice. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Indicators of Effective Management:
  1. Goal Achievement: Consistently meeting or exceeding organizational objectives and targets.
  2. Efficiency: Optimal use of resources (time, money, personnel) to achieve outputs with minimal waste.
  3. Employee Satisfaction & Morale: High levels of employee engagement, motivation, low turnover, and a positive work environment.
  4. Adaptability & Innovation: Ability to respond effectively to environmental changes and foster innovation.
  5. Stakeholder Satisfaction: Meeting the expectations of key stakeholders (customers, shareholders, community, etc.).
(b) Contributions of the Contingency Approach:
  1. Rejection of Universal Principles: Emphasizes that there is no single best way to manage; the approach depends on the situation.
  2. Situational Analysis: Encourages managers to analyze the specific situation (environment, technology, people) before choosing a management technique.
  3. Flexibility: Promotes adaptability and flexibility in management styles and organizational structures.
  4. Integration of Theories: Allows for the integration of various management theories (classical, behavioral, systems) based on situational appropriateness.
  5. Improved Decision Making: Leads to more effective decisions by tailoring actions to the specific context and variables involved.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Explain five benefits that may accrue to an organization from applying management by objectives approach to management. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five factors that should be considered when determining the number of employees under each supervisor. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Benefits of Management by Objectives (MBO):
  1. Improved Planning: Encourages systematic goal setting and planning at all organizational levels.
  2. Clarity of Roles & Objectives: Ensures employees understand their specific objectives and how they contribute to overall goals.
  3. Increased Motivation & Commitment: Employee participation in goal setting often leads to higher commitment and motivation.
  4. Objective Performance Appraisal: Provides clear, measurable criteria for evaluating employee performance.
  5. Enhanced Communication & Coordination: Facilitates communication between managers and subordinates regarding goals and progress.
(b) Factors Determining Span of Control (Number of Employees per Supervisor):
  1. Complexity of Tasks: More complex, non-routine tasks require narrower spans of control (fewer employees per supervisor).
  2. Subordinate Competence & Training: Highly skilled and trained employees require less supervision, allowing for wider spans.
  3. Supervisor's Abilities: Experienced and capable supervisors can manage wider spans effectively.
  4. Degree of Interaction Required: Jobs requiring frequent interaction between supervisor and subordinates necessitate narrower spans.
  5. Clarity of Plans & Procedures: Well-defined plans, procedures, and standardized tasks allow for wider spans of control.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five methods that can be used to enhance communication between management and staff in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five reasons that make it necessary for organizations to establish control mechanisms in their operations. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Methods to Enhance Management-Staff Communication:
  1. Regular Meetings: Formal staff meetings, team briefings, and departmental updates to share information and gather feedback.
  2. Open Door Policy: Managers making themselves accessible for staff to discuss issues, concerns, or ideas informally.
  3. Feedback Mechanisms: Implementing suggestion boxes, employee satisfaction surveys, 360-degree feedback, and regular performance reviews.
  4. Internal Publications & Platforms: Using newsletters, intranets, emails, or internal social media to disseminate information and encourage interaction.
  5. Social Events & Team Building: Organizing informal gatherings, team lunches, or team-building activities to foster better relationships and open communication.
(b) Reasons for Establishing Control Mechanisms:
  1. Ensure Goal Achievement: To monitor progress towards organizational objectives and ensure activities stay on track.
  2. Resource Optimization: To prevent waste, ensure efficient utilization of resources (financial, human, physical), and control costs.
  3. Adaptation to Change: To detect deviations from plans caused by environmental changes and allow for timely corrective actions.
  4. Error Detection & Prevention: To identify mistakes, inefficiencies, or irregularities early and prevent their recurrence.
  5. Standardization & Quality Assurance: To ensure processes are followed consistently and outputs meet predefined quality standards.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Explain five reasons for establishing salary scales for employees in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Environmental pollution is one of the global issues impacting on management practice. Outline five measures that managers of the manufacturing firms may take to minimize the vice. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Reasons for Establishing Salary Scales:
  1. Internal Equity: Ensures fair pay relative to other jobs within the organization based on skill, effort, and responsibility.
  2. External Competitiveness: Helps attract and retain employees by aligning pay levels with the external labor market.
  3. Motivation & Performance: Provides a clear structure for pay progression, potentially linking increases to performance or tenure.
  4. Budgeting & Cost Control: Allows for predictable labor costs and better financial planning and control over compensation expenses.
  5. Legal Compliance & Transparency: Helps ensure compliance with equal pay legislation and provides transparency in compensation decisions.
(b) Measures to Minimize Environmental Pollution (Manufacturing Firms):
  1. Adopt Cleaner Technologies: Invest in production processes that generate less waste and pollution.
  2. Waste Management Programs: Implement reduce, reuse, and recycle programs for materials used in production.
  3. Pollution Control Equipment: Install filters, scrubbers, or treatment facilities to reduce emissions and effluent discharge.
  4. Sustainable Sourcing: Use raw materials from sustainable sources and suppliers with good environmental practices.
  5. Compliance & Monitoring: Adhere to environmental regulations and regularly monitor emissions and waste output.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) There are certain factors that may limit organizational engagement to corporate social responsibility. Outline five such factors. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five disadvantages of applying bureaucratic principles in running an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Factors Limiting Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) Engagement:
  1. Cost: Implementing CSR initiatives can be expensive, impacting profitability, especially for smaller firms.
  2. Lack of Resources: Shortage of time, personnel, or expertise needed to effectively manage CSR programs.
  3. Focus on Core Business: Management priority may be on core operations and maximizing shareholder value over social initiatives.
  4. Difficulty in Measuring Impact: Challenges in quantifying the direct benefits and return on investment of CSR activities.
  5. Stakeholder Skepticism: Concerns about "greenwashing" or lack of genuine commitment can hinder stakeholder support.
(b) Disadvantages of Bureaucratic Principles:
  1. Rigidity & Inflexibility: Strict rules and procedures can make it difficult to adapt to changing circumstances quickly.
  2. Slow Decision Making: Hierarchical structures and formal processes can lead to delays in decision-making.
  3. Impersonality: Emphasis on rules over individuals can lead to demotivation and lack of employee engagement.
  4. Stifled Creativity & Innovation: Focus on conformity and following rules can discourage initiative and new ideas.
  5. Red Tape: Excessive paperwork and complex procedures can lead to inefficiency and frustration.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Describe the steps that should be followed when making a decision in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five limitations of using internal audit as a control tool in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Steps in Decision Making:
  1. Identify the Problem/Opportunity: Clearly define the issue or situation requiring a decision.
  2. Gather Information: Collect relevant data and facts related to the problem and potential solutions.
  3. Identify Alternatives: Brainstorm and list possible courses of action or solutions.
  4. Evaluate Alternatives: Analyze the pros and cons, feasibility, costs, and benefits of each alternative against set criteria.
  5. Select the Best Alternative: Choose the option that best meets the decision criteria and objectives.
  6. Implement the Decision: Put the chosen alternative into action, allocating necessary resources.
  7. Monitor and Evaluate: Track the results of the decision and evaluate its effectiveness, making adjustments if needed.
(b) Limitations of Internal Audit as a Control Tool:
  1. Independence Issues: Internal auditors are employees of the organization, which may compromise their objectivity.
  2. Scope Limitations: Management may restrict the scope or access of internal auditors.
  3. Resource Constraints: The internal audit function may lack sufficient budget, staffing, or expertise.
  4. Familiarity Threat: Auditors may become too familiar with operations or personnel, potentially overlooking issues.
  5. Management Override: Management can potentially ignore or override internal audit findings and recommendations.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Coordination of activities among various departments in an organization is a key function of a manager. Explain five ways in which this function can be carried out. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five ways in which strategic decisions differ from operational decisions. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Ways to Carry Out Coordination:
  1. Clear Communication Channels: Establishing effective formal (meetings, reports) and informal communication systems between departments.
  2. Standardized Procedures & Rules: Implementing common procedures, rules, and workflows that require inter-departmental cooperation and define interactions.
  3. Liaison Roles/Coordinators: Appointing specific individuals or teams (e.g., project managers) responsible for linking and coordinating activities across different units.
  4. Shared Goals & Planning: Developing overarching organizational goals and integrated plans that require departments to work together.
  5. Hierarchical Coordination: Using the formal chain of command where common superiors coordinate the activities of their subordinates in different units.
(b) Differences Between Strategic and Operational Decisions:
Feature Strategic Decisions Operational Decisions
Scope & Impact Broad, organization-wide, significant long-term impact Narrow, department/task-specific, short-term impact
Time Horizon Long-term (years) Short-term (days/weeks/months)
Level of Management Typically made by Top Management Typically made by Lower/Middle Management
Nature & Complexity Unstructured, complex, high uncertainty, less frequent Structured, routine, low uncertainty, frequent
Information Needs Requires broad environmental and internal information Requires specific, detailed internal information
