Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five roles that may be undertaken by managers in the day-to-day running of an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Management by Objectives (MBO) is one of the strategies that may be adopted by organizations in order to achieve effectiveness. Explain five benefits of this strategy to an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Managerial Roles (Mintzberg):
  1. Interpersonal Roles:
    • Figurehead (Symbolic duties)
    • Leader (Motivating, directing subordinates)
    • Liaison (Maintaining external contacts)
  2. Informational Roles:
    • Monitor (Scanning environment for information)
    • Disseminator (Sharing information internally)
    • Spokesperson (Transmitting information externally)
  3. Decisional Roles:
    • Entrepreneur (Initiating change, identifying opportunities)
    • Disturbance Handler (Addressing unexpected problems/crises)
    • Resource Allocator (Distributing resources like budget, time, staff)
    • Negotiator (Representing the organization in negotiations)
*(Note: Only five roles were required)*
(b) Benefits of Management by Objectives (MBO):
  1. Improved Planning: Forces managers and employees to set specific objectives and plan how to achieve them.
  2. Clarity of Objectives: Ensures that both managers and employees have a clear understanding of expected results.
  3. Increased Motivation & Commitment: Employee participation in goal-setting often leads to greater commitment and motivation to achieve those goals.
  4. Objective Performance Appraisal: Provides clear, measurable criteria for evaluating employee performance against agreed-upon objectives.
  5. Enhanced Communication & Coordination: Facilitates communication between managers and subordinates regarding goals, progress, and necessary support.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five disadvantages of group decision making in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Outline five ways though which co-ordination may be achieved in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Disadvantages of Group Decision Making:
  1. Time-Consuming: Reaching consensus or agreement among group members often takes longer than individual decision-making.
  2. Potential for Conflict: Disagreements among members can lead to interpersonal conflicts that hinder the process or damage relationships.
  3. Domination by Few: More vocal, assertive, or high-status members might dominate the discussion, suppressing other valuable viewpoints.
  4. Groupthink: Pressure for conformity can lead the group to avoid critical evaluation of alternatives, resulting in poor or irrational decisions.
  5. Diffusion of Responsibility: No single individual feels fully accountable for the group's decision, which can sometimes lead to riskier or less carefully considered choices.
  6. Compromise Decisions: The need to appease different viewpoints may lead to watered-down compromises that are not the optimal solution.
(b) Ways to Achieve Coordination:
  1. Hierarchy/Chain of Command: Using formal authority where managers coordinate the activities of their subordinates and resolve interdepartmental issues.
  2. Rules & Procedures: Standardizing activities and interactions through established guidelines, policies, and processes.
  3. Planning & Goal Setting: Aligning departmental activities through shared objectives, integrated plans, and common targets.
  4. Liaison Roles/Coordinators: Appointing specific individuals or teams (e.g., project managers, brand managers) responsible for linking and integrating the work of different units.
  5. Committees & Task Forces: Utilizing cross-functional groups to discuss shared problems, coordinate specific projects, or facilitate communication between departments.
  6. Effective Communication Systems: Implementing robust systems for timely and accurate information sharing (e.g., meetings, reports, shared databases, collaborative software).

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five factors that should be considered when designing an effective control system in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five benefits of human resource planning to an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Factors in Designing an Effective Control System:
  1. Alignment with Objectives: Controls should be designed to monitor progress towards specific organizational goals and plans.
  2. Accuracy & Reliability: The information generated by the control system must be accurate and dependable.
  3. Timeliness: Control information should be provided promptly to allow for timely corrective action before significant deviations occur.
  4. Flexibility: The system should be adaptable enough to accommodate changing circumstances or unforeseen events.
  5. Cost-Effectiveness: The benefits derived from the control system should outweigh the costs of its implementation and operation.
  6. Understandability: Controls should be simple and clear enough for managers and employees to understand and use effectively.
  7. Focus on Strategic Points: Controls should concentrate on critical activities or key result areas where deviations are most significant.
(b) Benefits of Human Resource Planning (HRP):
  1. Anticipates Future Needs: Helps forecast future workforce requirements (numbers and skills) based on strategic goals.
  2. Optimizes Staffing: Ensures the organization has the right number of people with the right skills in the right places at the right time.
  3. Reduces Labor Costs: Helps avoid shortages (requiring expensive overtime or agency staff) and surpluses (leading to layoffs or underutilization).
  4. Supports Employee Development: Identifies skill gaps and informs training and development programs to prepare employees for future roles.
  5. Improves Recruitment & Selection: Provides a basis for targeted recruitment efforts based on forecasted needs.
  6. Facilitates Change Management: Helps manage workforce transitions during periods of growth, downsizing, or restructuring.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Explain the benefits that may be realized in an organization that adopts the participative style of leadership. (10 marks)

(b) Outline five assumptions of Theory ‘Y’ of motivation as advocated by Douglas McGregor. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Benefits of Participative Leadership Style:
  1. Increased Acceptance & Commitment: Employees involved in decision-making are more likely to understand, accept, and be committed to the outcomes.
  2. Higher Quality Decisions: Utilizes the diverse knowledge, skills, and perspectives of team members, potentially leading to better, more creative solutions.
  3. Improved Employee Morale & Motivation: Fulfills higher-level needs (esteem, self-actualization) by giving employees a sense of ownership, value, and trust.
  4. Employee Development: Provides opportunities for employees to develop critical thinking, problem-solving, and decision-making skills.
  5. Enhanced Communication & Teamwork: Fosters open communication, collaboration, and better relationships within the team.
(b) Assumptions of McGregor's Theory Y:
  1. Work is Natural: Employees view physical and mental effort in work as natural and potentially satisfying, not inherently disliked.
  2. Self-Direction & Self-Control: People will exercise self-direction and self-control towards objectives they are committed to, without needing external coercion.
  3. Commitment to Objectives: Commitment is a function of the rewards associated with achievement, particularly intrinsic rewards like satisfaction and self-actualization.
  4. Acceptance (and Seeking) of Responsibility: Under proper conditions, the average person learns not only to accept but also to actively seek responsibility.
  5. Creativity & Ingenuity: The capacity for imagination, ingenuity, and creativity in solving organizational problems is widely distributed among the workforce, not just limited to management.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Henri Fayol is the chief advocate of the administrative theory of management. Explain the contributions of this theory to modern management practice. (10 marks)

(b) Outline the measures that an individual employee may take in order to improve on time management. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Contributions of Administrative Theory (Fayol) to Modern Management:
  1. Functions of Management: Identified key managerial functions (Planning, Organizing, Commanding, Coordinating, Controlling) which still form the basis of management study.
  2. Principles of Management: Proposed 14 principles (e.g., division of work, authority, discipline, unity of command, scalar chain, equity) that provide general guidelines for effective management.
  3. Universality of Management: Argued that management principles are applicable to all types of organizations, not just businesses.
  4. Management as a Skill: Emphasized that management is a skill that can be taught and learned, leading to formal management education.
  5. Focus on Top-Level Management: Provided insights into the roles and responsibilities of senior managers in structuring and running an organization.
(b) Measures for Individual Employee Time Management:
  1. Goal Setting & Prioritization: Clearly defining daily/weekly goals and prioritizing tasks based on importance and urgency (e.g., using Eisenhower Matrix).
  2. Planning & Scheduling: Creating to-do lists, scheduling tasks in a calendar or planner, and allocating specific time blocks for activities.
  3. Minimizing Procrastination: Breaking down large tasks into smaller steps, setting deadlines, and starting with difficult tasks first ("Eat the Frog").
  4. Managing Distractions: Identifying common time-wasters (e.g., unnecessary meetings, emails, social media) and implementing strategies to minimize them (e.g., batching emails, turning off notifications).
  5. Learning to Say No: Politely declining requests or commitments that do not align with priorities or overload one's schedule.
  6. Using Time Management Tools: Utilizing calendars, planners, apps, or software to organize tasks and reminders.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five reasons that may make it necessary for an organization to adopt change. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five circumstances under which conflicts may be beneficial to an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Reasons for Adopting Change:
  1. External Environment Shifts: Changes in market demand (customer preferences), technology, competition, economic conditions, or regulations force organizations to adapt.
  2. Performance Gaps: Declining profitability, market share, productivity, or failure to meet objectives signals the need for change.
  3. Growth Opportunities: Pursuing new markets, developing new products/services, or engaging in mergers/acquisitions requires organizational change.
  4. Internal Inefficiencies: Identifying outdated processes, structures, systems, or skill gaps that hinder performance necessitates change.
  5. Changing Workforce: Shifts in employee demographics, values, expectations (e.g., work-life balance), or skill sets may require changes in HR practices or organizational culture.
(b) Circumstances Where Conflict Can Be Beneficial:
  1. Stimulates Creativity & Innovation: Constructive disagreement can challenge assumptions, encourage diverse perspectives, and lead to new ideas or solutions.
  2. Improves Decision Quality: Healthy debate and critical evaluation of alternatives can prevent groupthink and lead to more robust decisions.
  3. Identifies Problems: Conflict can bring underlying issues, differing viewpoints, or systemic problems to the surface so they can be addressed.
  4. Increases Engagement: Moderate levels of task-related conflict can increase participant involvement and energy in finding solutions.
  5. Facilitates Change: Conflict can challenge the status quo and create the necessary tension to drive organizational adaptation and change.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Explain five ways through which the nature of work can be a source of stress to an individual employee. (10 marks)

(b) Business organizations have developed new and innovative forms of employment in order to enjoy lower costs of labour. Outline these new forms of employment. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Nature of Work as a Source of Stress:
  1. Work Overload: Having too much work to do in the available time, leading to pressure and burnout.
  2. Role Ambiguity: Lack of clarity about job responsibilities, expectations, or performance standards.
  3. Role Conflict: Receiving conflicting demands or expectations from different supervisors or stakeholders.
  4. Lack of Control/Autonomy: Having little say over how work is performed, schedules, or decision-making processes.
  5. Poor Working Conditions: Unsafe, uncomfortable, or unpleasant physical work environment (e.g., noise, poor lighting, hazards).
  6. Monotony/Lack of Challenge: Performing repetitive, uninteresting tasks that lack stimulation or opportunities for skill utilization.
(b) New/Innovative Forms of Employment (for Lower Labor Costs):
  1. Outsourcing: Contracting out specific business functions or processes (e.g., IT support, customer service, manufacturing) to external providers, often in lower-cost locations.
  2. Offshoring: Relocating entire business units or operations to foreign countries with lower labor costs.
  3. Contingent Workforce (Gig Economy): Utilizing freelancers, independent contractors, temporary workers, or consultants on a project or short-term basis, avoiding costs associated with permanent employees (benefits, payroll taxes).
  4. Flexible Work Arrangements: Implementing models like remote work, flextime, or compressed workweeks, which can sometimes reduce overhead costs (office space) and attract talent willing to trade some benefits for flexibility.
  5. Automation & AI: Replacing human labor with technology, robots, or artificial intelligence for certain tasks to reduce long-term labor costs (though initial investment can be high).
