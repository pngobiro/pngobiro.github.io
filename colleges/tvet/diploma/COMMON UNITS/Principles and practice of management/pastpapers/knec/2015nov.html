Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five ways in which technical skills are useful to a manager when carrying out managerial tasks in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Give five reasons why a manager may find it necessary to apply the scientific principles of management when carrying out his duties in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Usefulness of Technical Skills to a Manager:
  1. Understanding Work Processes: Enables managers (especially lower-level) to understand the tasks performed by subordinates.
  2. Training & Coaching: Allows managers to effectively train, guide, and assist employees with technical aspects of their jobs.
  3. Problem Solving: Helps in identifying and solving technical problems related to operations or equipment.
  4. Credibility & Respect: Earns respect from subordinates when the manager demonstrates technical competence.
  5. Effective Decision Making: Facilitates informed decisions regarding technology, equipment, and process improvements.
(b) Reasons to Apply Scientific Management Principles:
  1. Increased Efficiency/Productivity: Finding the "one best way" to perform tasks through time-and-motion studies can significantly boost output.
  2. Standardization: Standardizing tools, methods, and working conditions leads to consistency and predictability.
  3. Scientific Selection & Training: Matching workers to jobs based on aptitude and providing specific training improves performance.
  4. Reduced Waste: Systematic analysis helps eliminate wasted time, effort, and materials.
  5. Basis for Incentive Schemes: Provides objective measures of performance for differential piece-rate systems or other incentives.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five limitations of using the Management By Objectives approach to provide management solutions in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Managers may sometimes be reluctant to adopt the product divisional basis for structuring organizations. Give five reasons that the managers may have for such reluctance. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Limitations of Management By Objectives (MBO):
  1. Time-Consuming: Setting and agreeing upon objectives requires significant time and effort from both managers and subordinates.
  2. Overemphasis on Quantifiable Goals: May neglect important qualitative goals or long-term objectives that are difficult to measure.
  3. Potential for Conflict: Disagreements can arise during the objective-setting or performance appraisal stages.
  4. Rigidity: Objectives set for a period might become outdated due to rapid environmental changes, but the system can be inflexible.
  5. Increased Paperwork & Administration: Can involve substantial documentation and administrative overhead to track progress and performance.
(b) Reasons for Reluctance to Adopt Product Divisional Structure:
  1. Duplication of Resources/Functions: Each division might require its own functional departments (e.g., marketing, finance), leading to duplication and higher costs.
  2. Coordination Challenges: Difficulty in coordinating activities and ensuring consistency across different product divisions.
  3. Potential for Conflict: Divisions might compete for resources or pursue conflicting goals, hindering overall organizational objectives.
  4. Loss of Economies of Scale: Functional specialization across the entire organization might offer greater economies of scale than duplicating functions within divisions.
  5. Difficulty in Finding/Developing General Managers: Requires capable managers who can oversee all functional aspects within a product division.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) Outline five ways through which employees can ensure effective utilization of their working hours in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) Give five reasons that justify the need for organizations to purchase stock in appropriate quantities. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Ensuring Effective Utilization of Working Hours (by Employees):
  1. Prioritization: Identifying and focusing on high-priority tasks that contribute most to objectives.
  2. Planning & Scheduling: Organizing the workday, scheduling tasks, and allocating appropriate time slots.
  3. Minimizing Distractions: Reducing non-work-related interruptions (e.g., personal calls, excessive socializing, social media).
  4. Effective Time Management Techniques: Using tools like to-do lists, time blocking, or techniques like Pomodoro.
  5. Taking Appropriate Breaks: Using scheduled breaks effectively to rest and recharge, improving focus during work periods.
  6. Avoiding Multitasking: Focusing on completing one task before moving to the next to improve quality and efficiency.
(b) Justification for Purchasing Stock in Appropriate Quantities:
  1. Minimize Holding Costs: Reduces costs associated with storage space, insurance, security, handling, and potential obsolescence or spoilage of excess inventory.
  2. Prevent Stockouts: Ensures sufficient stock is available to meet customer demand or production needs, avoiding lost sales, customer dissatisfaction, or production stoppages.
  3. Optimize Cash Flow: Avoids tying up excessive working capital in inventory that is not immediately needed.
  4. Reduce Risk of Damage/Spoilage/Obsolescence: Minimizes the chance of inventory becoming unusable due to long storage periods, damage, or changes in market demand/technology.
  5. Efficient Warehouse Management: Appropriate quantities make storage, handling, and tracking of inventory more manageable and efficient.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Wage differences among employees in an organization are unavoidable and serve certain purposes. Explain five such purposes. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five circumstances under which a manager may adopt the participative style of leadership in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Purposes of Wage Differences:
  1. Reflect Skill & Responsibility Levels: Compensate employees based on the complexity, skill requirements, and level of responsibility of their jobs.
  2. Reward Performance & Experience: Provide incentives for higher performance and recognize accumulated experience and seniority.
  3. Attract & Retain Talent: Offer competitive wages relative to the market to attract skilled individuals and retain valuable employees.
  4. Motivate Employees: Use pay differentials as a tool to motivate employees towards higher performance or promotion.
  5. Reflect Market Rates: Align pay with prevailing wage rates for similar jobs in the industry or geographic location.
(b) Circumstances for Using Participative Leadership:
  1. When Employees are Knowledgeable & Experienced: Subordinates possess relevant expertise and insights that can contribute to the decision.
  2. When Tasks are Complex & Non-Routine: Problems are unstructured, requiring creative solutions and diverse input.
  3. To Increase Acceptance & Commitment: Involving employees in decisions that affect them increases their buy-in and commitment to implementation.
  4. For Employee Development: To develop subordinates' decision-making skills and prepare them for higher responsibilities.
  5. When Time Permits: Participative decision-making often takes longer than autocratic approaches.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) Highlight five limitations that may be associated with the use of money to motivate employees in an organization. (10 marks)

(b) The Marketing Manager at Bass Ltd has recommended a change in the pricing of one of the organizationâ€™s products. Outline five reasons that may have led to this recommendation. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Limitations of Using Money as a Motivator:
  1. Temporary Effect: The motivational impact often diminishes over time as employees adapt to the higher pay level (Hedonic Adaptation).
  2. Diminishing Marginal Utility: Beyond a certain income level, additional money provides less satisfaction and motivation compared to other factors.
  3. Equity Concerns: Perceived unfairness in how monetary rewards are distributed can lead to demotivation, resentment, and conflict.
  4. Neglect of Intrinsic Motivation: Over-reliance on money can overshadow intrinsic motivators like achievement, recognition, interesting work, and personal growth.
  5. Entitlement Mentality: Employees may come to expect regular monetary rewards regardless of performance, reducing their incentive value.
(b) Reasons for Recommending a Price Change (Bass Ltd):
  1. Changes in Production/Input Costs: An increase in raw material, labor, or manufacturing costs might necessitate a price increase to maintain profit margins.
  2. Competitor Pricing Strategies: Responding to price changes by competitors (either matching cuts to remain competitive or increasing prices if competitors do).
  3. Changes in Market Demand: Increasing prices if demand is high and supply is limited, or decreasing prices to stimulate demand if it's low.
  4. Product Life Cycle Stage: Adjusting price based on the product's stage (e.g., skimming price at introduction, competitive pricing at maturity, price cuts during decline).
  5. Strategic Objectives/Positioning: Changing price to align with a new strategy, such as penetrating a new market (lower price) or repositioning as a premium brand (higher price).
  6. Economic Conditions: Responding to broader economic factors like inflation (requiring price increases) or recession (potentially requiring price cuts).

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) The level of conflict among employees at Nete Ltd has been on the increase in the recent past. Explain five measures that the management should take to address this problem. (10 marks)

(b) Explain five organizational factors that may contribute to stress among employees in a sales and marketing firm. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Measures to Address Increased Employee Conflict (Nete Ltd):
  1. Identify Root Causes: Investigate the underlying reasons for the conflict (e.g., resource scarcity, communication breakdown, personality clashes, unclear roles).
  2. Facilitate Open Communication: Create safe channels for employees to voice concerns and discuss issues openly and respectfully.
  3. Mediation/Intervention: Use a neutral third party (manager, HR) to mediate discussions between conflicting parties and help find common ground.
  4. Clarify Roles & Responsibilities: Ensure job descriptions, tasks, and reporting lines are clear to minimize ambiguity and potential turf wars.
  5. Team Building & Training: Conduct team-building activities and conflict resolution training to improve interpersonal skills and understanding.
  6. Establish Clear Policies & Procedures: Implement fair policies for resource allocation, performance appraisal, and conflict resolution.
(b) Organizational Factors Contributing to Stress (Sales & Marketing):
  1. High Performance Pressure/Targets: Unrealistic sales quotas, constant pressure to meet targets, and commission-based pay structures.
  2. Role Ambiguity/Conflict: Unclear job responsibilities, conflicting demands from different managers or clients.
  3. Work Overload/Long Hours: Excessive workload, frequent travel, and pressure to work long hours, impacting work-life balance.
  4. Lack of Control/Autonomy: Limited freedom in how to approach sales or marketing tasks, micromanagement.
  5. Poor Interpersonal Relationships: Conflict with colleagues, lack of support from management, or difficult client interactions.
  6. Job Insecurity: Fear of not meeting targets leading to potential job loss, especially in commission-heavy roles.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) Emerging technology may pose a number of challenges to organizations today. Highlight five ways in which a manager may deal with such challenges. (10 marks)

(b) Outline five techniques that a manager may adopt in order to enhance coordination in an organization. (10 marks)

Answer

(a) Dealing with Challenges of Emerging Technology:
  1. Continuous Learning & Training: Encourage and provide resources for managers and employees to stay updated on relevant technological advancements.
  2. Strategic Integration: Proactively assess how new technologies can be integrated into the business strategy to create competitive advantage or improve efficiency.
  3. Pilot Projects & Experimentation: Test new technologies on a smaller scale before full implementation to assess feasibility and manage risks.
  4. Change Management: Implement structured change management processes to help employees adapt to new tools and workflows, addressing resistance and providing support.
  5. Collaboration & Partnerships: Partner with technology providers, consultants, or research institutions to gain expertise and insights.
  6. Investment in Infrastructure: Ensure the organization has the necessary IT infrastructure to support new technologies.
(b) Techniques to Enhance Coordination:
  1. Clear Communication Channels: Utilizing meetings, reports, emails, and collaborative platforms effectively.
  2. Common Goals & Plans: Ensuring all units understand and work towards shared organizational objectives outlined in integrated plans.
  3. Standardization: Implementing common rules, procedures, and processes across departments.
  4. Liaison Roles/Coordinators: Appointing individuals or teams to link different departments or projects.
  5. Cross-Functional Teams: Forming teams with members from various departments to work on specific tasks requiring collaboration.
  6. Hierarchy: Using the formal chain of command where managers coordinate the activities of their subordinates.
