Interlocutory Applications

‚ÑπÔ∏è Overview & Procedure

Interlocutory (or Interim) applications are processes that occur between the filing of the suit and the trial. They seek temporary protection, adjustments, or remedies to maintain a just situation pending final determination.

Objectives:

  • Maintain or revert to peaceable status.
  • Avoid hardship or prejudice occurring before the case is decided.
  • Prevent undeserved advantage through unfair conduct.
  • Keep a fair balance of things and prevent abuse of legal process.

Mode of Application (Order 51):

  • Notice of Motion: The standard mode for most applications. Supported by an affidavit sworn by the applicant.
  • Chamber Summons: Used where specifically provided by rules (e.g., Ex parte applications).
  • Oral Application: Allowed in specific instances (e.g., Order 8 rule 8 for amendment, Order 33 Rule 3 for pauper suits).
02

üö´ Disposal of Suits Without Trial

1. Default Judgment (Order 10)

Arises upon default of plea (e.g., failure to enter appearance or file defence).

  • Liquidated Claim: Final judgment for amount claimed.
  • Pecuniary Damages: Interlocutory judgment (liability settled), set down for assessment of damages.

2. Summary Judgment (Order 36)

Applied for by the Plaintiff when they believe the Defendant has no defence. Available for:

  • Debt or liquidated claim.
  • Recovery of land (landlord vs tenant).

Court Options: Dismiss application, Give judgment for Plaintiff, or Grant leave to defend (conditional/unconditional).

3. Striking Out Pleadings (Order 2 Rule 15)

The court may strike out pleadings at any stage on the grounds that:

  • a
    It discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence.
  • b
    It is scandalous, frivolous, or vexatious.
  • c
    It may prejudice, embarrass, or delay fair trial.
  • d
    It is an abuse of the process of the court.
03

‚ö° Types of Interim Orders

A. Orders for a Commission

Used to take evidence outside the court when a witness cannot attend or for specialized tasks. Grounds include:

  • Examination of witnesses (sick, abroad, safety concerns).
  • Local investigations (e.g., assessing market value of property).
  • Examination of accounts.
  • To make partitions.
  • Scientific investigation.

B. Arrest/Attachment Before Judgment (Order 39)

  • Arrest: Where defendant plans to leave jurisdiction to obstruct or delay execution.
  • Attachment: Where defendant is disposing of property to defeat a potential decree.

C. Appointment of Receiver (Order 41)

An impartial person appointed to collect and manage rents/profits of property in dispute. Used to protect property from waste or damage during litigation.

D. Security for Costs (Order 26)

Defendant may apply for plaintiff to give security for costs if plaintiff resides outside Kenya or has no property within jurisdiction, or is a "man of straw" (applies mainly to nominal plaintiffs or companies).

04

üõë Injunctions (Order 40)

Principles for Granting (Giella vs. Cassman Brown)

  1. 1
    Prima Facie Case: Applicant must show a case with probability of success.
  2. 2
    Irreparable Injury: The injury must be such that damages would not be adequate compensation.
  3. 3
    Balance of Convenience: If in doubt, the court decides based on which party suffers more inconvenience.
üí°

Special Injunctions

Mareva Injunction: Restrains respondent from removing assets from jurisdiction or dissipating them to defeat a judgment. Requires full disclosure and a good arguable case.

Anton Piller Order: Allows applicant to enter respondent's premises to search for and seize evidence that might otherwise be destroyed. Requires an extremely strong prima facie case and clear evidence of possession of incriminating material.

05

üîç Discovery, Inspection & Admissions

Machinery to simplify the hearing by allowing parties to know the case of the other party.

1. Discovery of Facts (Interrogatories)

Written questions served on the opposing party to be answered on oath. Requires leave of court. Must be relevant to matters in issue.

2. Discovery of Documents

Compelling the opposite party to disclose documents in their possession. Usually done via a "List of Documents" or "Affidavit of Documents" (Schedule 1: documents willing to produce; Schedule 2: documents objecting to produce).

3. Inspection

Right to inspect and take copies of documents referred to in pleadings or affidavits.

4. Admissions (Order 13)

A party may formally admit facts or documents to save time and costs. Failure to admit upon notice may result in costs sanctions.

06

üìÖ Pre-Trial Directions (Order 11)

A mandatory process to organize the case before trial, involving three conferences:

Conference Timing Objective
Case Conference Within 30 days after close of pleadings Identify issues, create timetable, explore resolution.
Settlement Conference Within 60 days of Case Conference Explore settlement, narrow issues, exchange briefs.
Trial Conference 30 days before hearing Plan trial time, amend pleadings, sign Trial Conference Memorandum.

Pre-Trial Questionnaire: Must be completed by parties 10 days before the Case Conference.
