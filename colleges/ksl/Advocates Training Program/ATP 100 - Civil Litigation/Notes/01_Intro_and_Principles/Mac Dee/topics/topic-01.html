üîç General Overview of Civil Procedure

What is Civil Procedure? Consider the following scenarios to understand how a civil procedure arises:

  1. Cause of Action: Ensure that you understand the cause of action displayed in the facts the client presents to you. What is the cause of action established by the facts?

  2. Parties: Who is the Plaintiff or Plaintiffs?

  3. Jurisdiction: Where do I file my case in which court?

  4. Remedy: What remedy does my client have or ought to get?

  5. Drafting: Actual drafting of the pleadings (this will depend on the cause of action established in the facts).

  6. Filing: File the pleadings once ready and drafted properly.

  7. Service: Preparation of summons, get them signed and paid for, served. This is catered for in the Civil Procedure Rules.

  8. Default: The defendant who has been properly served may choose not to defend either of free will or oversight. If served and chooses not to defend/file appearance, you seek judgment in default of appearance.

  9. Judgment Types: There could be an interlocutory or final judgment in default.

  10. Defence & Interlocutory Proceedings: If defense is filed, determine if you can bring the suit to an end (e.g., Summary Judgment under Order 35, or striking out under Order 2). Consider interlocutory applications like injunctions to preserve the subject matter.

  11. Pre-Trial: Prepare suit for trial, fix the suit for hearing, and summon witnesses.

  12. Right to Begin: Under Civil Procedure Rules, determine who begins. Sometimes the Defendant must begin if they argue the Plaintiff is not entitled to relief on a point of law.

  13. Trial Conduct: Opening statements, calling witnesses, cross-examination, and re-examination.

  14. Judgment & Decree: Court delivers judgment (signed and read). Extract the decree to allow for execution.

  15. Execution/Appeal: Execute the decree (attachment, winding up, etc.) or handle appeals (stay of execution, memorandum of appeal).

  16. Costs & Review: File bill of costs for taxation. Judicial Review is provided under Order 53.

üìö Key Concepts & Distinctions

Civil Law

Civil law regulates relations between private individuals by enforcing obligations or compensating injured parties. If a person's private rights are violated, they have a cause of action. Civil cases are styled as Plaintiff v Defendant.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Standard of Proof

The standard of proof in civil cases is on a balance of probabilities or on a preponderance of probabilities. It must be more probable than improbable that the plaintiff's allegations are true.

Civil Litigation Objectives

  • Ensure just determination of the case.
  • Ensure efficient determination.
  • Dispose of matters in a timely manner (expeditiously).
  • Proper application of technology.

Civil Law vs. Criminal Law

Civil Wrong Criminal Wrong
Private wrong against an individual. Public wrong against the state.
Parties: Plaintiff vs Defendant (or Applicant vs Respondent). Parties: Prosecution/State vs Accused.
Parties free to compromise/withdraw. Cannot be compromised (State is complainant) unless via Prosecutor withdrawal.
Standard of Proof: Balance of Probabilities. Standard of Proof: Beyond Reasonable Doubt.
Objective: Restore injured party (compensation, restitution). Objective: Punish wrong-doers.
Heard through Civil Procedure. Heard through Criminal Procedure.

üìú History of Civil Procedure in Kenya

  • 1844: Colonial government borrowed the Indian Civil Procedure Code.
  • 1897: East African Order in Council came into force. Protectorate court created to apply English Procedure (but Indian Code continued).
  • 1924: The core code was enacted as Ordinance No.3 (without rules of procedure).
  • 1927: Rules were made based on English procedure.
  • 2010: Civil Procedure Rules 2010 gazetted (effective 17th Sept 2010), borrowing from US practice (e.g., Pre-trial conference).

üèõÔ∏è Sources of Civil Procedure

1. The Constitution

The supreme law of the Republic.

  • Article 2: Constitution is supreme and binds all persons and State organs.
  • Article 2(4): Any law inconsistent with the Constitution is void to the extent of inconsistency.

2. Statute Law

Primarily the Civil Procedure Act (Cap 21).

üí°

Special Jurisdiction

Where a specific Act grants specific jurisdiction and prescribes procedure (e.g., Winding Up under Companies Act, Matrimonial Causes Act), that specific procedure prevails over the Civil Procedure Act (Section 3A).

3. Rules of Court

Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) are made by the Rules Committee under Section 81 of the Civil Procedure Act.

  • Rules Committee Composition: Two High Court judges, two Court of Appeal judges, Attorney General, two advocates (LSK/Mombasa Law Society).
  • Function: Regulate procedure of courts. They do not confer new rights or jurisdiction.
  • Conflict: If a rule is inconsistent with the Act, the Act prevails.
‚öñÔ∏è

Case Law: Conflict between Act and Rules

Central District Maize Millers Association v Maciel [1944] 6ULR J130

The court held that if a rule is inconsistent with the Act, it is ultra vires. If a rule limits an unfettered power conferred by the Act, it is inconsistent. If capable of two constructions, the court leans to the one consistent with the Act.

See also: S S Gupta v Inder Singh Bhamra [1965] EA 439.

4. Case Law

Judicial pronouncements form a source of procedure. Examples:

  • Tiwi Beach vs. Stamm (1991): Disclosure of material facts.
  • Giella vs Cassman Brown: Requirements for temporary injunctions.

‚ö° Inherent Jurisdiction

Section 3A of the Civil Procedure Act: "Nothing in this Act shall limit or otherwise affect the inherent power of the court to make such orders as may be necessary for the ends of justice or to prevent abuse of the process of the court."

üìù

Definition: Montreal Trust Case

Montreal Trust Company vs. Church Hill Forest Industries (1971): "The court inherent jurisdiction is the reserved or fund of powers, a residual source of powers which the court may draw upon as necessary whenever it is just or equitable to do so..."
