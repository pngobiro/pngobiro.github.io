‚öñÔ∏è The Concept of Jurisdiction

Jurisdiction refers to the power of a court to hear and determine a case. It is a creature of statute. "Jurisdiction is everything. Without it, a court has no power to make one step."

üî®

Locus Classicus

The Owners of the Motor Vessel "Lillian S" v. Caltex Oil (Kenya) Ltd (1989):

"Where a court has no jurisdiction, there would be no basis for a continuation of proceedings pending other evidence. A court of law downs its tools in respect of the matter before it the moment it holds the opinion that it is without jurisdiction."

Types of Jurisdiction

Type Description Key Legislation
Territorial Power over events/persons within a specific geographic area. S.12, S.15 CPA
Pecuniary Monetary limits on the value of the subject matter a court can handle. Magistrates Courts Act
Subject Matter Power to hear specific types of cases (e.g., Land, Employment). ELC Act, ELRC Act

Fig. 3.1: The Hierarchy of Courts in Kenya

A. Superior Courts

1. Supreme Court (Article 163)

  • Composition: Chief Justice (President), Deputy CJ, and 5 other judges. Quorum is 5.
  • Exclusive Jurisdiction: Presidential election disputes.
  • Appellate Jurisdiction: Appeals from Court of Appeal (as of right for Constitutional interpretation; otherwise requires certification of general public importance).
  • Advisory: Opinions on County Government matters.

2. Court of Appeal (Article 164)

  • Composition: Not fewer than 12 judges. President elected by judges.
  • Jurisdiction: Primarily appellate from High Court and other tribunals.
  • Limited Original Jurisdiction: Punishment for contempt, stay of execution pending appeal.

3. High Court (Article 165)

Unlimited original jurisdiction in criminal and civil matters. Organized into divisions (Commercial, Family, Criminal, Judicial Review & Constitutional).

Special Powers of the High Court:
  • Supervisory: Judicial Review (Mandamus, Certiorari, Prohibition, Habeas Corpus).
  • Constitutional: Interpretation of the Constitution (Bill of Rights).
  • Admiralty: Matters arising on high seas/territorial waters (Judicature Act s.4).
  • Election: Parliamentary election petitions.
  • Succession/Probate: Administration of estates.

B. Subordinate Courts (Article 169)

1. Magistrates Courts

Established under Magistrates Courts Act 2015. Pecuniary jurisdiction limits (Section 7(1)):

Rank Pecuniary Limit (Kshs)
Chief Magistrate 20,000,000
Senior Principal Magistrate 15,000,000
Principal Magistrate 10,000,000
Senior Resident Magistrate 7,000,000
Resident Magistrate 5,000,000

Note: The CJ can vary these limits. They also handle customary law matters (land, marriage/divorce/dowry, seduction, status/guardianship, intestate succession).

2. Kadhis' Courts (Article 170)

  • Jurisdiction: Muslim law relating to personal status, marriage, divorce, or inheritance.
  • Condition: All parties must profess Muslim religion and submit to jurisdiction.

3. Tribunals

Bodies with judicial/quasi-judicial functions supplementing ordinary courts. Examples include:

  • Rent Tribunals: Business Premises (controlled tenancies < 5 years), Residential Rent Restriction.
  • Political Parties Dispute Tribunal (PPDT): Disputes between party members, coalitions, etc.
  • Co-operative Tribunal: Disputes within co-operative societies.
  • Public Procurement Administrative Review Board (PPARB): Tender disputes.

üö´ Principles Limiting Jurisdiction

1. Sub-Judice (Section 6 CPA)

Stay of Suit: No court shall proceed with the trial of any suit where the matter in issue is directly and substantially in issue in a previously instituted suit between the same parties.

  • Purpose: Prevent conflicting judgments and concurrent adjudication.
  • Effect: The subsequent suit is stayed (not dismissed).

2. Res Judicata (Section 7 CPA)

Bar to Suit: No court shall try any suit or issue in which the matter has been directly and substantially in issue in a former suit between the same parties and has been heard and finally decided.

  • Purpose: Bring finality to litigation.
  • Effect: Absolute bar to subsequent suit on the same matter.

Distinction

Sub-judice applies when a case is pending. Res judicata applies when a case has been decided.
