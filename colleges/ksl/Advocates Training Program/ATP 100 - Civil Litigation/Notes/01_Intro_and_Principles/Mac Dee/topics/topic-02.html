üó£Ô∏è The Client Interview

Purpose and Scope

Taking instructions is the inception of any legal process. It establishes the fiduciary advocate-client relationship cemented by the retainer. The interview allows the advocate to:

  • Learn the client's intention.
  • Elicit facts as perceived by the client.
  • Afford a legal opinion.
  • Formulate a course of action.
üîí

Confidentiality

Knowledge acquired during the interview must be treated as confidential and not disclosed without client consent. King Woollen Mills Ltd. v. Kaplan & Stratton Advocates (1990-1994) EA 244.

Interview Stages

1. Preliminary Stage

Initial communication (phone/visit) to get a rough outline. Use this stage to:

  • Discuss fees and costs early.
  • Identify necessary documents for the client to bring.
  • Check for conflicts of interest or personal bias.
  • Set a specific date and time for the substantive interview.

2. Commencing the Interview

Make the client comfortable. Avoid "ego walls" initially. Ensure privacy and no interruptions.

3. Information Gathering

The most important stage. Let the client tell the story in their own words first.

  • Listen actively: Do not interrupt unless necessary.
  • Summarize: Retell the story to confirm understanding.
  • Clarify: Ask questions to fill gaps, but avoid judgmental cross-examination at this stage.
  • Assess: Evaluate the legal issues, defenses, and strategy.

4. Closing

Explain the plan of action, fees, and next steps. Establish trust.

Managing Difficult Clients

Indicators of a difficult client include:

  • Multiple previous advocates on the same matter.
  • Unrealistic expectations (time/cost).
  • Refusal to listen or provide instructions.
  • Verbal abuse or inability to pay.

Management Strategy: Document everything (calls, instructions in writing), set boundaries, manage expectations, and explain the importance of honesty.

‚öñÔ∏è Pre-Trial Preliminary Considerations

1. Cause of Action

A legally recognized right to relief. You must identify the specific elements of the substantive law (e.g., Tort of Negligence) and ensure facts exist to support each element.

üöó

Example: The Kombo Case

Facts: Kombo injured in a bus accident due to brake failure. Maintenance was by Allied Auto Repair.

Analysis (Negligence):

  1. Duty: Allied owed a duty of care to passengers.
  2. Breach: Brake failure implies improper maintenance.
  3. Causation: Failure caused the accident.
  4. Damages: Kombo sustained injuries.

Conclusion: Cause of action exists.

2. Time Limitations

Unless filed within the statute of limitations, a case will be dismissed. Limitation of Actions Act (Cap 22):

Action Type Limitation Period
Defamation 1 Year
Against Government/State Corps 1 Year (requires notice)
Tort 3 Years
Contract 6 Years
Land 12 Years

Calculation: Do not count the first day (day of incident), but count the last day.

Laches: Equitable principle preventing lawsuits where too much time has elapsed, even if within statutory limits (applies to equitable relief like specific performance).

3. Feasibility & Ethics

  • Feasibility: Is the lawsuit practical? (Cost vs Benefit).
  • Turning Down a Case: Must be done clearly in writing. Warn about statute of limitations.
  • Ethical Considerations:
    • Competency: Do not accept if you lack time/knowledge.
    • Frivolous Claims: Do not pursue meritless suits.
    • Conflict of Interest: Avoid suing current or former clients on related matters.

‚úâÔ∏è The Demand Letter Before Action

Purpose and Requirement

A formal notice demanding performance of a legal obligation. It is mandatory in most civil suits (Order 3, Rule 2(d)).

  • Objectives: Avoid litigation, give notice, elicit payment/compliance, avoid costs penalty.
  • Format: Written by person or lawyer, sent via registered mail or process server.

Contents of a Demand Letter

  1. Date and recipient details.
  2. "WITHOUT PREJUDICE" header (if applicable for negotiations).
  3. Statement of authority to act.
  4. Summary of the matter/facts.
  5. Specific demand (relief or payment).
  6. Deadline for compliance.
  7. Intention to sue (consequences of non-compliance).
‚ö†Ô∏è

What NOT to Include

  • Threats of criminal proceedings (Khanbhai v O'Swald).
  • Demand for advocate's collection costs (unless agreed upon later as fresh consideration).

Without Prejudice Communication

Protects communication attempting to resolve disputes from being used in court as admission of liability.

  • Scope: Only covers genuine attempts to settle.
  • Exceptions: Fraud, explaining delay, or proving a settlement was reached.
