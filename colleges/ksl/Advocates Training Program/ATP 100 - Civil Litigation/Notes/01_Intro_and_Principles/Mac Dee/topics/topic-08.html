Appeals & Reviews

üìö Orders 42 & 45, Procedures & Powers

‚öñÔ∏è Appeals (Order 42)

An appeal is a creature of statute; there is no inherent right of appeal. Every decree may be appealed unless barred by law. For orders, only those listed in Order 42 Rule 1 are appealable as of right; others require leave.

Appellate Hierarchy

  • Supreme Court: Final court. Appeals from Court of Appeal (as of right for Constitutional interpretation; otherwise requires certification of general public importance).
  • Court of Appeal: Appeals from High Court and tribunals.
  • High Court: Appellate jurisdiction from Magistrates' Courts and subordinate tribunals.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Leave to Appeal

Where no automatic right exists, leave must be sought from the court making the order (within 14 days) or the appellate court. Purpose: To prevent frivolous and needless appeals.

Timelines and Documents

Step Timeline Action
Notice of Appeal 14 days Lodged with the Registrar of the superior court. States intention to appeal.
Service of Notice 7 days Served on all persons directly affected.
Record of Appeal 60 days Lodging the Memorandum of Appeal and Record of Appeal (pleadings, notes, judgment, decree).

Extension of Time: Can be granted by the court for "sufficient reason" (Order 50 r 6).

02

üõë Stay of Execution

An appeal does not automatically operate as a stay of execution. The successful party can proceed to execute unless a stay order is obtained (Order 42 r 6).

Conditions for Granting Stay:

  1. 1
    Substantial Loss: The applicant must prove they will suffer substantial loss unless the order is made.
  2. 2
    No Delay: The application must be made without unreasonable delay.
  3. 3
    Security: The applicant has given security for the due performance of the decree as ordered by the court.

Procedure: Application is made by Notice of Motion. It can be made informally immediately after judgment delivery for a temporary stay while filing the formal application.

03

‚ö° Powers of the Appellate Court

The first appellate court has a duty to re-evaluate the evidence and come to its own conclusion, bearing in mind it did not see the witnesses.

Specific Powers:

  • Determine Finally: If evidence on record is sufficient.
  • Remand Case: Send back to lower court for re-hearing (e.g., if disposed of on a preliminary point).
  • Frame Issues: If trial court omitted essential issues, frame them and refer for trial.
  • Take Additional Evidence: Only if lower court refused admissible evidence or it is needed for judgment. (Strict conditions apply).
  • Order New Trial: If the trial was so defective (wrong law/facts) that reversal is not justifiable.
04

üîÑ Review (Order 45)

Review is a judicial re-examination of the same case by the same judge (or successor). It is an exception to the functus officio rule (that a court ceases to have control once judgment is signed).

üìú

Section 80 CPA: Who may apply?

Any person aggrieved by a decree/order from which an appeal is allowed but no appeal has been preferred, or from which no appeal is allowed.

Grounds for Review:

  1. 1
    Discovery of New Evidence: New and important matter which, after due diligence, was not within applicant's knowledge or could not be produced at the time.
  2. 2
    Mistake or Error Apparent: Error on the face of the record (self-evident, not requiring argument).
  3. 3
    Any Other Sufficient Reason: Analogous to the grounds above.

Procedure: Notice of Motion. Heard by the same judge who passed the decree.

üí°

Review vs Appeal

Review: Same court, error correction. Appeal: Higher court, error correction.

If an appeal has been instituted, review is generally not allowed. If review is rejected, you can appeal the rejection. If review is granted, the original decree is vacated.
