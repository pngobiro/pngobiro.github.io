üîç Determining the Existence of a Cause of Action

Not all issues determined in a client interview are resolved through the litigation process. A legal right must exist. This legally recognized right to relief is known as a cause of action.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Definition: Cause of Action

  1. Facts sufficient to support a valid suit.
  2. The legal theory upon which a lawsuit ("action") is based.

In the pleadings, this will include:

  • The pertinent facts distinct in each situation;
  • The particulars (if any) which relate to the actions of the defendant; and
  • The reaction of the plaintiff in relation to the acts of the defendant that caused the plaintiff to incur a loss or to be damaged.

Steps to Determine a Cause of Action

In determining whether a cause of action exists, you must examine both the law and the facts in the case:

  1. General Area: Determine what general area of substantive law applies to the case.
  2. Specific Topic: Narrow the general substantive law area and identify a more specific topic.
  3. Elements: Examine that specific area and determine what factors or elements must be present before a cause of action is created.
  4. Facts Review: Review the case itself to see if facts exist that support each of the elements.

Case Study: The Kombo Case

Seven months ago June Kombo was injured in an automobile-bus collision. The accident occurred when the brakes on the bus failed, resulting in the driver's inability to stop for a red light. The bus, in which Kombo was a passenger, was hit broadside by a car entering the intersection at the green light. The bus was owned and operated by the city. However, all maintenance on the bus was performed by Allied Auto Repair, a private company under contract with the city to maintain and repair all city buses.

üí°

Legal Analysis: Negligence

The Kombo case is controlled by the substantive law of torts, specifically negligence. To have a cause of action, the following elements must be shown:

Element Required Application to Facts
1. Duty of Care Allied Auto Repair owed a duty of care to passengers (Kombo) to maintain the bus properly.
2. Breach of Duty Brake failure implies improper maintenance, evidencing a breach.
3. Actual Cause If brakes hadn't failed, the accident wouldn't have happened.
4. Proximate Cause Injuries were a foreseeable consequence of failed brakes.
5. Damages Kombo sustained injuries and incurred expenses.

Conclusion: Because each element is supported by facts, Kombo has a cause of action against Allied Auto Repair for negligence.

Unless a case is filed within the appropriate time, the statute of limitations may preclude the institution of such suit regardless of the merits of the case. Only the court‚Äôs discretion can be exercised to revive such a case, with sufficient cause shown to explain delay.

See: Barclays Bank of Kenya v. Kepha Nyabera and 189 others and 2 others [2007] eKLR; E.M.S v. Emirates Airlines [2012] eKLR

Calculating Time

In calculating the statute of limitations, you do not count the first day, but you count the last day.

Example: Accident on Jan 28, 2013. Start counting Jan 29, 2013. Three-year limit expires Jan 28, 2016. If that day is a holiday, you have until the next working day.

Tolling the Statute of Limitations

When a statute is tolled, the time stops running. Common scenarios include:

  • Minority: Tolled during minority; begins running when minor reaches age of majority.
  • Fraud/Mistake: Time runs from discovery of fraud/mistake (Sec 26).
  • Presidential Incumbency: Article 143(2) precludes civil suits against an incumbent president; time is tolled until they leave office.

Doctrine of Laches

Laches is an equitable principle preventing lawsuits when, in fairness to the defendant, too much time has elapsed, even if the statute of limitations hasn't expired. It applies only to equitable cases (e.g., Specific Performance), not money damages.

üö´ Feasibility & Turning Down Cases

Feasibility of the Lawsuit

Even if a case has merit, it may not be practical. Litigation takes time and money (fees + disbursements). An advocate must review practicality and whether the matter is within their scope of knowledge.

See: Joseph Wanjohi Njau v. Benson Maina Kabau [2013] eKLR.

Turning Down a Case

If deciding not to accept a case:

  • Clearly communicate to the individual.
  • Do it in writing for the record.
  • Exercise care in stating opinions regarding merits.
  • Advise on Statutes of Limitation (Crucial!).
Sample Rejection Letter 
Mr. Mlalamishi
21 City Square
Nairobi County

Dear Mr. Mlalamishi,

Thank you for considering us regarding your dispute with ABC Corporation. As I explained to you on telephone, our law firm is presently unable to represent you in this matter. Please note that our inability to accept your case is not a reflection or comment on the merits of your case.

If you wish to pursue this matter you should consult other legal advice. If you decide to do so, you should act as soon as possible.

As we have previously explained to you, the statute of limitations in this kind of cases is three years from the date of injury. If you have not filed a lawsuit within that time you will be prevented from doing so.

Thanking you for considering us.

Regards.

‚öñÔ∏è Ethical Considerations

Before Accepting a Case

  • Competency: Do not accept if you lack ability, knowledge, or time.
  • Frivolous Claims: Do not pursue lawsuits with no merit. Risk of being sued personally or disciplinary proceedings.
  • Conflict of Interest: Arises when suing a party currently or previously represented. Determined by whom the firm represents, not just the individual advocate.

See: Oriental Commercial Bank Limited v. Central Bank of Kenya [2012] eKLR; King Woollen Mills & Anor v Kaplan & Stratton Advocates.
