üëã Entering Appearance

The first step for a defendant served with summons is to enter an appearance within the stipulated time (Order 6 Rule 2). This prevents a default judgment.

Memorandum of Appearance (Form No. 12)

The memorandum must contain:

  • Name of the defendant (as in summons).
  • Date of entering appearance.
  • Signature of defendant or advocate.
  • Address for Service: Physical and postal address.

Specific Scenarios

Defendant Type Requirement
Firm/Partnership Must list individual partners: "Partners in the firm of..."
Individual Trading As Must appear in own name: "[Name] trading as [Business]"
Corporation By an advocate or officer authorized under corporate seal.
02

üìù Written Statement of Defence (WSD)

The WSD is the defendant's formal reply to the allegations in the plaint. Its function is to state the grounds and material facts relied upon to defeat the plaintiff's claim.

‚è≥

Strict Timelines (Order 7 Rule 1)

  1. Enter Appearance: Within time stipulated in summons.
  2. File Defence: Within 14 days after entering appearance.
  3. Serve Plaintiff: Within 14 days from date of filing defence.

Mandatory Accompaniments (Order 7 Rule 5)

The defence must be accompanied by:

  • Affidavit (where there is a counterclaim).
  • List of witnesses to be called.
  • Written statements signed by witnesses (except experts).
  • Copies of documents to be relied upon.
03

‚ôüÔ∏è Strategies for Response

A defendant has several options when responding to a pleading:

‚ñ∂ 1. Traverse (Denial)

A traverse is a specific denial of an allegation.

  • Rule: Any allegation not specifically denied is deemed admitted (Order 2 Rule 11).
  • Exception: Damages are deemed traversed unless admitted.
  • Requirement: Must not be evasive. "General denials" are weak; deal with each allegation specifically.
‚ñ∂ 2. Confession and Avoidance

Admitting the facts but avoiding the legal consequences by asserting fresh facts.

Example: "The defendant admits dismissing the plaintiff (Confession) but denies it was wrongful because the plaintiff stole funds (Avoidance)."

‚ñ∂ 3. Raising a Point of Law (Order 2 Rule 9)

Instead of pleading facts, the defendant raises an objection based on law (e.g., lack of jurisdiction, limitation). This can lead to the suit being struck out preliminarily.

‚ñ∂ 4. Seeking Further Particulars

Requesting details on vague allegations (e.g., fraud) before filing a defence. This forces the opponent to clarify their case.

04

‚öîÔ∏è Counterclaim vs. Set-off

A defendant may have a claim against the plaintiff. They can file a separate suit or include it in the defence.

Feature Set-off Counterclaim
Nature A Defence (Shield) A Cross-Action (Sword)
Effect Extinguishes/reduces plaintiff's claim Independent judgment against plaintiff
Recovery Cannot recover excess Can recover excess balance
Survival Falls if suit is dismissed Survives if suit is dismissed

Counterclaim Specifics

  • Must be specifically pleaded.
  • Can include new parties (Order 7 Rule 9).
  • Plaintiff must file a Reply to Defence and Defence to Counterclaim within 14 days (15 days if new party involved).
