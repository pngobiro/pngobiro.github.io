üì¢ Purpose & Scope

A demand letter is a formal notice demanding that the addressee perform a legal obligation, such as rectifying a problem, paying a sum of money, or honouring a contractual commitment, on specific terms and within a specified time.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Core Function

The letter gives the recipient a chance to perform the obligation without being taken to court. It serves to inform the adversary of a pending claim.

Is it Mandatory?

In most civil suits guided by the Civil Procedure Rules 2010, a demand letter is mandatory.

  • Where pleaded, a demand is required to be made as a matter of law (Order 3, rule 2(d)).
  • Where it is excluded, a party may not be able to claim for costs in the suit.

Reasons for Issuing a Demand

  1. To avoid incurring additional costs of suit should the claim be admitted.
  2. To avoid suits that may be vexatious or brought out of malice.
  3. To give notice of intention to right a wrong against a legal right.

Signing Requirements

A demand letter should be signed by an advocate. Signing in the name of the firm is not sufficient as it may lay the demand letter open to challenge (Singh v Munshi Ram).

Professional Misconduct Warnings

1. Criminal Threats: Never include a threat that criminal proceedings would be initiated against the debtor in event of non-payment (Khanbhai v O'Swald).

2. Advocate's Costs: You may NOT demand from the debtor the costs of the advocate giving notice. There is both a statutory and professional bar to making such a demand.

Exception: When Can You Charge Costs?

If, subsequent to the original letter of demand, the debtor requests to be allowed to make payment by installments, and these terms are accepted, then it is permissible to add the advocate‚Äôs costs to the principal sum owing.

Reasoning: This is permissible because fresh consideration (time to pay) is being given by the creditor.

‚öñÔ∏è Special Considerations

"Without Prejudice" Communication

Upon reply, the person who is being claimed against will often quote the phrase "Without Prejudice".

See: Millicent Wambui v Nairobi Botanica Gardening Limited [2013] eKLR.

  • Scope: Protects communication between parties that genuinely attempts to resolve disputes.
  • Effect: Excludes use of admissions/concessions against the party in subsequent court proceedings.
  • Exception: Admissible if the issue is whether a settlement was reached or if the communication was made at all.

Variance with Plaint

Where the plaint is at variance with the demand letter, particulars in explanation must be given by the plaintiff (Abdulla v Esmail).
