Striking Out Pleadings

The court is clothed with wide and ample powers to enforce the formal requirements of pleadings. This power is conferred to compel parties to comply with the rules of pleadings and is exercised under the inherent jurisdiction of the court.

‚ö°

Summary Process

Striking out is a summary process without a trial. Generally, no oral evidence is admissible in these applications (Rule 15(2)).

Because it involves throwing out a case without a full hearing, this discretionary power must be exercised judiciously and only in clear-cut cases.

Grounds for Striking Out (Order 2 Rule 15)

The court may order any pleading to be struck out or amended on the grounds that:

  • It discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence.
  • It is scandalous, frivolous, or vexatious.
  • It may prejudice, embarrass, or delay the fair trial of the action.
  • It is an abuse of the process of the court.
02

üîç Analysis of Grounds

1. No Reasonable Cause of Action

The cause of action must be one recognized by the laws of Kenya (statutes, common law, or adopted English Common Law). If the claim is not supported by any law, it must be struck out. The cause of action must be "inconsistently bad" and beyond doubt unsustainable.

2. Scandalous, Frivolous or Vexatious

Term Meaning in Pleadings
Scandalous Matters that are indecent, offensive, or made merely to abuse/prejudice the opposite party. Also includes degrading charges or unnecessary details.
Frivolous Has no substance, is fanciful ("trifling with the court"), or not capable of reasoned argument.
Vexatious Lacks bona fides, brought for annoyance, or to gain a fanciful advantage leading to no possible good.

3. Prejudice, Embarrass or Delay

A pleading is embarrassing if:

  • It is ambiguous or unintelligible.
  • It raises immaterial matters, enlarging issues and costs.
  • The defendant does not clearly state how much is admitted vs. denied.

4. Abuse of Court Process

The process of the court must be used honestly and in good faith. Misusing court machinery for oppression constitutes an abuse of process.

03

üìö Judicial Principles

In Peter Ngugi Kabiri v. Esther Wangari Githinji & Another [2013] eKLR, the court outlined key factors:

  • Assumption of Truth: In deciding if a claim discloses triable issues, the court assumes all allegations in it are true.
  • Incurability: To be struck out, the claim must be so badly drawn that no amendment could cure it.
  • Caution: The power must be exercised with extreme caution. A pleading cannot be struck out merely because it is demurrable; it must show the action will not lie in law.
üí°

Alternatives to Striking Out

Where a pleading is merely irrelevant or embarrassing but not fatal, it may be wiser to apply for further and better particulars or leave to amend, rather than striking out.
