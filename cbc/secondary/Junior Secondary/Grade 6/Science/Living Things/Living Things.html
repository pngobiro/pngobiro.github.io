Living Things and Plant Biology

Living Things

The term "living thing" refers to any organism or life form that possesses or shows characteristics of life or being alive.

Plants

Plants are living things. The grouping of plants together with common characteristics or features is called the classification of plants.

Types of Plants

There are different types of plants, including:

  • Trees

    Trees are big plants with a single stem called a trunk and many strong branches. Examples of trees include mango, coconut, and avocado trees.

  • Shrubs

    Shrubs are shorter than trees and have many thin, woody stems. Examples of shrubs include hibiscus, rose, and cotton plants.

  • Herbs

    Herbs are small plants with soft green stems. Examples of herbs include mint and coriander.

  • Grasses

    Grasses are short plants with narrow leaves.

Parts of a Plant

The external parts of a plant include:

  • Roots
  • Stem
  • Leaves
  • Flowers
  • Fruits

Functions of Plant Parts

Part of a Plant Function
Leaves
  • Breathing (exchange of gases through stomata)
  • Manufacture of food through photosynthesis
  • Storage of food (in some plants)
  • Removal of excess water through transpiration
Stem
  • Transports water and mineral salts from roots to leaves
  • Carries food from leaves to roots for storage
  • Holds or supports the upper parts of the plant
  • Protects the plant
  • Storage of water and food (in some plants)
Fruits
  • Storage of food (in some plants)
  • Protecting seeds
Seeds Germinate into new plants
Flowers Reproductive organs that develop into fruits

Types of Roots

There are two main types of roots:

  • Tap Roots

    Tap roots consist of one main root that grows down into the soil, with side roots arising from the main root. They grow deep into the soil vertically downwards. Plants with tap roots include legumes, acacia, and fruit trees.

  • Fibrous Roots

    Fibrous roots do not have a main root; all roots are similar and arise from the same place. They are shallow and grow horizontally in all directions. Plants with fibrous roots include cereals, oats, grass, sisal, onions, and sugarcane.

Comparison of Tap Roots and Fibrous Roots

Characteristic Tap Roots Fibrous Roots
Main Root Structure They consist of one main root that grows down into the soil They do not have a main root
Lateral Roots They have lateral roots that arise from the main root All roots are similar and arise from the same place
Depth They grow deep into the soil They are shallow
Growth Direction They grow vertically downwards into the soil They grow horizontally in all directions
Examples Legumes, Acacia, Fruit trees, Coconuts Cereals, Oats, Grass, Sisal, Onions, Sugarcane

Other types of roots include:

  • Aerial roots (for breathing)
  • Prop roots (used in maize for support)

Transpiration in Plants

Transpiration is the process in which plants lose excess water through small holes called stomata. Transpiration is high when it's hot, sunny, dry, and windy, and low when it's cold, wet, calm, and rainy.
