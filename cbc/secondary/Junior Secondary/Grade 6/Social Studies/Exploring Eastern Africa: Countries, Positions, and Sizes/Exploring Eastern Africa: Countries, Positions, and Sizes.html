Exploring Eastern Africa: Countries, Positions, and Sizes

Introduction to Eastern Africa

Eastern Africa is a vibrant region located on the eastern side of the African continent. It is home to eleven amazing countries, each with its own unique culture and landscapes. Let's explore these countries and learn about their positions and sizes!

Countries of Eastern Africa

The eleven countries that make up Eastern Africa are:

  • Sudan
  • Ethiopia
  • Tanzania
  • Somalia
  • South Sudan
  • Kenya
  • Uganda
  • Eritrea
  • Burundi
  • Rwanda
  • Djibouti

Did you know? Sudan is the largest country in Eastern Africa, while Djibouti is the smallest. Some countries, like Burundi, Uganda, Rwanda, Ethiopia, and South Sudan, don't have a coastline and are called "landlocked" countries.

Exploring Positions

We can use a compass to understand where each country is located in relation to its neighbors. For example, Kenya is located:

  • Northeast of Tanzania
  • South of Ethiopia
  • Southeast of South Sudan
  • East of Uganda
  • West of Somalia

This means that Kenya shares borders with Tanzania, Uganda, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and Somalia. These are its immediate neighbors.

Locating Places with Latitudes and Longitudes

To pinpoint exact locations on a map, we use special lines called latitudes and longitudes. They're like imaginary lines that criss-cross the Earth!

Latitudes: Lines Going East-West

Latitudes, also known as parallels, are imaginary lines that run horizontally across the Earth, from east to west. The main line of latitude is the equator, which is at 0 degrees and divides the Earth into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

Here are some key characteristics of latitudes:

  • They are parallel to each other.
  • They have different lengths, with the equator being the longest.
  • They influence climate patterns.
  • They are measured in degrees, starting from 0 degrees at the equator.
  • There are 180 lines of latitude in total.

Important lines of latitude include:

  • Equator: 0 degrees
  • Tropic of Cancer: 23.5 degrees North
  • Arctic Circle: 66.5 degrees North
  • Tropic of Capricorn: 23.5 degrees South
  • Antarctic Circle: 66.5 degrees South

Longitudes: Lines Going North-South

Longitudes, also called meridians, are imaginary lines that run vertically across the Earth, from the North Pole to the South Pole. The main line of longitude is the Prime Meridian, which is at 0 degrees and passes through Greenwich, England.

Here are some key characteristics of longitudes:

  • They run from north to south, converging at the poles.
  • They are not parallel to each other.
  • They affect time zones.
  • They are all the same length.
  • They are measured in degrees, east or west of the Prime Meridian.
  • There are 360 lines of longitude in total.
